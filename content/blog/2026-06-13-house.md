## Where we are

Five days in. Day one was land and bylaws, day two was the design, day three was the foundation and the frame, day four was the envelope, and yesterday was water, power, and plumbing. If you visited the site today, the house would look almost complete from a distance. The concrete is cast, the walls are up, the roof is insulated and waterproofed, the windows and external doors are fitted, the plumbing is pressure-tested and buried in the walls, the electrical wires are pulled into the conduits and the main distribution board is waiting to be energised. Everything works. Nothing is pretty.

Inside, the walls are raw grey plaster. The floors are bare concrete. The ceilings are unfinished. The kitchen is a set of pipe stubs and electrical points on a back wall. The bathrooms are a waterproofed cement shell with a toilet flange in the floor and a tap stub on the wall. The tea shop is an empty cavity with a shutter opening at the front. The house has working bones and no clothes.

Today, Part 6, is the day the clothes go on.

Interior finishing is the longest, messiest, and most visible stage of the whole build. It takes 8 to 14 weeks, it involves more trades than any other stage (tile layers, painters, carpenters, electricians, plumbers coming back for fittings, shutter installers, false-ceiling specialists, polishers), and every single decision you make shows up on the final photograph. Unlike the foundation or the envelope, you cannot hide mistakes here. A crooked tile line you will see every morning for the next twenty years.

It is also the part of the budget where first-time builders most commonly overspend. The glossy granite in the showroom looks irresistible. The imported bathroom fittings cost "only" a little more than the Indian ones. The chandelier that looked like NPR 20,000 in the catalogue somehow costs NPR 60,000 installed. Multiply by a hundred small decisions and the finishing budget doubles.

So today we slow down, walk through the finish decisions patiently, and keep the budget in mind for every one. No programming. No code. Just the patient craft of turning a shell into a home.

## Part 1. The three finish tiers

Before we talk about any specific material, build the mental model. There are essentially **three finish tiers** for a Kathmandu residence, and most houses are actually a mix of tiers across different rooms. You do not have to pick one tier for the whole house. The right answer is usually **mid-range in the high-visibility rooms, basic in the low-visibility rooms, and one or two premium upgrades where they will actually be noticed and used.**

**Basic finish.** The contractor's default. Ceramic floor tiles at NPR 35–50 per square foot. Basic emulsion paint at NPR 15–25 per square foot. Flush wooden doors with basic hardware. Indian sanitary ware. Locally-made kitchen cabinets with Formica laminate. No false ceiling anywhere. Electrical fittings from Nepali or budget-Indian brands (Fybros, Legrand, Havells budget line). Usable, durable enough for 10–15 years, and unmistakably economy-grade to any visitor who pays attention.

**Mid-range finish.** What most middle-class Kathmandu homes in 2026 look like. Vitrified tiles at NPR 140–220 per square foot. Mid-range emulsion paint at NPR 30–45 per square foot. Solid-core flush doors with branded hardware (Hettich, Hafele). Jaquar, Cera, or Hindware bathroom fittings. Mid-range plywood kitchen with branded laminate finish and a granite countertop. False ceiling in the living room only. Schneider, Havells, or Anchor electrical fittings. Looks good, ages well for 15–20 years, and does not shout either "cheap" or "luxury."

**Premium finish.** Italian marble or large-format imported vitrified tiles at NPR 300–500 per square foot. Royale or premium-line emulsion paint at NPR 50–80 per square foot. Engineered or solid-hardwood doors with premium hardware. Kohler or Grohe bathroom fittings. Modular kitchen with BWR-grade plywood, branded hardware (soft-close drawers), and quartz countertops. False ceiling throughout with integrated cove lighting. Branded international electrical fittings (Legrand full line, ABB). Looks beautiful, lasts 25+ years gracefully, and costs two to three times the mid-range tier per square foot.

**The honest planning rule:** budget for **mid-range across the whole house**, then decide, consciously, where to upgrade one or two items to premium, and where to drop to basic. Do not try to do premium everywhere; you will run out of budget at about 70% complete. Do not do basic everywhere; you will regret it in the bedroom and the kitchen, the two rooms you actually spend time in.

For the NPR 1.6 crore target budget, **mid-range across the whole house with selective premium in the master bathroom and the kitchen** is the right calibration. That is what this article will specify. Each section will mention the basic tier (for comparison) and the premium tier (for "if budget allows" upgrades), but the recommendations default to mid-range.

## Part 2. Flooring — the decision you see every day

Flooring is the largest single visible surface in the house, and it is also the single line item where material cost varies most — from NPR 35 per sq ft for basic ceramic all the way to NPR 500+ per sq ft for imported marble. For a 3,840 sq ft built-up area, the flooring material alone can cost anywhere from NPR 1.5 lakh (cheap ceramic, whole house) to NPR 20 lakh (marble, whole house). That is a 13× range on one line item.

Four main choices for a Kathmandu residential floor:

**Ceramic tiles.** The budget option. 300 × 300 mm or 400 × 400 mm format, glazed surface, decent print variety, NPR 35–50 per sq ft material cost, NPR 50–80 per sq ft installed including cement and labour. Acceptable for utility spaces (staircase, tea shop storeroom, rear service spaces). The glaze is thin and will show wear in 5–7 years in high-traffic areas. The grout between tiles catches dirt and is hard to clean in a kitchen. **Recommend only for back-of-house spaces.**

**Vitrified tiles.** The mid-range default for most Kathmandu homes in 2026. 600 × 600 mm, 800 × 800 mm, or 1000 × 1000 mm formats. The clay is fired at much higher temperature than ceramic, giving a denser, less porous tile that is more durable, more water-resistant, and much larger — meaning fewer grout lines and a cleaner finish. Prices: NPR 140–220 per sq ft material for standard mid-range, NPR 200–260 per sq ft for better-quality brands like Kajaria. Installed, add NPR 50–80 per sq ft for labour. **This is the right choice for the main residential rooms in your house — living room, dining, kitchen, bedrooms.**

**Wood-look vitrified planks.** Long, narrow vitrified tiles printed with a wood grain. Gives the warmth of hardwood flooring at ceramic-tile durability and ceramic-tile maintenance. NPR 180–280 per sq ft material. A popular choice for bedrooms where a cooler feel is wanted in summer and the look of wood is wanted in winter. **Consider for the two bedrooms.**

**Marble.** Natural stone, premium finish, permanent (easily 50+ years). Indian marble (Makrana, Rajasthan) at NPR 200–350 per sq ft material; Italian marble (Botticino, Statuario) at NPR 400–800+ per sq ft. Installation labour is higher because marble requires careful laying, polishing, and sealing. **Worth considering only for the living-room floor and possibly the staircase treads**, where the permanence and the look genuinely reward the cost. Not recommended for bathrooms (slippery when wet), kitchen (stains from oil and turmeric), or bedrooms (too cold underfoot in winter).

### Three flooring mistakes to avoid

**Glossy tiles in the kitchen and bathrooms.** Glossy tile is slippery when wet, and wet happens daily in a bathroom and a kitchen. Use matte or textured (anti-skid) tile in any room where water touches the floor. Your guests and your elderly parents will thank you.

**Dark tile in a dark room.** A small north-facing bedroom with dark grey tile feels like a cave. Dark tiles absorb light; light tiles bounce it. Use darker tiles only in rooms that get lots of natural light and where the contrast is the design intent (the tea shop, the living room if there is a big south-facing window).

**Grout colour contrast.** White grout between grey tiles looks great on day one and filthy by month six. Match the grout colour to the tile, not against it, unless you are prepared to re-grout every two years. Dark grey grout hides kitchen spills; cream grout hides living-room dust.

## Part 3. Wall paint — what actually matters

After flooring, wall paint is the next most visible finish. The walls are the single largest surface area in the house, and the paint is what you see when you look at anything other than the floor.

### Paint types

Four broad categories to know, in increasing order of quality:

**Distemper (whitewash).** The cheapest option. Water-based, low durability, not washable. NPR 80–150 per litre material, covers about 80 sq ft per litre. Acceptable only in the most budget-constrained storage or service rooms. **Do not use in any habitable room.**

**Emulsion paint, basic.** Water-based acrylic paint, washable with a damp cloth, decent colour range. NPR 250–400 per litre, covers 80–100 sq ft per litre per coat. Two coats on a well-primed wall gives 5–8 years of reasonable appearance before it starts to look tired. **The minimum acceptable tier for habitable rooms.**

**Emulsion paint, mid-range ("silk" or "satin" finish).** Acrylic paint with slightly better binder chemistry, better washability, subtle sheen that hides minor surface imperfections. NPR 400–600 per litre. Better wear life (8–12 years), better colour retention, easier touch-up. **The default for most Kathmandu middle-class homes.**

**Premium emulsion ("royal" or "luxury" lines).** Asian Paints Royale, Berger Royale, Nerolac Impressions. NPR 600–900+ per litre. Excellent washability, deep colour, 15–20 year life, essentially invisible touch-up. Worth considering for the living room and the master bedroom only.

### Brands available in Nepal 2026

The Kathmandu paint market is dominated by Indian brands, widely distributed through local paint shops:

- **Asian Paints.** The market leader. Product tiers from Tractor Emulsion (entry, NPR 250–350/L) up through Royale Luxury (premium, NPR 700–900/L).
- **Berger.** A close second. Product tiers from Easy Clean Emulsion (basic, NPR 280–380/L) through Silk Glamour and Royale Silk (NPR 500–750/L).
- **Nerolac (Kansai Nerolac).** Strong mid-range offering. Products like Nerolac Excel, Pearls Emulsion, Impressions (NPR 280–700+/L).
- **Dulux (AkzoNobel).** Premium-positioned. Weathershield exterior lines (NPR 400–600/L), Velvet Touch interior lines (NPR 500–900/L).
- **Nippon Paints.** Japanese-origin brand; good mid-to-premium quality (NPR 400–800/L).

For a mid-range Kathmandu house, mid-range emulsion from any of the top three (Asian, Berger, Nerolac) is a safe choice. All three have effectively equivalent quality at the same price point; the differences are more about specific colour ranges and dealer relationships than product performance.

### Interior versus exterior

**Interior paint** is formulated for indoor conditions — low UV exposure, stable humidity, mild washing. Lower resistance to sun, higher resistance to human contact. Not the same product as exterior paint.

**Exterior paint** is formulated for sun, rain, and mildew. UV-resistant pigments, water-repellent binders, anti-fungal additives. Exterior paint costs 20–40% more per litre than equivalent-grade interior paint. NPR 350–750 per litre range.

**Never use interior paint on an exterior surface.** It will peel and fade in one monsoon.

### The painting sequence (what happens when)

Painting is not a single job. It is a sequence of steps spread over weeks. Your painter should do them in this order, and the contract should enforce it:

1. **Surface preparation.** The plaster must be dry (28 days minimum from when the wall was plastered), clean, and free of loose particles. Any cracks are filled with wall putty. Any high spots are sanded. This step is often rushed; it is the single biggest cause of paint failure 2–5 years out.
2. **Wall putty application.** Two coats of JK Putty, Asian Primer Putty, or equivalent, spread thinly with a trowel, sanded between coats. This creates a smooth, uniform surface that paint can adhere to. NPR 30–50 per sq ft of wall area for putty material plus labour.
3. **Primer coat.** One coat of alkali-resistant primer. Rs. 100–150 per litre. Bonds the paint to the putty and prevents alkaline salts from the wall bleeding through to the topcoat.
4. **First coat of finish paint.** Full coverage. Allowed to dry 6–12 hours.
5. **Second coat of finish paint.** Allowed to dry 24 hours before any room is occupied.

Skipping the putty step or the primer step is the contractor's most common shortcut. Check that your painter is doing both. They account for about 40% of the total labour time for a paint job and they are the difference between a 5-year finish and a 15-year one.

For 3,840 sq ft of built-up area with typical wall-to-floor ratios, you have roughly 12,000 sq ft of wall area (ceilings, interior walls, and exterior faces combined). At mid-range rates:

About NPR 5.3 lakh for paint. Within the Part 1 allocation of 3% of 1.6 crore (~NPR 4.8 lakh), with a small overrun that comes from the exterior paint premium. Reasonable.

### Three paint mistakes to avoid

**Painting before the plaster is fully cured.** Fresh plaster contains alkaline moisture that will push paint off the wall over months. Wait 28 days from plaster to paint; many contractors rush to 14 and cause 18 months of peeling.

**Skipping primer under a dark colour.** Dark colours need two coats of primer for adequate coverage and bleed-through prevention. Skip one and you get blotchy coverage that shows through for years.

**Using "extension" additives to stretch paint.** A contractor adds 20% water to the paint to get another wall painted per litre. Looks fine in week one; faded and chalky by year two. Watch for this especially if you are not paying for the paint yourself by the litre.

## Part 4. False ceilings — where they belong and where they don't

A **false ceiling** (also called a drop ceiling or suspended ceiling) is a secondary ceiling hung below the structural slab, creating a gap between the two that can hide wiring, ducts, and lighting. Two common types in Kathmandu:

**Gypsum false ceiling.** Gypsum board (typically 12.5 mm) fixed to a metal grid frame, painted with emulsion. Flat or shaped surfaces, integrated lighting, clean appearance. NPR 150–350 per sq ft installed depending on complexity. Durable, dust-resistant, easy to maintain. **The modern default.**

**POP (Plaster of Paris) false ceiling.** A gypsum plaster applied over a metal lath or gypsum board substrate, then shaped and finished by hand. Allows more sculptural shapes, cove details, and decorative mouldings. NPR 120–300 per sq ft installed. More traditional aesthetic, moderately dust-attracting, hard to repair cleanly. Being replaced by gypsum-board false ceilings in most new construction.

### Where false ceilings belong

- **Living room.** Integrated cove lighting, hides the structural beams, anchors the design. Worth the NPR 40,000–80,000 for a mid-range living room false ceiling.
- **Master bedroom (optionally).** A simple tray false ceiling with indirect lighting adds atmosphere. NPR 20,000–40,000.
- **Over the kitchen dining area (optionally).** If the kitchen is open-plan and you want to differentiate the dining zone visually.

### Where false ceilings do not belong

- **Second bedroom.** The marginal aesthetic benefit does not justify the cost or the reduced ceiling height (false ceilings eat 150–300 mm of headroom).
- **Bathrooms.** Gypsum absorbs moisture; bathroom false ceilings require waterproof PVC panels or aluminium composite, which look institutional. Skip.
- **Staircase, landings, utility spaces.** Never. A waste of budget.
- **Kitchen cooking area.** Steam and grease build up on the ceiling surface; hard to clean.

### Specification

For your house:

- Living room: simple gypsum false ceiling with peripheral cove, NPR 60,000.
- Master bedroom: tray false ceiling with indirect LED strip, NPR 30,000.
- Dining area: a small floating panel, NPR 15,000.
- Total false ceiling budget: **NPR 105,000** (around 0.7% of total build).

Resist the urge to add false ceilings elsewhere. The non-false-ceiling rooms simply get their concrete ceilings painted the wall colour or a subtle off-white, and they look clean and modern.

## Part 5. Internal doors

Different from the external doors we covered in Part 4. Internal doors separate rooms; they do not need weather stripping or thermal sealing. But they are present in the house at roughly one per room — maybe 12 to 15 doors for a three-storey residence — and each one is a touch-point that the family uses many times a day.

### Types

**Hollow-core flush doors.** A thin skin of plywood or HDF on a honeycomb cardboard interior. NPR 2,500–4,500 per door for a standard 30" × 80" leaf. Light weight, cheap, adequate acoustics in a family home. The default in most middle-class Kathmandu homes.

**Solid-core flush doors.** Same exterior appearance as hollow-core, but the interior is particle board or engineered wood core. Heavier, better sound isolation (about 25–30 dB reduction versus 15 dB for hollow-core), more resistant to impact damage. NPR 4,500–9,000 per door. **The mid-range default for bedroom doors, where sound isolation matters.**

**Panel/moulded wooden doors.** Traditional paneled door aesthetic, made from solid wood or engineered wood with a decorative routed surface. NPR 8,000–20,000 per door. Longer-lasting and visually warmer; harder to match stylistically in a modern interior.

### Hardware

Good hardware is worth the upgrade. The hinges, the lock, and the handle are what you touch every day.

- **Hinges.** Hettich or Hafele butt hinges at NPR 800–1,500 per pair, versus local hinges at NPR 150–300. The branded hinges do not creak at year three, do not sag, and have a warranty.
- **Locks.** Godrej, Europa, Dorma for bedroom and bathroom locks. NPR 1,500–3,500 per lock. Cheaper locks fail in 5–8 years.
- **Handles.** Stainless steel or powder-coated. NPR 800–3,000 per handle set.

### Specification for your house

Count the doors: ground floor half-bath (1), storeroom (1), shop interior door (1), shop-to-back-of-house door (1), staircase door (1) = 5 on the ground floor. First floor: living room (none — open), kitchen (1), half-bath (1), balcony (1), bedroom (0 on this floor) = 3. Second floor: master bedroom (1), master bath (1), second bedroom (1), second bath (1), utility (1) = 5. Plus one to the terrace. **Total: ~14 internal doors.**

- 10 × solid-core flush doors at NPR 6,000 = NPR 60,000
- 4 × hollow-core flush (for utility/storage) at NPR 3,500 = NPR 14,000
- Hardware (hinges, locks, handles) for 14 doors at NPR 2,500 per door = NPR 35,000
- Labour for hanging all doors: NPR 25,000
- **Total internal doors: ~NPR 134,000**

This is embedded in the door-and-window line from Part 1 (~NPR 11.2 lakh).

## Part 6. The kitchen — where the money usually goes wrong

The kitchen is the single most-used room in a Nepali home and also the room where finishing-budget overruns are most common. First-time builders see the glossy showroom kitchens, fall in love, and commit to a spec that consumes 10–15% of the whole house budget. Premium modular kitchens in Kathmandu can reach NPR 8–10 lakh for the cabinetry alone, not counting appliances or countertops.

Before you commit, understand what a kitchen actually is. It is three things working together:

1. **A work triangle** — the three-step circulation among stove, sink, and refrigerator. Get this right and the kitchen works. Get it wrong and no amount of fancy hardware fixes it.
2. **Storage** — enough cabinets to hold everything that is currently in the cardboard boxes scattered around your current kitchen.
3. **Surfaces** — durable, cleanable, not-too-slippery horizontal surfaces that take the punishment of Nepali cooking.

Everything else is decoration.

### Layout

We talked about this in Part 2 — for your house, an **L-shape kitchen** on the first floor, opening to the dining area, is the right layout for the floor plan we designed. Roughly 80 to 120 square feet of kitchen floor area, with cabinets along two adjacent walls.

### Cabinet materials — the BWR plywood decision

The skeleton of every modular kitchen is plywood. The quality of that plywood decides how long the kitchen lasts.

**C-grade plywood.** Budget grade, not water-resistant. NPR 1,800 per sq ft of modular kitchen (cabinet boxes). Fails in the first 3–5 years when moisture reaches it — the cabinet near the sink swells, the bottom of the cabinets under the stove warps. **Do not use in a kitchen.**

**BWP (Boiling Waterproof) plywood / BWR (Boiling Water Resistant) plywood.** Moisture-resistant grade specifically for kitchens and bathrooms. NPR 2,500–3,000 per sq ft of modular kitchen. Lasts 15–20 years in typical kitchen conditions. **This is the minimum acceptable grade for a kitchen you plan to live with.**

**Marine-grade plywood.** Premium waterproof grade, often used for boat-building. NPR 3,000–4,000 per sq ft. Overkill for a residential kitchen; the incremental benefit over BWP is small.

### Laminate versus acrylic versus PU

The outer surface of the cabinet doors:

**Laminate.** A thin printed plastic sheet glued to the plywood substrate. Wide colour and pattern range, durable enough for 15–20 years, NPR 150–500 per sq ft of door face depending on brand (Merino, Greenlam, Royale). **The mid-range default.**

**Acrylic.** A glossy plastic facing that gives a high-gloss mirror-like finish. NPR 400–800 per sq ft. Beautiful, but shows every fingerprint, every water drop, and every minor scratch. Requires daily wiping. Premium-looking, high-maintenance.

**PU (polyurethane) painted finish.** Plywood doors painted with polyurethane topcoat, producing a smooth, paintable surface in any colour. NPR 500–900 per sq ft. Can be refinished down the road (repaint after 10 years); can chip at corners; shows fewer fingerprints than acrylic.

### Countertop

Five options, in increasing order of cost and permanence:

**Laminate countertop.** NPR 300–500 per sq ft installed. Cheap, lightweight, acceptable in rental units. Scratches under normal kitchen use; burns if you set a hot pan down. Not recommended for a home you are building.

**Tile countertop.** Vitrified tile or granite tile on a cement substrate. NPR 500–800 per sq ft. Grout lines catch grease and bacteria; individual tiles can crack. Out of fashion; skip.

**Granite countertop.** Natural stone. NPR 590–1,200 per sq ft installed, depending on colour and grade. Durable (25+ years), heat-resistant, locally available in Nepal from Indian quarries. **The Kathmandu default.**

**Engineered quartz countertop.** Quartz aggregate bonded with resin. NPR 3,000–5,000 per sq ft installed. More consistent pattern than granite, more stain-resistant (non-porous), similar durability. Premium but justifiable.

**Marble countertop.** Natural marble. NPR 1,200–3,500 per sq ft. Beautiful but porous — it stains from turmeric and oil within months. Not recommended for a working kitchen.

### Specification for your kitchen

For a 100 sq ft L-shaped kitchen in mid-range finish:

- Base cabinet length: roughly 12 running feet (6 feet on each leg of the L).
- Wall cabinet length: similar.
- Cabinet structure: BWP plywood throughout, NPR 2,800 per sq ft of modular kitchen = approximately NPR 3,00,000 for cabinet structure.
- Cabinet doors: branded laminate (Merino or Greenlam), NPR 250 per sq ft = NPR 80,000.
- Countertop: granite, 12 running feet at 2 feet depth = 24 sq ft at NPR 800 = NPR 19,200.
- Hardware: soft-close hinges, drawer slides (Hettich or Hafele), pull-out baskets, NPR 50,000.
- Sink: single-bowl SS304, NPR 8,000.
- Sink tap: branded mixer, NPR 6,000.
- Chimney: auto-clean, 60 cm, Faber or Elica, NPR 25,000.
- Gas stove: 4-burner built-in, NPR 15,000.
- Labour (carpenter, installation): NPR 40,000.
- **Total kitchen: approximately NPR 5.4 lakh.**

This is solidly mid-range and consumes 3.4% of the total build budget. A premium modular kitchen with acrylic doors and quartz countertops would roughly double this to NPR 10–11 lakh. A basic kitchen with C-grade plywood and tile countertop would roughly halve it to NPR 2.5–3 lakh.

**Recommendation:** stay mid-range as specified. The premium upgrades in a kitchen are the drawer hardware (soft-close, already in the mid-range spec above) and the chimney (make sure it vents directly outside, not back into the room). The glossy acrylic doors look amazing on day one and announce every fingerprint for fifteen years; the mid-range laminate ages invisibly.

## Part 7. Bathrooms — details that decide the next twenty years

Two-and-a-half bathrooms in your house (one on each floor, plus the shop half-bath on the ground floor). The two full bathrooms on the second floor are the high-use ones; the spec there matters most.

### Waterproofing (which should already be done)

Before tiles, every bathroom floor and the lower 1.2 m of every bathroom wall should have a continuous waterproofing membrane — bituminous membrane or liquid-applied polyurethane — bonded to the cement substrate. This happens before the tile installation, ideally during the same week as the roof waterproofing we covered in Part 4. If your contractor is about to tile a bathroom without membrane, stop them.

Cost: about NPR 150 per sq ft of treated surface, typically NPR 10,000–20,000 per bathroom. Non-negotiable.

### Tile

Bathroom tile has two zones: floor tile and wall tile.

**Bathroom floor tile.** Must be anti-skid (matte or textured finish), small format (300 × 300 mm or 600 × 600 mm) to give more grout lines for traction. Vitrified tile in matte finish at NPR 160–260 per sq ft material. For a 6 × 8 ft bathroom floor, 48 sq ft at NPR 220 installed = NPR 10,500 per bathroom.

**Bathroom wall tile.** Glazed ceramic or vitrified, typically 300 × 600 mm or 300 × 450 mm format. The full height of the wet zone (shower area) should be tiled; the dry zone can be tiled to 1.2 m or to ceiling. NPR 140–250 per sq ft material. Per bathroom, 80–150 sq ft of wall tile.

For the three bathrooms in your house:

- Master bathroom: full-height wall tile (8 ft), 150 sq ft at NPR 220 = NPR 33,000 walls, plus floor NPR 11,000 = NPR 44,000.
- Common bathroom: 1.2 m wall tile with dry zone above, 100 sq ft at NPR 200 = NPR 20,000 walls, plus floor NPR 10,000 = NPR 30,000.
- Ground floor half-bath: same, smaller, NPR 18,000.
- **Subtotal bathroom tiling: NPR 92,000.**

### Sanitary ware and fittings

Mid-range Indian brands are the Kathmandu default and are reliable:

- **Toilets.** Wall-mounted or floor-mounted, dual-flush cistern. Hindware, Cera, Parryware all at NPR 8,000–15,000 per unit. Jaquar NPR 15,000–25,000.
- **Wash basins.** Wall-mounted or counter-top. Hindware, Cera at NPR 3,000–6,000. Jaquar or Kohler at NPR 6,000–15,000.
- **Shower mixer.** Jaquar or Cera at NPR 4,000–8,000.
- **Overhead shower + hand shower combo.** Jaquar at NPR 5,000–10,000.
- **Angle valves, bibs, waste fittings.** NPR 500–1,200 each. Budget NPR 8,000 per bathroom for the small fittings.
- **Mirror, towel rod, toilet-paper holder.** NPR 3,000–5,000 per bathroom.
- **Ceiling exhaust fan.** NPR 1,500–3,000 per bathroom. Non-negotiable; moisture removal.

### Two bathroom details worth paying for

**A glass shower enclosure in the master bathroom.** NPR 25,000–60,000 for a fixed or sliding glass partition that contains the shower spray. Keeps the rest of the bathroom dry, dramatically reduces mildew, and makes the bathroom feel more like a modern residence. Worth it in the master bath only.

**A separate water heater for each bathroom.** The solar water heater serves both bathrooms from the roof, but in winter when the solar tank is cooler, you want each bathroom to have a local electric instant heater (NPR 8,000–15,000) as a backup. Wired to the geyser circuit from Part 5; installed overhead in the shower area.

## Part 8. Wardrobes and built-in storage

Freestanding almirahs eat floor area. Built-in wardrobes, designed into the wall plane at the construction stage, are one of the highest-value uses of small-house real estate.

For your second-floor master bedroom, a built-in wardrobe along one wall — 8 to 10 running feet, full-height (floor to ceiling), with shelves, hanging space, and drawers — gives you the equivalent of three freestanding almirahs in the floor area of zero freestanding almirahs.

- Plywood carcass (BWP grade): NPR 2,500 per sq ft of wardrobe face = NPR 60,000 for an 8 × 8 ft wardrobe.
- Laminated doors: NPR 200 per sq ft = NPR 12,000.
- Hanging rods, shelves, drawers, hardware: NPR 20,000.
- Soft-close hardware premium: NPR 10,000.
- Mirrored sliding door (optional): NPR 25,000 extra.
- **Per-bedroom wardrobe: NPR 90,000 to NPR 130,000.**

For both bedrooms, budget NPR 180,000 to NPR 240,000 for wardrobes total. This is a separate line from the main kitchen cabinetry; it gets grouped with "interior carpentry."

Other built-in storage to design in:

- **Under-stair cabinet.** NPR 15,000–25,000. The dead space under the first flight of stairs becomes a utility cupboard. Invaluable.
- **Kitchen pantry cabinets** (separate from kitchen). A narrow floor-to-ceiling storage cabinet in the kitchen-to-dining transition area. NPR 20,000–40,000.
- **Hall coat / shoe storage.** A bench with shoe racks below and hooks above, built in near the main entrance. NPR 15,000–30,000.

All together, built-in carpentry outside the kitchen will run NPR 250,000–350,000 for this house. Included in the Part 1 "doors and windows" (7%) allocation in practice since it shares trades.

## Part 9. The tea shop fit-out

The tea shop is a commercial space that needs a different finish from the residence. Three principles:

**Durability first, aesthetics second.** The floor gets muddy boots and spilled chai every day. The walls get splatter and bag-scuffs. Specify surfaces that clean easily and do not show wear.

**Clear separation from the residence.** The shutter at the front, the interior door behind it, a separate electrical meter, a separate water connection (if allowed by KUKL). These are the boundaries that keep the shop from leaking into the house.

**Utility over show.** Customers buy tea, not atmosphere. A clean, well-lit space with comfortable seating for three to five people is all that is needed. Do not invest in chandeliers.

### The rolling shutter — the front face

A **rolling shutter** is a rolled-up metal curtain that descends from a housing above the shop opening to cover the full front when the shop is closed. Two questions: manual or motorised, and what gauge of steel.

**Manual.** A hand-operated pull strap or crank. Cheaper, simpler, requires no electricity. For a typical 8 ft wide × 8 ft tall shop opening, manual shutter costs roughly NPR 180–250 per sq ft of shutter area — that is 64 sq ft × NPR 220 = **about NPR 14,000**. Plus installation (NPR 5,000–10,000) and a rolled-steel lintel above the opening (NPR 8,000). **Total manual shutter installed: NPR 25,000–35,000.**

**Motorised.** A tube-motor drives the shutter up and down from a wall switch or remote. Cost: shutter plus motor installed is roughly NPR 80,000–150,000. The motor itself is NPR 45,000–60,000; the centre-drive or side-drive hardware adds NPR 15,000–25,000.

**Recommendation:** manual shutter for the first version of the shop, with the wiring run to the shutter location for future motorisation. The manual shutter is fine for a single-person-operated shop where the shopkeeper opens at 7 AM and closes at 7 PM. Upgrade to motorised in year two or three if the shop is busy and the shopkeeper is not the same person every day.

### Shop floor

The shop floor takes daily abuse. Specify a **darker-coloured anti-skid vitrified tile** at about 400 × 400 mm format, NPR 180–220 per sq ft. Lighter-coloured tile shows every tea drip; darker tile forgives. Spill-resistant grout (epoxy grout NPR 50–80 per sq ft additional) is worth the upgrade in the shop's high-traffic zone — grout lines are where bacteria build up.

### Shop walls

**Lower 4 feet: glazed wall tile**, easy to wipe down, hygienic. NPR 160–220 per sq ft.

**Upper wall: washable emulsion paint**, light colour (off-white or pale yellow) to reflect light.

### Shop lighting

Three layered levels:

1. **Ambient LED tube lights** in the ceiling (4 feet tubes, 24 W each). NPR 1,200–2,000 per fixture, three to four fixtures for an 8 × 12 ft shop.
2. **Task lighting** over the tea-making counter — a focused spot or two.
3. **Accent lighting** for any product display shelves.

Total shop lighting: NPR 10,000–15,000.

### Shop electrical sub-panel

From yesterday's Part 5 — a dedicated 15-A commercial meter feeds a small sub-distribution board in the shop, with separate circuits for shop lights, refrigerator, kettle/grinder, and general outlets. NPR 30,000–50,000 for the shop electrical fit-out.

### Shop plumbing

A small basin and tap for the shopkeeper's use, connected to the shop's half-bath. NPR 8,000–15,000 for the fittings.

### Shop counter and shelving

Made by the same carpenter doing the kitchen cabinets. A 10-foot long shop counter with underside storage and a small refrigerator cubby: NPR 35,000–60,000. Wall shelving for display: NPR 15,000–25,000.

## Part 10. Lighting — the layer people forget

A well-lit house has three layers of light, working together:

**Ambient light.** General room lighting. Usually ceiling-mounted LED panels or pendants that give even, shadow-less light.

**Task light.** Focused light where work happens — reading chair, kitchen counter, bathroom mirror, desk. A desk lamp, an under-cabinet strip, a mirror light.

**Accent light.** Decorative or atmospheric — cove lighting in the false ceiling, picture lights, a decorative pendant over the dining table.

Most Kathmandu houses install ambient lighting and forget the other two. A room with only ambient light feels institutional; adding task and accent transforms it.

### LED choices

LED has almost completely replaced CFL and incandescent in Kathmandu retail. Three quality tiers:

**Basic.** Generic LED bulbs (9W E27 screw, 15W B22 bayonet). NPR 150–350 per bulb. 5,000-hour rating but often fails earlier. Fine for utility spaces.

**Mid-range.** Branded LED (Philips, Syska, Havells, Wipro) with 2-year warranty. NPR 250–500 per bulb. 25,000-hour rating, honest. **The default for habitable rooms.**

**Premium.** Smart LED (colour-tunable, WiFi-connectable, dimmable). Philips Hue, Syska Smart, TP-Link Tapo. NPR 1,500–3,500 per bulb. Consider for living room and master bedroom only, if you value the remote-control convenience.

### Fixture count per room

As a rule of thumb:

- Bedroom: 4–6 light points (ceiling, side-wall reading, closet, bathroom door). About 40–60 W total LED.
- Living room: 6–10 light points (ambient ceiling, floor lamp, accent). 60–90 W total LED.
- Kitchen: 4–6 points (ambient, task, under-cabinet). 40–60 W total LED.
- Bathroom: 2–3 points (ambient, mirror, shower). 15–30 W total LED.

The electrical drawings from Part 5 should already have the light points located. Today the fittings go in.

### Budget

About 35 LED light points in a three-storey Kathmandu house, at mid-range rates:

- LEDs (branded mid-range): 35 × NPR 400 = NPR 14,000.
- Ceiling fixtures + housing: NPR 3,500 per ceiling fixture × 20 = NPR 70,000.
- Decorative fixtures (chandelier, pendant, wall sconces): NPR 25,000–60,000 for a typical mid-range house.
- **Total lighting: NPR 110,000–150,000** (about 1% of the build).

## Part 11. Finishing sequence — what happens when

A typical Kathmandu finishing stage takes 8 to 14 weeks. The correct sequence:

1. **Week 1–2.** All internal plumbing and electrical conduits verified and closed. Any remaining wall and ceiling plastering completed. Putty coat on all surfaces.
2. **Week 2–4.** Primer and first paint coat on walls and ceilings. Bathroom waterproofing completed and tested.
3. **Week 4–6.** Floor tile laying, ground floor up. Baseboard tiling in bathrooms.
4. **Week 6–8.** Wall tile laying in bathrooms and kitchen (splash zone). Granite countertop templating and fabrication.
5. **Week 8–10.** Kitchen cabinet installation. Wardrobe installation. Internal doors hung.
6. **Week 10–12.** Sanitary ware installed. Electrical fittings installed. Lighting fixtures installed.
7. **Week 12–13.** False ceiling installation (last to avoid damage during other work). Final paint coat.
8. **Week 13–14.** Final cleaning. Snag list walkthrough. Handover.

Deviations from this sequence cause damage. Tile laid before waterproofing means leaks. Paint applied before plumbing means stains. A good contractor manages the sequence; a bad one lets trades trip over each other.

## Part 12. Where to upgrade, where to save

If budget allows and you want to spend the extra rupee well, these are the highest-return upgrades:

**1. Upgrade kitchen plywood from BWP to full marine-grade and go with a quartz countertop.** NPR 1.5–2 lakh extra. Fifteen extra years of life.

**2. Upgrade the master bathroom to premium tier — glass shower enclosure, branded (Jaquar or Kohler) fittings throughout, a larger vanity.** NPR 1–1.5 lakh extra. A bathroom is a daily space; the upgrade is felt every day.

**3. Add a gypsum false ceiling with cove lighting in the living room.** Already in the mid-range spec at NPR 60,000; upgrading to a tiered design with better lighting adds NPR 30,000–50,000. Anchors the main living space.

**4. Wood-look vitrified planks in both bedrooms.** Already in the mid-range spec; upgrade to premium plank dimensions or imported brands adds NPR 40,000–80,000. The cool-in-summer, warm-looking-in-winter advantage is real.

Where to save:

**1. Skip acrylic kitchen doors.** Stick with good laminate; the aesthetic difference does not justify the daily maintenance.

**2. Keep hollow-core doors in utility rooms.** No need for solid-core everywhere. The bedrooms and the main shop-to-house door are where solid-core matters.

**3. Skip imported sanitary ware.** Mid-range Indian brands (Jaquar, Cera, Hindware) are reliably good. The Kohler-Grohe premium is real but mostly invisible after year one.

**4. Skip chandeliers and decorative lighting in every room.** One nice living-room fixture is enough; the rest can be clean, simple ceiling panels.

## Part 13. What never to skip

Seven things. Each is cheap to include now and expensive to retrofit later:

1. **Waterproofing membrane under every bathroom floor and in the wet zone of every bathroom wall.** NPR 50,000–80,000 across the house. Catastrophic to skip.
2. **BWP plywood in kitchen and bathroom cabinetry.** NPR 50,000–80,000 extra over C-grade. Non-negotiable.
3. **Exhaust fans in every bathroom.** NPR 6,000 for three. Dramatic improvement in mildew and air quality.
4. **Anti-skid floor tile in bathrooms and kitchen.** No cost premium over glossy; just specify it. Prevents falls.
5. **Door sweeps on external doors.** Covered in Part 4. NPR 2,000 total for the house; prevents bugs and drafts.
6. **Proper cabinet hardware (Hettich or Hafele).** NPR 30,000–50,000 over generic. Soft-close drawers do not fail at year three; generic ones do.
7. **Granite or anti-skid tile on the staircase treads.** NPR 70,000 for the whole staircase. Polished marble treads are a falls risk for the rest of your life in the house.

If the contractor proposes to "save money" on any of these seven, push back firmly. None of them is visible in the finished product, and all of them shape the next 25 years of the house.

## Resources and further reading

For Kathmandu-specific resources on interior finishing:

- **Kajaria Ramesh Tiles Ltd** — the leading tile supplier in Nepal; their 2026 price lists are published online and are the de facto benchmark for the Kathmandu market.
- **Asian Paints, Berger, Nerolac** — all three have colour consultation services available through their local Kathmandu showrooms. Worth a visit for the colour library alone.
- **Panchakanya Group** — Nepali supplier for PPR piping, UPVC windows, and electrical cables. Not a retailer for paint or tiles, but their profile systems and cables show up across the finishing stage.
- **Hilltake, Sintex, Kishan** — water tank brands we covered yesterday.
- **Godrej Locks, Dorma, Hettich, Hafele** — all have Kathmandu distributors; worth visiting the showroom for a physical look at the hardware.
- **Housing Nepal, House Design in Nepal, Skill Sewa, Expert Sewa** — several Nepali real-estate blogs publish updated price lists for tiles, paints, kitchen modules, and other finishing items. Cross-reference for sanity-checking contractor quotations.

For a well-written general reference on residential finishing (useful for any first-time builder):

- **Sarah Susanka's "The Not So Big House" series** — American but the principles translate. Essential reading on where space and detail belong.
- **Indian Building Code (SP 7) Volume 5** — the Indian equivalent of the Nepali NBC, with extensive sections on finishing materials and their performance standards. Widely used as a de facto reference in Nepali practice.

For an international reader: the three-tier finish model (basic, mid-range, premium) works in any market. The specific product names and prices change; the proportions and the "where-to-upgrade" logic do not.
