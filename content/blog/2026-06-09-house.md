## Yesterday, today, tomorrow

Yesterday in Part 1 we settled the pre-work: we measured the six aana (2,053 square feet), we walked through the setback, coverage, and FAR rules that decide how much of it you can build on, we introduced the naksa pass process, we named the Nepal National Building Code 105:2025 and the reasons why earthquake-resistant design is not optional, and we laid out a budget framework — aim for NPR 1.6 crore of build cost, hold NPR 40 lakh in reserve, keep the total under NPR 2 crore. We ended with two documents: a one-page written brief and a decision log.

Today, Part 2, is about translating that brief into a design.

This is the most creative part of the whole project, and it is also the part where first-time builders most often hand the pencil to someone else — the architect, the contractor, the uncle who has built three houses — and trust that the design will "just come out right." It usually does not come out right. Or rather, it comes out *adequate*: a house that is legal, that does not fall down, that has roughly the rooms you asked for. But "adequate" is not the same as "comfortable." The difference between an adequate Kathmandu house and a comfortable one is almost entirely decided at the design stage, before the first spade touches the earth.

So today we slow down and do the design work properly. We will walk the plot. We will trace the sun. We will sketch the three floors. We will talk about how to choose an architect. And at the end, you will have enough vocabulary to sit across the desk from a registered architect and have a real conversation, rather than nodding politely at someone who assumes you do not know the difference between a setback and a staircase.

No programming. No code. Plain English, plain pencil, plain common sense.

## Part 1. Before you draw anything, walk the plot

This is the single most useful thing I will tell you in the entire seven-part series, and it is free.

Before you talk to an architect, before you open a floor-plan book, before you even revise your one-page brief from yesterday — **spend three days walking your plot at three different hours of the day.** Morning (around 7:30 AM), midday (around noon), and late afternoon (around 4:30 PM). Take photos each time. Bring a notebook.

What are you looking for? Five things.

**The sun.** Stand on the plot. Look up. Where is the sun rising? Where does it go at noon? Where does it set? You are in Kathmandu Valley, at about 27.7° north latitude. On June 21st, the summer solstice, the sun rises almost due east, arcs high across the southern sky, and sets almost due west. The sun at solar noon on that day is about 85° above the horizon — nearly straight overhead. On December 22nd, the winter solstice, the sun rises south of east, arcs low across the southern sky, and sets south of west. At solar noon on December 22nd, the sun is only about 39° above the horizon — a shallow angle that lets winter sun come in through windows and warm rooms well past dinner.

What this means for design, in plain English: **in Kathmandu, the south-facing side of your house gets sun in every season. The north-facing side gets almost no direct sun ever. The east-facing side gets morning sun. The west-facing side gets afternoon sun, which in May and June is brutal.** This is the starting point of every design decision about where to put bedrooms, living rooms, and windows.

**The wind.** In the Kathmandu Valley the prevailing wind is generally from the west, with significant variation by season — westerlies dominate most of the year, with easterly components during some parts of the monsoon. Stand on the plot on a breezy afternoon and feel which direction the wind is coming from. That is your cooling wind. You want windows on that face, and windows on the opposite face, and a path between them. We will talk about cross-ventilation in Part 3 of today's article.

**The neighbours.** Where do the neighbouring houses sit, and where are their windows? A bedroom window that faces directly into a neighbour's bedroom window eight feet away is a privacy nightmare that you will try to patch later with blinds and lace curtains. A window that opens to a blank wall, or to a tree, or to the sky, is a window that actually gets used. Map where the neighbours' windows are, and plan yours to either offset or face something pleasant.

**The road.** Where does the plot meet the street? How wide is the road? Does traffic noise reach the plot? Which direction will you enter from? If there is a tea shop on the ground floor, the shop must face the street. This single constraint usually decides the orientation of the whole building.

**The view.** This one is a gift when it is there. If one side of your plot has a view of the hills, or of a nice neighbourhood temple, or of the Bagmati valley opening out, you want habitable rooms (bedrooms, living rooms) facing that side, and utility rooms (bathrooms, storage, stairs) facing the blank-wall side.

Spend three days on this. Take the photos at the same spot each time so you can compare them side by side. Write one paragraph of notes per walk. On day four, sit down with the photos and the notes and draw a rough outline of the plot on a clean sheet of paper. Mark north, south, east, west. Mark where the sun was in each photo. Mark where the wind was. Mark the road. Mark the neighbours' windows.

That paper is now the single most valuable document in the design process. Bring it to every architect interview. Every good architect will look at it and start asking better questions than they would have without it.

## Part 2. The sun path, in plain English

Let us spend a little more time on the sun, because it shapes almost everything that follows.

The sun does not actually move across the sky — the earth rotates. But from the ground, it looks like the sun travels along a curved path from east to west every day, and the height of that path changes with the season. In the northern hemisphere, the sun is lower in the sky in winter and higher in summer. The specific numbers depend on how far north you are.

Kathmandu is at latitude 27.7° N. At that latitude:

- **Summer solstice (June 21st):** sun rises at about 5:10 AM, sets at about 6:50 PM. At solar noon, the sun is about 85.5° above the horizon — essentially straight up. Summer shadows at noon are tiny.
- **Equinoxes (March 20th and September 22nd):** sun rises at about 6:00 AM, sets at about 6:00 PM. At solar noon, the sun is at about 62° above the horizon. This is a comfortable, medium-height sun.
- **Winter solstice (December 22nd):** sun rises at about 6:45 AM, sets at about 5:15 PM. At solar noon, the sun is only about 39° above the horizon. Winter shadows are long; winter sunlight pours in through low windows at a shallow angle.

The practical design implications of this are remarkable once you see them.

**The south wall gets dramatically different amounts of sun by season.** In summer, when the sun is nearly overhead, a small roof overhang of about two feet (60 cm) above a south-facing window will completely shade that window from the midday sun — because the sun is so high that its rays come in almost vertically, and the overhang intercepts them. In winter, the same overhang lets the shallow, low-angle winter sun pour right in underneath it, warming the floor. This is called **passive solar heating**, and it has been used in Nepali vernacular architecture for centuries, intentionally or not.

So: **design south-facing windows with a two-foot overhang above them.** You will be astonished at how much summer cooling and winter heating that costs-nothing detail gives you. Your architect should put this in automatically; if they do not, ask why.

**The west wall is the problem wall.** In May and June, the afternoon sun from the west hits your west-facing wall from about 3 PM onwards, at a low angle, and it heats the interior of the house brutally. An overhang does not help here, because the sun is too low to intercept. The only real defences against the west sun are:

1. **Minimise windows on the west wall.** Put bathrooms, stairs, or storage on the west face.
2. **If you must have a window on the west, put deciduous trees or a vertical shade (like a jali or perforated screen) outside it.** A tree with leaves in summer and bare branches in winter is the perfect shade — it blocks the hot summer sun but lets the welcome winter sun through. This is why traditional Kathmandu courtyards had deciduous trees on the west side.
3. **Use thick masonry on the west wall.** A heavy wall absorbs heat slowly and releases it slowly. If the wall is thick enough, the heat from a 3 PM June sun does not reach the inside of the wall until 10 PM, by which point you are ready for it to be warm because the night air is cooling. This is called **thermal lag**, and it is the reason traditional Newar houses with 18-inch brick walls are more comfortable in summer than modern houses with 5-inch walls.

**The east wall is fine.** Morning sun is gentle. East-facing windows give you a lovely wake-up glow and do not overheat the house. Put a bedroom on the east. Put the kitchen on the east. Enjoy.

**The north wall gets no direct sun.** Ever. It is always in shadow. This is good for evenly-lit workrooms — rooms where you want soft, shadow-less light — and bad for anything that needs warmth. In Kathmandu's cold winters, a bedroom on the north face will be noticeably colder than one on the south face. Put bathrooms, a pantry, stairs, or a storeroom on the north side, not a bedroom or a living room, if you can possibly help it.

In one sentence, the Kathmandu sun-path rule of thumb is: **long axis east-west, most-used rooms on the south, bathrooms and circulation on the north, morning rooms on the east, protected rooms on the west.**

Every time you look at a floor plan from now on, check it against this rule. If it fails the rule, the architect has probably prioritised something else — the road side, the view, the neighbours — and that trade-off needs to be a conscious decision, not an accident.

## Part 3. Cross-ventilation, in plain English

Here is the thing nobody tells you about Kathmandu summers. The reason most Nepali houses feel stuffy in May and June is not that the temperature is too high — maximum daytime temperatures in Kathmandu rarely go above 32°C, which is warm but not hot by the standards of Delhi or Phoenix. The reason they feel stuffy is that the **air inside the house is not moving.**

Still air at 28°C feels hotter than moving air at 30°C. This is not an opinion; it is physics. Moving air carries heat and moisture away from your skin, and your skin is what senses temperature. A ceiling fan works by moving the indoor air around. Cross-ventilation works by moving *outdoor* air through the house. Cross-ventilation is better, because it replaces the warm indoor air with cooler outdoor air, while a fan only stirs what is already there.

So how do you design for cross-ventilation? Four rules.

**Rule 1: Openings on two opposite walls.** For air to flow *through* a room, it needs somewhere to come in and somewhere to go out. A window on one wall is useless. A window on one wall *and* a window on the opposite wall is a cross-ventilation path. If both windows are the same size, air moves gently. If one window is smaller than the other, the smaller one acts as a nozzle and the air speeds up. This is why bathrooms with a small ventilator high up on one wall and a larger window low on the other wall ventilate surprisingly well — the "chimney effect" pulls air through.

**Rule 2: Windows on the prevailing-wind face and its opposite.** In Kathmandu that usually means the west face (inlet, where the prevailing westerly wind arrives) and the east face (outlet). If your plot is oriented such that the east-west axis is blocked — for example, there is a tall neighbour's wall directly to your east — then the south-north axis is your second choice.

**Rule 3: No big obstructions between the inlet and the outlet.** A closed door between the two windows kills the cross-ventilation. A tall piece of furniture blocks it. An internal wall blocks it. The best cross-ventilated rooms are the ones where you can see from one window to the other without anything in the way. When you are reviewing floor plans, draw the arrow from each window to the window on the opposite wall. If the arrow hits a wall, there is no cross-ventilation in that room.

**Rule 4: Stack ventilation is your friend.** Hot air rises. If you have a tall stairwell that opens to the roof (with a skylight or a ventilator), the hot air from all three floors rises up the stairwell and out at the top, and fresh air gets pulled in through the lower windows to replace it. This is called **stack ventilation**, and it is essentially free cooling once you have built the stairwell. Traditional Newar houses with a central courtyard open to the sky used exactly this principle — the courtyard was the chimney that pulled cool air through all the surrounding rooms.

For your six-aana Kathmandu house, the practical implications are:

- Every habitable room (bedroom, living room, kitchen) should have windows on at least two walls, on opposite or adjacent faces, not both on the same wall.
- The staircase should have a window or ventilator at the top, and ideally at every landing, so it acts as a stack ventilator.
- Bathrooms should have a small high window (for hot moist air to escape) and a slightly larger low window or ventilator slot (for fresh air to come in), even if the low one is just a 4" × 24" slit near the floor.
- The tea shop on the ground floor should have a front shutter (which opens wide during business hours, giving excellent ventilation) and a back ventilator (for cross-flow when the shutter is half-closed on quiet afternoons).

Cross-ventilation costs you nothing in rupees. It costs you a little in design attention. It saves you a fortune in air conditioning later. This is the single highest-return design decision in the whole project, and it is invisible on the final plan because it is about *where openings are placed*, not about what is built.

## Part 4. The plot orientation question

Now let us put sun and wind together and decide how the house sits on the plot.

The ideal orientation for a Kathmandu residence, from a pure passive-design perspective, is **long axis east-west.** This means the building is longer on its east-west dimension than its north-south dimension. The long south face gets winter sun in abundance; the long north face stays cool. Most rooms can touch a south-facing wall. Fewer rooms touch the problematic west or exclusively-north walls. Traditional Kathmandu vernacular architecture almost always follows this pattern when the site allows.

However — and this is important — **on a small urban plot, you rarely have the freedom to pick your orientation.** The road is where it is. The plot shape is what it is. The neighbours are where they are. You cannot rotate the plot. So the orientation question becomes: *given the constraints of the plot, how can I make the long axis as close to east-west as possible?*

Four common scenarios:

**Scenario A: Plot is roughly east-west, road on the south.** This is the best possible case. The front of the house faces south onto the road. You get the long south face for winter sun, the tea shop on the sunny south face gets a lively, warm frontage, and the north side of the plot is free for a courtyard or small garden. This is what every architect quietly prays for.

**Scenario B: Plot is east-west, road on the north.** Also fine. The tea shop fronts the road on the north (tea shops do not mind being in shade; it is cooler for customers). The long south face becomes the private face, where you put the living room and a small balcony. Bedrooms above get the south sun through their windows.

**Scenario C: Plot is north-south, road on the north or south.** This is the hardest case, because the plot is long in the wrong direction. The building will naturally want to be a tall, narrow strip, and its long face will face east or west — the two problem orientations. Your architect will need to work harder: careful placement of windows, deliberate use of the north face for service rooms, a deeper west-face overhang, maybe a small internal courtyard to bring light into the middle of the building.

**Scenario D: Plot is irregular or on a corner.** You have more flexibility. Use it to orient the *building* closer to east-west even if the *plot* is at a different angle. The setback zones around the building don't care about the building's orientation; they only care about the distance from the plot boundary.

For each scenario, your architect should be able to explain, in one sentence, why they have placed the building where they have, relative to the sun. If the answer is "because it looked right" or "this is how we always do it," you have hired the wrong architect.

## Part 5. The stacking strategy — what goes on which floor

Now we come to the core design decision for your three-storey house: what goes on the ground floor, what goes on the first floor, and what goes on the second floor? This is called **stacking**, and the Nepali vernacular has strong opinions about it, most of them correct.

The classical Newari stacking for a multi-storey urban house was: animals and storage on the ground floor (the floor is cool and the animals warm the floor above), sleeping rooms on the first floor (away from the cold ground and the street noise), the kitchen and altar on the top floor (because the kitchen stove could vent directly to the roof and because the altar needed the best light). That stacking worked for three hundred years. It is still a pretty good model, with updates.

For your house — tea shop, plus 2 BR, plus kitchen-living, plus 2.5 bathrooms — here is the stacking I would strongly recommend:

### Ground floor: tea shop, half-bath, small storage, staircase

The ground floor has two big pieces. The tea shop must be on the street side, with a shutter opening directly to the road and the shop counter facing the street. Ceiling height 10 to 11 feet (higher ceilings make a shop feel more generous; it also raises the first-floor ceiling by the same amount, which is good for the kitchen and living room above). 

Behind the tea shop, you need: a half-bath with its own external access (so shop customers can use it without walking into the house), a small storage room for shop goods (sacks of sugar, crates of bottles), and the staircase up to the residential floors. The staircase should have its own internal entry separate from the shop — ideally a small lobby or vestibule where you leave your shoes, receive a visitor, and then proceed up.

If there is any space left over, use it for parking a motorbike or scooter. Forget car parking on six aana; a car takes 120 to 140 square feet and the plot does not have it to spare.

Total ground floor footprint: the full 1,280 square feet we estimated yesterday.

### First floor: kitchen, dining, living room, half-bath (the "half" shared with the shop)

The first floor is the main daytime living level for the family. Kitchen and dining on the east side (morning light, warmth for morning coffee, kitchen smoke rising directly into a vent on the east face). Living room on the south side (afternoon and evening light, a balcony if possible). Small family half-bath, tucked near the staircase, for quick use during the day.

The kitchen deserves its own paragraph. Kitchens in Kathmandu get used heavily — dal-bhat twice a day, frying in hot oil, pressure cookers whistling. A bad kitchen is one of the fastest ways to ruin a house. Good kitchen design for this plan:

- **An exhaust hood vented directly to the outside wall**, not to a shaft and not to a ceiling void. The hood run should be no more than about six feet of duct, straight. Long ducts lose suction and leak grease.
- **A window over the sink** so the person washing dishes can look at something pleasant rather than a wall.
- **Counter space on three sides of the gas stove**, not two. One side for the cut vegetables, one for the spices, one for the next pot. Nepali cooking involves many simultaneous pots; a cramped kitchen produces cramped food.
- **A gas line brought in from an external cylinder cabinet**, with a valve on the outside wall, not a cylinder sitting under the counter. Cylinders under counters are a common Kathmandu fire hazard.
- **A small pantry or storage room** off the kitchen, ideally on the north side so it stays cool. Rice, lentils, masalas, onions — they all keep longer in a cool pantry.

Total first floor footprint: the full 1,280 square feet. Of that, roughly 220 for the kitchen, 100 for the pantry, 160 for dining, 350 for living room, 60 for the half-bath, 120 for the staircase and circulation, 50 for a balcony. Numbers add to around 1,060; the remainder is wall thickness and buffer. Your architect will refine these.

### Second floor: two bedrooms, two full bathrooms

The second floor is the sleeping floor. Two bedrooms, two full bathrooms (showers and toilets both), and the staircase.

**Master bedroom on the south-east corner.** Morning sun through the east window, afternoon and evening sun (light, not glare) through the south window. Cross-ventilation from east to west across the room. One full bathroom attached to this bedroom (called an *en-suite*), small, efficient, with its own window on the north face.

**Second bedroom on the south-west corner** — yes, the west face, which we said is the problem face. The reason: if you put the second bedroom on the *north*, it will be cold in winter (no sun) and will feel like a consolation-prize room. If you put it on the south-west, you at least get warm winter afternoons and some view; the summer-afternoon heat problem can be solved with a deep overhang and an overhanging deciduous tree on the west side of the plot, if one can be planted. The second full bathroom serves this room; place it on the north wall so the bedroom still gets a south window.

**Staircase and landing in the middle.** Both bedrooms should open off a small landing, not directly off the staircase. A landing gives you a moment of transition and lets you close the bedroom door against the kitchen smells wafting up.

**Light well or internal ventilator in the middle.** If possible, put a small skylight or clerestory window above the staircase, so light comes down into the core of the house and so the stack-ventilation effect draws air upward through the building.

Total second floor footprint: the full 1,280 square feet. Of that, roughly 240 per bedroom, 55 per bathroom, 150 for staircase and landing, 30 for a utility closet, 60 for a terrace or balcony if you can afford one. Numbers add to around 830; the remainder is wall thickness and what you do with the extra.

### Rooftop: water tanks, solar, clothesline, small terrace room (optional)

On the roof you put the overhead water tank (we will discuss this in Part 5), the solar water heater if you install one (Part 5), a clothesline shelter, a small tool shed, and if budget allows, a single-room *tahakha* (roof-top room, penthouse-style) of about 300 to 400 square feet for use as a guest room, a study, or a meditation room.

The rooftop terrace is one of the Kathmandu house's most loved spaces. Summer evenings on the roof are the best summer evenings. Leave some open roof; do not build out the whole top.

### Total built-up, re-checked against budget

Three floors of 1,280 sq ft = 3,840 sq ft. Plus a 400 sq ft terrace room = 4,240 sq ft. At NPR 4,200 per sq ft (mid-range, leaning economical), that is NPR 1.78 crore. Over the NPR 1.6 crore target but under the NPR 2.0 crore ceiling, with the reserve squeezed.

If money is tight: drop the terrace room. Back to 3,840 sq ft × NPR 4,200 = NPR 1.61 crore. On budget. The terrace room can be built later as phase 2 if the tea shop generates enough income over five years. Many Nepali families build exactly this way — the shell of the terrace room (a concrete slab and stub columns for future walls) is cast now, and the walls and finish are added in year five or seven. The incremental cost of the phase-2 finishing is small because the hard structural work is already done.

## Part 6. The staircase problem

Staircases are harder to design well than first-time builders imagine. They are also the single most expensive-per-square-foot element of the house, because every floor needs one, they require a complicated void in the slab, and the code enforces specific minimum dimensions.

Two numbers to remember:

**Staircase footprint per floor: about 80 to 120 square feet.** A modest three-storey house with a straight-run or U-shaped staircase will give up this much floor area on each floor to the stairs and the landing above them. Over three floors that is 240 to 360 square feet of area permanently committed to vertical movement. On a 1,280 sq ft footprint, that is 6–10% of every floor dedicated to the stairs. Not small.

**Minimum code dimensions for residential staircases in Nepal:** The step width (*tread*) should be at least 250 mm (10 inches), and the step height (*riser*) should be at most 190 mm (7.5 inches). The staircase width should be at least 900 mm (3 feet), with 1,000 mm (3.3 feet) preferred for comfortable two-way traffic. There must be a handrail on at least one side, at 900 mm (3 feet) above the step. A landing is required every 12 to 14 steps.

The three common staircase layouts for a narrow Kathmandu plot:

**Straight run.** A single flight of stairs from ground to first floor, with a landing, then another straight flight to second floor. Easiest to build, easiest to use, but eats a long strip of the plot (typically 12–14 feet long × 3.5 feet wide per flight).

**U-shape (*dog-leg*).** The stairs run half the distance, turn 180 degrees on a landing, and run back the other way. Total footprint is a rectangle about 9 feet × 8 feet. This is the most common Kathmandu staircase layout because it fits compactly into a corner.

**L-shape (*quarter-turn*).** The stairs run a few steps, turn 90 degrees on a small landing, and continue. Useful when the room geometry forces a turn. Slightly more complex to build.

For your house, the U-shape is almost certainly the right choice, tucked into the north-west corner of the plot where it can double as a buffer against the west sun. The staircase landings become convenient places to put a window or a small bookshelf.

A few staircase details that make a real difference:

- **Underside of the stairs** on the ground floor should not be wasted. It is a natural spot for a small cabinet, a shoe rack, or a pantry.
- **Each landing should have a window**, even a small one. Unventilated staircases in Kathmandu become hot in summer and cold in winter, and both extremes are unpleasant to walk through twenty times a day.
- **A skylight or roof vent at the top of the stairwell** turns the whole staircase into a stack ventilator, as we discussed earlier. This detail is almost free to include during design and expensive to retrofit later.
- **Handrails on both sides** if you think an elderly family member may live in the house. The marginal cost is tiny; the benefit when knees go bad is large.
- **Step nosing in a grippy material** (not polished marble). Kathmandu stairs kill people. Do not add glossy surfaces to stairs.

## Part 7. Making six aana feel like eight

Six aana is tight. A well-designed six-aana house can feel spacious; a poorly designed one can feel claustrophobic. Here are the half-dozen design tricks that make small spaces feel large. Most of them cost nothing; a few cost a little.

**Volume over area.** Ten-foot ceilings feel much larger than nine-foot ceilings, even though the floor area is the same. The marginal structural cost of an extra foot of ceiling height is a few lakh over the whole house. The perceived-size benefit is significant. Budget for 10-foot ground floor ceilings and 9.5-foot ceilings above.

**Long sight lines.** A room where you can see all the way from one corner to the opposite corner feels bigger than one where the view is blocked. Open up the kitchen-to-living transition with a wide opening or half-wall, rather than a solid wall with a doorway. A clear view from the living room window, across the room, to the dining area makes the first floor feel like one connected space rather than two boxes.

**Light coming from two sides of every room.** A room with one window feels like a cave. A room with windows on two adjacent walls feels open, even if the total glass area is the same. The brain uses the contrast between two light sources to judge the size of the space; with two sources, it judges correctly.

**Pale, consistent colours.** White walls, off-white ceilings, pale floor tiles. Colour can go on accent items (a coloured feature wall, coloured sofa cushions, a coloured rug), but the underlying palette should be pale and consistent. Dark walls absorb light and make rooms feel smaller. This is free; you just have to not be tempted into the dark wood panelling look that was briefly fashionable in the 2010s.

**Built-in storage in every corner.** A six-aana house does not have room for freestanding almirahs in every bedroom. Build storage into the walls — under the stairs, along one wall of each bedroom, above doorways (a high shelf at 7 feet is invisible and useful). Built-in storage takes up much less space than freestanding furniture because it absorbs the wall thickness into the storage depth.

**A roof terrace doubles the usable "outdoor" space.** Six aana at ground level is not enough for a garden and a courtyard both. Six aana plus a 600 sq ft flat terrace is plenty for all your outdoor-ish activities — morning tea, drying clothes, a small herb garden in pots, evening gatherings in spring and autumn. Design for the terrace from day one — parapet walls (code minimum 3 feet), a water tap, a drain, a shade canopy attachment point.

**Avoid corridors.** A hallway is wasted space in a small house. On each floor, aim to have the staircase open directly into a small lobby that each room opens off of, so there is no dedicated corridor area. This saves 60–80 square feet per floor.

## Part 8. How to find an architect

Now, the question every owner asks at this stage: **how do I find a good architect?**

There are about 1,500 registered architects in Nepal, most of them working in and around Kathmandu Valley. A small house like yours — 4,000-ish square feet, mixed-use, three storeys — is a project that most of them can design competently. But "competently" is a wide range. The top 10% produce beautiful, livable, climate-sensitive, budget-respecting designs. The bottom 30% produce generic photocopies of the last house they did. You want to hire from the top 10%.

Three sources to start from:

1. **Ask to see finished houses you like.** Drive around the neighbourhoods near your plot. When you see a house you like, note the address. Ring the bell (or wait for the owner to come out and gently ask). Most Kathmandu homeowners are flattered to be asked who their architect was; they will tell you gladly. This is by far the highest-signal way to find an architect.
2. **Ask your structural engineer.** If you have already found a structural engineer you trust, they have worked with many architects and know which ones produce designs that are structurally sensible. The best architect-engineer pairs are well-known to each other; ask for a short list.
3. **Online portfolios.** Most Nepali architects have Facebook or Instagram pages with photos of finished projects. Useful for a first impression, less useful for depth. Look for architects whose portfolio shows climate-responsive design, not just photogenic finishes.

Avoid: architects who do not want to visit the site before drawing, architects who send you a portfolio of 20 nearly identical houses, architects who will not give you the phone numbers of past clients, architects who quote a fee that is dramatically lower than others (they either do not know the work, or they will cut corners to cover their losses).

### The fee conversation

Architectural fees in Nepal are typically 3–6% of the total construction cost for a residential project, with the exact percentage depending on the firm's reputation, the project's complexity, and the scope of services — design-only versus full project supervision. For your NPR 1.6 crore target, that is NPR 4.8 lakh to NPR 9.6 lakh. Structural engineer fees are additional; 2–5% for combined architect-plus-engineer is a realistic range.

Three fee structures you will encounter:

**Percentage of construction cost.** The standard model. 3–6% as noted. Advantage: scales with the project naturally. Disadvantage: a small incentive for the architect to specify expensive items, since their fee goes up.

**Flat fee.** A fixed rupee amount regardless of construction cost. Common for small projects. NPR 3 lakh to NPR 8 lakh for a three-storey mixed-use house. Advantage: aligned incentives — they have no reason to gold-plate the design. Disadvantage: they may cut the scope of services (fewer site visits, less detailed drawings) to protect their margin.

**Stage-based payments.** The fee is split into milestones: concept design, preliminary drawings, naksa pass submission, detailed construction drawings, site supervision. Typical split is roughly 20% / 20% / 20% / 20% / 20%. Almost all fee contracts should be stage-based for payment timing, whether the total is percentage or flat. **Never pay the full fee upfront.**

Standard scope of services for a residential project should include:

- **Concept design:** 2–3 options for the floor plan, with rough sketches of the elevation. One round of revisions.
- **Preliminary design:** one option developed in detail, all floor plans, front and side elevations, rough finish specifications. Two to three rounds of revisions.
- **Naksa pass drawings:** the full submission package for the municipality, with setbacks, coverage calculation, structural engineer's stamp co-ordination, soil report integration.
- **Construction drawings:** the detailed working drawings that the contractor will build from. Floor plans with dimensions, door and window schedules, bathroom elevations, kitchen elevations, electrical layout, plumbing layout, structural drawings (usually produced by the structural engineer in coordination).
- **Site visits during construction:** typically fortnightly or monthly visits to check that the build matches the drawings. Number of site visits should be written into the contract.
- **Completion and handover:** a final set of "as-built" drawings and the paperwork for the municipal completion certificate.

If any of these is missing from the proposed scope, ask why.

## Part 9. Five questions to ask at the architect interview

Interview three architects. Not one. Not two. Three. The difference in their approaches will teach you more than a hundred articles like this one.

At each interview, bring: your one-page brief from yesterday, your plot-walk notes from the last three days, and this list of five questions. Ask every architect the same five questions. Write down their answers. Compare them that evening.

**Question 1: "Here is my plot and my brief. Before I even tell you more, what three questions would you ask me?"**

The best architects answer this brilliantly. Their three questions will reveal what they actually think about first. "Who will live here in ten years?" (thinking about the future). "What is the hottest and coldest indoor temperature you can tolerate?" (thinking about passive design). "Where will you work, and what is your commute like?" (thinking about lifestyle). Weak architects answer with procedural questions: "Do you want marble floors?" "What colour paint?" These are decoration questions, not design questions.

**Question 2: "How will you orient the house on this plot, and why?"**

Watch them look at the plot drawing. Watch them think about the sun. A great architect will draw the sun-path on a napkin and explain which way the long axis should run. A mediocre architect will say, "we'll face it towards the road." Acceptable, if they can then explain the trade-offs. Unacceptable if they have no further thought.

**Question 3: "The budget ceiling is NPR 2 crore for construction, and my target is NPR 1.6 crore. What scope of design can you deliver at that budget, and what should I be prepared to cut if we run over?"**

A professional architect will answer this directly, with specific items: "At 1.6 crore for 4,000 sq ft we are at mid-range. The place to cut, if we run over, is the terrace room and the finish level. I would not cut structural quality or insulation." A weak architect will say, "we can do anything within the budget" — which is not true and suggests they have not thought about the trade-offs.

**Question 4: "What is your approach to earthquake-resistant design? Do you work with a structural engineer, or do I need to hire one separately?"**

The right answer is: "I work with [name of specific engineer], we have done [number] of houses together, and their stamp will be on all structural drawings. My designs are NBC 105:2025 compliant by default, and I avoid irregular plans, cantilever extensions beyond a certain size, and discontinuous columns." The wrong answer is vague: "We follow the code" or "We use standard details."

**Question 5: "Can I visit two houses you have designed that have been lived in for at least three years, and can I talk to the owners?"**

Every good architect has a portfolio of finished work. Every great architect has owners who will vouch for them. If the answer is evasive, this is a big red flag. If the answer is "of course, here are three, let me call the owners" — you have probably found your architect.

After three interviews, sit with your partner or a trusted friend, lay out the notes, and compare. One architect will stand out. It will probably not be the cheapest. That is fine. A good architect on a small house saves you more than their fee in avoided mistakes alone.

## Part 10. What the first meeting with the architect you hire will produce

Once you have hired your architect, the first working meeting should produce three deliverables within four to six weeks:

**1. A site-analysis document.** A small report (five to ten pages) documenting the plot, the sun path, the wind pattern, the neighbour context, the setbacks, the coverage limit, the FAR, and the climate data. This is the foundation of the design; it should be shared with you and with the structural engineer.

**2. Two or three concept designs.** Rough sketches of the floor plan, at 1:100 scale, showing the stacking, the room sizes, and the major circulation. Each concept should have a one-paragraph description of why it exists and what it prioritises. Compare them; pick a favourite; ask for revisions.

**3. A preliminary cost estimate.** Based on the chosen concept, a rough cost estimate per square foot for the construction, a category breakdown (as we laid out in Part 1), and a red-flag list of items that might push the budget up.

Expect this stage to take four to six weeks. It feels slow. It is not slow. It is the work that makes the next eighteen months of building go well.

Resist the urge to skip ahead to detailed drawings. The concept and preliminary design is where the house is really designed; everything after that is elaboration. Spend the time here.

## Resources and further reading

For climate-responsive design specifically in the Nepali context, the research literature is small but excellent. Three sources:

- *Climate Responsive Building Design in the Kathmandu Valley*, Anir Kumar Upadhyay et al., Tribhuvan University — a foundational paper on the applicability of vernacular Newar design principles to modern construction.
- The *Guidelines for Climate Responsive Building Design in Three Regions of Nepal*, published through the Tribhuvan University Institute of Engineering — a readable overview of passive-design strategies for subtropical, temperate, and cold regions.
- *Climate responsive building design strategies of vernacular architecture in Nepal*, published in Energy and Buildings (2014) — a comparative analysis of traditional Nepali architecture across climate zones.

For architecture firm directories, the Society of Nepalese Architects (SONA) maintains a list of member firms. Not every competent architect is a member, but SONA membership is a reasonable first filter.

For an international reader following along: the sun-path arithmetic in this article is specific to latitude 27.7° N. If you are building elsewhere, use a free online tool like SunCalc or the University of Oregon sun-path chart generator to produce a site-specific diagram for your latitude. The passive-design principles — long axis east-west, most-used rooms on the sun side, bathrooms and service rooms on the shade side, cross-ventilation through every habitable room — are universal; only the specific angles change.

## Part 12. The short version, in one paragraph

On a six-aana plot in the Bhaktapur side of the Kathmandu Valley, a three-storey mixed-use house with a 1,280 sq ft footprint (after setbacks), a ground-floor tea shop on the road side, a first-floor kitchen-and-living level, and a second-floor with two bedrooms and two-and-a-half bathrooms is best designed by first walking the plot at three hours of the day, tracing the sun path (long axis east-west if the plot allows), identifying the prevailing wind, and laying out the rooms so that habitable spaces face south and east, service rooms face north and west, and every habitable room has openings on two walls for cross-ventilation; the staircase should be a U-shape in the north-west corner with a skylight at the top for stack ventilation; the right architect is found by interviewing three, asking the five questions, visiting their finished projects, and comparing honestly; the architect's fee will be 3–6% of the construction cost and the structural engineer's an additional 2%; and the design phase should take four to six weeks of concept and preliminary work before any detailed drawing begins.

Everything in Parts 3 through 7 will build on the design produced by this stage. If that paragraph is the only thing you take from today, you are still in good shape.
