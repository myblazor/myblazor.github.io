## Welcome to the Most Stressful Meeting of Your Career

Picture this: it is a Tuesday afternoon, and you are sitting in a conference room that smells of old carpet and someone's reheated lunch. You are three weeks into a new job — or maybe you were just handed a project that nobody else wanted — and you are looking at a legacy ASP.NET Web Forms or MVC application that was last significantly modernized sometime before your company switched from Internet Explorer 11 to "whatever the default browser is now." The application works. It does what it is supposed to do. But the user interface looks like it was designed by a committee in 2008, and every time somebody asks you to add a new feature, you have to trace through three layers of UpdatePanel spaghetti or a Razor view with ViewBag properties being passed around like a hot potato at a county fair.

Somebody in leadership — it is always somebody in leadership — has heard that modern web applications should be built with React. Maybe they read an article. Maybe their nephew who graduated six months ago mentioned it. Maybe a consultant said the word "component-based architecture" in a presentation and everyone nodded like they understood what that meant. Now the team is having a Conversation. Capital C, because it is the kind of conversation that is going to determine what the next two years of your professional life look like.

And there, sitting silently in the back of the room, is the elephant: React.

Except — and here is the thing nobody mentioned in the meeting — there is another option on the table. Because you are a .NET developer, and Microsoft has been building something called Blazor WebAssembly, which lets you write front-end web applications in C#. Real C#. Not TypeScript pretending to be C#. Not JavaScript that kind of looks like C# if you squint. Actual, first-class, System-namespace-importing, `async`/`await`-using, `IServiceCollection`-registering C#, compiled to WebAssembly and running directly in the browser without a server required.

This series exists for you. All seven parts of it. Because the React-versus-Blazor conversation is not simple, and anyone who tells you it is simple has probably not spent enough time with both technologies to know what they do not know. We are going to dig deep — not just into "here is how you build a button in each framework" but into the genuine architectural differences, the mental model differences, the ecosystem differences, and the real-world production consequences of choosing one path over the other.

You do not need to be an expert in either technology before reading this. You need to be an ASP.NET developer who can write C#, who understands HTTP requests and responses, who knows what a database is, and who is willing to sit down and think carefully about some genuinely interesting problems. If you barely know which end of a keyboard to type on, do not worry — we are going to explain everything from the ground up. We are going to be patient. We are not going to assume you know what a Virtual DOM is, or what JSX does, or how WebAssembly executes in a browser. We are going to start from the beginning and build up.

## Part 2: Setting the Stage — What Year Is It, and Why Does That Matter?

Before we talk about React versus Blazor, we need to establish where we are in history. Both technologies are moving targets, and a lot of advice you will find on the internet was written for versions that are now significantly out of date.

### React in 2026

React was created by Jordan Walke at Facebook (now Meta) and was first used internally in 2011. It was open-sourced in May 2013. For several years after that, it was seen as an interesting experiment from a company with very specific engineering problems — Facebook's news feed is a genuinely unusual interface, and the team invented React to solve problems they had with their existing approach.

React 18 landed in March 2022 and introduced Concurrent Mode, Suspense improvements, and automatic batching — all of which were significant architectural changes. React 19.0 was released on December 5, 2024, and brought Actions, new hooks (`useActionState`, `useOptimistic`, `useFormStatus`), the `use()` hook for reading Promises in render, first-class support for `<title>` and `<meta>` tags, the React Compiler (experimental, which automatically handles memoization), and improved Server Components support. React 19.1 followed in March 2025, and React 19.2 arrived in October 2025 with `Activity`, `useEffectEvent`, and React Performance Tracks. As of April 2026, the latest stable version is React 19.2.1 (released December 2025).

React is, without any serious debate, the most widely adopted JavaScript UI library on the planet. According to the 2025 State of JavaScript survey, React usage among JavaScript developers sits north of 80%. It has a massive ecosystem, enormous community, and more third-party libraries than any other front-end framework by a significant margin.

### Blazor WebAssembly in 2026

Blazor was announced by Steve Sanderson at Microsoft at NDC Oslo in 2017 as an experimental project. It shipped as an officially supported product in .NET Core 3.1 in May 2020 (for Blazor Server) and in Blazor WebAssembly (WASM) in the same release. With .NET 6, Blazor received major improvements including hot reload. With .NET 7 and 8, Blazor got render mode infrastructure, streaming rendering, and enhanced navigation. .NET 10, released in November 2025 and current as of this writing in 2026, continues to improve Blazor's performance, startup time, and tooling.

Blazor WebAssembly specifically means: your C# code is compiled to WebAssembly, which runs directly in the browser. The .NET runtime itself (a version of the Mono runtime compiled to WASM) is downloaded by the browser and executes your IL code. In the AOT (ahead-of-time) compilation mode, your application code is compiled to native WebAssembly bytecode rather than IL, which makes it significantly faster to execute once loaded, at the cost of a larger initial download.

The key phrase to understand for this series is "static deployment." Blazor WebAssembly can be published as a set of static files — HTML, CSS, JavaScript glue code, and WebAssembly files — that can be served from any static file host, including GitHub Pages, Netlify, Azure Static Web Apps, or an S3 bucket. There is no server required at runtime. Your C# code runs in the browser. This puts Blazor WASM in the same deployment category as a React single-page application — both are static, both can run without a server, and both can call APIs over HTTP.

## Part 3: The Legacy Project Problem — What Are You Actually Modernizing?

Let us be specific about the scenario this series is addressing, because "modernizing a legacy ASP.NET application" can mean wildly different things depending on where you are starting from.

### Scenario A: ASP.NET Web Forms

ASP.NET Web Forms launched with .NET Framework 1.0 in 2002. It is a server-side event-driven programming model that was designed to help Visual Basic 6 developers move to web development by hiding HTTP from them as much as possible. You have a `.aspx` page, a code-behind `.aspx.cs` file, and a collection of server controls that render HTML on the server and communicate with the client through a mechanism called ViewState and UpdatePanel (which is essentially a partial-page AJAX update wrapper around the old event model).

If you have a Web Forms application in 2026, you have a problem that is simultaneously technical and organizational. The technical problem is that Web Forms is not supported on modern .NET — it runs only on .NET Framework, which means it runs only on Windows, which means you are stuck on an old runtime with no path to modern .NET 10 features unless you rewrite. The organizational problem is that a lot of Web Forms applications have years of business logic baked directly into the code-behind files, making the task of extracting that logic before modernizing the front-end genuinely difficult.

### Scenario B: ASP.NET MVC (the Classic Kind)

ASP.NET MVC launched in March 2009 with .NET Framework 3.5 SP1. It introduced a proper model-view-controller separation, Razor views, and a testable architecture that was dramatically better than Web Forms. If you have an ASP.NET MVC application on .NET Framework 4.x (4.6, 4.7, 4.8), you are in a somewhat better position than Web Forms, because the separation of concerns is cleaner and there is a more direct migration path to ASP.NET Core.

The migration path from classic ASP.NET MVC to ASP.NET Core MVC is genuinely feasible and has been documented extensively by Microsoft. If your application is primarily server-rendered HTML with some jQuery-based interactivity, you might not need React or Blazor at all — you might just need to move to ASP.NET Core with modern Razor Pages or MVC, which gives you a vastly better server-side rendering story.

But if your application has grown to require significant client-side interactivity — real-time updates, complex data grids, multi-step forms with complex validation, drag-and-drop, charts that update live — then you are at the crossroads this series is written for.

### Scenario C: ASP.NET Core MVC or Razor Pages — But the UI Is Aging

Perhaps you are already on ASP.NET Core. The server-side is in good shape. But the front-end is a combination of jQuery, some Bootstrap, possibly a plugin or two from npm that was added three years ago and nobody is sure how it got there, and one page that uses React because a contractor added it and then left. You want to move toward something coherent and modern, and the question is: React or Blazor?

This is actually the most common scenario for teams in 2026, and it is the scenario we are going to spend the most time addressing.

## Part 4: The Elephant Examined — Why React Is Such a Powerful Default Choice

Let us be fair to the elephant. React is the default choice for good reasons, and dismissing it as "JavaScript chaos" when you are a comfortable C# developer is intellectually lazy. Here is why React has the position it has.

### Network Effects and the Ecosystem

React has been the dominant UI library for about a decade. That means there is an enormous ecosystem of high-quality, actively maintained libraries built specifically for React. You need a date picker? There are seven of them. You need a data grid that handles 100,000 rows with virtualized scrolling? AG Grid, TanStack Table, and React Window are all mature, well-documented options. You need charting? Recharts, Victory, Nivo, Visx. You need rich text editing? Slate.js, TipTap, Lexical. You need drag-and-drop? dnd-kit. You need forms with complex validation? React Hook Form with Zod.

This ecosystem represents years of effort by thousands of developers. When you choose React, you are choosing to have access to this ecosystem immediately, with npm install and a few lines of code.

### The Talent Pool

React developers are abundant. When you hire someone who says they are a "front-end developer" in 2026, there is an overwhelming probability that they know React. This matters enormously for team velocity, hiring, and onboarding. A team of ASP.NET developers who have never written React will need to climb a learning curve. A team of React developers does not need to climb any curve.

### The Default Stack for New Applications

If you look at the most popular new application stacks in the JavaScript ecosystem — Next.js with React, Remix with React, Expo with React Native — React is the constant. If you are building a new product and you want to hire quickly, build quickly, and access the widest possible library ecosystem, React is the path of least resistance.

### Why This Matters for Your Legacy Project Decision

If your team has front-end experience, existing React code, or needs to hire front-end engineers quickly, React has a strong argument. If you are adding a new front-end team that will work separately from the back-end .NET team, React is often the right default.

## Part 5: The Other Elephant — Why Blazor WebAssembly Is a Serious Contender

Now let us give Blazor WebAssembly its fair hearing. Because if you are reading this on My Blazor Magazine, you presumably have at least some suspicion that C# in the browser might be a real option.

### The C# Developer Experience

If you have spent years writing C#, Blazor WebAssembly offers something genuinely remarkable: you write your front-end code in the same language as your back-end code, with the same IDE, the same debugger, the same package manager (NuGet), the same dependency injection container, the same logging infrastructure, the same test framework (xUnit or NUnit), and the same type system.

This means that a ASP.NET developer can be productive in Blazor almost immediately. The learning curve is dramatically smaller. You are not context-switching between C# and JavaScript/TypeScript. You are not maintaining two separate sets of model classes — you can share your domain models between the server and the client directly. You can define a `BlogPost` class in a shared class library, reference it from your ASP.NET Core API, and reference it again from your Blazor WebAssembly client. Both sides of the wire use the same type. When you serialize to JSON and deserialize on the client, you are using `System.Text.Json` on both ends.

### Code Sharing and Shared Libraries

One of the most powerful aspects of Blazor WASM is the ability to create shared class libraries (`.csproj` with `<TargetFramework>net10.0</TargetFramework>`) that are referenced by both the server project and the client project. Your business logic, your validation rules, your data transfer objects, your utility functions — all of it can live in one place and be used everywhere.

In a React application, you typically have separate TypeScript interfaces that mirror your C# classes on the server. When you change the server model, you have to remember to update the TypeScript interface. Forget to do that, and you have a type mismatch that only shows up at runtime. In Blazor, this problem simply does not exist — the types are defined once and shared.

### First-Class Dependency Injection

ASP.NET Core's dependency injection system is elegant, well-understood by .NET developers, and deeply integrated into the platform. When you write a Blazor WebAssembly application, you get the same `IServiceCollection` registration model, the same `IServiceProvider` resolution, and the same singleton/scoped/transient lifetime semantics that you already know. 

Registering a service in Blazor WASM looks like this:

```csharp
// In Program.cs
builder.Services.AddScoped<IBlogService, BlogService>();
builder.Services.AddSingleton<ITelemetryService, TelemetryService>();
builder.Services.AddHttpClient<IWeatherService, WeatherService>(client =>
{
    client.BaseAddress = new Uri(builder.HostEnvironment.BaseAddress);
});
```

Then consuming it in a component:

```csharp
@inject IBlogService BlogService
@inject ITelemetryService Telemetry

@code {
    private List<BlogPostMetadata>? _posts;
    
    protected override async Task OnInitializedAsync()
    {
        _posts = await BlogService.GetAllPostsAsync();
        Telemetry.TrackPageView("Blog");
    }
}
```

This is not a thin abstraction layer that pretends to be dependency injection. This is the real thing, running directly in the browser, resolving your service graph from a proper DI container, enforcing lifetimes, and disposing services when their lifetime scope ends. For a .NET developer, this is home.

### Testability

Because Blazor components are C# classes, they can be tested with standard .NET test tooling. The `bUnit` library (which is free and open source under the MIT license) provides a component testing framework that lets you render Blazor components in memory, inspect their rendered output, and trigger user interactions, all from within an xUnit or NUnit test. Your test file might look like this:

```csharp
[Fact]
public void BlogCard_ShowsReadingTime_WhenProvided()
{
    // Arrange
    using var ctx = new TestContext();
    ctx.Services.AddSingleton<IBlogService>(new MockBlogService());
    
    // Act
    var cut = ctx.RenderComponent<BlogCard>(parameters => parameters
        .Add(p => p.Title, "Understanding WebAssembly")
        .Add(p => p.ReadingTimeMinutes, 12));
    
    // Assert
    var badge = cut.Find(".reading-time-badge");
    Assert.Equal("12 min read", badge.TextContent.Trim());
}
```

Writing tests like this requires no special setup beyond the NuGet packages and your standard xUnit test runner. You can run these tests with `dotnet test`, wire them into a CI/CD pipeline, and get the same test infrastructure guarantees you get from testing any other .NET code.

### The Static Deployment Advantage

A published Blazor WebAssembly application is a folder full of static files. You can serve it from GitHub Pages, Netlify, Cloudflare Pages, Azure Static Web Apps, or any web server that can serve files. There is no Node.js server to manage. There is no `next start` process. There is no server infrastructure. This makes deployment genuinely simpler for .NET shops that are accustomed to deploying server-side code but find the JavaScript front-end deployment model confusing.

## Part 6: The Actual Question — What Makes Your Specific Situation Different?

Here is the dirty secret of "React versus Blazor" articles: most of them are written without enough context about the specific situation. Let us enumerate the factors that actually determine which is the right answer for your team.

### Factor 1: Your Team's Current Skill Set

If your team consists entirely of .NET developers with no JavaScript experience beyond what slipped into Web Forms pages fifteen years ago, Blazor WebAssembly gives you a meaningful head start. You will be productive from day one. The component model is familiar (it maps very well to the ASP.NET Core component model), the language is familiar, and the tooling is familiar.

If your team already has strong front-end JavaScript or TypeScript experience, the calculus changes. React will feel natural to those developers, and forcing them to write C# in the browser creates friction.

### Factor 2: The Nature of the UI You Are Building

Not all web application UIs are the same. If you are building a data-entry application — forms, tables, CRUD operations, validation messages — Blazor's model maps very naturally to this. The `EditForm` component, `DataAnnotationsValidator`, and `ValidationSummary` work exactly like you would expect if you have used MVC model binding before.

If you are building something that requires deep integration with browser APIs — WebGL canvas manipulation, complex audio processing, real-time collaborative editing with operational transforms, full-featured rich text editing — the JavaScript ecosystem has a decade-long head start, and JSInterop (the mechanism by which Blazor C# code can call JavaScript) becomes a significant consideration. It works, but it introduces friction.

### Factor 3: The Breadth of the Third-Party Library Ecosystem You Need

This is where React wins decisively against Blazor in most cases. The npm ecosystem contains millions of packages. Many of them are terrible. But the good ones are very, very good, and the sheer volume means that for almost any specialized UI requirement you can imagine, somebody has already built it for React.

NuGet has a growing collection of Blazor component libraries — Radzen, MudBlazor, Telerik (commercial), Syncfusion (commercial), Blazor Fluent UI — but the depth and breadth of the React ecosystem is still ahead. This gap is narrowing, and for most business application UIs, the Blazor component ecosystem is already more than sufficient. But it is a real difference that you should not pretend does not exist.

### Factor 4: Your Long-Term Hiring Strategy

Where are you going to find your future developers? If you are a Microsoft-stack shop that primarily hires .NET developers, Blazor keeps your hiring pool coherent. Your front-end developers and back-end developers can work in the same language. A back-end developer can fix a front-end bug. A front-end developer can understand the server-side code. This reduces the siloing that plagues many teams.

If you are a product company that actively hires front-end specialists, you will find React developers in much greater abundance than Blazor developers. In the 2026 hiring market, "React developer" is a career identity; "Blazor developer" is still mostly a subset of "C# developer."

### Factor 5: Performance Requirements

Both React (in a well-optimized application) and Blazor WebAssembly (with AOT compilation enabled) can deliver excellent performance for typical business applications. But they have different performance profiles.

React's initial page load can be very fast if the bundle is small and server-side rendering is used (via Next.js or Remix). Client-side React, without server-side rendering, requires downloading a JavaScript bundle and then executing it before any UI appears — this is the "white screen of death" problem that SPA architectures face.

Blazor WebAssembly's initial load time is slower than React because it needs to download the .NET runtime and your application's compiled WebAssembly files. This can be a significant initial download (several megabytes even with AOT compilation and trimming). Once loaded, Blazor WASM is fast — C# executing as WebAssembly is genuinely performant. But the initial cold start is still Blazor's Achilles heel compared to a small React SPA.

However, for internal business applications where users will be loading the app repeatedly (and the browser caches the WASM files), the initial download cost amortizes quickly across sessions.

## Part 7: A Historical Interlude — How Did We Get Here?

It is worth spending a few minutes understanding how both React and Blazor arrived at their current positions, because the history tells you something important about their design philosophies.

### React's History in Brief

React emerged from a specific problem Facebook had: their news feed was complicated, stateful, and needed to update frequently without full page reloads. The traditional approach at the time — manipulating the DOM directly with JavaScript or jQuery — was error-prone and slow when the page became complex. Jordan Walke's insight was to represent the UI as a function of state. Given a state object, your component renders the same output every time. When state changes, you re-render the component. The diffing algorithm (the Virtual DOM) figures out the minimum set of actual DOM operations needed to update the screen.

This "UI as a function of state" model was radical in 2013 and is now foundational to virtually every modern UI framework. Even Blazor, as we will see in Part 2, uses a similar model.

React initially used class-based components with lifecycle methods. In 2018, React 16.8 introduced Hooks, which replaced class components with functional components and a new model for managing state and side effects. This was another significant philosophical shift — one that introduced the `useEffect` trap that Part 3 of this series is dedicated to. The introduction of React Server Components (RSC) in React 18 and their stabilization in React 19 represent yet another paradigm shift — one that is still being absorbed by the ecosystem.

### Blazor's History in Brief

Blazor was Steve Sanderson's experiment to see whether WebAssembly could make C# in the browser practical. The name "Blazor" is a combination of "Browser" and "Razor" — Razor being the templating language that ASP.NET Core uses for server-side HTML generation. The idea was simple: what if the same Razor templates you write for server-side rendering could be compiled and run client-side in the browser, with C# instead of JavaScript?

The early demos were impressive but the download size was daunting (initial Blazor WASM apps could be 10+ megabytes). Over successive .NET versions, the team has worked relentlessly on trimming, compression, and caching to bring this number down. By .NET 10, a minimal Blazor WASM application with AOT compilation and aggressive trimming can be under 2 MB gzipped — still larger than a minimal React app, but within the realm of practical for most applications.

The addition of Blazor Server mode (where the component rendering happens on the server and changes are pushed to the client via a SignalR connection) gave teams a way to get the Blazor programming model without the download problem, at the cost of a persistent server connection. The render mode infrastructure introduced in .NET 8 allows mixing server and client rendering within the same application in sophisticated ways.

For the purposes of this series, we are focused on Blazor WebAssembly specifically — the static deployment model where C# runs in the browser — because that is the model that competes most directly with React in a static site or SPA deployment scenario.

## Part 8: Your First Week — What This Feels Like on Monday Morning

Let us make this concrete. You have just been told to build a new feature: a blog dashboard that shows recent posts, allows filtering by tag, shows post metadata, and lets editors navigate to an edit screen for any post. This is a genuinely simple but real feature. Here is what building it feels like in each technology.

### Building It in React

You start by creating a new React project, likely with Vite:

```bash
npm create vite@latest blog-dashboard -- --template react-ts
cd blog-dashboard
npm install
```

You now have a React + TypeScript project. You write a `BlogDashboard` component:

```tsx
import { useState, useEffect } from 'react';

interface BlogPost {
  id: number;
  title: string;
  date: string;
  tags: string[];
  summary: string;
}

function BlogDashboard() {
  const [posts, setPosts] = useState<BlogPost[]>([]);
  const [selectedTag, setSelectedTag] = useState<string | null>(null);
  const [loading, setLoading] = useState(true);

  useEffect(() => {
    fetch('/api/posts')
      .then(res => res.json())
      .then(data => {
        setPosts(data);
        setLoading(false);
      });
  }, []); // ← This empty array is the dependency array. We will have A LOT to say about this in Part 3.

  const filteredPosts = selectedTag
    ? posts.filter(p => p.tags.includes(selectedTag))
    : posts;

  if (loading) return <p>Loading...</p>;

  return (
    <div className="dashboard">
      <TagFilter posts={posts} onTagSelect={setSelectedTag} />
      <PostList posts={filteredPosts} />
    </div>
  );
}
```

This looks reasonable. But notice a few things already: you have a `useEffect` hook that fetches data. You have a dependency array (`[]`). You have a `loading` state variable that you manage manually. You have TypeScript interfaces that you define separately from your server-side C# models. Each of these things is a topic we will dig into deeply in later parts.

### Building It in Blazor WebAssembly

You create a new Blazor WASM project:

```bash
dotnet new blazorwasm -n BlogDashboard --no-https
cd BlogDashboard
dotnet run
```

You write a `BlogDashboard.razor` component:

```razor
@page "/blog-dashboard"
@inject IBlogService BlogService

@if (_loading)
{
    <p>Loading...</p>
}
else
{
    <TagFilter Posts="@_posts" OnTagSelected="FilterByTag" />
    <PostList Posts="@_filteredPosts" />
}

@code {
    private List<BlogPost>? _posts;
    private List<BlogPost>? _filteredPosts;
    private bool _loading = true;
    private string? _selectedTag;

    protected override async Task OnInitializedAsync()
    {
        _posts = await BlogService.GetAllPostsAsync();
        _filteredPosts = _posts;
        _loading = false;
    }

    private void FilterByTag(string? tag)
    {
        _selectedTag = tag;
        _filteredPosts = string.IsNullOrEmpty(tag)
            ? _posts
            : _posts?.Where(p => p.Tags.Contains(tag)).ToList();
    }
}
```

Notice the differences: `OnInitializedAsync` is the Blazor equivalent of the `useEffect` with an empty dependency array (runs once, on component initialization). There is no separate `loading` state toggle required if you use `null` as a sentinel value. The `FilterByTag` method is a regular C# method that modifies state and triggers a re-render. The `BlogPost` type is defined in a shared class library and used directly without any TypeScript interface duplication.

Neither example is dramatically simpler than the other for this particular use case. The conceptual models are different, but the amount of code is similar. However, as complexity increases — as you add real-time updates, complex interactions, shared state, and testing — the differences in mental model become more significant. That is exactly what Parts 2 through 6 will explore.

## Part 9: The Question Nobody Asks — What Are You Actually Choosing Between?

There is one more thing to establish before we dive into the technical comparisons, and it is the thing that most "React versus Blazor" discussions skip: you are not just choosing between two UI frameworks. You are choosing between two mental models, two ecosystems, two communities, and two career trajectories.

### The JavaScript Mental Model

React is a JavaScript library. Even with TypeScript, you are fundamentally working in the JavaScript ecosystem, which has a set of values and assumptions baked into it: runtime flexibility, duck typing as a core concept (TypeScript is an overlay, not a replacement), a package ecosystem (npm) that moves extraordinarily fast and sometimes breaks things, a build tooling landscape (Webpack, Vite, esbuild, Rolldown) that has changed significantly every few years, and a community that tends to favor small, composable libraries over large, opinionated frameworks.

This mental model has genuine strengths. The flexibility of JavaScript means that front-end developers have been able to invent and reinvent patterns rapidly, discovering what works and discarding what does not. The npm ecosystem's breadth means that you rarely need to build a UI component from scratch. The community's comfort with functional programming patterns has produced some genuinely elegant state management solutions.

### The C# Mental Model

Blazor WebAssembly is a C# framework. It is built on the assumption that you are working in the .NET ecosystem, which has a different set of values and assumptions: static typing as a core design pillar (not an overlay), a comprehensive standard library that covers most common needs, a package ecosystem (NuGet) that moves more slowly but is also more stable, build tooling (MSBuild, the dotnet CLI) that has been consistent for decades, and a community that tends to favor opinionated, "batteries included" frameworks that do a lot in one package.

This mental model also has genuine strengths. The static type system catches errors at compile time that JavaScript/TypeScript catches only partially and JavaScript alone catches not at all. The .NET runtime is mature, well-tested, and performs extremely well. The standard library is comprehensive enough that you rarely need a third-party package for common tasks. The build and tooling story is consistent across many years.

### The Honest Assessment

Neither mental model is objectively superior. They are adapted to different situations, different teams, and different problems. The purpose of this series is to give you enough understanding of both that you can make an informed choice — not to convince you that one is obviously better than the other.

What we will argue, with evidence, is that for ASP.NET development teams who primarily hire .NET developers, build line-of-business applications, prioritize code sharing between front-end and back-end, and need strong testability with familiar tools, Blazor WebAssembly makes a compelling case that is often underestimated. And that for teams with existing JavaScript front-end experience, strong React component libraries in use, and a need to hire from the broader front-end talent pool, React is often the pragmatically correct choice even if it introduces a language context switch.

The answer is "it depends," yes — but we are going to make "it depends" actually mean something by specifying exactly what it depends on.

## Part 10: Before We Begin — A Note on Our Assumptions and Limitations

A few honest caveats before you proceed.

This series assumes that you are targeting a purely static deployment — that is, your front-end application (whether React or Blazor WASM) will be served as static files from a CDN or static hosting service, and it will communicate with your back-end via HTTP APIs. We are not covering Blazor Server (which requires a persistent server) or Next.js with server-side rendering (which requires a Node.js server) in depth, because those are different deployment models with different trade-offs.

This series assumes .NET 10 and React 19.2 as the current stable versions. Things will change. React 20 may be around the corner. .NET 11 will introduce new Blazor features. The fundamentals, though — the mental models, the architectural patterns, the ways of thinking about rendering — are stable enough that this series will be useful for years even as specific version details change.

This series is written for developers who are making a real decision on a real project. We are not going to pretend this is all academic. We are going to give you actual code, actual arguments, and actual recommendations, while being honest about the uncertainty involved.
