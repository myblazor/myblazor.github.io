## We Promised You Would Not Leave Empty-Handed

Six articles ago, you sat down in that conference room. The legacy application was waiting. The team was gathered. React was the elephant everyone was tiptoeing around. And we promised that by the time you finished this series, you would have enough information to walk back into that room and say something intelligent.

This is that article.

We are not going to hedge. We are not going to give you a purely academic summary and say "weigh the factors and decide for yourself." We have spent six articles giving you the technical depth — now we owe you practical guidance.

We will start with a decision framework, then give concrete recommendations for the most common scenarios, then acknowledge the real trade-offs you will live with either way, and end with the things that actually matter most that no framework comparison article ever talks about.

---

## Part 1: The Decision Framework

There are five questions to answer. Answer them honestly for your specific situation. The answers will point you toward one technology much more strongly than any feature comparison chart.

### Question 1: Who Is Currently on Your Team?

This is the most important question, and most articles underweight it because it feels non-technical. But the right technology for a team of five .NET developers with zero JavaScript experience is different from the right technology for a team of three .NET developers and two React developers, which is different again from a team that is about to hire two dedicated front-end engineers.

**If your entire team writes C# and ASP.NET for a living, and nobody has significant React experience:** Choose Blazor. The productivity gain from staying in C# — familiar IDE, familiar debugger, familiar test tooling, no context-switching — will outweigh Blazor's ecosystem limitations for the vast majority of business applications. You will not need to hire a front-end specialist. You will not have a "we have to hand this to the JS team" bottleneck. Your back-end developers can fix front-end bugs.

**If your team has at least one developer who is confident with React and TypeScript:** The equation is more balanced. That developer can lead the React implementation and establish patterns that the rest of the team learns. React is still viable.

**If you are about to hire front-end engineers from the general market:** The React talent pool is dramatically larger. "React developer" is a career identity; "Blazor developer" is rare. Hiring React developers is faster, easier, and gives you more candidates.

### Question 2: What Kind of Application Are You Building?

Not all UIs are alike. The answer matters a lot for framework selection.

**Line-of-business application** (forms, tables, CRUD, reports, dashboards with data loaded from an API): Both frameworks handle this well. Blazor may have a slight edge because its `EditForm`, data binding, and validation model maps directly to ASP.NET patterns your team already knows. This is the scenario where Blazor shines most brightly.

**Real-time, highly interactive consumer application** (collaborative editing, real-time trading dashboards, games, social media feeds): React's ecosystem has a significant depth advantage here — libraries for complex interaction patterns, real-time subscriptions, optimistic UI updates, and fine-grained reactivity (signals) are mature. Blazor can do these things, but you will spend more time building infrastructure.

**Marketing site or content site with occasional interactive components**: Neither React nor Blazor WASM is optimal for this — you want a static site generator (Hugo, Astro) with progressive enhancement. Both can be embedded as "islands" in a mostly-static site, but this is not where either framework is designed to shine.

**Internal tool or developer-facing application** (admin panels, monitoring dashboards, configuration UIs): Blazor is excellent here. Internal tools rarely need to optimize for first-load performance (your employees will load it once and then use the cached version all day), the audience is known and likely on good hardware and fast networks, and the rapid development speed of staying in C# is a major productivity win.

### Question 3: What Does Your Third-Party Library Requirement Look Like?

Be specific and concrete. Write down the five most important third-party components your application will need. Then look up whether each one has:
- A native, first-class Blazor implementation
- A JavaScript-only implementation that would require JSInterop integration
- An open-source, free equivalent that works in both

Common components and their status in 2026:

**Data grids**: AG Grid has no official Blazor binding. MudBlazor's `MudDataGrid`, Radzen's `RadzenDataGrid`, and Blazor Fluent UI's `FluentDataGrid` are all good native Blazor options for most use cases. If you specifically need AG Grid's enterprise features (Excel export with custom formatting, complex cell renderers, SSRM for server-side rendering of millions of rows), you will need JSInterop.

**Charts**: Chart.js and D3 are JavaScript-only but have JSInterop wrappers. Blazor has native chart components (ChartJs.Blazor, ApexCharts.Blazor) that are adequate for most business charting needs.

**Rich text editors**: TipTap and Lexical are React-native and would require JSInterop in Blazor. A native Blazor rich text editor is an area where the ecosystem is thinner.

**Maps**: Leaflet and Mapbox have JSInterop wrappers for Blazor. This is manageable.

**PDF generation**: Blazor actually has an advantage here — you can use server-side PDF generation libraries (QuestPDF, which is free for non-commercial use) and stream the result to the client, avoiding the need for any browser-side PDF library at all.

If your list of "JavaScript-only" requirements is short (one or two items) and bounded (you know exactly what you need), JSInterop is viable and the hybrid architecture described in Part 4 handles it. If your list is long or unbounded ("we might need whatever library comes up"), React's direct access to the npm ecosystem is a meaningful advantage.

### Question 4: What Are Your Initial Load Performance Requirements?

Be honest about who your users are and what their network conditions look like.

**Internal enterprise application, users on corporate network or VPN**: Initial load time is rarely critical. The first load might take 8 seconds for a Blazor WASM application on a slow VPN connection, but subsequent visits are cached. This is acceptable for most internal applications.

**Public-facing application, users on mobile networks**: The Blazor WASM payload (4–7 MB Brotli-compressed for a typical application) is genuinely problematic. A user on a 3G connection downloading 5 MB before seeing anything is a bad experience. In this scenario, React's smaller initial payload (60–250 KB for a typical application) is a real advantage.

**SEO-critical application (blog, marketing, product catalog)**: Neither React SPA nor Blazor WASM is appropriate without server-side rendering, because both render only after JavaScript/WebAssembly loads, which search engine crawlers may not wait for. For this use case, Next.js with React (server-side rendering) or Blazor Server are better options.

### Question 5: What Is Your Testing Strategy?

Both frameworks are testable, but the test tooling differs:

**Blazor**: bUnit for component testing (MIT license, free). xUnit or NUnit for everything else. The same test runner and tooling you use for your server-side code. Integration testing with `WebApplicationFactory` and bUnit can cover almost everything. Test code is C#.

**React**: Vitest or Jest for unit testing. React Testing Library for component testing. Playwright or Cypress for end-to-end testing. The test code is JavaScript/TypeScript. These are all excellent, mature tools — but they are additional things to configure and maintain.

If your team is strong in writing .NET tests but weak in JavaScript testing tooling, Blazor's test story is simpler to adopt. If your team is already doing JavaScript testing, React's testing ecosystem is excellent.

## Part 2: The Concrete Recommendation Matrix

Based on the five questions above, here is a concrete recommendation for the most common scenarios:

### Scenario A: Pure .NET Team, Line-of-Business App, Internal Users

**Recommendation: Blazor WebAssembly**

You have a C# team building a data-entry, data-viewing, workflow management application for internal users. There is no SEO requirement. Users are on reasonable networks. The component library requirements are standard (grids, forms, charts, modals).

Choose Blazor. Your team will be productive from day one. The shared type model eliminates a whole category of serialization bugs. Testing is straightforward. You will not need to hire front-end specialists. MudBlazor or Blazor Fluent UI will cover your component needs. The larger initial download is acceptable for your user base.

### Scenario B: Mixed Team With React Experience, Customer-Facing App

**Recommendation: React (with Next.js if SEO matters)**

You have two or three developers with React experience, the application is customer-facing with unknown network conditions, and you are competing for attention spans in the market. 

Choose React. Your React developers will lead. Your .NET developers will learn. The smaller initial bundle size matters for your users. Next.js (if you can afford a server) or Vite (if you are doing static deployment) both give you excellent tooling. Use your .NET skills on the server side — the API, the business logic, the database — and React for the front-end.

### Scenario C: Legacy React App, Considering Migration to Blazor

**Recommendation: Strangler Fig with Blazor for new features**

Do not rewrite everything. Use the Strangler Fig pattern described in Part 4: keep the existing React application running, and build new features or pages in Blazor WebAssembly. Route new pages to the Blazor application. Over time, as the Blazor coverage grows, the React coverage shrinks. This gives you a migration path that is low-risk and delivers value continuously.

### Scenario D: Legacy ASP.NET Web Forms or MVC, Needs Modern UI

**Recommendation: Depends on team skill, but Blazor deserves serious consideration**

If you are migrating from Web Forms or classic MVC to a modern UI framework, you are already making a significant investment. The question is whether that investment pays for a new language (JavaScript/TypeScript for React) or stays in a familiar one (C# for Blazor).

For teams that have been writing C# for years and are comfortable with it, adding Blazor WebAssembly is a smaller cognitive leap than adding React. The Razor template syntax is familiar (Blazor's templates are Razor templates). The DI system is the same. The component model maps to what MVC developers already understand about separation of concerns.

For teams that already have junior developers who know JavaScript and React, React reduces the learning curve.

In cases where there is no clear skill advantage either way, we lean toward Blazor for this scenario specifically because the code-sharing potential between the old server-side code and the new Blazor front-end is significant, and the ability to gradually onboard the rest of the .NET team (rather than requiring everyone to learn JavaScript) is a practical advantage.

### Scenario E: New Greenfield Project, No Existing Code

**Recommendation: Depends entirely on team skills and hiring plans**

For a greenfield project with a primarily .NET team and no front-end hiring planned: **Blazor**.
For a greenfield project with planned front-end hires from the general market: **React**.
For a greenfield project with a deadline that requires immediate productivity: **whichever the team already knows**.

## Part 3: The Things Nobody Tells You

Every technology comparison article focuses on the technical factors. Very few talk about the non-technical factors that turn out to matter more in practice.

### Maintenance Is the Real Cost

Building a feature takes weeks. Maintaining that feature takes years. The framework you choose will be in your codebase for 5–10 years, minimum. Whatever choice you make, you will outlive the current version of the framework, upgrade through breaking changes, train new hires, and maintain code written by people who no longer work there.

React has been in production for over a decade. It has changed significantly in that time — class components to hooks, Webpack to Vite, `createRoot` instead of `render`, Server Components in React 19. Each change required existing codebases to update. Teams that did not keep up found themselves on old React versions with security vulnerabilities and compatibility issues.

Blazor has been in production since .NET Core 3.1 (2020) and has changed significantly too — the Blazor Server vs. WebAssembly vs. Hybrid distinctions, the render mode infrastructure, the interactivity model changes in .NET 8. But .NET has a stronger backwards-compatibility story than the JavaScript ecosystem — Microsoft maintains longer support windows and provides better migration tooling.

**Practical implication**: Budget for framework upgrade work in both cases. For React, budget for NPM package version management — keeping 50+ dependencies current is a real operational cost. For Blazor, budget for .NET upgrade cycles — but these happen on a slower, more predictable schedule.

### Documentation and Community Resources

React's documentation (react.dev) is excellent. There are millions of blog posts, YouTube tutorials, Stack Overflow answers, and GitHub issues for almost any React problem you will encounter.

Blazor's documentation (learn.microsoft.com) is good and improving. But the community is smaller. When you search for solutions to obscure Blazor problems, you may find less help than for equally obscure React problems. This is a real practical cost for teams that rely heavily on Stack Overflow and community forums.

**Practical implication**: For teams that learn primarily from blog posts and tutorials, React has a larger library of teaching resources. For teams that learn from official documentation and Microsoft Learn courses, Blazor's official docs are excellent.

### The "It Works Everywhere" Story

React applications are JavaScript SPA applications. They work on every browser that supports modern JavaScript (all major browsers since 2020). They can run in web views embedded in iOS and Android applications (React Native is a separate story). They can be deployed to Electron for desktop applications.

Blazor WebAssembly works in every browser that supports WebAssembly (all major browsers since 2017, and all current mobile browsers). For native desktop applications, Blazor Hybrid (which hosts Blazor in MAUI's WebView) is the path.

The "works everywhere" story is comparable. Neither technology has a significant portability advantage for standard web deployment.

### What the Junior Developer Experience Is Like

Imagine you hire a junior developer with six months of experience. They come in excited, eager to learn, and have some familiarity with one of these technologies from a bootcamp.

A junior developer with JavaScript/React bootcamp experience can contribute to a React application on day one — writing new components, fixing simple bugs, following established patterns. They will make mistakes, but those mistakes will be visible and correctable.

A junior developer with no prior .NET experience will need to learn C# before they can contribute meaningfully to a Blazor application. C# is not difficult, but the ramp-up time is real. On the other hand, a junior developer with C# experience (from a CS degree or a .NET-focused bootcamp) will find Blazor immediately approachable.

**Practical implication**: The right junior developer profile depends on which framework you choose, and that depends on which bootcamps and CS programs your candidates come from. In most markets, there are more React bootcamp graduates than Blazor/C# graduates in the junior developer pool.

## Part 4: The Things Both Frameworks Get Right

After six articles of comparison, it is worth taking a moment to appreciate that both React and Blazor WebAssembly represent genuine engineering achievements.

React — particularly React 19 with its Server Components, Compiler, and refined concurrent model — is the product of a decade of iteration on the most used front-end library in the world. The amount of thought that has gone into its design, and the practical wisdom embodied in its patterns, is remarkable. Its ecosystem is the most comprehensive in the history of front-end development.

Blazor WebAssembly represents something genuinely unprecedented in web development: a mainstream, production-quality path for writing front-end web applications in a language other than JavaScript, without any server required. The fact that C# code, compiled to WebAssembly, can run in a browser on a GitHub Pages static deployment and deliver a responsive, capable web application is a remarkable technical achievement.

Both technologies are free. Both are open source. Both run in all major browsers. Both have strong corporate backing (Meta for React, Microsoft for Blazor). Neither is going away.

## Part 5: Our Honest Opinion

We have been careful throughout this series not to advocate too strongly for either technology, because the honest answer to "React or Blazor?" is genuinely "it depends." But since you have read seven articles and deserve our opinion:

**For a typical ASP.NET development team building a typical line-of-business web application in 2026, Blazor WebAssembly is underrated and underused.**

The C# developer experience advantage is real and significant. The code-sharing story between front-end and back-end is genuinely powerful. The testing story is excellent. The first-class DI system eliminates a whole category of state management complexity. The render tree architecture is efficient. The tooling (Visual Studio or Rider) is mature and familiar.

The ecosystem gap — the fact that React has more third-party libraries — is real but often overstated for line-of-business applications. The standard Blazor component libraries (MudBlazor in particular) cover the needs of 90% of business application UIs. The cases where you genuinely need a JavaScript-only library are narrower than most teams assume when they first make the comparison.

The initial download size is the most legitimate objection to Blazor WASM for public-facing applications. It is real, it matters, and if your application serves users on constrained networks and initial load performance is critical, React has a meaningful advantage.

**But for a team of .NET developers building internal tools, line-of-business applications, or enterprise UIs? Try Blazor. Seriously try it. Build a complete feature in it. Run the tests. Experience the DI. Share a model class between the front-end and back-end. It may surprise you.**

## Part 6: What to Do Monday Morning

You have read the series. You have the framework. You need to actually make a decision. Here is a practical action plan:

**Step 1 (This week)**: Document your answers to the five decision questions honestly. Be specific: not "we might need charting" but "we specifically need a candlestick chart with volume overlay for the trading dashboard." Not "we might hire front-end devs" but "we have approval to hire one front-end engineer in Q3."

**Step 2 (Next two weeks)**: Build the same feature in both frameworks. Not a hello-world demo. A real feature: the dashboard page, the CRUD for one entity, the main navigation. Time how long it takes. Count the lines of code. Run the tests. See which one fits your team's brain.

**Step 3 (After the prototype)**: Present the comparison. Not just the technology comparison — the team-fit comparison. Which framework did your team find more natural? Which had more questions that nobody could answer? Which prototype has more tests?

**Step 4**: Make the decision, document it, and commit. A written Architecture Decision Record (ADR) explaining why you chose what you chose, what alternatives you considered, and what the known trade-offs are. This document will be invaluable two years from now when a new team member asks "why did we use Blazor?" or when a new requirement challenges an assumption you made.

```markdown
# ADR-001: Front-End Framework Selection

## Status
Accepted

## Context
We are modernizing the customer portal from ASP.NET MVC 5 to a modern UI framework.
The team consists of four .NET developers (no dedicated front-end experience).
The application serves internal users on corporate hardware and network.
The primary UI patterns are: CRUD forms, data tables, approval workflows, and reports.

## Decision
We will use Blazor WebAssembly with MudBlazor component library.

## Consequences

### Positive
- Development team can use existing C# skills immediately
- Shared domain models between server API and client application
- First-class DI with IServiceCollection — no state management library needed
- Test code in C# with bUnit — no separate JavaScript test tooling

### Negative
- Larger initial download than a React SPA (~5 MB vs ~200 KB)
- Smaller third-party library ecosystem than npm/React
- Build times are longer when publishing with AOT compilation
- If we need AG Grid enterprise features in the future, we will need JSInterop integration

## Alternatives Considered
- **React + Vite**: Better ecosystem breadth, smaller initial download. Rejected because the team has no React experience and the initial download difference is acceptable for internal users.
- **Angular**: Rejected because it is TypeScript-only and does not provide the C# code-sharing benefit that Blazor does.
```

## Conclusion: The Elephant Has a Name

You walked into that conference room three weeks ago and the elephant did not have a name. It was just "React," hovering there in the air, implying that maybe all the cool kids are doing it and you should too.

The elephant's name is "JavaScript," and JavaScript is excellent at what it does. React is one of the best things built on top of JavaScript in the history of the platform. It is not a fad. It is not going away. It is the right choice in a huge number of situations.

But there is another elephant in the room that often does not get invited to the meeting: the C# developer who has been writing excellent, maintainable, testable, performant web applications in ASP.NET for years, who now has the option to continue writing C# for the front-end as well, without giving up static typing, IDE tooling, unit testing with familiar tools, or dependency injection.

That elephant's name is Blazor, and it is real, and it is ready for production.

Now you have enough information to decide which elephant belongs in your room. The choice is yours. Make it deliberately, document it carefully, and revisit it in twelve months to see how it played out.

Thank you for reading. If you have questions or experiences to share about your own React-versus-Blazor decision, we would love to hear them in the comments.
