## Where we are

We are four days in. Three days ago we settled the land, the bylaws, and the budget framework — aim for NPR 1.6 crore of build, hold NPR 40 lakh in reserve, stay under the NPR 2 crore ceiling. Two days ago we designed the house — three storeys, 1,280 sq ft footprint, tea shop on the ground floor, kitchen and living room on the first, two bedrooms plus two-and-a-half bathrooms on the second. Yesterday we poured the foundation and raised the structural frame — isolated footings or a raft depending on what the soil test said, Fe500D rebar, M25 concrete for columns and beams, NBC 201 dimensions, NBC 105:2025 detailing.

The structural frame is up. The columns stand. The beams span between them. The slabs are cast. If you stood on the plot today you would see a concrete skeleton, three floors tall, with the staircase visible and the outlines of the rooms suggested by the column spacing. The house has weight. It has strength. It has, as yet, no skin.

Today, Part 4, is about the skin.

## Part 1. What a building envelope is, in plain English

The **building envelope** is the complete outer layer of the house — the walls, the roof, the windows, the external doors, the floor over any unheated space below — taken together as a system. Everything inside the envelope is "indoors." Everything outside it is "outdoors." The envelope is the physical boundary between the two.

The envelope's job is deceptively simple: **keep the weather out, and keep the comfort in.** On a February morning in Kathmandu, when the outdoor temperature is 4 degrees and the indoor temperature is 18, the envelope is the thing that is keeping those fourteen degrees of difference in place. On a June afternoon, when the outdoor temperature is 32 and you want the indoor to be 26, it is the envelope that is doing the work. Every minute of every day, the envelope is being pushed on by the weather, and how well it pushes back decides almost everything about how the house feels and how much it costs to run.

Here is the thing that most first-time builders do not understand, and that I want to make very concrete today: **the envelope decides your running costs for the entire life of the house.** A house with a well-built envelope costs a little more to build and almost nothing to keep comfortable. A house with a poorly-built envelope costs a little less to build and a fortune to heat in winter and cool in summer, every year, forever.

A concrete example. A fan that runs one extra hour a day consumes about 2 units of electricity per day (roughly 50 watt-hours per hour × 30 days ÷ 1000 = 1.5 units), or about 550 units a year. At a residential rate of roughly NPR 12 per unit in Kathmandu, that is NPR 6,600 a year. An air conditioner that runs one extra hour a day on a 1.5-ton unit consumes about 1.5 units of electricity per hour, or about 550 units a year on that extra hour alone, costing NPR 6,600 a year. Over twenty-five years of the house's life, that is NPR 1.65 lakh per appliance per hour-per-day of extra run-time. Multiple appliances, multiple hours, multiple rooms, and the numbers become meaningful. And none of that includes the fact that appliances running harder wear out faster, so you also replace them more often.

So the single mental model for today is: **every rupee spent on the envelope at construction time returns rupees of saved electricity, reduced wear, and improved comfort for the next fifty years.** This is the highest-return part of the entire budget, and, like the structural frame we covered yesterday, it is almost entirely invisible once the house is finished.

Spend on the box. The envelope is the box.

## Part 2. R-value, U-value, and thermal mass, in plain English

Three terms will come up throughout this article. Pin them down now and the rest will follow.

**R-value** is a measurement of how much a material *resists* heat flow. Higher R-value means better insulation. The unit is square-metre-kelvin-per-watt (m²·K/W) in metric, or square-foot-degree-Fahrenheit-hour-per-BTU (ft²·°F·h/BTU) in US units. The two unit systems use the same *name* but different numerical scales — a metric R-value of 2.0 m²·K/W is equivalent to a US R-value of about 11.4. In practice, Nepali technical documents use both conventions, and the conversion factor is about 5.7 between them. When comparing numbers, always check which convention is in use.

For quick intuition, three useful reference points:

- A 230 mm solid brick wall (standard Kathmandu construction) has a metric R-value of roughly 0.25 m²·K/W, or a US R-value of about R-1.5. Very poor.
- A 150 mm AAC block wall has a metric R-value of roughly 0.75 m²·K/W, or a US R-value of about R-4.3. Three times better than solid brick.
- A typical wood-frame US house wall with cavity insulation has a US R-value of R-13 to R-21 — five to ten times better than AAC.

In other words, Kathmandu's traditional solid brick walls are terrible insulators by modern global standards. The pink fibreglass "meat" your Nepali-American friend mentioned in their US house is *ten times* more thermally resistant than a standard 230 mm Kathmandu brick wall. This is not a small difference. This is the single biggest reason that Kathmandu houses feel cold in January and hot in June, and why adding an air conditioner to a traditional brick house is like adding a hair dryer to an ice rink — the work is all going out through the walls.

**U-value** is the reciprocal of R-value, and it measures how much heat *flows* through a material per unit area per unit temperature difference. U = 1/R. Lower U-value means better insulation. U-value is the unit window manufacturers and building codes often quote, because it is more intuitive for thinking about the performance of a whole assembly.

For reference:
- A 230 mm solid brick wall has a U-value of about 4.0 W/m²·K. Very leaky.
- A 150 mm AAC block wall has a U-value of about 1.3 W/m²·K. Three times better.
- A single-pane window has a U-value of about 5.8 W/m²·K. Even leakier than the brick wall.
- A double-pane window with a 12 mm air gap has a U-value of about 2.8 W/m²·K.
- A modern insulated UPVC window with argon-filled double glazing has a U-value of about 1.6 W/m²·K.

Notice that **windows, in almost any configuration, are worse insulators than even mediocre walls.** This is why the size and quality of your windows has an outsized impact on your house's thermal performance. Single-pane window with aluminium frame is a hole in the envelope. Double-pane UPVC window is a bandage over the hole.

**Thermal mass** is a different kind of thermal property. It is the *capacity* of a material to absorb and store heat. High thermal mass means the material acts as a thermal battery — it warms up slowly during the day and releases that warmth slowly through the night. Low thermal mass means the material heats up and cools down quickly.

Masonry (brick, stone, concrete) has very high thermal mass. Wood has moderate thermal mass. Fibreglass insulation has very low thermal mass. This matters because **in a climate like Kathmandu where day and night temperatures differ a lot — often 15 degrees or more between afternoon high and pre-dawn low — thermal mass can do a surprising amount of work for free.** A heavy masonry wall warmed by afternoon sun keeps releasing that warmth into the interior through most of the cool evening, so the house does not go cold until much later in the night. The catch is that thermal mass only works this way *if the outside of the wall is well insulated.* If the wall is bare masonry exposed to the weather, the mass re-radiates its heat right back outside and is essentially wasted.

The practical insight: Kathmandu traditional architecture had high thermal mass but no insulation, which made houses that were tolerable in the summer (mass absorbs the heat of the day, releases it at night) but frigid in the winter (mass also loses heat slowly through the day, never really warms up). The modern upgrade is not to throw the mass away and build wood-frame houses like the US; it is to *add insulation on the outside of the mass.* You keep the summer benefit (mass holds the coolness of the night deep into the day) and gain the winter benefit (the insulated shell lets the mass warm up and stay warm).

That is the design target for today.

## Part 3. The wall question — AAC blocks versus solid brick

Every Kathmandu house-builder confronts this question around month four of the project, when the masonry crew shows up to start the infill walls. Let us resolve it carefully.

### What are AAC blocks?

**AAC stands for Autoclaved Aerated Concrete.** It is a lightweight cellular concrete made from cement, lime, fine sand, water, and a small amount of aluminium powder. The aluminium reacts with the lime-cement paste to produce hydrogen gas, which creates millions of tiny bubbles throughout the mix. The block is then cured in an autoclave (a pressurised steam chamber) which locks in the cellular structure and gives it its final strength. The result is a block that looks a little like a slightly-porous biscuit — grey, soft-looking, surprisingly light to pick up.

AAC blocks came into the Nepali market roughly a decade ago and have grown rapidly since. The market in Nepal currently stands at approximately 1.5 million cubic metres per year, with projections of 15% year-over-year growth through 2026. Over 85% of AAC blocks in Nepal are imported, primarily from India and China, with Kathmandu Valley accounting for nearly 60% of consumption.

### AAC dimensions and pricing (Nepal, 2026)

AAC blocks come in several standard sizes, quoted in millimetres as length × height × thickness. The common ones:

- **4-inch (600 × 200 × 100 mm):** for internal partitions. Price roughly NPR 130–150 per block in Kathmandu.
- **6-inch (600 × 200 × 150 mm):** the workhorse size for internal and external walls in low-rise residential construction. Price roughly NPR 190–230 per block.
- **8-inch (600 × 200 × 200 mm):** for external walls where more thermal mass is wanted, or for load-bearing applications in single-storey structures. Price roughly NPR 260–300 per block.

For comparison, conventional Kathmandu red clay bricks (standard size 240 × 115 × 57 mm) cost NPR 16–25 per brick; machine-made Chinese bricks, which give better dimensional accuracy, cost NPR 22–28 per brick. One AAC 6-inch block covers approximately the same wall area as 19 red clay bricks stacked in two rows. At the top of the AAC price range and the top of the brick price range, the AAC block costs NPR 230 versus NPR 475 for the equivalent number of bricks — AAC is actually cheaper per unit of wall area, contrary to the initial impression. Once you factor in mortar savings (AAC uses thin-bed mortar in much smaller quantities than brick), overall wall cost savings of 20–30% over brick are reported by Nepali AAC suppliers.

### What AAC does well

**Thermal insulation.** As we noted above, an AAC block wall has about three times the R-value of a solid brick wall of the same thickness. This is the headline benefit. Every other advantage of AAC is secondary to this one, for the comfort-minded Kathmandu homeowner.

**Weight.** An AAC block weighs about one-third of a solid brick of the same volume — a typical 240 × 115 × 57 mm brick weighs about 2.5 kg, while an AAC block of the same volume weighs about 900 grams. Lighter walls mean less weight for the structural frame to carry, which means smaller columns and beams, which means less concrete and rebar. The savings flow through the structure.

**Speed.** AAC blocks are larger than bricks, so walls rise faster. Masons familiar with AAC typically lay three to four times as many square feet of wall per day with AAC as with brick, cutting your masonry time dramatically.

**Sound.** The cellular structure of AAC absorbs sound better than solid brick. A 150 mm AAC wall gives roughly 40 dB of sound reduction, versus about 45 dB for a 230 mm brick wall — actually slightly worse for sound than the thicker brick wall, but better than you would expect for the thinner assembly, and better than 115 mm brick.

**Fire resistance.** AAC is a mineral product and does not burn. A 150 mm AAC wall provides roughly four hours of fire resistance, which is better than most brick walls and dramatically better than any wood-frame construction.

**Cutting and fixing.** AAC is soft enough to cut with an ordinary wood saw and to drill with an ordinary masonry drill. This makes it far easier to route services (electrical conduits, plumbing lines) through the walls than with brick, and it means the finish work goes faster.

### What AAC does not do

**Structural load-bearing.** AAC blocks are not a structural material in multi-storey construction. They cannot carry the weight of floors above themselves. In the RCC frame with masonry infill system we chose yesterday, this is fine — the concrete frame carries the loads, the AAC walls are purely infill and cladding. But if you are building a single-storey load-bearing house, AAC is not the right choice.

**Curing with water during construction.** Unlike brick walls, AAC walls should not be cured with water, because the water can cause the blocks to crack. This surprises masons familiar only with brick, and it is a source of many early cracks in AAC construction where the mason forgot.

**Heavy fixings.** Hanging a heavy cabinet on an AAC wall requires specific AAC wall anchors, not the expansion plugs that work in brick. Using the wrong anchor makes the cabinet fall in a few months. This is solvable, but it is a training issue for the carpenter who hangs your kitchen cabinets.

**Moisture sensitivity.** AAC absorbs water faster than brick, so external AAC walls must be well plastered and, ideally, finished with a waterproofing primer before the topcoat paint. A poorly finished AAC wall that gets rain on it for years will eventually show moisture damage that a brick wall would have shrugged off.

### The recommendation for your house

For the six-aana mixed-use house we have been designing, use **150 mm AAC blocks for all internal partitions and all external walls where possible, and reserve traditional solid brick for two specific places**:

1. **The tea shop front and street-facing ground-floor walls.** The street side takes more abuse — bumps from motorbikes, kicks, leaning signboards, a decade of grime — and solid brick is more durable than AAC against all of these. The ground-floor street wall is a place to pay the durability premium.
2. **Wet walls in bathrooms.** The interior walls of a bathroom, where plumbing is embedded and where the wall will be tiled, can be solid brick for the additional water-tolerance. Some builders use AAC here too, with careful waterproofing; it works, but it is an extra detail to get right.

With this specification, roughly 70% of your wall area is AAC, 30% is solid brick. The overall wall cost is a little less than an all-brick build, the overall comfort is dramatically better, and the thermal mass of the solid-brick ground floor (which is in contact with the ground and benefits most from thermal stability) gives the house a pleasant ground-floor feel year-round.

## Part 4. The roof — the single highest-return decision

If the walls are the envelope's largest surface, the roof is the envelope's weakest point. And in Kathmandu specifically, the roof is almost always the single biggest thermal leak in the house.

Four reasons:

**One.** The roof gets the most sun. In summer, the sun is nearly overhead in the Valley and the roof is the surface most directly exposed to solar radiation. A bare concrete roof can reach 55–60°C on a June afternoon — twenty-five degrees hotter than the surrounding air — and that heat transfers straight down into the top-floor ceilings.

**Two.** Hot air rises, which means that in summer, the hottest air inside the house accumulates at the ceiling of the top floor, right against the underside of the roof slab. In winter, the warmest air in the house also rises to the top floor, and if the roof is uninsulated it escapes straight through it. The roof is the thermal exit door in both seasons.

**Three.** Roof area per unit of enclosed space is highest on the top floor. The second-floor bedrooms have one floor area of roof above them. The first floor has zero roof area above it (it has the second floor). Per bedroom, the second-floor bedrooms have far more roof exposure than the first-floor living room has ceiling exposure, so the second floor is most vulnerable to roof thermal losses and gains.

**Four.** Roof insulation is cheap to add during construction and extremely expensive to retrofit. Once the tiles are laid and the finish is done, adding roof insulation means pulling up the tiles, laying insulation, re-waterproofing, re-tiling. It is a major job. Doing it at construction time is easy — the insulation goes on the slab before the tiles.

### The roof insulation options, for a Kathmandu residence

For your flat RCC roof, five insulation approaches, with plain-English explanations:

**Option 1: EPS (Expanded Polystyrene, "thermocol") boards on top of the slab, under the tiles.** EPS is the white foam material used in packaging. It is cheap, lightweight, easy to cut, and has a good R-value per thickness (roughly R-4 per inch in US units, or about 0.7 m²·K/W per 25 mm in metric). A 50 mm layer of EPS under the roof tiles gives about R-8 (US) / 1.4 m²·K/W, which is a dramatic improvement over no insulation. EPS boards are widely available in Kathmandu from suppliers like Insulation Nepal, Prefab House Nepal, and others. Price is modest — NPR 150–250 per square foot installed for a 50 mm layer. **This is the baseline to beat for any Kathmandu roof insulation decision.**

**Option 2: XPS (Extruded Polystyrene) boards.** XPS is EPS's higher-performance cousin — a rigid foam made by a different process, with better moisture resistance, higher compressive strength, and slightly better R-value per thickness. XPS is the right choice for a roof that will be walked on regularly, or for an inverted roof assembly where the insulation sits above the waterproofing. Cost is roughly 40–60% higher than EPS for the same R-value. For a terrace that will be used as an outdoor living space (recommended, see Part 2), XPS is worth the premium.

**Option 3: Rockwool (mineral wool) batts.** Rockwool is spun from molten rock into a cotton-wool-like mat. Excellent thermal insulation, excellent sound absorption, excellent fire resistance, moisture-permeable. The common Kathmandu application is in a ceiling-void assembly — a suspended ceiling below the slab, with rockwool batts laid on top of the suspended ceiling. This works well for the top-floor rooms if you can afford the extra 100–150 mm of ceiling depth. Rockwool is available in Nepal but is priced at a premium — roughly NPR 300–500 per sq ft installed for 100 mm. For a two-bedroom top floor, it adds NPR 1.5 to 2.5 lakh to the project.

**Option 4: A ventilated cavity with reflective foil.** On flat Kathmandu roofs, a common traditional "improvement" is to lay a second thin slab, or a screed, on top of the structural slab with an air gap between. If that air gap is lined with reflective aluminium foil, the foil reflects radiant heat away from the structural slab below, and the air gap itself adds R-value. This is cheap to add during construction (a few thousand rupees of foil and 50–75 mm of spacing material) and it gives about half the insulation benefit of a proper EPS layer. Useful as a supplementary technique, not as a primary insulation.

**Option 5: Reflective roof coating (white paint or specialised elastomeric).** A white or silver roof coating reflects solar radiation instead of absorbing it, which can reduce the roof surface temperature by 15–20°C on a sunny day. This is the cheapest intervention of all — a litre of quality elastomeric roof coating covers about 6–8 square feet and costs NPR 400–600 per litre. For a 1,280 sq ft roof, the total materials cost is NPR 10,000–15,000. This is not an insulator per se; it is a reflector. Combined with option 1 (EPS insulation underneath) it is devastatingly effective.

### The specification for your house

**Do all three of these on your roof, without exception:**

1. **50 mm EPS or XPS boards over the structural slab**, under the screed and tiles. This is the thermal baseline. Budget: about NPR 2.5 to 4 lakh for a 1,280 sq ft roof.
2. **Reflective elastomeric white roof coating** on the finished terrace surface. This is additive and cheap — NPR 10,000–15,000 for the roof.
3. **Plan for a rockwool ceiling insulation layer** in the top-floor bedrooms later, if budget allows. This can be retrofitted without much disruption as long as the ceiling is planned for it from the start.

Total: roughly NPR 3 to 4 lakh for the primary roof insulation, or about 2.5% of the NPR 1.6 crore target. In return you get a top floor that is 5–8°C cooler in summer afternoons, 3–5°C warmer in winter mornings, and you save on the cost of air conditioning and heating for the lifetime of the house. This is by every reasonable calculation the single highest-return decision in the entire construction project, and it is invisible once the house is finished.

Do not let the contractor talk you out of this. This is the place where their ignorance or their margin-cutting can cost you the comfort of your house forever. Write "Roof insulation: minimum 50 mm EPS plus reflective coating. Non-negotiable." in your Part 1 brief, today if you haven't already.

## Part 5. Windows — frames, glass, and seals

Windows are complicated because they are three things in one: a frame (the material around the glass), the glass itself, and the gap where the window meets the wall. All three have to work for the window to be a good envelope element.

### Frame materials

Four common frame materials in the Kathmandu market, ranked from worst to best for thermal performance:

**Aluminium, without thermal break.** Still common in Kathmandu, especially in budget construction. Aluminium is an excellent thermal *conductor*, which means heat pours through an aluminium window frame as fast as heat can be supplied — approximately 200 times faster than through a wood frame, per unit of cross-section. The frame becomes a thermal bridge and the glass is almost beside the point. Avoid for any new construction unless budget is desperate. Price: NPR 400–700 per square foot of window installed.

**Aluminium with thermal break.** The frame is made of two aluminium sections bonded together by a low-conductivity polymer strip. The thermal break drastically reduces the frame's conductivity. Performance is much better than plain aluminium, though still not as good as UPVC. Price: NPR 700–1,200 per square foot.

**Wood.** Traditional Nepali window material. Good thermal performance (wood is a poor conductor), good aesthetics, but high maintenance — wood swells and shrinks with humidity, paint peels, termites find it interesting. Price varies enormously by wood species: NPR 500–1,500 per square foot installed for local softwood; more for hardwoods.

**UPVC (unplasticised polyvinyl chloride).** Plastic extrusion profiles, typically reinforced internally with galvanised steel. Excellent thermal performance (UPVC conducts heat about 1/2000 as fast as aluminium), excellent weather resistance, good sound insulation when combined with proper sealing. Nepali brands available in 2026 include Encraft, Wincraft, Nepal Windoor, Panchakanya, and Beersheba, with most using international-standard European technology. Price: NPR 1,000–1,800 per square foot installed depending on profile and hardware, which is roughly comparable to or a little above good aluminium-with-thermal-break.

**Recommendation:** UPVC for all new windows unless the family specifically wants wood for aesthetic reasons in one or two locations. Nepali-manufactured and Nepali-supplied UPVC systems have matured significantly in the last five years; the quality is now reliably good for the price.

### Glass

Four main choices for the glass itself:

**Single pane (5 mm or 6 mm clear glass).** The default in older Kathmandu construction. Thermal performance is poor (U-value roughly 5.8 W/m²·K — worse than most brick walls). Single pane is acceptable only in utility rooms and low-use spaces. For any habitable room, upgrade.

**Double pane (DGU — Double Glazed Unit).** Two panes of glass separated by a spacer with a sealed air or argon gas gap between them, typically 8–16 mm. The U-value drops to roughly 2.8 W/m²·K for air-filled, 2.2 for argon-filled. Sound reduction also improves — an average sound separation of 22–25 dB for single-glazed, rising to 30 dB for double-glazed. For a bedroom facing a noisy street, this difference alone justifies the upgrade. Cost premium over single pane: roughly 40–60% of the window cost.

**Low-E double pane.** The inside surface of the outer pane is coated with a thin metallic film that reflects long-wavelength infrared radiation. This prevents heat from escaping outward in winter and prevents solar heat from coming inward in summer. U-value drops to about 1.6 W/m²·K. Cost premium over plain double pane: 10–20%.

**Triple pane.** Three panes, two gas-filled cavities. U-value drops further, to about 0.8–1.0 W/m²·K. In Kathmandu climate, triple pane is overkill for residential — the energy saving over good double pane does not justify the weight and cost. Skip.

**Recommendation:** Good double pane windows, with low-E coating in the bedroom windows and on any window that faces west. Plain double pane elsewhere. No single pane in habitable rooms.

### Window installation details — the seal matters as much as the window

A premium UPVC window installed badly is worse than a cheap aluminium window installed well. The gap between the window frame and the wall opening is where most air leakage happens, and it is invisible once the plaster is done. Three specific details to insist on:

**1. A continuous bead of flexible sealant around the entire window frame,** between the frame and the rough opening in the wall. The sealant should remain flexible over time (silicone or a high-grade PU sealant), because buildings move and rigid sealants crack. NPR 500–1,500 per window for good sealant.

**2. Foam backer rod in the gap before the sealant.** For gaps wider than about 6 mm, a foam rod is pushed into the gap first, then the sealant is applied on top. The rod backs the sealant and prevents it from sinking into the gap. Without the rod, the sealant will eventually fail.

**3. Window sill flashing and drip edge on the outside.** On the exterior side of each window, a horizontal piece of flashing (thin aluminium sheet or PVC) directs any water that reaches the window sill outward, away from the wall. Without this, water wicks down behind the plaster and eventually causes damage inside. NPR 300–500 per window for the flashing.

All three of these are small details. Together they are the difference between a window that is draft-free and watertight for 30 years and a window that leaks from day one.

## Part 6. Doors — the forgotten envelope element

External doors matter more than most builders realise, for the simple reason that doors open and close constantly. Every time an external door opens, the entire air volume of the vestibule is exchanged with outdoor air in a few seconds. If there are a dozen such exchanges a day, you are pushing several cubic metres of conditioned air out of the house and pulling equivalent outdoor air in.

Three external doors to think about carefully:

**The main residential entrance.** A solid wooden door (45–50 mm thick), a frame that seals tightly, and weather stripping around three of the four edges (the top and the two sides), plus a **door sweep** (a flexible rubber or brush strip) on the bottom that contacts the threshold when the door is closed. A door sweep is a tiny piece of hardware — NPR 300–800 per door — and it is the single biggest leak-stopper in the house. Most Kathmandu houses do not have one, and that missing strip is why you feel the afternoon breeze pulling under the front door in the hallway. Install it. Every external door.

**The tea shop shutter.** A rolling metal shutter, open during the day, closed at night. The shutter itself is not a thermal barrier — it is security. But behind the shutter, there should be a proper thermally-sealed shop door with its own weather stripping, so that when the shutter is up and the shop is open, the shop interior is separate from the residential floors above. When the shutter is down and the shop is closed, the sealed door behind it prevents the cold night air from pouring into the residential staircase. Budget NPR 1.5–2.5 lakh for the shutter, plus NPR 30,000–50,000 for a good sealed interior door behind it.

**The door from the shop to the back half-bath and storage.** Often forgotten. This door separates the semi-outdoor shop space from the rest of the residence's back-of-house services. A cheap hollow-core door here defeats the point. Specify a solid door with proper weather stripping.

**The internal doors** (between rooms) matter much less thermally — they separate conditioned spaces from conditioned spaces, and a little air leakage between bedrooms is not a comfort problem. Save money on internal doors; spend on the external ones.

## Part 7. Weather stripping, in plain English

You raised this in your own notes, and it is worth its own section because it is the most misunderstood and under-specified element in Kathmandu construction.

Your instinct is exactly right: **weather stripping is the rubber or foam strip that seals the small gap between two surfaces that come together**, like a door closing against its frame, or a window sash closing against the window frame. The analogy with the rubber seal around a car door is precise. The seal does not bear weight. It does not lock anything. It simply fills the gap so that air cannot flow through.

In a Kathmandu house, weather stripping belongs in four specific places:

**1. Around every external door.** Self-adhesive foam strip on the frame, contacting the door edge when closed, on the top and both sides. Plus a door sweep on the bottom. Total cost per door: NPR 500–1,500.

**2. On every operable window.** UPVC windows come with factory-fitted EPDM rubber gaskets around the sash — this is one of the reasons UPVC is so much better than aluminium at air-sealing. For aluminium and wooden windows, adhesive foam strip should be added around the sash perimeter. If your window opens and you can hear the wind whistling, the weather stripping is missing or degraded.

**3. Around the lid of the underground water tank.** Mentioned yesterday. A foam or rubber gasket around the manhole cover prevents insects, small animals, and surface contamination from entering the tank.

**4. Around electrical outlets and switches on external walls.** This is the one nobody in Kathmandu does, and it matters. Behind each electrical switch or outlet on an external wall, there is an electrical box set into the wall. Between the box and the wall itself, there is a small gap — usually invisible once the switch plate is mounted. That gap lets cold outdoor air leak into the wall cavity and then into the room through the small gap at the edge of the switch plate. A foam gasket behind the switch plate (sold in the US as "outlet gasket foam"; improvised in Nepal with a small piece of weatherstripping foam) seals this. Trivial to install, measurable difference in air leakage. For a house with twenty external-wall outlets and switches, budget NPR 2,000–3,000 for gasket material — effectively a rounding error.

### How to tell if the weather stripping is working

Two field tests, both free, both useful:

**The hand test.** On a windy day, with all external doors and windows closed, walk slowly around the perimeter of each door and each window with your hand. Wherever you feel a draft, the weather stripping is missing or failing there.

**The paper test.** Close a door with a strip of ordinary paper half-inside, half-outside. The paper should grip firmly when you try to pull it out. If it slides out easily, the door is not sealing against the frame. Repeat on all four edges of the door and on every operable window.

Both tests take five minutes and locate every air leak in the house.

## Part 8. Thermal bridges — the sneaky ones

A **thermal bridge** is a point in the building envelope where a conductive material (like a concrete column, a steel lintel, or an aluminium window frame) runs from inside to outside, allowing heat to flow through it much faster than through the adjacent insulated surface. Even if the rest of the envelope is well insulated, a handful of thermal bridges can degrade performance significantly.

In an RCC-frame masonry-infill Kathmandu house, the most common thermal bridges are:

**1. The reinforced concrete columns and beams themselves.** These are continuous from outside to inside, and their R-value is poor (about R-1 for typical 230 mm concrete). The insulated AAC walls between the columns are much better performers than the concrete columns and beams around them. On a cold winter morning, you can often feel the cold spots on the inside wall right where the columns are. The fix, if you want to go to the effort, is to add a layer of external insulation (XPS board, usually 25–50 mm) on the outside face of the concrete elements only, before the external plaster. This is uncommon in Nepal but standard in European passive-house construction. For a budget-constrained Kathmandu build, this is an optional upgrade, not a must-have.

**2. The concrete slab edges where they project outside the wall line (e.g., at balconies).** A concrete balcony that cantilevers out from the slab is a large, highly conductive protrusion from the envelope. The slab edge is a thermal highway. The fix (rare in Nepal, common in Europe) is a *thermal break connector* — a specialised structural connector that allows the concrete to extend but interrupts the thermal path. For your house, the pragmatic alternative is to design balconies as separate lightweight structures (a steel balcony bolted to the building rather than a cantilevered concrete slab), or to accept the thermal loss on the limited balcony area.

**3. Window frames.** Covered above. The single biggest thermal bridge in most houses; solved by UPVC frames with thermal breaks built in.

**4. The parapet wall around the roof terrace.** A 3-foot concrete parapet projecting above the roof is a surface that is exposed on three sides to outdoor air, with its base rooted in the roof slab. It acts as a giant fin that wicks heat out of the house in winter and into it in summer. The fix is an EPS cap on top of the parapet and XPS board on the outer face, under the plaster. Add this to your roof insulation specification.

**5. Service penetrations.** Every pipe, every wire, every duct that goes through the envelope is a potential air leak and thermal bridge. The kitchen exhaust fan on the external wall, the bathroom ventilator, the main incoming water line, the waste pipes. Each penetration should be sealed with a proper fireblock collar or expanding foam, not left as an open hole that the plaster happens to cover. NPR 100–200 per penetration in foam sealant; cumulative impact is real.

Thermal bridges are, by definition, small-area details. But their cumulative effect on comfort is significant. Even a basic envelope upgrade that handles three or four of these sneaky details (UPVC windows, parapet insulation, sealed service penetrations, and outlet gaskets) will give a noticeably better-feeling house than one that nails the walls and the roof but leaves these details for the plaster to hide.

## Part 9. The complete envelope specification for your house

Pulling it all together, here is the envelope specification I would write into your contract with the main contractor:

### Walls
- Ground floor front and street-facing walls: solid brick, 230 mm, English bond, with cement-sand plaster 15 mm on each face. (Durability + thermal mass on the ground floor.)
- All other external walls above ground floor: 150 mm AAC blocks, thin-bed mortar, cement-sand plaster 12 mm internal, 15 mm external waterproofed.
- Internal partitions: 100 mm AAC blocks, thin-bed mortar, cement-sand plaster 10 mm on both faces.
- Wet walls in bathrooms: 150 mm solid brick, 12 mm cement plaster, waterproofing membrane before tiling.

### Roof
- Over the structural slab: 50 mm XPS board (for walkable terrace use), laid in a continuous layer with staggered joints.
- Over the XPS: 50 mm cement screed with 1:4 mix, laid to 1:100 fall for drainage.
- Over the screed: waterproofing membrane — either 4 mm APP bituminous membrane or liquid-applied PU, as contractor prefers.
- Over the membrane: anti-skid roof tiles set in cement mortar.
- On the tile surface: reflective elastomeric roof coating, white or light grey.
- Parapet wall: 25 mm XPS on outer face and top cap, cement-sand plaster finish.

### Windows
- All habitable room windows: UPVC frames, double-glazed with 12 mm argon-filled gap, low-E coating on bedroom windows and any west-facing windows.
- Utility-room windows (bathrooms, kitchen, staircase): UPVC or powder-coated aluminium-with-thermal-break, plain double-glazed.
- All windows: EPDM rubber gaskets on operable sashes (factory-fitted for UPVC), external flashing on sill, silicone sealant bead around frame-to-wall interface on both internal and external faces.

### External Doors
- Main residential entrance: 50 mm solid hardwood (or engineered equivalent), with foam weather-strip on frame top and sides, door sweep on bottom threshold.
- Tea shop rolling shutter: heavy-gauge steel, motorised option included in budget.
- Interior shop door behind shutter: UPVC or solid wood with full weather stripping.
- Shop-to-residence door: solid wood with full weather stripping.
- Terrace door to roof: UPVC double-glazed door, full weather stripping, door sweep.

### Air Sealing Details
- Foam gaskets behind all electrical switch and outlet plates on external walls (estimated 20 locations).
- Expanding foam sealant at every service penetration through the envelope — water inlet, waste outlets, kitchen exhaust, bathroom exhaust, HVAC rough-ins, gas line.
- Continuous silicone sealant bead at every window and door frame perimeter.
- Gasket seal on underground water tank manhole cover.
- Attic hatch (if any) fitted with foam weather stripping and insulated on top.

That is a complete envelope specification. Give it to the main contractor as part of the scope. Any contractor who pushes back on these line items is telling you that they are not used to building this way, and is asking for permission to build a house that will be 20% more expensive to run than it needs to be.

Let us add up the envelope spending against the Part 1 budget framework.

| Envelope item | Budget | NPR |
|---------------|--------|------|
| Brickwork + blockwork + plaster (Part 1 allocation: 15%) | As budgeted | 24 lakh |
| Roof + insulation + waterproofing + reflective coating (Part 1 allocation: 5%) | As budgeted | 8 lakh |
| Doors + windows (Part 1 allocation: 7%) | As budgeted | 11.2 lakh |
| **Envelope subtotal** | | **43.2 lakh** |

The upgrades we have specified over a "standard" Kathmandu envelope:

- **AAC instead of solid brick for most walls:** slight cost saving (10–20%) offset by higher block unit cost. **Net effect: approximately neutral** on the brickwork line; modest saving of NPR 1–2 lakh possible.
- **XPS roof insulation (50 mm) over the slab:** **approximately NPR 2.5 lakh additional** within the roof line, offset in part by reduced HVAC sizing later.
- **UPVC double-glazed windows instead of single-pane aluminium:** **approximately NPR 3–5 lakh additional** within the door/window line. This is the envelope's biggest marginal spend, and by far its biggest marginal return.
- **Weather stripping and air sealing details:** **approximately NPR 50,000–100,000 additional** in small items throughout the build.

Net additional spend on an "upgraded" envelope vs standard Kathmandu construction: roughly NPR 6–8 lakh on the NPR 43.2 lakh envelope budget, or 15–20% more.

Net expected return, over a 25-year horizon: a 30–50% reduction in cooling and heating electricity (realistic for this kind of envelope upgrade in Kathmandu climate), savings of roughly NPR 20,000–40,000 per year in electricity alone, plus longer appliance lifespan, plus the comfort benefit that does not appear on any invoice. Payback period: 15–25 years on energy alone. Comfort payback: immediate.

The math supports the upgrade. The comfort math makes it a no-brainer.

Within the overall NPR 1.6 crore target, the envelope upgrades shift the budget by about 5% within the envelope line and under 1% of the total. The terrace room still has to come out if things tighten; the envelope does not.

## Part 11. Common mistakes — the five most expensive envelope shortcuts

Envelope mistakes, ranked by cost-to-fix:

**1. No roof insulation.** A house without roof insulation can be retrofitted — pulling up tiles, laying EPS, re-waterproofing, re-tiling — for roughly NPR 5 lakh if you can find a contractor willing to do it. Or, more commonly, you live with the discomfort and pay the electricity bill forever. Don't skip it during construction.

**2. Single-pane aluminium windows.** Replacing single-pane aluminium with UPVC double-pane after the house is done costs roughly NPR 2,500–4,000 per square foot of window — five to ten times the in-construction price differential. For a house with 150 sq ft of windows, that is NPR 4–6 lakh to retrofit what would have cost NPR 1.5 lakh to upgrade at construction time.

**3. No weather stripping and no door sweeps.** Cheap to add later (a few thousand rupees of parts, a weekend's work with a screwdriver), but most retrofits are never done, because the owner lives with the draft and calls it "the wind." Add it at construction time; it is in the nature of the house rather than a fix.

**4. Aluminium without thermal break in a climate that needs it.** Upgrading frames after installation is a full window replacement. Same cost as mistake #2.

**5. Exposed electrical outlets on external walls without gaskets.** Fixing this is a quick job — pulling every switch plate, fitting a gasket, replacing the plate. A handyman can do the whole house in a day. But most never do, and the cumulative air leakage remains forever.

Every one of these is cheaper at construction time by a factor of five to ten. Every one of these is cheaper today than it will ever be again. Build right.

## Resources and further reading

For the serious reader on building envelopes:

- **Building Science Corporation** (buildingscience.com) — the best plain-language resource in English on how buildings perform thermally. Their articles on thermal bridges and air sealing are classics.
- **The Passive House Institute** (passivehouse.com) — the European passive house standard is the gold standard for envelope design. Overkill for Nepal, but the underlying principles are universal.
- **Kishore Kumar Yadav, "Energy Efficient Building Envelope for Warm Climate,"** (available via Indian institutional repositories) — a plain-English Indian-climate guide to envelope design principles, most of which translate directly to Kathmandu.
- **Panchakanya, Encraft, and Wincraft** — Nepali UPVC window manufacturers with reasonable technical literature on their websites, including U-value data for their profile systems.
- **Insulation Nepal, Prefab House Nepal** — local suppliers for EPS, XPS, rockwool, and related materials. Call for current pricing before committing to a specification.

For the technical reader, **NBC 109 "Masonry (Unreinforced)"** and **NBC 110 "Plain and Reinforced Concrete"** specify the Nepali standards for wall and concrete construction, including finishing requirements that intersect with envelope performance.

For an international reader: the principles in this article — insulate the roof first, insulate the walls second, upgrade the windows third, air-seal everything — are universal. The specific materials and price points are Nepal 2026; the building physics is eternal.
