There is a small concrete pad beside my office door that has been there longer than I have. It is about four feet square, three inches thick, and it leans, very slightly, to the south-west. If you put a marble on it the marble rolls. The pad does not crack. It does not fail. It does not collapse. It just leans, the way an old man leans on a cane, telling you a story about a soil it has been arguing with for thirty years.

That is, in miniature, what every foundation is — a conversation between a building and the ground it sits on. The conversation has only a few possible endings. Either the building wins, and the foundation transmits its weight cleanly into a soil that politely absorbs it without complaint. Or the soil wins, and the building cracks, settles, tilts, leaks, sags, racks, or in the worst case falls down. Or, most commonly, the two reach a slow uneasy truce — a marble that rolls, a door that sticks in August, a hairline crack above the front window that the homeowner re-paints every five years and never quite understands.

This article is about that conversation. It is about what foundations actually are, what they actually do, why they take the dozen-or-so different forms they take, and how a builder, an inspector, a homeowner, or just somebody who has walked past a construction site and wondered what those orange iron rods are for can think about them clearly and from first principles.

It is a long article. It is meant to be. The previous instalments of this magazine spent seven days walking through every other layer of a house — the brief, the design, the envelope, the plumbing, the wiring, the finishes, the day you move in. We saved foundations for last because foundations, more than any other system in a building, are the one you cannot fix later. You cannot retrofit a foundation in any meaningful sense; you can only repair it, expensively, while the building is still standing. Get the foundation wrong on day one and you spend the next fifty years paying for it. Get it right and it disappears, the way a good carpenter's mitre joint disappears, and the rest of your house simply works.

We will cover the United States and the Kathmandu Valley side by side. The two contexts are very different. The codes are different. The climate is different. The soil is different. The labour markets, the materials, and the tradition are all different. But the underlying physics is the same everywhere, and so are the failure modes. A foundation that bridges a sinkhole in central Florida and a foundation that bridges a soft pocket of Kalimati clay in central Kathmandu are doing essentially the same job, just dressed differently. By the time we are done you should be able to look at a hole in the ground and tell, just from the dirt that came out of it, roughly what foundation it needs.

A short admin note before we begin. This is not a code book and it is not engineering advice for your specific building. Buildings are designed by licensed engineers for very good reasons, and what is written here will not substitute for a stamped drawing or a soil report. What it will do is help you understand the drawing when you read it, ask better questions of the engineer who stamped it, and recognise on site the difference between work that will last and work that will not. With that caveat in place, let us go underground.

## Part 1. What a foundation actually does

Picture, for a moment, a 2 × 4 stud standing vertically on a sheet of dry sand. The stud is bearing your weight — perhaps 80 kilograms, perhaps 180 pounds. Push down. The stud sinks. It does not sink because the wood is weak; it sinks because the contact area is small (a single nominal 2 × 4 has a cross-section of about 5.25 square inches, roughly 34 square centimetres) and the load per unit area at that contact is enormous. Put the same stud on a square of plywood eight times its area and it stops sinking. The wood did not change, the load did not change, the sand did not change. What changed is the *pressure*, which is force divided by area.

That, in one sentence, is what a foundation does. It takes the loads of the building above — its own weight, the weight of the people and furniture inside, the weight of the snow on the roof, the wind pushing against the walls, the earthquake shaking the ground sideways — and it spreads those loads over enough surface area of soil that the soil can carry them without failing.

This is true for every foundation type ever built. A shallow continuous strip footing under a load-bearing wall does it by being wider than the wall. A drilled shaft 80 feet deep does it by reaching down through bad soil to good rock. A raft does it by spreading the entire building's weight over essentially its full footprint. A pile group does it by transferring load through skin friction along the sides of the piles into the surrounding soil. The forms are different. The job is the same.

The job has four sub-parts, and it is worth being precise about each of them, because most foundation problems are really failures in just one of the four:

The first sub-part is **bearing**. The foundation must be wide enough, and the soil under it strong enough, that the soil does not shear and squeeze out from underneath. This is what people mean when they say a soil has "low bearing capacity" — they mean the soil can only carry so many kilonewtons per square metre, or pounds per square foot, before it gives way and the foundation punches through. A typical residential dense sandy loam in good condition will carry 100 to 150 kilopascals (roughly 2,000 to 3,000 pounds per square foot) safely. Soft Kathmandu Valley clay can be as low as 50 to 60 kilopascals — about a third of that. Hard rock under a Vermont hillside can carry 10,000 kilopascals or more. Same building, three soils, three completely different footing widths required.

The second sub-part is **settlement**. Even if a soil does not fail in shear, it will compress when you load it. All soils compress to some degree under load — clays a lot, dense sands a little, rock essentially not at all. A foundation system must not only avoid catastrophic bearing failure, it must keep the building from settling so much, or so unevenly, that the building tears itself apart. There is no useful structural distinction between a building that has fallen down and a building that has settled six inches at one corner; in both cases the building is unusable. The codes accept some settlement (typically up to about 25 millimetres, or one inch, total, with about half that in differential between adjacent footings) but the design must demonstrate that the soil under the planned footing will not compress more than that under load.

The third sub-part is **lateral resistance and overturning**. A building does not just push downward. Wind pushes sideways. Earthquakes shake sideways. Hill slopes push sideways. Water against a basement wall pushes sideways. A foundation must resist these horizontal loads — through friction at its base, through passive earth pressure on its sides, through the weight of the building itself which keeps it from tipping over, and in some cases through tension elements like anchor bolts and tiedowns that hold the building down to the foundation when uplift forces (mostly hurricane winds, but also seismic) try to lift it.

The fourth sub-part is **durability**. The foundation has to do the first three jobs not just on day one but for the design life of the building — typically 50 years for a residential structure, 100 years for an institutional building, longer for civil works. Concrete must not corrode through. Reinforcement must not rust. Wood, if used, must not rot. The foundation must accommodate frost heave, expansive soil movement, seasonal water table swings, and the slow chemical attack of sulphates and chlorides that are present in most soils to some degree.

Almost everything else you will read in this article — the difference between strip and pad footings, the choice between slab-on-grade and basement, the question of whether to drive piles or pour drilled shafts — is just a different combination of solutions to those four problems, calibrated for a particular soil, climate, building type, and budget. Once you see the four problems clearly, the dozen foundation types stop being a confusing menu and become a coherent design space.

## Part 2. The soils every foundation rests on

If foundations are conversations with the soil, then it pays to know who you are talking to. Soil is not a uniform material. It is a complicated mineral-and-organic-and-water-and-air assemblage that varies enormously in space, in time, and with depth. The geotechnical engineer's first job, on any project of consequence, is to figure out what is actually under the building.

Soils are classified, broadly, by particle size. The standard US system is the Unified Soil Classification System (USCS) per ASTM D2487. Particles larger than 75 millimetres are *cobbles and boulders*. Particles between 4.75 millimetres and 75 millimetres are *gravel*, subdivided into coarse and fine. Particles between 0.075 millimetres and 4.75 millimetres are *sand* — coarse, medium, and fine. Anything smaller than 0.075 millimetres (the No. 200 sieve) is a *fine* — silt or clay. Silt is distinguished from clay by plasticity rather than size: silt is non-plastic or slightly plastic, clay is plastic. The plasticity index, the difference between the liquid limit and plastic limit, distinguishes them.

This taxonomy matters because the four foundation problems above behave very differently in different soil types, and the right foundation depends on which soil you have.

**Gravels and clean sands** are the friendliest substrate to build on. They have high friction angles (35° or more), high bearing capacity (often 200 to 400 kilopascals at modest depths even without any additional confinement), they drain freely, they do not shrink or swell with moisture, and they compress only slightly. A typical compacted gravel layer under a slab provides excellent bearing and excellent drainage. The downside is that loose, saturated, fine sand is the single most liquefaction-prone material on Earth — under earthquake shaking it can lose all its strength in seconds. Coastal Florida, the Kanto plain in Japan, the Christchurch suburbs of New Zealand, and the river-bank neighbourhoods of central Kathmandu have all seen catastrophic liquefaction-driven foundation failures.

**Silts** are an intermediate case. They have lower bearing capacity than sands (typically 75 to 150 kilopascals), they hold water well, they can be frost-susceptible (the worst frost-heave soils in North America are silty clays and clayey silts), and they can be liquefiable when saturated and loose. The grey silty beds of the Kalimati Formation under central Kathmandu are silts with a substantial clay fraction, and they govern the foundation design across most of the central Valley.

**Clays** are the trickiest. Their bearing capacity depends enormously on their consistency — soft clay (under 25 kilopascals undrained shear strength) is the worst possible building soil, while stiff to hard clay (75 to 200 kilopascals undrained shear strength) is perfectly acceptable. Clays compress slowly and a great deal, so settlement governs more often than bearing failure. Clays do not freeze deeply because they hold their water tightly, but the water itself migrates and forms ice lenses that cause the worst frost heave of any soil type. And, critically, *expansive* clays — clays containing the smectite mineral group, especially montmorillonite — swell when wet and shrink when dry, sometimes by 30% or more of their volume. The Blackland Prairie of central Texas, including most of the Dallas–Fort Worth metroplex, is built on expansive clay. The Permian-age Yazoo clay under Jackson, Mississippi is one of the most aggressive expansive soils in the world. Similar soils underlie parts of South Africa, India's Deccan Plateau, and pockets of central Australia.

**Organic soils** — peats and highly organic clays — are essentially never acceptable as direct bearing strata. They are too compressible, too weak, and too biologically active. When found in the upper layers of a building site, they must either be removed and replaced with engineered fill, or else the foundation must penetrate through them to a competent stratum below.

**Bedrock**, when sound and within reach, is the gold standard. A foundation bearing directly on rock has effectively unlimited bearing capacity at residential scales and essentially zero settlement. The cost is excavation and the requirement that the rock be of appropriate quality (fresh, unweathered, not heavily fractured). Rock under shallow soil is a feature of much of New England, Appalachia, the Canadian Shield, the Scottish Highlands, and the rocky valleys of the Nepal hills outside the alluvial floodplains.

The geotechnical engineer's other major concerns are:

**Groundwater table**. The depth of the water table affects bearing capacity (saturated soil can have half the bearing capacity of the same soil dry), buoyancy on basements (a basement is essentially a boat hull, and groundwater will try to float it if the water table is high enough), waterproofing requirements, and liquefaction susceptibility. Kathmandu Valley's water table sits typically 2 to 5 metres below grade in the wet season, much deeper in the dry season. Coastal Florida sits with a water table often within inches of grade. Northern Minnesota's frost level can extend below the seasonal water table.

**Soil profile**. Soils occur in layers (called horizons or strata), and the foundation engineer must understand the entire profile to depths greater than the influence depth of the foundation — typically twice the foundation width below its base. A 6-foot wide footing is influenced by soil at least 12 feet below it, often more. A pile foundation is influenced by soil throughout its full driven length plus several diameters below its toe.

**Soil chemistry**. Sulphates in the soil attack ordinary Portland cement concrete; high sulphate soils require sulphate-resistant cement (Type V in the US, or blended cements with high slag/fly-ash content). Chlorides accelerate corrosion of reinforcement. Acidic soils degrade galvanised steel. The geotechnical report should always include sulphate and chloride content for any project larger than a single-family house.

**Soil age and origin**. Soils are products of geology. *Residual* soils form in place by weathering of bedrock and tend to be irregular, with rock outcrops and weathered zones interspersed. *Alluvial* soils are deposited by rivers and tend to be horizontally bedded, often with strong layering. *Lacustrine* soils, deposited in former lakes, tend to be very fine and very horizontally consistent. The Kathmandu Valley's central deposits are dominantly lacustrine — the famous Kalimati Formation is, geologically speaking, the bottom of an ancient lake, and that single fact explains most of why building foundations in Kathmandu is harder than building foundations on, say, Pittsburgh's residual hillside soils. *Aeolian* soils, deposited by wind, include the loess deposits of the central US and central China — they are very low density and prone to collapse on wetting. *Glacial* soils, deposited by ice, include the dense till that underlies most of the upper Midwest and New England, which is excellent for shallow foundations because the glacier essentially pre-loaded it for us.

The point of this taxonomy is that you cannot decide on a foundation without knowing which soil you have, and you cannot know which soil you have without testing it. Which brings us to the soil report.

## Part 3. The soil test: what it is and why every serious project gets one

There is a temptation, on small residential projects, to skip the soil test. The argument runs: my neighbour built a house here ten years ago, the local builder has been pouring slabs in this neighbourhood for thirty years, the city does not require a geotechnical report for a single-family residence under such-and-such code, and a soil test costs anywhere from $1,500 in a US suburb to NPR 60,000–120,000 in Kathmandu. Why bother?

The answer is that soil varies on the scale of metres, sometimes on the scale of centimetres, and your neighbour's house being fine is not very strong evidence that yours will be. We have seen, in the Kathmandu Valley alone, two adjacent plots in the same ward where one bore down to dense sand at 4 metres and the other still had soft black clay at 12 metres. The houses on those plots required completely different foundations. Both houses are still standing. The one on the soft-clay plot required pile foundations that cost about NPR 14 lakh more than the isolated footings used on the dense-sand plot.

A residential geotechnical investigation in the United States typically consists of the following:

A site reconnaissance, where the engineer walks the lot, looks for surface evidence of soil type (clay heaves up at the surface differently from sand; loess shows characteristic fissuring; expansive clay shows characteristic shrinkage cracks in the dry season), checks for surface water and drainage, notes the slope, and notes any nearby existing structures and how they are doing.

Two to four soil borings, drilled to 15 to 30 feet (5 to 10 metres) depth depending on the project. The borings are made with either a hollow-stem auger rig (the most common method in residential work) or, for deeper or more difficult investigations, a tracked drill rig with mud rotary or sonic capabilities. At regular intervals (typically every 2.5 or 5 feet) a Standard Penetration Test (SPT) is performed using a split-spoon sampler driven by a 140-pound hammer falling 30 inches; the number of blows required to drive the sampler 12 inches is recorded as the *N value*, which correlates with bearing capacity, density, and other engineering parameters. Disturbed samples are retrieved with the SPT, and undisturbed samples (Shelby tubes) are sometimes pushed in cohesive soils for laboratory shear strength testing.

Laboratory testing of selected samples for grain-size distribution, Atterberg limits (liquid limit, plastic limit, plasticity index), moisture content, density, and where appropriate consolidation parameters and direct or triaxial shear strength.

A written report, usually 20 to 40 pages, summarising the soil profile, recommending foundation type(s), giving allowable bearing pressures at design depths, addressing seismic site classification, noting any special soils (expansive, collapsible, organic), and providing construction recommendations.

In the Kathmandu Valley the equivalent investigation is mandated by NBC 206 for any building of three storeys or more, and is universally specified by competent designers for residential buildings of any scale. The local practice is broadly similar to the US practice — typically two to four boreholes drilled with a tripod-and-tube percussion rig (because hollow-stem augers are uncommon in the local equipment fleet), SPTs every 1.5 metres, samples sent to one of a half-dozen recognised geotechnical labs in Kathmandu (Soil Test Pvt. Ltd., Multi Lab, Pashupati Drillers, Geotechnical and Associates, and a few others), and a report prepared in English using SI units. Cost in 2026 is typically NPR 60,000 to 150,000 for a residential investigation, depending on depth and number of boreholes.

The two most important numbers in the report, for a residential designer, are usually:

1. The allowable bearing pressure at the design footing depth — typically 100 to 200 kilopascals (kPa) for shallow foundations on competent Kathmandu Valley soil, or 1,500 to 4,000 pounds per square foot (psf) for typical US residential soils.
2. The depth to a competent bearing stratum, if shallow soils are unsuitable. This drives the decision between shallow and deep foundations.

Other important findings include the seismic site class (which affects the lateral-load design), the depth to groundwater (which affects waterproofing and constructability), the presence of expansive or collapsible soils, and any special hazards (sinkholes in karst country, abandoned mines in coal regions, fill of unknown origin in older urban lots, liquefaction susceptibility in seismic regions).

A useful rule of thumb: if the SPT N value at the planned footing depth is less than about 5, you are looking at a very soft soil and shallow foundations are probably a bad idea. If it is between 5 and 10, shallow foundations are possible but require care and probably wider-than-typical footings. If it is between 10 and 30, you are in the standard residential range and ordinary footings will work. If it is above 30, you have dense soil and footings can be relatively narrow. If you hit refusal (50 blows for less than 6 inches of penetration) you are essentially on rock or extremely dense till.

## Part 4. Climate and the loads a foundation must carry

A foundation does not just carry the building. It also carries — or resists — whatever the climate throws at the building. We will deal with the structural loads in detail later, but it is worth pausing here to enumerate the climate loads, because they are the single biggest reason why the same house is built on different foundations in different places.

**Frost.** When water freezes it expands by about 9% by volume. Soils with substantial fines (silts and silty clays) hold capillary water, and when that water freezes in the pore spaces and especially in the form of *ice lenses* — laminated horizontal plates of segregated ice that form by water migration to the freezing front — the soil can heave upward by tens of centimetres. A foundation bearing on soil shallower than the frost line will be lifted, and lifted unevenly, with predictably destructive consequences. The IRC requires footings to extend below the frost line specified by the local jurisdiction, with a 12-inch absolute minimum depth regardless of frost. Frost depths vary enormously across the United States. From the south coast of Florida and Hawaii, where the frost depth is effectively zero, to the Texas coast at six inches, to Atlanta and Memphis at about 12 inches, to Kansas City and Cincinnati at 30 inches, to Chicago at 42 inches, to Minneapolis at 42 inches in the southern part of the state and 60 inches in northern Minnesota, to Fargo at 75 inches, to Anchorage at 120 inches in cast-in-place piers, to interior Alaska where deeper still and where permafrost itself becomes the controlling consideration. The Kathmandu Valley does not have a meaningful frost depth — winter ground temperature in central Kathmandu rarely drops below 8°C even in January — and so frost is one fewer consideration for valley residential foundations. (It does become a consideration for buildings in the higher hill stations, places like Nagarkot, Daman, and the upper reaches of the surrounding watershed, where ground frost can extend to 30 to 60 centimetres in cold winters.)

**Wind.** Wind exerts pressure on the walls and roof of a building. In severe wind events — hurricanes, severe thunderstorms, tornadoes — the wind also creates uplift on the roof, which transmits down through the structure and tries to pull the building off its foundation. Building codes include wind speed maps; the IRC's basic wind speed map has values from 90 mph in interior, low-wind regions to 180 mph in the most exposed coastal areas. Wind drives requirements for anchor bolts (the bolts that hold the wood sill plate to the concrete foundation), hurricane straps (the metal connectors that tie the roof framing to the wall framing), and overall structural continuity from roof to foundation. In Kathmandu Valley wind speeds are modest by global standards — design basic wind speed under NBC 104 is 47 m/s in most of the central valley, equivalent to about 105 mph — and wind rarely governs residential foundations.

**Seismic.** Earthquakes shake the ground horizontally. The building must move with the ground, transferring shear forces between the structure and the foundation, between adjacent foundation elements, and into the soil. In high-seismic areas (most of the western US, almost all of Nepal, much of Japan and Indonesia and Italy and Mexico), seismic loads usually govern the lateral design of the foundation. Seismic design also brings in the secondary hazard of *liquefaction*, where saturated loose sands and silty sands lose all their shear strength during shaking, becoming temporarily a thick liquid. A foundation on liquefiable soil that has not been designed for liquefaction will sink, tilt, or punch through. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake in Nepal produced extensive liquefaction in the central Kathmandu Valley, especially along the floodplains of the Bagmati and Bishnumati rivers, and the foundation damage there was concentrated almost entirely in liquefiable soils. The 2011 Christchurch earthquake in New Zealand produced even more spectacular liquefaction damage. Seismic foundation design in the US is governed by ASCE 7 (linked to the IBC) and ACI 318 Chapter 18 (for concrete); in Nepal it is governed by NBC 105:2020.

**Flood.** Building in a floodplain — riverine or coastal — adds the requirement that the foundation withstand the pressures of moving water, the impact of debris carried by the water, scour (the erosion of soil from around the foundation), and in coastal cases the dynamic forces of breaking waves. The US National Flood Insurance Program, administered by FEMA, requires that buildings in mapped flood zones be either elevated above the base flood elevation (in A zones, by a foundation system that may include flood vents to equalise pressure) or supported on open piles or columns above the base flood elevation (in V zones, where wave action governs). The Florida Building Code and its various local supplements implement these federal requirements; in the highest-risk V zones (Coastal High Hazard Areas), the building must be on piles, the lowest horizontal structural member must be at or above the design flood elevation, and any walls below the elevation must be *breakaway* walls — walls designed to fail under a defined load without damaging the supporting structure. Kathmandu Valley flood risk is concentrated along the rivers (Bagmati, Bishnumati, Manohara) and along certain low-lying drains; the relevant Nepali planning rules (the relevant ward-level building bye-laws and the riparian setback requirements in the most recent KMC and BKT bye-laws) prohibit building within 20 metres of the river edge in central reaches.

**Expansive soils.** The soils of the Blackland Prairie under Dallas, Fort Worth, Waco, and much of central Texas; the Yazoo clay under Jackson, Mississippi; the Pierre shale under parts of Colorado, Wyoming, and the Dakotas; and the heavy clays of the southern Kathmandu Valley around Lubhu and Harisiddhi all share the property of expansiveness — they swell substantially when wet and shrink when dry. The seasonal volume change can be 10 to 30% in highly expansive clays. A foundation on expansive soil that has not been designed for expansion will heave in the wet season and settle in the dry season, often unevenly, and will tear itself apart. Expansive soils drive the choice of pier-and-beam foundations in older Texas houses, and post-tensioned slabs in newer Texas houses. They also drive the legendary "slab leak" foundation problems that have given Texas one of the largest residential foundation-repair industries in the world.

**Collapsible soils.** Loess (windblown silt) and certain alluvial fan deposits in arid regions can be metastable — they appear strong dry, but lose substantial volume when wetted. Foundations on collapsible soils that get wet from a leaking sewer line or an unanticipated landscape irrigation system can settle dramatically, sometimes 5 to 15 centimetres, in a matter of days. Loess is widespread in the Mississippi Valley, the Palouse region of eastern Washington, much of central China, and parts of central Europe. Collapsible alluvial fan soils underlie parts of Albuquerque, Las Vegas, and Phoenix.

**Karst and sinkholes.** Where limestone bedrock is dissolved by groundwater, voids form. When the soil cap above a void is overloaded or when groundwater levels drop, the cap collapses, producing a sinkhole. Florida sits on Florida limestone and is the global capital of sinkhole damage. Parts of Kentucky, Tennessee, central Pennsylvania, Texas's Edwards Plateau, and the Yucatán of Mexico are also active karst regions. Foundations in karst country require special investigation — typically including ground-penetrating radar and microgravity surveys for any structure of significance — and may require either deep foundations bearing below the cavity-prone zone or compensation grouting to fill voids.

**Permafrost.** The northernmost parts of Alaska, much of the Canadian and Russian Arctic, and the high Tibetan Plateau sit on perennially frozen ground. Building on permafrost is a special problem because the building heat tends to thaw the permafrost beneath it, with catastrophic settlement consequences. Foundations on permafrost must either be designed to keep the permafrost frozen (insulated piles, cooled foundations, ventilated underfloor spaces) or to bear on the active layer alone, or to penetrate to bedrock below the permafrost. None of this is relevant to the lower Kathmandu Valley but it matters for high-altitude construction in the upper Himalayan villages and trekking lodges.

**Termites.** In a remarkable amount of the global building code, termites are a foundation issue. Termites enter wood-framed buildings most commonly through the foundation — through cracks in slabs, through the gaps between sill plates and concrete, through unprotected wood-to-soil contact in crawl spaces. The IRC has an entire termite-protection regime, including pre-treatment of soil under slabs, requirements for treated wood at the sill, and prohibitions on any wood-to-soil contact within a specified distance of the building. The Kathmandu Valley has its own termite problem (Coptotermes and Odontotermes species are common), and termite shields and treated sill timbers are standard practice.

That is the climate's contribution to the foundation problem. Now we are ready to talk about codes.

## Part 5. The codes — a brisk tour of who governs what

Codes are not the most thrilling reading, but they are the language in which building decisions are made, and a designer who does not know the codes is operating in the dark. We will cover the major US codes and the Nepali codes; if your country is elsewhere, the structure will be similar and the major reference standards (Eurocode 7, Eurocode 8, AS 2870, IS 456 in India, etc.) will play similar roles.

In the United States, residential foundations are governed primarily by the **International Residential Code (IRC)**, currently the 2024 edition with the 2027 edition under development at the time of writing. The IRC is an International Code Council document, adopted with state and local amendments by virtually every US jurisdiction (although with significant variation, especially on the West Coast). Chapter 4 of the IRC covers foundations: footings (R403), foundation walls (R404), drainage (R405), waterproofing and dampproofing (R406), columns (R407), and crawl spaces (R408). The IRC's footing tables (R403.1(1) through R403.1(3)) prescribe footing widths and depths based on soil bearing capacity, the number of storeys above, and the seismic design category. Footing minimum widths range from 12 inches for a one-storey wood-frame house on 3,000 psf soil up to 32 inches and more for three-storey houses on weaker soils. Minimum footing thickness is 6 inches and minimum depth is 12 inches below grade, with deeper requirements where frost line, expansive soil, or seismic considerations apply. The IRC also includes a frost-line table (R301.2) where the local jurisdiction is required to fill in the local frost depth for code purposes.

For commercial and multi-family construction, the **International Building Code (IBC)** applies, currently the 2024 edition, with the 2027 IBC expected to incorporate the 2025 edition of ACI 318 by reference. Chapter 18 of the IBC covers soils and foundations; it is more rigorous than the IRC and often requires a geotechnical report for any non-residential or large-residential construction.

The structural concrete itself is governed by **ACI 318: Building Code Requirements for Structural Concrete**, published by the American Concrete Institute, with the most recent edition being ACI 318-25, released in print on July 9, 2025. ACI 318-25 contains a substantially revised treatment of deep foundations — consolidating the IBC and ACI deep foundation standards into a single framework — and tightens up several concrete-design provisions for both seismic and non-seismic regions. It is the bible of structural concrete in the US and is referenced by both the IRC and the IBC. The 2025 edition is expected to be adopted by reference in the 2027 IBC.

Loads are governed by **ASCE 7: Minimum Design Loads and Associated Criteria for Buildings and Other Structures**, published by the American Society of Civil Engineers. The current edition is ASCE 7-22 with ASCE 7-28 in development. ASCE 7 covers dead, live, snow, wind, seismic, flood, and other loads, and it is the document that drives the wind speed maps, the seismic hazard maps (the Risk-Targeted Maximum Considered Earthquake or MCEr maps), and the flood load equations.

For frost-protected shallow foundations, **ASCE 32: Design and Construction of Frost-Protected Shallow Foundations** is the controlling reference. The IRC R403.3 incorporates a simplified prescriptive version of ASCE 32 for residential use; the full ASCE 32 standard is used for engineered designs.

The relevant flood-zone construction is governed by FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program rules and by **ASCE 24: Flood Resistant Design and Construction**.

That is the federal layer. On top of this, every state has its own building code apparatus, often closely tracking the IRC/IBC with state-specific amendments. Florida has the Florida Building Code, with very detailed coastal construction provisions. California has the California Building Code with extensive seismic provisions. Texas largely defers to local jurisdictions. Minnesota has its own residential code with the famous Zone I / Zone II frost provisions (60 inches in northern Minnesota, 42 inches in southern Minnesota, both substantially exceeding the IRC default).

In Nepal, the relevant codes are the **Nepal Building Code (NBC)** series, developed by the Department of Urban Development and Building Construction (DUDBC). The most relevant codes for residential foundations are:

**NBC 105:2020 — Seismic Design of Buildings in Nepal**, the controlling seismic code, which replaced the 1994 edition after extensive revision following the 2015 Gorkha earthquake. NBC 105:2020 introduces a modern force-based seismic design procedure, distinguishes between ductile and limited-ductility structural systems, and incorporates updated hazard maps based on probabilistic seismic hazard analysis. Zoning factors for the Kathmandu Valley are typically Z=1.0 in the most recent code, the highest in the country, reflecting the valley's seismic and site-amplification hazard. NBC 105:2020 also classifies sites by soil shear-wave velocity into categories A through D, with most of the Kathmandu Valley falling into the softest category (D, exceptionally soft soil).

**NBC 201:1994 — Mandatory Rules of Thumb for Reinforced Concrete Buildings without Masonry Infill**. This is the prescriptive standard that governs most of the small-to-medium reinforced concrete frame residential construction in the Kathmandu Valley. It specifies column sizes, beam sizes, slab thicknesses, reinforcement ratios, and detailing requirements for buildings up to three storeys with a fixed plan layout. Many municipal building permit offices will accept NBC 201 designs without requiring a stamped engineering drawing.

**NBC 206:2003 — Architectural Design Requirements**, including site-development setbacks, ground-floor coverage limits, plot ratios, and the like.

**NBC 105:2020 site soil classification**, which directly drives the seismic design coefficient and therefore the column and footing sizes.

The Kathmandu Metropolitan City and the various other municipalities of the Valley (Lalitpur, Bhaktapur, Kirtipur, Madhyapur Thimi, and others) each have their own building bye-laws ("naksa pass" rules) that overlay the national codes with local setback requirements, plot-coverage rules, height limits, and architectural-character provisions (especially in the heritage cores of Bhaktapur, Patan, and the Kathmandu Durbar Square area).

A note on Indian standards, since Nepal historically follows India closely: **IS 456:2000** (concrete code), **IS 1893** (seismic code), and **IS 1904** (foundation code) are sometimes referenced in Kathmandu engineering practice, especially where NBC standards are silent. In practice, modern Kathmandu engineers use NBC for the main structural design and IS as a supplementary reference.

That is the formal framework. Now we can finally talk about the foundations themselves.

## Part 6. Strip footings — the workhorse of low-rise residential construction

A *strip footing* (also called a *continuous footing* or, more loosely, a *spread footing* when it runs continuously under a wall) is a long, narrow, reinforced concrete footing that runs continuously beneath a load-bearing wall. It is the most common foundation element in low-rise construction worldwide. If you have ever poured a footing for a typical wood-framed house basement or crawl space — anywhere from northern Minnesota to upstate New York to the rural Midwest — you have poured strip footings.

The geometry is simple. A strip footing is wider than the wall above it, so that the wall load spreads out into the soil at a wider area. The width is set so that the bearing pressure (the wall load per linear foot, divided by the footing width) does not exceed the soil's allowable bearing capacity. The thickness is set so that the footing itself does not break in shear or bending under the moments that develop as the wall load tries to bend the projecting "wings" of the footing.

A typical strip footing for a one-storey wood-framed house on 2,000 psf bearing soil, per IRC Table R403.1(1), is 12 inches wide and 6 inches thick. For a two-storey house on the same soil, you go to 15 or 18 inches wide. For a three-storey on softer 1,500 psf soil, you might go to 26 or 28 inches wide. The thickness scales with the projection — IRC R403.1.1 requires footing thickness at least equal to the projection beyond the wall, and not less than 6 inches (8 inches for plain concrete).

In the Kathmandu Valley, strip footings under load-bearing brick walls (the dominant construction system before the rise of RCC frame construction in the 1990s) are typically 750 to 900 millimetres wide and 300 to 450 millimetres thick, sometimes wider for two-storey or larger buildings. The traditional Newari brick courtyard houses of Bhaktapur and Patan are built on stepped brick footings, with each successive course of footing brick stepping outward, distributing the wall load progressively over a wider area. These ancient footings, three to five hundred years old in some cases, have survived multiple major earthquakes and centuries of monsoon weather. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake damaged many of these traditional buildings in the upper storeys but rarely at the foundation level.

The reinforcement of a modern strip footing depends on whether it is "plain" (unreinforced) concrete or reinforced concrete. The IRC permits plain concrete footings for most residential construction, but engineers typically include at least minimal reinforcement — two longitudinal bars, often 12mm or No. 4 (#4) bars top and bottom in the US, with 8mm or 10mm transverse stirrups every 200mm — for ductility, crack control, and resistance to differential settlement. In the Kathmandu Valley under NBC 105:2020 for any reinforced concrete frame building, the strip footing or its connecting plinth beam is fully reinforced with main bars (typically 4 to 6 numbers of 16mm or 20mm Fe500D bars) and stirrups (8mm at 150 to 200mm centres).

**A worked example.** Suppose you are designing a strip footing for a two-storey wood-frame house in suburban Kansas City. Soil bearing is 2,500 psf per the geotechnical report. The dead and live load coming down the load-bearing wall is 1,800 pounds per linear foot. The required footing width is therefore 1,800 / 2,500 = 0.72 feet, or about 8.6 inches wide. But the IRC minimum is 12 inches and your wall plus a reasonable projection is at least that, so you go with a 16-inch wide footing, which gives you a comfortable factor of safety. Footing thickness 8 inches. Concrete grade 3,000 psi (about 21 MPa). Reinforcement: two #4 bars longitudinal top and bottom, with #3 transverse bars at 18-inch centres. Place on undisturbed natural soil, at minimum 30 inches below grade for frost protection (assuming a Kansas City frost depth of 30 inches), with 3 inches of clear concrete cover to the reinforcement. Step the footing up where the grade rises, in increments not exceeding the footing thickness in elevation per IRC R403.1.5.

**A worked example, Kathmandu version.** Two-storey reinforced concrete frame house in Bhaktapur on 100 kPa soil. Dead and live load coming down the central column is 200 kN; this is an isolated column footing rather than a strip footing, but the principles are similar. Required footing area = 200 / 100 = 2.0 square metres, so 1.5 m × 1.5 m would be your minimum, but for an NBC 201 building you would typically use 1.6 m × 1.6 m with a 300mm thickness. Concrete grade M20 (20 MPa) per NBC 201, with M25 increasingly common for engineered designs. Reinforcement: a mat of 12mm Fe500D bars at 150mm centres each way, top and bottom, with 50mm clear cover. Pour on a 75mm lean concrete blinding (PCC) layer over compacted gravel. Provide a tie beam (plinth beam) at plinth level connecting all column footings to limit differential movement.

## Part 7. Isolated and combined footings

Where a building uses point loads (columns) rather than continuous wall loads, the footing under each column is an *isolated footing* (also called a *pad footing*). Isolated footings are universal in reinforced concrete frame buildings, which means they are the dominant residential foundation in the Kathmandu Valley and a common foundation in commercial construction worldwide.

The geometry is similar to a strip footing in section, but rather than running along under a wall, it is a discrete square or rectangular pad under each column. Sizes depend on the column load and the soil bearing capacity. Typical residential isolated footings in the Kathmandu Valley are 1.2 to 2.0 metres on a side and 250 to 450 millimetres thick, with rebar mats top and bottom and a starter cage of column bars rising up out of the centre.

Where two columns are close together — say, two adjacent columns of a frame near a property boundary, where each column's required isolated footing would overlap with the other's — the two footings can be merged into a **combined footing** spanning under both columns. A combined footing is a pair of column loads on a single rectangular or trapezoidal footing pad, with reinforcement designed to deal with the bending moments along the long axis. They are common at building corners, at the junctions between frame buildings and shear walls, and (in Kathmandu) wherever the strict 1.5 m setback from the property line would otherwise require an isolated footing to extend across the boundary, which is illegal.

A particularly elegant combined-footing variant is the **strap footing** (also called a cantilever footing), where two isolated footings are connected by a relatively shallow but stiff "strap" beam that distributes load between them while keeping each pad mostly under its own column. Strap footings are particularly useful where one of the two columns is at the property boundary and cannot have a footing centred on it.

The reinforcement of an isolated footing is a two-way mat — bars running both directions, top and bottom — sized to resist the bending moments that develop when the column load tries to bend the projecting edges of the footing. The footing must also be checked for one-way shear (beam shear), two-way shear (punching shear), and bearing under the column itself. Punching shear failure — where the column literally punches through the footing — is the worst footing failure mode, and is the reason that footing thickness is often set by punching shear rather than by bending.

On the topic of footing depth: isolated footings, like strip footings, must extend below the local frost line (or be protected per ASCE 32) and must rest on undisturbed natural soil, controlled engineered fill, or a competent bearing stratum. The minimum 12-inch depth still applies. In Kathmandu Valley practice, isolated footings are typically founded at 1.2 to 2.0 metres depth — partly for bearing reasons (the upper metre of urban Kathmandu soil is often disturbed fill or weak made-ground) and partly to integrate with the underground water tank, which is almost universally cast as a single concrete monolith with the foundation system.

## Part 8. Slab-on-grade — the modern American default

If you build a house in suburban Houston, suburban Phoenix, suburban Atlanta, or suburban Las Vegas in 2026, you almost certainly build it on a slab-on-grade. Slab-on-grade is the default residential foundation in most of the southern and western United States, in much of Australia, and in a substantial fraction of new construction worldwide. It is fast, it is economical, it is straightforward to build, and where it is appropriate it works very well.

A slab-on-grade is exactly what it sounds like: a single concrete slab, typically 4 to 6 inches thick, poured directly on a prepared sub-base, with thickened edges that act as integral footings carrying the perimeter walls of the building. The top of the slab is the finished floor of the building (or, more often, has a finish flooring of tile, vinyl, hardwood, or carpet placed over it).

A typical residential slab-on-grade in the southern US has the following layers, working from bottom to top:

A *prepared subgrade* — the natural soil, scraped clean of organics and topsoil, compacted to 95% of standard Proctor density.

*Granular fill* — typically 4 to 6 inches of compacted aggregate (#57 stone, crushed limestone, or pea gravel), which provides drainage, breaks capillary rise of water from the soil, and serves as a working surface for the slab forms.

A *vapour retarder* — a 6-mil to 15-mil polyethylene sheet, lapped at seams and sealed at penetrations, which prevents water vapour from migrating up through the slab and into the building. This is required by the IRC in essentially all habitable construction. A continuous vapour retarder under a slab is the difference between a slab that stays dry for fifty years and a slab that wets the carpet and grows mould three years in.

The *concrete slab* itself, typically 4 inches thick at the centre with thickened edge footings (perimeter footings) 12 to 24 inches deep and 12 to 18 inches wide, integrated with the slab in a single pour. The slab is reinforced — most commonly with welded wire fabric (a 6 × 6 W2.9 × W2.9 mesh is very common) or with light deformed bar reinforcement (#3 bars at 18-inch centres each way), or with synthetic fibre reinforcement, or in expansive-soil regions with post-tensioned cables. The slab edges and any deeper drop-thickenings are reinforced with continuous longitudinal bars top and bottom.

Concrete strength is typically 3,000 to 4,000 psi (21 to 28 MPa). Specified slump is 4 to 5 inches. Air content is 3 to 5% in temperate climates, 5 to 7% where freeze-thaw exposure exists.

There are several major variants of the slab-on-grade.

**The conventional rebar-reinforced slab.** Used in most non-expansive-soil regions. The slab is poured monolithically (slab and edge footings in one pour) or in two pours (footings first, then slab). Reinforcement is rebar or wire mesh.

**The post-tensioned slab.** Developed for expansive-soil regions (Texas, parts of California, parts of Arizona). The slab is reinforced with high-strength steel cables that are tensioned after the concrete has cured, putting the slab into compression. A post-tensioned slab can flex more without cracking under the differential heave and shrinkage of expansive clay than a conventional rebar slab can. Post-tensioned slabs are now the dominant residential foundation in DFW new construction. They are also the most commonly mis-built slab in the country — the cables must be tensioned in a specific sequence at a specific time, and contractors who treat them as glorified rebar produce slabs that crack badly.

**The waffle slab.** A variant where the slab has additional internal reinforcement ribs creating a pattern of concrete "waffles" of greater depth. Used where soil conditions are particularly poor and a stiffer slab is needed. Common in Houston and on parts of the Texas Gulf Coast.

**The thickened-edge slab.** The simplest variant, used for sheds, garages, and outbuildings, where the footing perimeter is thickened to perhaps 12 inches but there is no continuous below-grade foundation wall.

The IRC and the IBC permit slab-on-grade construction in any climate, with the requirement that the slab be protected from frost. In northern climates, the perimeter of the slab is protected by extending the edge footing below the frost line — a 60-inch-deep edge footing in northern Minnesota, for example — which is expensive. The alternative, the *frost-protected shallow foundation* per IRC R403.3 and ASCE 32, achieves the same effect with horizontal and vertical insulation rather than excavation, and is increasingly used in cold-climate slab construction (we will return to FPSF in detail in Part 12).

In expansive-soil regions, the slab must additionally be designed for the differential heave and shrinkage of the underlying clay. The post-tensioned slab is the most common solution; a properly designed post-tensioned slab can tolerate up to 1.5 inches of differential vertical movement without significant distress. Texas-style post-tensioned slabs typically use 1/2-inch diameter, 270-ksi unbonded monostrand cables at 4 to 5 foot spacing each way, tensioned to 33,000 pounds per cable about 3 to 5 days after the concrete pour.

In coastal hurricane regions, slabs must be tied down to the wall above with continuous anchor bolts (typically 1/2-inch or 5/8-inch anchor bolts at 6-foot maximum spacing, with two within 12 inches of each end of each plate), so that the wind cannot lift the wall off the slab. This tie-down requirement is one reason that slab construction is harder to retrofit than it appears — once the walls are framed, you cannot easily go back and add anchor bolts without cutting up the slab.

In seismic regions (most of the western US and all of the Kathmandu Valley), slabs must additionally be designed to transfer lateral shear from the building above through the slab edge into the soil. This typically means continuous reinforcement around the slab perimeter and adequate connection between the wood (or RCC) walls and the slab edge.

In the Kathmandu Valley, slab-on-grade is comparatively rare for residential construction. Most Valley houses are reinforced concrete frame with isolated column footings, a void underfloor space (sometimes used as a basement or storage), and a structural slab at the plinth level rather than a slab on natural grade. The reasons are practical: the soft Kalimati and Patan formation soils give isolated footings a reliable bearing path, the underground water tank is conventionally integrated with the foundation system, and the Newari and post-Newari construction tradition strongly favours an elevated plinth above the monsoon-saturated ground. We will see more of this in Part 19.

## Part 9. Crawl space foundations

A *crawl space* foundation lifts the building floor a foot or two above the natural grade, with a continuous foundation wall — concrete, concrete masonry unit (CMU), or in some traditions wood — running around the perimeter, and the inside of the perimeter exposed earth (or, increasingly, a concrete rat slab and a sealed encapsulation system). The space between the floor structure and the ground is the *crawl space* — it is, as the name suggests, only tall enough to crawl through.

Crawl space foundations were the dominant residential foundation in much of the United States from roughly 1900 until the 1960s, and remain very common in the South-East, in Appalachia, and in the older neighbourhoods of every Southern city. They are also common in Texas (the older "pier and beam" houses are a crawl space variant) and in much of the rural and suburban Mid-Atlantic.

The structural advantages of a crawl space are several. The building floor is elevated above the moist surface soil, which keeps the floor framing dry. Plumbing and HVAC ductwork run in the crawl space, accessible for maintenance. The continuous foundation wall provides excellent perimeter rigidity and lateral resistance. The crawl space accommodates seasonal soil movement better than a slab-on-grade, which is why crawl spaces are still preferred in expansive-soil regions for high-end construction. The wood floor structure is more comfortable underfoot than concrete and easier to retrofit (you can pull up a board, run a new pipe, and replace the board, rather than chipping out and re-pouring concrete).

The structural disadvantages are real too. The crawl space is dark, damp, and a habitat for moulds, insects, rodents, and reptiles. The wood floor structure can sag or bounce if undersized. The ductwork in the crawl space loses energy to the unconditioned space. The plumbing in the crawl space can freeze in cold climates. And the smell — anyone who has crawled through a poorly ventilated, moist Southern crawl space knows what we mean — can permeate the building above.

Modern best practice is the **encapsulated crawl space**: a sealed, conditioned crawl space with the perimeter walls insulated, the floor covered with a sealed vapour barrier (often a 12 to 20 mil reinforced poly), the perimeter sealed, and the space dehumidified or actively conditioned with a small return-air supply from the house HVAC. An encapsulated crawl space functions essentially as a low basement, with all the moisture and pest issues of a traditional crawl space eliminated. It costs more — perhaps $3,000 to $8,000 more on a typical 2,000-square-foot house — but it pays back in floor warmth, energy efficiency, and the absence of the unique smell of an unventilated crawl space.

Crawl space wall heights are governed by the IRC: the minimum interior clearance is 18 inches from the underside of floor framing to grade in unventilated crawl spaces, and 12 inches from the floor framing to grade for parts of the crawl space directly under the building (R408.1, R408.4 variants depending on edition). Most contractors build to a 24-inch (or higher) clearance for ease of crawl access. The wall itself is typically 8 inches CMU or 8 inches poured concrete, with footings at the base extending below frost line.

Foundation vents are required by the older versions of the IRC for unconditioned crawl spaces, at 1 square foot of vent area per 150 square feet of crawl space floor. This is the source of the familiar small rectangular vents low on the perimeter walls of older houses. The 2024 IRC has shifted toward closed (unvented) crawl spaces with a sealed vapour barrier and a small mechanical exhaust as the preferred construction, especially in the South.

In the Kathmandu Valley, the equivalent of a crawl space is the *plinth space* — the void between the natural ground and the structural floor of the ground-floor slab, typically 600 to 900 millimetres tall, retained by perimeter plinth walls of brick or concrete and topped with the structural ground-floor slab. The plinth space is usually inaccessible (no crawl access), houses the sub-grade plumbing, and is often filled with compacted soil. In two-storey buildings with a tea-shop or commercial use on the ground floor, the plinth wall doubles as the perimeter foundation wall, and the plinth space may be eliminated or reduced to a simple PCC layer beneath the slab.

## Part 10. Full basements

A *full basement* foundation extends a full storey (typically 7 to 9 feet, 2.1 to 2.7 metres of clear ceiling height) below grade, with a finished floor and waterproofed perimeter walls. The basement adds a habitable storey to the building at relatively low marginal cost — the perimeter walls and floor would have been built anyway as part of the foundation, so the additional cost of a basement is mostly waterproofing, finishing, an egress window, and the labour of going deeper.

Full basements are the dominant foundation in the upper Midwest and the North-East of the United States, where the deep frost line means the perimeter foundation is going to be 4 or 5 or 6 feet deep anyway, and you might as well excavate a few more feet and have a basement to show for it. From Cleveland to Minneapolis to Boston to Buffalo, the typical residential foundation is a full basement, 8 feet of poured concrete or CMU foundation walls, a 4-inch slab floor with a vapour barrier, perimeter drainage, and waterproofing of the exterior wall faces.

Full basements are nearly absent in the South-East and South-West US (cost-prohibitive given shallow frost and lack of cultural tradition), nearly absent in the Pacific Coast states (seismic and groundwater concerns), and rare in coastal Florida (high water table). They are virtually unknown in the Kathmandu Valley as a Western-style basement (the local equivalent is the partly-elevated plinth with a non-habitable underfloor void, plus the underground concrete water tank which functions as a quasi-basement utility space).

The structural design of a basement wall is dominated by lateral earth pressure. The retained soil on the outside of the wall pushes inward, and the wall must resist this push without cracking, leaking, or collapsing inward. The IRC R404 has prescriptive tables for foundation walls of different heights, soil types, and reinforcement; engineered designs use the lateral earth pressure equations from soil mechanics with active, at-rest, or passive coefficients depending on the wall's flexibility. In a typical 8-foot residential basement wall on Class 2 silty sand backfill, the bottom of the wall sees a lateral pressure of roughly 30 to 40 pounds per square foot per foot of depth, peaking at 240 to 320 psf at the bottom.

Reinforcement in a basement wall is usually vertical bars at 12 to 24 inch centres, with a few horizontal bars (typically two #4 or #5 bars at the top and bottom, plus intermediate bars where needed). Cover is typically 1.5 to 2 inches on the dry interior face and 2 to 3 inches on the wet exterior face.

The exterior face of the basement wall must be either *dampproofed* or *waterproofed*, depending on the expected groundwater regime. Dampproofing is cheaper and simpler — a bituminous coating brushed or sprayed onto the wall — and is required for foundations in soils that are typically dry. Waterproofing is more elaborate — typically a self-adhesive rubberised membrane, or a fluid-applied elastomeric coating — and is required for foundations expected to be subject to hydrostatic pressure. The IRC R406 specifies which is required where.

A perimeter footing drain (a 4-inch perforated drainpipe in a gravel envelope) at the base of the basement wall, day-lighted to a sump or to grade, is essentially universal. Without a footing drain, hydrostatic pressure builds up against the wall and water will eventually find its way through any imperfection in the waterproofing.

The basement floor slab is typically 4 inches of concrete over a 4-inch crushed-stone capillary break and a 6-mil polyethylene vapour barrier. The slab is sometimes reinforced with welded wire fabric, sometimes plain (with control joints sawcut at 10 to 12 foot spacing), sometimes with synthetic fibre reinforcement.

A radon mitigation system is required by the 2024 IRC in most of the upper Midwest and North-East. This consists of a 3-inch pipe stubbed up through the slab from a layer of crushed stone beneath, and either passively vented through the roof or actively exhausted by an inline fan. In areas with mapped high radon (parts of Pennsylvania, the upper Midwest, the Reading Prong, and many other locations), the active system is required. In Kathmandu Valley residential construction, radon is not a code-mandated concern, although recent indoor-air-quality studies have found measurable radon levels in some rocky-soil neighbourhoods of Kirtipur and Bhaisepati — it is a comer issue that may eventually filter into building practice.

Egress from a basement is required by the IRC: every habitable space below grade must have an egress window (with minimum dimensions and a window well at least 36 inches deep by 36 inches wide, accessible by a ladder if more than 44 inches deep). This is non-negotiable and routinely missed in DIY basement finishing — the code requires, and life safety demands, that you can get out of a burning basement without going up the stairs.

## Part 11. Pier and beam foundations

A *pier and beam* foundation (also called *post and beam*, *post and pier*, or *raised foundation*) is a discrete-pier system. Instead of a continuous perimeter wall, the building is supported by a grid of individual concrete (or, in older work, brick or stone) piers at regular intervals — typically 6 to 8 feet on centre — with wood or steel beams spanning between piers and supporting the floor framing above. A concrete-block perimeter "skirt wall" or wood lattice is often added to enclose the underfloor space cosmetically, but the structure is the piers and the beams, not the skirt.

Pier and beam was the dominant residential foundation in much of the US South and South-East from roughly 1880 to 1950, and remained common in Texas, Oklahoma, Louisiana, and Mississippi well into the 1970s. Roughly 40% of older Dallas–Fort Worth area homes still stand on pier and beam foundations, especially homes built between the early 1900s and the 1960s. The reason was practical: pier and beam handles expansive clay better than slab-on-grade, because the discrete piers can move independently as the soil heaves and shrinks, and the wood floor structure can flex slightly without cracking. A pier and beam house in DFW will move a quarter-inch up and down with the seasons; the residents may notice nothing, while the next-door slab house cracks open at the back wall.

The piers themselves are typically 12 to 16 inches square (or 16 to 20 inches diameter for round piers), reinforced with four #4 or #5 vertical bars, with a 3- to 4-foot deep base footing. The base of each pier sits below the local frost line. In DFW, frost is essentially not a concern (Dallas frost depth is 6 inches per the local IRC amendment), but pier depth is governed instead by getting below the seasonal moisture-active layer of the expansive clay — typically 4 to 6 feet, sometimes deeper.

The beams resting on the piers are typically 4×8 or 6×8 lumber (older houses), or pressure-treated 4×6 or 4×8 (newer construction), or steel I-beams (modern engineered designs). The beams support floor joists at 16 inches on centre, which support a sub-floor of plywood or oriented strand board, which supports the finished floor above.

The advantages of pier and beam:

- **Movement tolerance.** The discrete piers move with the soil; the wood structure flexes; nothing cracks.
- **Plumbing access.** All plumbing is in the crawl space and easily repaired. Compare this to a slab leak, which requires jackhammering through concrete to fix.
- **Adjustment.** A pier and beam house can be levelled in place by jacking individual piers and shimming. A slab house cannot be levelled at all without major work.
- **Insulation.** The floor cavity can be insulated, and the underfloor space ventilated, giving better thermal comfort underfoot.
- **Termite resistance.** Wood that is well above grade and well ventilated is far less susceptible to subterranean termite attack than wood close to or in contact with the ground.

The disadvantages:

- **Higher initial cost.** A pier and beam foundation typically costs 15 to 30% more than a slab-on-grade for the same footprint.
- **Underfloor maintenance.** The crawl space can develop moisture, mould, pest, and rodent problems if not properly ventilated and inspected.
- **Wood deterioration.** The structural beams can rot if exposed to chronic moisture; piers can crack and tilt; shimming can come loose; the floor can develop a "bouncy" feel.
- **Floor temperature.** Floors are colder in winter than slab floors (the underside is exposed to outdoor air), unless properly insulated.

The Texas-specific issue is that pier and beam was the standard for decades, and then was largely replaced by slab-on-grade as the cheap default in the 1960s and 1970s. Many older DFW houses sit on pier and beam foundations that are now 80 or 100 years old, with the wood beams decaying, the piers deteriorating, and the floors visibly out of level. There is a thriving foundation-repair industry — companies like HD Foundations, Perma Pier, and dozens of others — specialising in pier and beam repair, typically at $250 to $600 per pier replaced, $5,000 to $25,000 for a full house levelling.

In the Kathmandu Valley, pier-and-beam foundations are essentially unknown for primary residential construction, though they are sometimes used for accessory structures (verandas, lean-to rooms, hill-station summer houses) and for traditional wooden temple structures where individual stone piers (called *padasthana* or *adhar shila*) carry timber columns. The traditional Newari pati structures and small mandir buildings of the historic cores are pier-and-beam, though built of stone and timber rather than concrete and steel.

## Part 12. Frost-protected shallow foundations

The frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF) is a clever piece of building physics that has been written into US code since 1995 (CABO, the predecessor to the IRC) and is now governed by IRC R403.3 and the more rigorous ASCE 32 standard. The fundamental idea is this: in cold climates, the conventional approach to frost protection is to dig the perimeter footing below the frost line — 42 inches in southern Minnesota, 60 inches in northern Minnesota, 75 inches in North Dakota — at considerable expense in excavation, concrete, and labour. The FPSF replaces deep excavation with insulation. By insulating the perimeter of the foundation, with vertical insulation against the foundation wall and horizontal insulation extending outward from the foundation, you trap the heat that the heated building leaks into the ground, and that trapped heat keeps the soil under and around the foundation from freezing. The footing can then be as shallow as 12 to 16 inches even in northern Minnesota.

The savings are real. In Minnesota, going from a 60-inch conventional footing to a 16-inch FPSF can cut excavation and concrete costs by 40% or more on a typical small residential project.

The technique requires:

1. The building must be heated to a monthly mean temperature of at least 64°F (18°C) for the FPSF to work. Unheated structures cannot use FPSF directly — they require a different design (essentially a ground-insulation mat extending beyond the building perimeter, designed to ASCE 32 rather than the simpler IRC R403.3).

2. The vertical insulation must run continuously from the top of the foundation wall to below the footing depth, with R-values calibrated to the local Air Freezing Index (AFI). The AFI is essentially a measure of how cold the local winter is, expressed in degree-Fahrenheit-days below freezing per year.

3. Horizontal insulation extends outward from the foundation, typically 12 to 40 inches at corners (where two-dimensional cold flow makes more demand on the insulation), with R-values again set by the AFI.

4. The insulation must be a closed-cell rigid foam — typically extruded polystyrene (XPS) or expanded polystyrene (EPS) — that can hold its R-value when buried. Manufacturer R-values are de-rated by code for buried applications, because the insulation absorbs some moisture in service: XPS, for example, is de-rated from R-5 per inch (manufacturer's listed value) to R-4.5 per inch when buried, and EPS more aggressively.

A typical northern-Minnesota FPSF for a heated home, per IRC R403.3 and Table R403.3(1) at an AFI of 3,500 BF-days, is:

- Footing: 16 inches below grade
- Vertical insulation: R-7.8 (about 2 inches of XPS)
- Horizontal insulation: R-6.5 along walls, R-8.6 at corners
- Horizontal extension: 12 inches along walls, 40 inches at corners

The FPSF is also widely used in Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Russia — Scandinavia essentially invented the technique and the US code adopted it from theirs. It is also the default for slab-on-grade construction in cold climates anywhere a true frost-line footing would be prohibitive.

In Kathmandu Valley, FPSF is not relevant — frost is essentially absent. The technique is mentioned here for completeness and because the underlying physics (trapping building heat to keep adjacent soil unfrozen) is quietly elegant.

## Part 13. Raft (mat) foundations

Where the building footprint is large relative to the soil's bearing capacity, the most rational foundation is a **raft** (or **mat**) — a single thick reinforced concrete slab that covers the entire building footprint and distributes the building's load over the entire footprint area. Rafts are common for:

- Multi-storey buildings on weak soils, where individual column footings would be so large they overlap
- Heavy buildings on compressible soils where uniform settlement (a raft) is less damaging than differential settlement (isolated footings)
- Buildings with basements where the basement floor slab would be poured anyway
- Buildings on rocky outcrops or other irregular bearing strata where a raft "averages" the variable bearing
- Storage tanks, silos, transformer stations, and other point-loaded heavy structures
- Buildings on liquefiable soils where the raft serves as a rigid, integrated unit that resists differential settlement during liquefaction

The raft thickness depends on the column loads and spacing. For a typical residential or low-rise commercial raft, thicknesses of 300 to 600 millimetres (12 to 24 inches) are common. Heavy industrial rafts can be 1 to 3 metres thick. Reinforcement is a two-way mat top and bottom with additional reinforcement at column locations to handle punching shear.

In the Kathmandu Valley, raft foundations are universally specified for any building on the soft Kalimati or Patan formation soils with bearing capacity below about 80 kPa. For a typical four-storey residential building in central Kathmandu — say, the kind of mixed-use building that fills the streets of Naya Bazaar, Sanepa, or Bouddha — a 450-millimetre thick raft over the full footprint, reinforced with 16mm Fe500D bars at 150mm centres each way top and bottom, is a typical specification. The raft simultaneously serves as the floor of the basement (if any), the bearing element for all the columns, and the foundation for the integrated underground water tank that is universal in Valley practice.

The geotechnical engineer in raft design is concerned mostly with **settlement** rather than bearing failure. A raft on soft clay is not at risk of bearing failure — the bearing pressure is low because the area is so large — but it can settle, and settle differentially. Modern raft analysis uses three-dimensional finite-element soil-structure interaction modelling for any project of significance.

A particular variant, the **floating raft**, is used in extremely soft soil. The principle is Archimedes': you excavate a volume of soil equal to the weight of the building, and the raft "floats" on the soil with no net stress increase. Floating rafts are common in Mexico City, where the soft lakebed soils have settled the entire city by metres over the centuries.

## Part 14. Driven piles

When the soils near the surface cannot carry the building, the foundation must reach down to a competent bearing stratum. This is the realm of *deep foundations*, and the oldest and most basic deep foundation is the **driven pile**.

A driven pile is a long, slender structural element — wood, steel, or precast concrete — driven into the ground by a hammer. The pile transfers load to the soil through some combination of *end bearing* (the load is transferred through the pile tip to the bearing stratum at the bottom) and *skin friction* (the load is transferred through friction along the sides of the pile to the surrounding soil). Most piles work in combination, with the proportion of end bearing versus friction depending on the pile type and soil profile.

**Timber piles** — long round logs of southern yellow pine, Douglas fir, or, historically, oak — were the original deep foundation. New York City and Boston are full of buildings supported on timber piles driven into the harbour mud in the 19th and early 20th century; some of those buildings are still standing, with the piles now over a century old. Timber piles are still used in marine and shore-protection work, but have been largely supplanted in building work by concrete and steel, which are more durable and predictable.

**Steel H-piles** are wide-flange or H-section steel sections, driven into the ground. They are excellent for end-bearing on rock (the H-section can be sharpened to bite into rock surfaces), and their slim profile lets them penetrate into fairly dense soils. They are common in heavy industrial, bridge, and port construction.

**Pipe piles** are open-ended or closed-ended steel pipes, often filled with concrete after driving. Pipe piles can be driven, vibrated, or augered into place. They are versatile and can be made to almost any depth by welding additional sections together.

**Precast concrete piles** are prestressed concrete sections, typically 12-inch to 24-inch square or octagonal, with strands running their full length. They are pre-cast in a yard, trucked to the site, and driven by a hammer. They are robust, economical for repetitive driving, and dominant in Florida coastal and Gulf-coast pile work.

**Cast-in-place concrete piles** (such as the Franki or pressure-driven pile) are driven as a steel casing, then filled with concrete. The casing is sometimes withdrawn during pouring, leaving an in-situ concrete column.

The driving itself is done by a *pile-driving rig* — a steel-frame derrick supporting either a drop hammer (a heavy weight repeatedly raised and dropped on the pile head) or a more modern diesel hammer or hydraulic hammer (which delivers rapid, controlled blows). Each blow drives the pile a small distance further. The number of blows required to drive the pile a unit distance — the *driving resistance* — is recorded and used to confirm the pile's load capacity.

Pile design is governed by ACI 318-25 Chapter 17 (with substantial updates in the 2025 edition for deep foundations), the IBC, and various proprietary pile-installation standards (the Pile Driving Contractors Association, the Florida pile-driving code, etc.). Pile capacity is determined by:

1. *Static analysis* — calculating end bearing and skin friction from soil parameters
2. *Dynamic analysis* — using the driving record and a wave equation analysis (CAPWAP, GRLWEAP, etc.) to back-calculate capacity
3. *Static load testing* — physically loading the pile with a known load and measuring deflection, the gold standard

In coastal Florida, every pile in a residential construction must be driven to a specified penetration, with the driving record reviewed by an engineer of record. Florida pile depths are typically 25 to 50 feet, sometimes more, with the pile tip embedded in dense sand or limestone. The piles are designed to remain stable after the predicted scour from a 100-year hurricane event, which means the design grade is below the natural grade, sometimes by several feet, and the pile must achieve adequate capacity below that design grade.

In the Kathmandu Valley, driven piles are uncommon for residential work — the soft clays and silts are not friendly to driving, and the noise/vibration is unacceptable in dense urban settings. Where deep foundations are needed in the Valley, the dominant technique is the **bored pile**.

## Part 15. Bored piles (drilled shafts)

A **bored pile** (also called a **drilled shaft**, **drilled pier**, or, in much of South Asia, a **cast-in-situ pile**) is a deep concrete column poured in a hole drilled into the ground. The hole is excavated by an auger or a bucket on a drilling rig, the steel reinforcement cage is lowered in, and concrete is poured to fill the hole. Where the soil is unstable (sandy, water-bearing), the hole is supported during drilling either by a temporary steel casing or by a slurry (typically bentonite or polymer drilling fluid).

Bored piles range from 400 millimetres to 3 metres in diameter, and from 5 metres to over 50 metres deep, depending on the application. They are the dominant deep foundation for high-rise construction worldwide, for bridge piers, and for any major building on poor surface soils.

For Kathmandu Valley residential and small-commercial construction, bored piles of 450 to 600 millimetre diameter, drilled to depths of 8 to 20 metres, are the standard solution where the surface soils cannot support shallow foundations. A typical four-storey commercial building on the soft soils of Maharajgunj or central Lalitpur might use 500-millimetre piles 12 metres deep, with three to four piles per column, capped by a reinforced concrete pile cap.

The reinforcement of a bored pile is a cylindrical cage of longitudinal bars (typically 6 to 12 numbers of 16mm to 25mm Fe500D bars) tied with spiral or helical hoops of 8mm to 10mm bar at 100 to 200 mm pitch. The cage is centred in the borehole with concrete spacers, and the concrete (typically M25 or M30 in Kathmandu, sometimes higher) is placed by a tremie pipe to ensure no segregation or contamination at the bottom.

Pile integrity testing is critical for bored piles, because unlike driven piles you cannot directly observe the pile during installation. Sonic logging tubes are cast into the pile, and after curing a sonic log is run to detect any voids, necking, or other defects. Cross-hole sonic logging is the most reliable method.

The pile cap — the concrete element that ties the pile group together and connects the piles to the column above — is typically a thick reinforced concrete block, designed for punching shear from the column and bending across the pile group. ACI 318-25 introduced significantly improved provisions for pile cap design, particularly for two-way shear and for the size effects in deeper caps.

## Part 16. Helical piers and micropiles

Two relatively modern deep-foundation systems deserve their own treatment: **helical piers** and **micropiles**. Both are widely used in foundation repair and in installations where heavy equipment cannot be brought to site (interior of an existing building, tight urban infill lots, etc.).

A **helical pier** (also called a screw pile, or a Chance/A.B. Chance pile in the US) is a steel shaft with one or more helical plates welded to its leading end. The pier is rotated into the ground by a hydraulic torque motor, and the helical plates pull the pier downward in a controlled screwing motion. The bearing capacity of the pier is directly proportional to the installation torque — a fundamental relationship that lets the installer verify capacity in real time.

Helical piers are commonly used to:

- Underpin existing foundations that have settled or moved (the most common DFW foundation-repair technique)
- Support new structures on soils too poor for shallow foundations but where driving is impractical
- Install from inside existing buildings (where ceiling height and access are limited)
- Provide tension anchorage (helical piers work in tension as well as in compression — useful for tiedowns, retaining walls, and uplift resistance)

A typical residential helical pier is a 2.875-inch or 3.5-inch diameter steel shaft with 8-inch to 14-inch helical plates, installed to torque resistances of 3,000 to 7,000 foot-pounds. Each pier supports 10,000 to 30,000 pounds of load. A typical DFW foundation underpinning might use 8 to 20 helical piers around the perimeter of a settled house.

A **micropile** is a small-diameter (typically 4 to 12 inch) drilled and grouted pile, with a single high-strength steel bar or pipe as reinforcement, installed in two stages: first the pile is drilled, the bar is inserted, and primary grout is pumped to fill the hole; then the upper portion is post-grouted under pressure to densify the surrounding soil and increase skin friction.

Micropiles are common for underpinning historic structures, where access is limited and the existing structure cannot tolerate heavy vibration. They are also used in rocky soils where larger piles cannot be installed without blasting. FEMA's Fact Sheet 3.1 on foundations specifically identifies grouted micropiles and helical piles as standard methods for strengthening existing foundations, particularly in flood-prone coastal areas.

## Part 17. Stone columns and ground improvement

Sometimes the right answer is not to bypass the bad soil with deep foundations, but to *fix the bad soil*. This is the realm of *ground improvement*.

A **stone column** is a vertical column of densely packed crushed stone, installed in soft cohesive soil to improve the soil's bearing capacity and reduce settlement. A vibroflot or a similar equipment penetrates the soft soil; aggregate is fed into the hole; and the column is built up by repeated penetration and withdrawal. The aggregate column reinforces the surrounding soil, drains the soil (allowing consolidation of the surrounding clay), and shares the load with the surrounding soil in a composite ground.

Stone columns are widely used for:

- Improving soft clay sites for shallow-foundation construction
- Pre-loading soft soils to accelerate consolidation
- Mitigating liquefaction in saturated sands (the stone columns drain pore pressures during shaking and prevent liquefaction)
- Reinforcing embankments and retaining structures

In the Kathmandu Valley, stone columns are increasingly being studied as a liquefaction mitigation technique for the soft soils of the central valley. Research has found stone columns of 0.5 metre diameter increase allowable bearing capacity by an average of 98% on isolated footings in Valley soils. The technique is not yet mainstream in Nepali residential practice, but it appears in some larger commercial and infrastructure projects.

Other ground-improvement techniques include:

- **Dynamic compaction** — dropping a heavy weight from a great height to densify loose soils. Used for industrial sites and runway construction. Loud and vibrating; not suitable for urban work.
- **Vibrocompaction** — vibratory probes penetrating loose granular soil to densify it. Common for liquefaction mitigation.
- **Deep soil mixing** — mixing in-situ soil with cement or lime via large augers, creating columns of soil-cement that act as composite reinforcement. Common in Japan, Scandinavia, and some Asian markets.
- **Compaction grouting** — injecting a low-mobility cement grout to densify surrounding soil. Used to fill voids and to remediate sinkhole-prone karst.
- **Permeation grouting** — injecting low-viscosity grout (chemical or microfine cement) into granular soil to bond particles and reduce permeability.
- **Pre-loading with surcharge fill** — placing a temporary fill on the site, allowing the underlying soft soil to consolidate under the surcharge weight, then removing the surcharge before construction. Cheap and effective where time permits (typically 6 months to 2 years of consolidation time required).
- **Wick drains (PVD)** — vertical strip drains installed into soft soil to accelerate the rate of consolidation under a surcharge. Often paired with pre-loading.

Ground improvement is rarely a stand-alone solution; it almost always works in combination with conventional foundations, reducing settlement and improving soil quality enough that ordinary footings or rafts can be used.

## Part 18. Foundations for different building types

So far we have walked through foundation types more or less in the abstract. Now let us look at the specific foundations used for the major residential building types.

### Single-family homes, stick-built

The dominant US single-family construction is *stick-built* — site-framed wood construction, with 2×4 or 2×6 stud walls, dimensional or engineered floor joists, and conventional roof framing. Stick-built houses are typically light (10,000 to 80,000 pounds of structural dead load, depending on size and number of storeys, plus live and snow loads), are well-distributed (the loads come down through the perimeter walls relatively uniformly), and have fairly modest seismic and wind demands compared to heavier construction.

Stick-built single-family houses use, in rough rank order of frequency:

- **Slab-on-grade** in the Sun Belt (everywhere from southern California to the Mid-Atlantic, with Texas and Florida especially dominant)
- **Full basement** in the upper Midwest and North-East (Pennsylvania, Ohio, Michigan, Wisconsin, Minnesota, New York, New England)
- **Crawl space** in the South-East and Appalachia (the Carolinas, Tennessee, Kentucky, Virginia, West Virginia)
- **Pier and beam** in older construction in Texas and the Gulf Coast
- **Frost-protected shallow foundation** as a slab variant in cold climates
- **Pile or column foundations** in coastal flood zones (Florida Keys, Outer Banks, Gulf Coast)
- **Drilled pier or helical pier** on hillsides and in expansive-clay regions where surface soils are unsuitable
- **Stem wall on continuous footing** in many California neighbourhoods, where seismic and the lack of deep frost combine to favour this hybrid

### Single-family homes, manufactured (mobile home) construction

Manufactured homes — what older terminology calls mobile homes, and what HUD now formally calls "manufactured housing" — are built in factories and transported to site as one or more sections (single-wide or double-wide, occasionally triple-wide). They are governed by the federal HUD Code, not the local building code, and their foundations are typically much simpler than site-built homes.

The simplest manufactured-home foundation is a **pier-and-tie-down system**: concrete or steel piers at intervals along the chassis frame of the home, with tie-down anchors to resist wind uplift. The piers are not connected to a continuous foundation; the home essentially rests on a grid of discrete supports.

A more substantial manufactured-home foundation is a **permanent foundation** that meets HUD's "Permanent Foundations Guide for Manufactured Housing", typically a perimeter foundation wall (concrete or CMU) with the chassis bolted down to it. A permanent foundation is required for FHA, VA, and most conventional mortgage financing of a manufactured home.

In tornado-prone regions (the central US, the Gulf Coast), tie-down requirements are critical: the HUD code mandates ground anchors and tie-down straps, with greater requirements in higher-wind zones. Mobile home parks in tornado country sometimes have communal storm shelters, because the homes themselves cannot reasonably be made tornado-proof.

In the Kathmandu Valley, manufactured housing in the US sense is essentially unknown. The nearest equivalent — pre-fabricated steel-frame buildings used for some commercial structures and cabin-style hill-station accommodation — uses ordinary isolated footings or strip footings depending on the structural type.

### Modular construction

A modular home is a site-installed building made from one or more factory-built modules (units that are typically the size of a shipping container or larger). Unlike manufactured homes, modular homes meet the local building code and are essentially indistinguishable, structurally, from site-built construction once installed. They can be placed on any of the standard residential foundations — slab, crawl, basement, or pier-and-beam.

### Duplexes and townhouses

A *duplex* is a two-unit residence; a *townhouse* (or *rowhouse*, or *terrace house*) is a long row of attached single-family-style units. Both have foundations that essentially extend the single-family foundation horizontally. The complications are at the *party walls* (the walls separating adjacent units), which must be both structurally continuous from foundation to roof and acoustically and fire-rated to separate the units.

In townhouses, the typical foundation is a continuous strip footing under the entire row of party walls, with isolated footings under any internal columns. The party-wall foundation must be designed to handle the full live and dead load from both adjacent units, and must be continuous (no joint at the property line, since the roofs and floors above are also continuous).

In Seismic Design Categories C and higher, IRC R403.1.2 requires continuous solid or fully grouted masonry or concrete footings under exterior walls and required interior braced wall panels of townhouses, even where they would not be required for stand-alone single-family homes. This is because the long, thin, multi-unit form of a townhouse is more susceptible to torsional response in an earthquake than a stand-alone house.

In the Kathmandu Valley, the local equivalent of a townhouse is the *attached row house* common in newer planned developments (Bansbari, Budhanilkantha, the eastern Lalitpur expansion). The foundation is typically isolated column footings tied with plinth beams, with the party walls bearing on the plinth beams.

### Accessory dwelling units (ADUs)

An ADU is a small secondary dwelling unit on the same lot as a primary residence. It can be a converted garage, a basement suite, or a stand-alone structure. The foundation for an ADU is determined by its size and its connection to the main residence. A stand-alone ADU is essentially a small single-family residence and uses the same foundation types. An attached ADU often shares foundation elements with the main residence.

ADUs have boomed in California, Oregon, and Washington over the past decade as state law has been amended to encourage them. They are a major retrofit market for foundation contractors.

### Sheds and accessory structures

A garden shed, a backyard storage building, a small workshop — these are accessory structures, not habitable buildings, and their foundation requirements are minimal but not zero.

The IRC explicitly exempts free-standing accessory structures of 600 square feet or less, of light-frame construction, with eave heights of 10 feet or less, from frost-protection requirements (R403.1.4 exception). This means a garden shed can be placed on a frost-shallow foundation regardless of climate. In practice, sheds use one of:

- **A skid foundation** — pressure-treated 4×4 or 4×6 timbers laid directly on a gravel pad, with the shed framed on top. Cheapest. Adequate for sheds up to 8×12 or so. Will move slightly with frost heave but the shed simply rises and falls with the soil.
- **A concrete pier foundation** — 8 to 12 inch diameter, 24 to 48 inch deep concrete piers (Sonotube, Bigfoot, or similar) at corners and along sides. Better for larger or heavier sheds.
- **A concrete slab** — typically 4 inches of concrete over a compacted gravel base, with thickened edges. The standard for garages, larger workshops, and any shed that will house heavy equipment.
- **A concrete block (CMU) perimeter foundation** — 4 or 8-inch CMU on a poured footing, with the shed framed on top. A middle ground between slab and pier.

Some local jurisdictions amend the IRC to specifically permit shallow slab foundations for small accessory structures regardless of frost. Sycamore, Illinois, for example, permits a continuous slab on ground for non-occupied accessory structures up to 900 square feet, single-storey or 20 feet in height, on a 4-inch crushed limestone base, with a minimum 6-bag concrete mix, and welded wire mesh reinforcement.

In Kathmandu Valley, sheds and small accessory structures (a tea-shop kiosk, a small godown, a pump-house, a generator enclosure) typically sit on a simple PCC pad — 75mm of plain cement concrete on a 100mm sand-and-gravel base — with no engineered foundation. This is informal but works because frost is not an issue and the structures are very light.

## Part 19. Regional case studies — the United States

Let us walk through eight regions of the United States, each representing a distinctive foundation problem, and see how the local engineering tradition has solved it.

### 19.1 Coastal Florida — V zones, hurricanes, and pile foundations

Florida's Atlantic and Gulf coasts present perhaps the most challenging residential-foundation problem in North America. The combination of hurricane-force winds (design wind speeds up to 180 mph in the Florida Keys and parts of Miami-Dade), storm surge (up to 20+ feet in major hurricanes), wave action, scour, soft beach sand and limestone bedrock, and a high water table all add up to a foundation problem that ordinary slab-on-grade simply cannot solve.

The solution, codified in the Florida Building Code and FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program rules, is the **elevated pile foundation**. In FEMA V zones (Coastal High Hazard Areas), the building must be supported on an open foundation of piles or columns, with the lowest horizontal structural member at or above the Design Flood Elevation (DFE = Base Flood Elevation + freeboard). The space below the lowest floor must be either free of obstructions or enclosed by *breakaway walls* — walls that fail under defined wind and wave loads without damaging the supporting piles or the elevated building above.

Pile design for V zone construction typically uses 12 to 14-inch precast prestressed concrete piles, driven to depths of 25 to 50 feet, with the design grade set below the natural grade by an amount equal to the predicted scour from a 100-year hurricane. The structure must be elevated to a height where the water from a hurricane storm surge and wind-blown waves can pass safely below the first horizontal beam supporting the living floor, and pile penetration must take into account the anticipated loss of soil above the design grade.

Anchor bolt design at the top of the piles is critical. The connection between the pile and the wood (or concrete) framing above must transfer uplift, shear, and overturning loads — sometimes simultaneously — and the connectors must be galvanised or stainless steel for corrosion resistance. FEMA's Technical Bulletin 8 covers connectors for coastal construction in detail.

A typical Florida coastal home foundation (say, a single-family home in Naples, Sanibel Island, or the Florida Keys) might use:

- 16 piles, 14 inch square prestressed concrete, driven to 35 feet
- Pile spacing 8 to 10 feet on centre, in a regular grid
- Pile cap (or "pile cap beam") of 16×24 inch reinforced concrete tying the piles together at the lowest floor level
- Lowest horizontal structural member set 12 to 18 feet above grade (depending on BFE)
- Open space below, with breakaway lattice or louvered enclosures permitted for screen porches and limited storage
- Wood frame house above, with continuous load path tiedowns from the roof rafters through the wall framing to the pile cap

A particularly poignant case study is Hurricane Ian (September 2022), which devastated parts of Florida's Gulf Coast. Houses built to the post-2002 V-zone elevation requirements largely survived; older houses on slab foundations or low-pile foundations were swept off their foundations or filled with water. The contrast was dramatic enough that the Florida insurance market substantially repriced flood and wind risk in 2023 and 2024, and many older coastal homes are now uninsurable without elevation work.

### 19.2 Dallas–Fort Worth — expansive clay and foundation repair

DFW sits on the **Blackland Prairie**, a band of dark, heavy clay soils running from the Red River south through Dallas, Waco, Austin, and into central Texas. The Blackland Prairie clay is among the most expansive soils in the world: clay with a plasticity index over 50 is highly plastic, and these high-plasticity clay soils can gain or lose up to 75% of their original volume between wet and dry seasons. The Texas climate, with its long dry summers and wet winters, drives the soil through repeated swell-shrink cycles, and the cumulative effect on residential foundations is severe.

The historical solution was *pier and beam* construction, which we covered in Part 11. The discrete piers move up and down with the soil; the wood floor flexes; nothing cracks. Many Dallas homeowners are familiar with the expansive clay soil, which swells when wet and shrinks when dry, creating constant movement that is tough on houses and makes the foundation one of the most critical parts of the home.

Modern DFW residential construction overwhelmingly uses **post-tensioned slab-on-grade**. The post-tensioned slab is a single concrete slab reinforced with high-strength steel cables tensioned after the concrete cures. The compression in the slab from the post-tensioning lets the slab tolerate significant differential heave and shrinkage of the underlying soil without cracking. A properly designed and built post-tensioned slab on Blackland Prairie clay can survive the seasonal soil movements indefinitely.

The trouble is that "properly designed and built" is a high bar. The slab design must:

- Account for the soil's plasticity index, expected moisture range, and predicted vertical movement (typically 1 to 3 inches differential)
- Specify a minimum slab thickness, typically 6 to 8 inches, with thickened edges (typically 12 to 24 inches) under all perimeter and load-bearing walls
- Specify cable spacing, profile, and tensioning sequence
- Require uniform site moisture conditioning before pouring
- Require post-pouring moisture management (the foundation perimeter must be watered in the dry season to maintain soil moisture, and drained in the wet season to prevent oversaturation)

The construction must:

- Prepare the subgrade properly (no organics, no soft spots, properly compacted)
- Place the cables at the correct elevations and profiles
- Tension the cables in the correct sequence at the correct concrete age (typically 3 to 5 days)
- Cut the tendon ends and grout the anchorages

Failures are common. The thriving DFW foundation-repair industry — companies like HD Foundations, Dallas Fort Worth Foundation Repair, Atlas Foundation Inc., G.L. Hunt, Perma Pier, and dozens of others — exists primarily because so many slabs fail under expansive-clay loading. Typical repair techniques include:

- **Pressed concrete piers** (also called "pressed pilings"): cylindrical concrete sections hydraulically pressed into the ground beneath the failed slab to lift and stabilise it. In the Dallas–Fort Worth area, pressed concrete piers are one of the most affordable and reliable methods used to stabilise foundation movement; steel piers are needed if depths to bedrock exceed 20 feet.
- **Steel piers**: similar to pressed concrete but driven to greater depths
- **Helical piers**: as described in Part 16
- **Slabjacking (mudjacking)**: pumping a grout mixture under the slab to lift and level it
- **Drainage correction**: French drains, surface drains, root barriers — often the single most cost-effective repair, because uncontrolled water around the foundation perimeter is the actual cause of much of the soil movement

A typical DFW foundation repair might cost $5,000 to $25,000, with extreme cases over $50,000.

The lesson for Kathmandu Valley engineers reading this: most parts of the southern Kathmandu Valley (Lubhu, Harisiddhi, Godawari) sit on similar high-plasticity clays, and the same kind of seasonal movement is observed there, although the practice has not yet developed a formal post-tensioned-slab tradition. As Valley residential construction densifies and slab-on-grade becomes more common, the same kinds of foundation problems will emerge unless the practice catches up.

### 19.3 Northern Minnesota — deep frost and the basement default

Northern Minnesota has the deepest frost in the contiguous United States. The Twin Cities area uses a 42-inch frost depth for code compliance; head north to Duluth or International Falls, and you are looking at 60 to 80 inches of frost penetration. State code (Minnesota Rule 1303.1600) specifies 60 inches in Zone I (northern Minnesota, generally north of St. Cloud) and 42 inches in Zone II (southern Minnesota).

The implication is straightforward: any conventional perimeter foundation in northern Minnesota must extend 60 inches below grade. At that depth, you might as well dig a few more feet and have a basement to show for it. Hence the upper-Midwest residential default of full poured-concrete or CMU basements.

A typical Twin Cities or Duluth single-family home foundation:

- 8 or 10-inch poured concrete basement walls, 8 to 9 feet tall
- 16 or 20-inch wide × 8-inch thick perimeter footings, 60 inches below grade
- 4-inch concrete slab over 4 inches of crushed stone and 6-mil polyethylene
- Perimeter drain tile (4-inch perforated pipe in gravel envelope, drained to a sump pit with electrical sump pump)
- Damp-proofing or waterproofing on the exterior wall faces, depending on soil conditions
- Insulation: per Minnesota energy code, R-15 continuous on the foundation walls or R-19 cavity (in the basement-finishing context)
- Egress window in any habitable basement space
- Radon mitigation passive stub-up (active in mapped high-radon areas)

The walls are frequently reinforced with vertical bars at 12 to 24 inch centres, especially in deep walls, walls supporting walkout-basement openings, or walls in soils with poor drainage.

The frost-protected shallow foundation per IRC R403.3 is increasingly used in northern Minnesota for slab-on-grade construction, particularly for:

- Garages and outbuildings
- Single-storey homes without basements
- Granny flats, ADUs, and similar small structures

The savings are substantial — a 16-inch FPSF instead of a 60-inch conventional footing cuts excavation, concrete, and labour by 30 to 50%.

### 19.4 Coastal Alaska — extreme frost, permafrost, and seismic

Anchorage frost design is dramatic. Anchorage requires 120 inches (10 feet) of pile depth for cast-in-place concrete piers, which may be reduced to 60 inches in non-frost-susceptible soils per the 2018 local amendments to the IRC. Fairbanks requires 42 inches; Juneau requires 32 inches. Anchorage and Fairbanks construction sometimes involves perpetually frozen ground (permafrost), where the design philosophy shifts from "dig below frost" to "preserve the frozen state of the ground". A heated building over permafrost will thaw the soil beneath it, with catastrophic results, unless the building is designed to maintain ground freezing.

Permafrost-foundation strategies include:

- **Thermosyphons**: passive refrigeration tubes that pull heat out of the ground in winter and prevent thaw in summer
- **Insulated piles**: piles wrapped in insulation to prevent heat conduction from the building into the ground
- **Ventilated underfloor space**: a void between the building and the soil with continuous airflow to keep the ground frozen
- **Adfreeze design**: piles designed for *adfreeze* — the bond between the pile and the surrounding frozen soil — for vertical capacity, with allowance for thaw of the active layer at the surface

Alaska is also seismically active — the 1964 Great Alaska Earthquake (M9.2) was the second-largest earthquake ever recorded, and the 2018 Anchorage earthquake (M7.1) caused significant infrastructure damage. Foundation design in Alaska therefore must address both extreme cold and seismic shaking, sometimes simultaneously.

### 19.5 California — seismic, expansive clay, and hillsides

California foundation design is dominated by seismic considerations. The state has the highest seismic-design demands in the country, particularly in the coastal regions from Ventura through San Francisco to the Oregon border.

A typical California single-family-home foundation:

- Stem wall on continuous footing, 12 to 18 inches wide, with a 6 to 8 inch wide stem wall extending up to 18 to 24 inches above grade
- Continuous reinforcement: at least 2 #4 bars top and bottom of footing, 1 #4 bar in stem wall mid-height, with vertical dowels
- Anchor bolts: 5/8-inch diameter at 4 to 6 foot spacing, with 7-inch minimum embedment
- Crawl-space cripple walls (if any) braced with plywood shear panels and tied down at corners
- Slab-on-grade in newer construction, with edge thickening and integral footings

In Seismic Design Category D (most of coastal California), continuous footings are required under all exterior walls and braced wall panels per IRC R403.1.2. Hold-downs at shear-wall ends are required to anchor the wall framing to the concrete foundation, and cannot be skipped or substituted with light-duty connectors.

California hillsides add a third complication: many homes are built on steep slopes, where the foundation must resist not just gravity but the lateral push of the slope itself. Hillside foundations often combine deep drilled piers (10 to 30 feet, into bedrock) with grade-beam connections to engage the slope mass. Proper hillside foundation design is one of the most engineering-intensive parts of California residential construction.

The **soft-storey retrofit** — addressing the seismic vulnerability of older garage-under-bedroom houses where the ground floor is mostly garage door — is now mandatory in San Francisco, Berkeley, Oakland, and several other Bay Area cities. The retrofit typically involves new shear walls, hold-downs, and continuous load paths between the foundation and the walls above.

### 19.6 Pacific Northwest — basements, organic soils, and the Cascadia subduction zone

Western Oregon and Washington share many characteristics with California (seismic, soft soils, hillsides), but with a substantially different climate and a different soil profile. The Cascadia Subduction Zone offshore is capable of M9+ earthquakes — comparable to the 2011 Tohoku earthquake — and the 2024 update of the seismic hazard maps significantly raised expected ground motions in coastal Oregon and Washington.

Foundation practice in Seattle, Portland, and the surrounding suburbs is broadly similar to California's: stem walls on continuous footings, with continuous reinforcement, anchor bolts, and tiedowns. The wetter climate means more attention to perimeter drainage and damp-proofing, and the prevalence of heavy clay soils (the Boring Lava soils of Portland, the Vashon till of Seattle) can require deeper footings or engineered fill.

Liquefaction is a major concern in the lowland portions of both cities. The Duwamish River valley in Seattle, the Willamette and Columbia river floodplains in Portland, and many of the harbour-fill areas of both cities are mapped as liquefiable. Major buildings in these areas typically require deep foundations (drilled shafts or driven piles) or ground improvement (stone columns, soil mixing).

### 19.7 New Mexico, Arizona, and Las Vegas — collapsible soils and arid climate

The high deserts of the south-western US present a different soil problem: *collapsible soils*. Many of the alluvial fan deposits and loess deposits of New Mexico, Arizona, and Nevada are metastable — they appear stable when dry, but lose substantial volume when wetted. A foundation built on dry collapsible soil that subsequently gets wet (from a leaking sewer, an irrigation system, or a major rainfall event) can settle dramatically, sometimes 5 to 15 centimetres, in days.

The Albuquerque West Mesa, parts of Phoenix, much of suburban Las Vegas, and substantial portions of the Rio Grande valley sit on collapsible alluvial soils. Foundation practice in these areas involves:

- Removal of the upper several feet of collapsible soil and replacement with engineered fill
- Pre-wetting the site before construction to trigger collapse, then re-compacting
- Drilled piers extending below the collapsible zone
- Strict drainage and irrigation controls around the building perimeter

Frost is essentially not a concern in this region (Albuquerque frost depth is 18 inches; Las Vegas is 23 inches; Phoenix is effectively zero), so foundation depths are governed almost entirely by the collapsible-soil issue and by the IRC 12-inch minimum.

### 19.8 Hawaii — volcanic soils, lava flows, and the absence of frost

Hawaiian foundation engineering is its own world. The soils are volcanic — basaltic flows, ash, weathered tuff — and vary enormously over short distances. Some sites bear directly on solid basalt and require minimal foundation work; others sit on loose volcanic ash with very low bearing capacity. The big island of Hawai'i has active lava flows that have buried entire neighbourhoods (the 2018 Lower Puna eruption in particular), creating what is, geologically speaking, a special category of foundation hazard.

Hawaii has effectively no frost (the highest peak frost depth in the state is in the upper elevations of the Big Island and Maui, and even there it does not affect residential construction). The dominant residential foundation is post-and-pier (a Hawaiian variant of pier and beam), with concrete piers on shallow footings supporting timber framing, often with the floor several feet above grade for ventilation in the humid climate.

Termite resistance is a major concern: Hawaiian termites are aggressive, and treated lumber and termite shields are universal.

Hurricane resistance, while not as critical as the Atlantic and Gulf coasts, is real — Hurricane Iniki (1992) and Hurricane Lane (2018) demonstrated that the islands are not immune. Hurricane tiedowns are standard.

## Part 20. Regional case studies — the Kathmandu Valley

The Kathmandu Valley is geologically a single sediment-filled basin, but the foundation engineering varies substantially across the Valley because the underlying soil deposits vary substantially. Let us walk through six neighbourhoods, each representing a distinctive foundation problem.

### 20.1 Central Kathmandu (Bhrikutimandap, Sundhara, Thapathali) — soft Kalimati clay

The geological centre of the Valley is dominated by the **Kalimati Formation**, the lacustrine deposits of the Paleo-Kathmandu Lake. Beneath the upper alluvial deposits, the Kalimati Formation consists of a thick deposit of black silty beds, mainly present in the southern valley, representing an older lacustrine environment. The Kathmandu Clay, also called the black clay, is found near the sub-surface of the valley sediments, and is characterised by low strength and high compressibility.

The bearing capacity of these soils is low. Studies have found that most of the places in the Kathmandu Valley consist of silty clay soil with very low consistency, and the highest and lowest bearing capacity values were 151.2 kN/m² and 61.66 kN/m² respectively in the Lalitpur District. For a building of any significant size, this means either a raft foundation, a pile foundation, or substantial ground improvement.

A typical four-storey commercial-residential building in central Kathmandu (say, in the Maharajgunj or Sundhara area):

- **Bored piles**, 500 mm diameter, 12 to 15 metres deep (penetrating the Kalimati clay to reach denser sand layers below)
- **Pile cap**, 400 to 500 mm thick, reinforced with 16mm and 20mm Fe500D bars
- **Ground beam (plinth beam)** at +0.6 m above grade, tying all pile caps together
- **Underground water tank** integrated with the pile cap level, typically 5 cubic metre capacity for a residential building, larger for commercial
- **RCC frame** above, with M25 concrete and Fe500D rebar per NBC 105:2020 detailing rules

Construction sequence:

1. Site clearing and benchmark survey
2. Boring of pile holes by tripod-percussion or rotary rig
3. Reinforcement cage lowered into each hole, concrete poured by tremie
4. Excavation for pile caps once piles are cured
5. Pile caps and plinth beams cast monolithically with the underground water tank
6. Above-ground frame construction proceeds

### 20.2 Bhaktapur side (Suryabinayak, Madhyapur Thimi) — better soils and isolated footings

The eastern part of the Valley, including most of Bhaktapur and Madhyapur Thimi, sits on a different geological deposit — primarily the **Thimi Formation** and the **Gokarna Formation**, which are fluvio-deltaic sands and silts. The Gokarna and Thimi formations are dominant over the northern and northeastern part of Kathmandu Valley and consist of fluvio-deltaic or fluvio-lacustrine origin sediments, primarily sandy facies. These soils have substantially higher bearing capacity than the central Kalimati clays — often 150 to 250 kPa at modest depths.

For a typical two-storey residential house in Suryabinayak or eastern Madhyapur Thimi:

- **Isolated column footings**, 1.5 m × 1.5 m × 300 mm thick, founded at 1.5 to 1.8 m depth
- **Plinth beam** at +0.6 m, 250 × 300 mm, reinforced
- **Underground water tank**, 4 cubic metre capacity, integrated with plinth structure
- **RCC frame** with M20 concrete and Fe500D rebar per NBC 201

The same house in central Kathmandu would require either bored piles or a raft, at substantially higher cost. The Bhaktapur side's better soil is one of the quiet reasons that newer family-home construction is increasingly clustered in the eastern Valley.

### 20.3 Lalitpur (Patan, Sanepa, Jawalakhel) — Patan Formation

Central Lalitpur sits on the **Patan Formation**, a younger deposit overlying the Kalimati. The Patan Formation consists of fine to medium sand and silt inter-bedded with clay and fine gravels at some sites, and most of the areas in the major administrative centre of Nepal are comprised of Patan Formation. The bearing capacity is intermediate — better than central Kalimati, but worse than the eastern Gokarna/Thimi areas.

A typical Patan or Sanepa residential foundation:

- **Raft foundation** for buildings of three storeys or more (the Patan Formation interbedded layers can compress unevenly under isolated footings, so a raft averages out the differential settlement)
- **Isolated footings with good plinth beams** for two-storey houses on confirmed bearing strata at 1.5 to 2.0 m depth
- **Pile foundations** for commercial buildings of four storeys and above, typically bored piles 8 to 12 m deep

### 20.4 Bhaisepati and southern Lalitpur — softer southern soils

Moving south from Patan, the Valley transitions to softer soils — the southern fringes of the Kalimati basin and the marginal lacustrine deposits of the Lukundol Formation. Bearing capacity drops; bored piles or rafts become essential for any building of significance.

Bhaisepati specifically — a relatively new residential area south of the ring road — sits on soils that are softer than they appear from surface inspection. Several recent commercial buildings in Bhaisepati required bored piles to 18 to 22 metres depth, more than typical for the Valley, because the upper 15 metres of soil were too soft to provide adequate friction.

### 20.5 Riverbank zones (Bagmati, Bishnumati, Manohara) — liquefaction and flood

The floodplain margins of the Valley's rivers — the Bagmati through Teku and Tripureshwor, the Bishnumati through Balaju, the Manohara through eastern Bhaktapur — are the highest-liquefaction-risk zones. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake produced significant liquefaction along these floodplains. Where rivers are present with presumably saturated soils, these areas represent the position of highest liquefaction potential, and this potential reduces towards the valley edge.

Foundation design in these zones requires either:

- Deep piles bypassing the liquefiable zone (typically reaching at least 10 metres into a non-liquefiable substratum)
- Stone column or other ground-improvement treatment of the liquefiable zone before construction
- Or — in the case of older buildings or low-cost construction — accepting liquefaction risk and not building there at all

The municipal bye-laws of KMC, Lalitpur, and Bhaktapur all impose riparian setbacks of 20 m or more from the river's natural bank, which has the effect of keeping the highest-risk zones building-free.

### 20.6 Hill margin and Kirtipur — residual soils and rock

The hills along the southern and western edges of the Valley — Kirtipur, Champadevi, the Phulchoki ridge — are a different world geotechnically. The deposits transition from lacustrine to residual soils derived from weathering of the underlying Phulchoki and Bhimphedi group rocks. Bearing capacity is typically much higher than the Valley floor — 200 to 400 kPa or more — and rock can be encountered at shallow depths.

A typical Kirtipur hillside residence:

- **Strip footings** under load-bearing stone or brick walls, 600 to 750 mm wide, founded at 1.0 to 1.5 m depth
- **Isolated footings** for any RCC frame elements
- **Step footings** down hillside slopes, with each step matched to a riser height not exceeding the footing thickness
- **Hill-cut and slope-stability provisions** including retaining walls, drainage, and (for steeper sites) drilled piers tying the building into stable bedrock

The trade-off is access (Kirtipur sites are often accessible only by narrow lanes or by foot) and the unpredictability of weathered-bedrock contacts (which can vary across a site by metres).

## Part 21. Sheds, decks, fences, and small structures

Now that we have walked through the world of habitable buildings, let us spend some time on the small accessory structures that most homeowners actually build themselves at some point. Foundations for these structures are simple, cheap, and often a homeowner's first introduction to the practical realities of soil and concrete.

### 21.1 The garden shed

A typical 8×10 garden shed weighs between 1,500 and 4,000 pounds dry, depending on construction. A 12×16 shed might weigh 4,000 to 8,000 pounds. Loaded with a riding mower, garden tools, and various stored items, the total live load can easily double the dry weight. The foundation requirement is therefore modest but real.

The cheapest option is the *gravel pad*. A 4-to-6-inch deep bed of compacted crushed stone, properly graded for drainage, with the shed framed on pressure-treated 4×4 or 4×6 skids, is adequate for any small shed in a non-frost climate or with frost-tolerant construction. Total cost (excluding the shed itself) is perhaps $200 to $600 in materials. Construction time is one weekend for a competent DIYer.

The *concrete pier* option — typically 6 to 12 inch diameter Sonotube or Bigfoot piers, 24 to 48 inches deep, filled with concrete — is appropriate for larger sheds, sheds that will house heavy equipment, sheds in regions with significant frost (where the piers extend below the frost line), or sheds where the wood-skid alternative is not workable. The shed framing rests on adjustable post anchors (Simpson EPB or similar) embedded in the pier tops. Cost: $400 to $1,200 for a small shed (4 to 8 piers).

The *concrete slab* option — a 4-inch slab on a 4-inch crushed stone base, 12-inch thickened edges — is appropriate for any shed of 100 square feet or larger, any shed that will house significant equipment (lawn tractor, ATV, motorcycle), or any shed that will see use as a workshop with concrete-floor work. Cost: $1,500 to $5,000 depending on size and local concrete pricing.

In regions with frost, the IRC's accessory-structure exemption (R403.1.4 exception for free-standing structures of 600 sf or less, light-frame, eave height 10 ft or less) lets you skip frost protection for the foundation. The shed will move slightly with frost heave, but it is light and rigid enough that the movement does not cause damage. For larger sheds (over 600 sf), or sheds with masonry, frost protection becomes mandatory.

The Kathmandu Valley equivalent — a small godown, kiosk, or pump-house — is typically built on a 75 mm PCC pad over a 100 mm gravel base, with 230 mm brick or 200 mm CMU walls. No engineered foundation, no licensed design, no permit issues; the structure is typically too small to require any of these.

### 21.2 The deck

A residential deck is essentially a free-standing structure attached to the side of the house. Deck foundations are governed by IRC R507 (for wood decks) and similar provisions in other codes. The basic requirement: deck supports must extend below the local frost line, in increments per the deck-load requirements of R507.3 and the table.

A typical residential deck:

- Concrete piers (Sonotube/Bigfoot) at each post location
- Pier diameter 8 to 12 inches, depth below frost line, with a footing pad at the base (often a 24-inch diameter Bigfoot footing)
- Adjustable post anchors (Simpson PB or similar) at the top of each pier
- 4×4 or 6×6 pressure-treated posts on the anchors
- Beams spanning the posts, joists spanning the beams, decking on the joists

The connection between the deck and the house — the *ledger* — is structurally critical and a frequent failure point. Building codes require specific ledger fastening (typically 1/2-inch lag bolts at 16-inch staggered spacing into the band joist of the house, with appropriate flashing) per IRC R507.2.

Frost is a special concern for decks. In a 60-inch frost zone, deck piers must extend 60 inches below grade — a substantial excavation. Many homeowners cheat this and place piers shallower than required; the result is that the deck heaves and tips with seasonal frost while the house does not, opening up gaps at the ledger and stressing the connection.

### 21.3 Fences

A residential fence of any height and length needs post foundations to resist wind loads and provide vertical stability. A typical wood-frame fence of 4 to 6 feet height uses 4×4 posts at 6 to 8 foot spacing, set in concrete-filled holes 24 to 36 inches deep. The post holes are typically dug with a hand auger or a power auger, with a 10 to 12 inch diameter at the bottom and a slight bell shape to resist uplift.

The IRC does not require frost protection for fences (they are not buildings), but in deep-frost climates it is a good idea to extend fence-post holes below frost line to keep the posts from heaving and racking the fence over time. A fence in northern Minnesota with 24-inch deep posts will heave; the same fence with 60-inch deep posts will not.

The standard wood-fence post installation puts the post directly into the concrete, with no separation. This gets the post wet, and the post rots from the inside out, often within 10 to 15 years. A better (though more expensive) installation uses a steel post anchor (a U-bracket with a buried base plate or a leg) embedded in the concrete, with the wood post bolted to the anchor above grade. The wood post stays dry and lasts 30+ years.

In Kathmandu, residential fences are typically masonry compound walls — 230 mm brick or 150 mm CMU, 1.5 to 2.0 metres tall, on a continuous strip footing 600 mm wide and 600 mm deep. The compound wall serves dual purpose as fence and as security/privacy barrier, and is essentially a small unreinforced or lightly reinforced masonry structure.

### 21.4 Retaining walls

A retaining wall holds back a vertical face of soil. Even modest retaining walls — 1 to 2 metre tall garden walls — generate substantial lateral earth pressures and require proper foundations.

Small retaining walls (under 4 feet / 1.2 metres) can typically be built without engineered design, using either:

- *Gravity walls*: concrete masonry block, segmental retaining wall units (SRW), or natural stone, where the weight of the wall itself resists the soil pressure
- *Cantilever concrete walls*: an L-shaped reinforced concrete section, with a horizontal toe extending into the retained soil to use the soil's weight as additional resistance

Larger retaining walls require engineered design, with consideration of:

- Active earth pressure (when the wall yields slightly outward)
- At-rest earth pressure (when the wall is rigid)
- Surcharge from anything on top of the retained slope
- Hydrostatic pressure if drainage is inadequate
- Seismic earth pressure increment (Mononobe-Okabe method)
- Bearing capacity and sliding resistance at the wall base
- Overturning stability

The most common cause of retaining-wall failure is inadequate drainage. A retaining wall must have a continuous drainage system behind it (typically a gravel envelope with a perforated drainpipe at the base, day-lighted to grade or to a sump) to prevent hydrostatic buildup. A small retaining wall with no drainage, fronted by a heavy rainfall, will fail dramatically.

In the Kathmandu Valley, retaining walls are universal in hill-margin and stepped-terrace residential construction. The traditional Newari technique is dry-stacked stone walls with a slight batter (a backward lean of about 1 in 6), keyed into the slope. Modern engineered walls use reinforced concrete cantilever or segmental concrete block construction. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake produced widespread retaining-wall failures throughout the hill margins of the Valley, and post-earthquake reconstruction has substantially upgraded design and detailing standards.

## Part 22. Drainage, dampproofing, and waterproofing

A foundation can be perfectly designed for its loads and yet fail catastrophically because of water. Water is, in foundation terms, the single most destructive natural force, and the management of water around foundations is at least as important as the structural design itself.

### 22.1 Surface drainage

The first line of defence is keeping water away from the foundation perimeter in the first place. The IRC R401.3 requires a minimum 6 inches of grade fall in the first 10 feet horizontally from the foundation, sloping the ground away from the building. This single requirement, faithfully observed, prevents perhaps the majority of foundation drainage problems. It is also the single requirement most commonly violated, because over 50 years a homeowner will plant flower beds, install patios, build retaining walls, and re-grade the yard, slowly destroying the original drainage and channelling water back toward the foundation.

Roof runoff is the second major source. Gutters and downspouts must be intact, must drain to extensions or splash blocks at least 5 feet from the foundation, and must not discharge directly against the foundation wall. A clogged gutter dumps water at the wall base, where it concentrates and either floods the basement (in basement construction) or saturates the soil and triggers expansive-clay heave (in slab construction).

Surface drainage devices — French drains, swales, catch basins — are appropriate where the basic 6-inch-in-10-feet grade cannot be achieved (e.g., where the building is at the bottom of a slope) or where surface water concentrates from large adjacent areas.

### 22.2 Subsurface drainage

A foundation drain (also called *footing drain*, *perimeter drain*, or *drain tile*) is a perforated pipe buried at the base of the foundation, surrounded by gravel, draining to a sump or to grade. The purpose is to relieve hydrostatic pressure on the foundation walls and floor, draining away groundwater that infiltrates from the surrounding soil.

The IRC R405 requires a foundation drain for any foundation that retains earth and encloses habitable or usable space below grade — i.e., any basement. The drain consists of:

- A 4-inch perforated pipe (typically corrugated polyethylene drainpipe with slot perforations)
- A gravel envelope of at least 6 inches around the pipe (or, in some details, a manufactured drain panel or geotextile-wrapped gravel)
- A drainage path to a sump or to grade

The drain pipe is laid with the perforations facing down (yes, that is correct — the perforations face down, so that water entering the gravel envelope can drain in by gravity from the top, rather than entering directly from the sides where it will carry soil into the pipe).

The drainage path question is critical. In older basements, the foundation drain often discharges to a sump pit inside the basement, with an electric sump pump removing the water. The sump pump is a notorious failure point — if the pump fails, or the power goes out during a flood, or the discharge line freezes, the basement floods. Modern best practice includes either:

- A redundant sump pump (a primary AC pump and a battery-backup secondary pump)
- A gravity drain to grade (where the site permits — on a sloped lot, this can be done by daylighting the drainpipe at a downhill location)
- A passive flood-resistance design (sealed foundation walls, no sump pit, designed to resist hydrostatic pressure)

### 22.3 Dampproofing vs waterproofing

The IRC distinguishes between *dampproofing* (R406.1) and *waterproofing* (R406.2). Dampproofing is required for basement walls in soils with no expected hydrostatic pressure; waterproofing is required where hydrostatic pressure is expected.

Dampproofing materials include:

- Asphalt emulsion (the original "tar" coating)
- Cement-based parge coats
- Modified bituminous coatings

Waterproofing materials include:

- Self-adhering rubberised asphalt sheet membranes (e.g., the Bituthene family)
- Liquid-applied elastomeric coatings
- Bentonite-clay panels
- Crystalline waterproofing admixtures (added to the concrete mix itself)

The distinction matters because the materials cost differently and perform differently. Dampproofing prevents capillary moisture migration but cannot withstand standing water; waterproofing can. A basement in a known-dry soil with a high water table at depth might use dampproofing; a basement in a high-groundwater suburban lot must have waterproofing. Mistaking one for the other is a common, expensive error.

### 22.4 Capillary breaks and vapour barriers

Even where there is no liquid water, water vapour migrates through soil and concrete. A concrete slab cast directly on soil will wick water vapour upward continuously. The carpet wets, the hardwood flooring cups, the laminate de-laminates, mould grows.

The standard solution is a *capillary break and vapour retarder* under any habitable slab:

- A 4 to 6 inch layer of clean coarse gravel or crushed stone (no fines), which breaks capillary continuity
- A 6 to 15 mil polyethylene sheet, lapped at seams and sealed, immediately under the slab
- The slab itself

The IRC R506 and R408 (for crawl spaces) prescribe the requirements. Modern practice in cold climates increasingly adds a layer of rigid foam insulation under the slab as well, which both insulates the slab and provides additional moisture protection.

In Kathmandu Valley practice, the equivalent is a 75mm PCC layer below the slab plus a polyethylene sheet, sometimes omitted in low-cost construction with predictable mould and dampness consequences.

### 22.5 The single most common foundation drainage failure

In our experience, the single most common foundation drainage failure is a clogged or broken footing drain that the homeowner does not know exists. The drain was installed at construction, the homeowner has lived in the house for 15 years, the drain pipe outlet has been buried by landscaping, the drain has clogged with sediment, the basement starts to leak, and the homeowner calls a foundation contractor to "fix the basement leak". The fix, properly done, requires excavating the perimeter, cleaning or replacing the drainpipe, re-grading the surface, and re-establishing the discharge — a $15,000 to $40,000 job that could have been prevented by a $200 inspection every 5 years and $50 of replacement landscaping.

## Part 23. Construction process and common defects

A foundation as designed and a foundation as built are not always the same thing. Let us walk through the construction process and call out the common defects at each stage.

### 23.1 Excavation

The excavation must reach undisturbed natural soil at the design depth. Common defects:

- **Over-excavation**: digging deeper than design, then back-filling with loose soil before pouring. The foundation now bears on uncompacted fill rather than natural soil, with predictable settlement.
- **Under-excavation**: stopping above the design depth, in disturbed or weak material. Less common but more dangerous.
- **Excavation in soft or unstable material without shoring**: the excavation walls collapse, contaminating the foundation bearing area with sloughed soil and creating a hazardous work environment.
- **Water in the excavation**: groundwater or rainwater accumulates in the excavation, must be pumped out, and if not the foundation is poured into water with severe quality consequences.

The fix is competent supervision: an engineer or experienced site superintendent should personally verify the excavation depth and condition before any concrete is poured. For deeper excavations (basements, deep footings), a temporary shoring system (sheet piling, soldier piles, slurry walls) may be necessary to keep the excavation stable.

### 23.2 Subgrade preparation

The bottom of the excavation must be compacted, level, and free of disturbed material. Common defects:

- **Uncompacted subgrade**: the excavator cleans out the bottom but does not compact it, leaving a soft layer that will settle differentially.
- **Frost-heaved subgrade**: in cold-season construction, the exposed subgrade freezes overnight and is poured on the next day without thawing. Subsequent thaw produces voids and settlement.
- **Wet or muddy subgrade**: rain has saturated the bottom; the contractor pours anyway. The wet soil compresses under the foundation load.
- **Organic material in the subgrade**: roots, peat, or other organics in the bearing soil. These will rot or compress and produce settlement.

The fix is again supervision plus a written subgrade-acceptance protocol. The subgrade should be inspected and approved before any concrete placement. Wet conditions should be remediated by drying or by replacement with compacted gravel.

### 23.3 Reinforcement placement

The reinforcement must be placed at the locations and orientations shown on the drawings, with proper concrete cover, properly tied at intersections, and properly supported above the subgrade. Common defects:

- **Insufficient cover**: bars too close to the soil face, where they will corrode rapidly in any moist environment.
- **Bars sitting on the subgrade**: with no chairs or supports, the bars cannot be at their design elevation in the slab.
- **Missing bars**: especially at corners, where the corner bars are routinely missed and the structure cracks at the corner.
- **Wrong bar size or grade**: the contractor substitutes a smaller or weaker bar without notifying the engineer.
- **Inadequate development length or lap splice length**: especially at column-footing connections, where the column dowels must extend a minimum length into the footing for proper load transfer.
- **No transverse reinforcement (stirrups) at corners and ends of beams**: weakening the shear capacity exactly where it is most needed.

The fix is a pre-pour reinforcement inspection, with the engineer or building inspector checking the placement before any concrete is poured. In Kathmandu Valley NBC 105:2020 detailing, the seismic detailing rules at column-beam joints and at column-footing connections are stringent and frequently violated; an engineer's pre-pour inspection is essential.

### 23.4 Concrete placement

The concrete must be placed at the correct rate, with proper consolidation, in temperature ranges that allow proper curing, and protected from premature drying or freezing. Common defects:

- **Cold-joint placement**: the previous lift of concrete has set before the next is placed, creating a weak plane that does not bond.
- **Segregation**: the concrete is dropped from too great a height (more than about 1 metre is risky), or is moved laterally with vibrators causing the aggregate to separate from the paste.
- **Inadequate consolidation**: the concrete is not vibrated thoroughly, leaving honeycomb voids especially around dense reinforcement.
- **Adding water at the truck**: the contractor adds water to make the concrete more workable, weakening the concrete proportionally.
- **Hot weather pouring without provisions**: in hot, dry weather, the concrete can lose moisture rapidly, leading to plastic shrinkage cracking and reduced strength.
- **Cold weather pouring without provisions**: concrete poured below 4°C (40°F) without insulating blankets or heaters can freeze before adequately cured, with severe strength loss.

The fix is proper placement, proper consolidation, slump testing of every truck (with rejection of non-compliant loads), test cylinders cast for compression-strength verification, and curing protocols for hot or cold weather.

### 23.5 Curing

Concrete strength gain requires moisture and time. A foundation that is poured and then allowed to dry rapidly will not achieve its design strength. The IRC and ACI require that concrete be kept moist for at least 7 days (preferably 14) at temperatures above 5°C. Curing methods include:

- Continuous wet covering (burlap or wet curing blankets, kept saturated)
- Curing compounds sprayed on the concrete surface
- Plastic sheeting laid over the concrete

In Kathmandu Valley dry-season construction (October through March), curing is critically important and frequently neglected. We have walked sites where the contractor poured a slab on Monday morning and removed the forms (and any moisture protection) by Wednesday afternoon. The concrete strength achieved in such conditions is perhaps 50 to 70% of the design strength — adequate to stand up the building in the short term, deficient over a 50-year service life.

### 23.6 Backfilling

After the foundation is poured and cured, the excavation must be backfilled. Common defects:

- **Backfilling before the structure is adequate**: the foundation walls cannot resist lateral earth pressure until the floor framing or the roof slab is in place to brace them. Backfilling against unbraced walls can crack or push them inward.
- **Backfilling with on-site soil that is unsuitable**: the original excavated soil is often the wrong material to backfill with (especially if it is expansive clay). Better backfill is granular fill or controlled engineered fill.
- **Not compacting the backfill**: the backfill settles after the building is occupied, dragging down the surface drainage and concentrating water at the foundation.
- **Damaging the waterproofing during backfill**: large rocks or sharp debris in the backfill puncture the waterproofing membrane, creating future leak points.

The fix is standard: backfill in lifts of 8 to 12 inches, compact each lift, use granular fill where possible, protect the waterproofing with a board or panel during backfilling.

## Part 24. Foundation repair and remediation

Even well-built foundations sometimes need work after years of service. The major repair techniques cluster into a few families.

### 24.1 Underpinning for settlement

When a foundation has settled — from soil consolidation, undermining by a leak, or deterioration of the bearing soil — the standard repair is **underpinning**: extending the foundation deeper to reach a competent bearing stratum, while supporting the existing structure during the work.

Underpinning techniques include:

- **Mass concrete underpinning** (the traditional method): excavate in segments under the existing footing, pour new concrete in each segment, repeat
- **Pier underpinning**: install hydraulically pressed concrete or steel piers to support the existing footing
- **Helical pier underpinning**: install helical piers and connect them to the existing footing with brackets
- **Micropile underpinning**: install grouted micropiles connected to the existing footing

The DFW industry has standardised on pressed concrete piers and helical piers as the primary residential underpinning techniques, at typical costs of $250 to $600 per pier. A whole-house underpinning with 15 to 25 piers might cost $5,000 to $25,000.

### 24.2 Crack repair

Foundation cracks come in many flavours, and not all of them indicate trouble. Hairline shrinkage cracks (typical width less than 0.3 mm) in concrete walls are normal and require no repair. Wider cracks (greater than 0.5 mm) or cracks showing displacement (one side of the crack moved relative to the other) indicate stress and may require structural repair.

Repair techniques include:

- **Epoxy injection**: for non-moving cracks, structural epoxy is injected into the crack to restore the wall's integrity
- **Polyurethane injection**: for moving cracks (especially in basements with seasonal water movement), flexible polyurethane is used instead of epoxy, providing a watertight but flexible repair
- **Carbon fibre strapping**: for cracks accompanied by wall bowing, carbon fibre straps are bonded to the interior face of the wall to restrain further movement
- **Steel reinforcement strapping**: heavier-duty version of carbon fibre, using steel C-channels bolted to the wall

### 24.3 Waterproofing retrofit

A basement that has begun to leak, after years of service, is one of the most common foundation-repair projects. The retrofit options:

- **Interior drainage system**: install a perimeter drain and sump pump system inside the basement, with the wall-floor joint cut and a drain channel installed. This does not stop water from entering the wall, but it captures and discharges it. Cost: $6,000 to $15,000.
- **Exterior excavation and waterproofing**: excavate the foundation perimeter, install proper waterproofing, footing drains, and grading. The most expensive but most thorough fix. Cost: $15,000 to $50,000+.
- **Helical pier and crack repair combined**: address structural movement and crack repair simultaneously. Cost: highly variable.
- **Bentonite injection**: in some cases, bentonite clay can be injected into the soil outside the basement wall to create a waterproof barrier. Less common; effectiveness varies.

### 24.4 Slab leak repair

In slab-on-grade construction with embedded plumbing (the typical post-1960 US construction in the South and South-West), a leak in an embedded supply or drain line is a major repair event. The water saturates the soil under the slab, causing differential settlement or expansive-clay heave; the leak itself wastes water and damages the floor finish.

Repair options:

- **Spot repair**: jackhammer through the slab at the leak location, repair the pipe, patch the slab. Cheapest option for an isolated leak. Disruptive and ugly.
- **Re-route**: abandon the failed line in the slab and run a new line overhead through the attic, dropping down at fixtures. Less invasive but adds visible plumbing.
- **Tunnelling**: tunnel under the slab from outside the foundation to access the leak, repair the pipe, and back-fill the tunnel. Preserves the floor finish but requires careful soil handling.
- **Pipe lining**: trenchless rehabilitation of the pipe in place, using inserted polyethylene liners or epoxy coatings. Newer technique; effectiveness varies.

In Kathmandu Valley construction, the embedded-plumbing problem is rare because the building tradition uses an above-slab plumbing layout (plumbing in walls, surface-mounted drains in the ground floor), specifically because the plinth space and the floor traditions make embedded plumbing less convenient.

## Part 25. Maintenance — a 50-year schedule

A foundation is not a fire-and-forget element. To get 50 years of service from it, you must maintain it. Here is a maintenance schedule that, if observed, will catch the major problems before they become major repairs.

**Every spring and autumn:**

- Walk the foundation perimeter. Look for cracks (note any that have widened, photograph for record), efflorescence (white salt deposits indicating water movement through the wall), surface damage, or areas where the grade is sloping toward the building.
- Check the gutters and downspouts. Clean if needed. Verify discharge is at least 5 feet from the foundation.
- Check sump pump operation (if one is present). Pour a bucket of water into the sump to verify the pump cycles.
- In expansive-clay regions, check whether the foundation perimeter has shrunk away from the surrounding soil. If yes, water the perimeter (drip hose around the perimeter) for 1-2 weeks to re-saturate the soil.

**Every year:**

- Inspect the basement (or crawl space) for moisture, cracks, mould, or pest signs.
- Clean and verify operation of the foundation drain discharge (where accessible).
- Check that no landscaping has been added that would impede surface drainage.

**Every 5 years:**

- Have a competent contractor inspect the foundation drain system and verify it is functioning.
- Check the basement waterproofing (or exterior dampproofing where accessible).
- Inspect the sump pump for wear and replace if approaching end of life (typical sump pump life is 7-10 years).
- For pier and beam, inspect all piers and beams for wood deterioration, pier cracking, or shimming displacement.

**Every 10 years:**

- Comprehensive structural inspection by an engineer, especially after any earthquake event or significant flooding.
- Re-test soil conditions if any settlement or movement has been noted.
- Renew exterior waterproofing if accessible.

**Every 20 years:**

- Major structural review, including foundation and superstructure.
- Repair or replace any failed waterproofing systems.
- Update mitigation systems (radon, sump pumps, drainage) to current technology.

In the Kathmandu Valley, this schedule has additional items reflecting the local context:

- **Every monsoon (June-September)**: monitor for water entry, check the underground water tank for leaks, verify drainage of the courtyard and rooftop systems.
- **After every felt earthquake**: visual inspection of all visible structural elements, especially column-beam joints and the foundation-superstructure interface. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake produced many "invisible" damage patterns that did not become apparent until subsequent earthquakes or until detailed inspection.
- **Every termite season (April-June)**: check for mud tubes on foundation walls, especially around the plinth, where subterranean termites enter wood elements.

## Part 26. Decision-making — what foundation does my project actually need?

Let us bring it together with a decision framework. When confronted with a new building project, the foundation choice is driven by the following questions, in roughly this order:

1. **What is the climate?** Frost depth, hurricane wind speed, seismic design category, flood zone — these set the basic envelope of what foundations are even possible at the site.

2. **What does the soil look like?** Bearing capacity, settlement potential, expansiveness, liquefaction susceptibility, depth to a competent bearing stratum, depth to groundwater, soil chemistry. This question is answered by a geotechnical investigation.

3. **What is the building?** Single-family home, manufactured home, townhouse, multi-storey commercial, accessory structure. Heavy or light. Continuous or discrete loads.

4. **What is the budget?** A house that needs deep foundations on liquefiable soil costs more than the same house on a competent bearing soil, sometimes substantially more.

5. **What is the local construction culture?** A foundation that can be built reliably by the local trades is preferable to one that requires specialised skills not available in the area. A post-tensioned slab in a town with no PT-experienced contractor is asking for trouble.

6. **What is the design-and-permit pathway?** A foundation that can be built with off-the-shelf prescriptive code provisions (like IRC R403 footing tables, or NBC 201 simple frame rules) is faster to permit than a fully engineered design — but the prescriptive paths only work within their stated limits.

A flowchart of typical residential decisions:

- *Building in deep frost (60+ inch frost depth)?* → Probably full basement, possibly FPSF for slab construction.
- *Building in expansive clay (DFW, Yazoo, southern Kathmandu)?* → Post-tensioned slab, or pier-and-beam with deep piers, or raft on engineered fill.
- *Building in V flood zone?* → Pile foundation, elevated above DFE, breakaway walls.
- *Building on liquefiable soil (Christchurch, central Kathmandu, Tokyo Bay)?* → Deep piles to a competent stratum, or stone-column ground improvement, or both.
- *Building on competent dry soil with shallow frost?* → Slab-on-grade with thickened edges. The boring, default, cheapest answer — and the right one for most of suburban America.
- *Building on hillside?* → Drilled piers with grade beams, or stepped strip footings, depending on slope.
- *Building a small accessory structure?* → Gravel pad, concrete pier, or small slab, with the choice based on size, climate, and intended use.

This is not a substitute for a stamped engineering drawing, but it is a useful first-cut framework for thinking about the problem.

## Part 27. The economics — what does this all cost?

Foundations are a substantial fraction of the total cost of a building, but a smaller fraction than most people imagine. As a rough rule:

- For a typical US single-family home, the foundation costs 8 to 15% of the total construction cost.
- For a Kathmandu Valley single-family home, the foundation (including the underground water tank, which is conventionally bundled with the foundation cost) costs 10 to 20%.
- For a multi-storey commercial building on poor soil, the foundation (with deep piles or a thick raft) can be 20 to 30% of the construction cost — substantially more than a residential foundation.

In dollar terms, a typical 2,500-square-foot US suburban single-family slab-on-grade foundation in 2026 costs $15,000 to $35,000 (varying widely by region — the same slab is twice as expensive in coastal California as in suburban Atlanta). A full basement under the same house costs $40,000 to $80,000. A pile-supported coastal house foundation can be $60,000 to $200,000 just for the piles and pile cap.

In Nepali rupee terms, a typical 1,500-square-foot Kathmandu Valley two-storey RCC frame house foundation in 2026 (including isolated footings, plinth beam, ground floor slab, and underground water tank) costs NPR 18 to 35 lakh, which is roughly 12 to 20% of the typical NPR 1.5 to 2.0 crore total build cost. A raft foundation for the same house adds NPR 5 to 12 lakh. Deep piles add NPR 8 to 25 lakh on top.

These numbers are not budgets — they are very rough rules of thumb, and your actual project will vary substantially based on site, soil, design, and contractor. But they are useful for planning.

A few cost-control principles that apply universally:

1. **Get the soil test early.** A $1,500 soil test that reveals the need for piles before you have committed to a slab design saves you tens of thousands of dollars later.
2. **Design for the soil, not for a default.** A standard 12-inch strip footing on weak soil is cheaper than the right footing — but not for long.
3. **Drainage is the cheapest insurance you will ever buy.** The 6-inches-in-10-feet grade rule and a working footing drain prevent more foundation problems than any other single intervention.
4. **Do not value-engineer the foundation.** Cost savings on the foundation come back, with interest, in the form of repairs in years 5, 10, 20, and 30. The foundation is the wrong place to economise.
5. **Pay for proper inspection.** A pre-pour reinforcement check, a slump test of every truck, and a competent walk-through of the curing period catch problems before they are buried under concrete.

## Part 29. The chemistry and craft of concrete — what is actually in your foundation

We have spent twenty-eight parts talking about concrete as if it were a single substance. It is not. Concrete is a family of materials, and the family has dozens of members, each with its own personality. The concrete in your shed pad and the concrete in a high-rise raft are related the way a backyard tomato and a commercial greenhouse tomato are related — same species, very different cultivar. If you are going to think clearly about foundations, you need to think clearly about concrete, because concrete is what 95 percent of all modern foundations are made of, and getting it wrong on day one means living with the consequences for fifty years.

Let us start at the beginning. Concrete is the product of mixing four ingredients: a hydraulic cement (almost always Portland cement or a Portland blend), water, fine aggregate (sand), and coarse aggregate (gravel or crushed stone). When the cement and water meet, they undergo a chemical reaction called hydration, in which the cement particles dissolve and recrystallise into an interlocking matrix of hydrated calcium silicates that binds the aggregates together into a stone-like solid. The reaction is exothermic — concrete generates heat as it cures — and it is irreversible. Once concrete has set, it cannot be un-set. You cannot melt it down and re-pour it. You can only break it up and start again.

The ratio of water to cement (the *water-cement ratio*, abbreviated w/c) is the single most important design variable in concrete. A low w/c ratio (around 0.4) produces a strong, dense, durable concrete. A high w/c ratio (above 0.6) produces a weaker, more porous, less durable concrete. The reason is microscopic: when the cement hydrates, only the water that chemically combines with the cement contributes to strength. Excess water leaves behind voids in the hardened paste, and these voids are weak points and pathways for water and chlorides to penetrate later. A foundation poured at w/c 0.5 has perhaps half the durability of one poured at w/c 0.4, and a foundation poured at w/c 0.7 — which sometimes happens on hot days when the contractor adds water at the truck to make the concrete workable — has perhaps a quarter of the durability. The same building, the same rebar, the same engineer, but a thirty-year service life instead of a hundred-year service life, decided in five minutes on a Tuesday morning when the truck driver added a hose-full of water without asking.

This is why every concrete pour is governed by a *mix design* — a precise specification of how much cement, how much sand, how much gravel, how much water, and how much of various admixtures should go into each cubic yard or cubic metre of concrete. The mix design is calibrated by the ready-mix supplier and approved by the structural engineer. On a serious project, every truck arrives with a delivery ticket showing the mix proportions and the time the concrete was batched. The batch plant is required, by ASTM standard, to deliver concrete within tight tolerances of the design mix. If a foundation contractor adds water on site, they are voiding the mix design and assuming personal liability for the result. Reputable contractors do not do this. Less reputable ones do, and the homeowner finds out twenty years later when the slab spalls.

Portland cement itself comes in five basic ASTM types, each suited to different conditions. **Type I** is ordinary Portland cement, used for most construction. **Type II** has moderate sulphate resistance and lower heat of hydration, suitable for foundations in soils with sulphate-bearing groundwater. **Type III** is high-early-strength cement, useful for cold-weather pours where you need to develop strength quickly before frost can damage fresh concrete. **Type IV** is low-heat cement, used in massive pours like dam foundations where the heat generated by hydration would otherwise crack the concrete from thermal stress. **Type V** is high-sulphate-resistant cement, required in soils with severe sulphate exposure, common in parts of the western United States and in some agricultural regions where sulphate fertilizers have leached into the groundwater. For a typical residential foundation in non-aggressive soils, Type I or Type I/II blended cement is fine. For foundations in coastal Florida or in DFW soils with measurable sulphate content, Type II or Type V is specified. The geotechnical report should report sulphate content; if it does not, ask.

Beyond the basic ASTM types, modern concrete almost always uses *supplementary cementitious materials* (SCMs) — fly ash from coal-fired power plants, ground granulated blast-furnace slag from steel mills, silica fume from silicon-metal manufacture, and natural pozzolans like calcined clay or volcanic ash. These materials are mixed in with the Portland cement (typically replacing 20 to 50 percent of the cement) and contribute to long-term strength and durability through a slower secondary hydration reaction. They also reduce the carbon footprint of the concrete substantially — Portland cement manufacture is one of the largest single sources of industrial CO2 emissions globally, and replacing 30 percent of the cement with fly ash cuts the embodied carbon roughly in proportion. For a foundation, an SCM-blended mix is almost always preferable to straight Portland cement: it cures more slowly (which is fine for foundations, where you do not need same-day strip times), generates less heat (helpful in massive pours), and produces a denser, more durable hardened paste. The tradeoff is slower early strength gain — at 7 days the concrete may be at 60 percent of its 28-day strength rather than 75 percent — but for a foundation that is going to sit underground for a century, an extra week of curing time is a non-issue.

Aggregates — the sand and gravel that make up roughly 70 percent of concrete by volume — also matter, and matter more than most people realise. Coarse aggregate must be hard, durable, well-graded (a mix of sizes from 3/8-inch up to perhaps 1.5-inch maximum size), and free of clay, organic matter, and reactive minerals. The maximum size matters because it determines how easily the concrete can flow into tight spaces — a foundation with closely spaced rebar (say, #5 bars at 6 inches on centre) cannot use 1.5-inch aggregate because the stones bridge across the bars and leave voids. Spec sheets typically call for 3/4-inch maximum aggregate for most foundation work. Reactive aggregates — those containing minerals that react with the alkalis in cement to form expansive gels — cause a slow, decades-long destruction of concrete called *alkali-silica reaction* (ASR). Areas with reactive aggregates (parts of New England, parts of the Midwest, and notably a swath of the Indian subcontinent) require either non-reactive aggregate sources, low-alkali cement, or chemical admixtures to suppress the reaction. The Kathmandu Valley's river-gravel aggregates are generally considered non-reactive, but no aggregate source should be used in significant volume without testing.

Fine aggregate — sand — needs to be clean, well-graded, and reasonably sharp-edged (rounded river sand is acceptable but produces slightly less strong concrete than crushed sand). The Kathmandu Valley historically used river sand from the Bagmati and Bishnumati, but two decades of intense extraction depleted those sources and damaged the river ecologies; modern Kathmandu construction uses crushed-stone sand from quarries in the surrounding hills, which is generally of acceptable quality for residential foundations. The transition from river sand to crushed sand changed the workability of concrete (crushed sand is angular and harder to pump) and required a learning curve for local contractors, who initially produced concrete with too little workability and too many voids. By the late 2010s the industry had adapted, but on small unregulated projects you still occasionally see badly graded sand and the consequent honeycomb defects in cured concrete.

Water for concrete needs to be potable. In practice this means clean municipal water; well water that is reasonably clear and not too salty is fine, but seawater is absolutely not — chlorides in seawater attack rebar and accelerate corrosion. On the Kathmandu Valley sites I have visited, the water for concrete typically comes from the project's tube well or from a tanker, and it is generally fine; on remote rural sites in the Tarai or in coastal areas I have seen contractors using brackish water in a pinch, and the consequences appear over decades as the rebar slowly rusts and the concrete slowly spalls.

Admixtures — chemical additions to the basic concrete mix — deserve their own chapter. There are five major families. **Water-reducers** (sometimes called plasticisers) allow the concrete to be made workable with less water, improving strength and durability without sacrificing placeability. Modern *high-range water reducers* (superplasticisers) can reduce water content by 25 to 30 percent for the same workability, producing concretes with w/c ratios of 0.30 or even lower. These are essentially mandatory for self-consolidating concrete and very useful in heavily reinforced foundations. **Retarders** slow the set time of concrete, useful in hot weather or for long pours where you do not want the first concrete poured to start setting before the last is placed. **Accelerators** speed up the set, useful in cold weather; calcium chloride was the traditional accelerator but is now generally avoided in reinforced concrete because chlorides corrode rebar; non-chloride accelerators based on calcium nitrate or other chemistries are used instead. **Air-entraining agents** introduce billions of microscopic air bubbles into the concrete, which dramatically improve its resistance to freeze-thaw damage. Any concrete that will be exposed to freezing temperatures while wet (which includes any foundation in a cold climate, since the upper portion is exposed to moisture and frost) should be air-entrained, typically to 5 to 7 percent total air content. **Corrosion inhibitors** — typically calcium nitrite — are added to concrete in chloride-exposure environments (coastal foundations, road salt exposure) to delay rebar corrosion. They are common in coastal Florida specifications and increasingly common in northern road-deicing environments.

Beyond these are dozens of more specialised admixtures: shrinkage-reducing admixtures, viscosity modifiers, anti-washout admixtures for underwater concrete, and so on. For a residential foundation in a benign environment, the basic admixture package is a water-reducer and (in cold climates) an air-entraining agent. For a coastal foundation, add a corrosion inhibitor. For a foundation in a sulphate-rich soil, use Type II or Type V cement and a low w/c ratio. The structural engineer specifies all of this; the homeowner does not need to memorise it; but knowing it exists helps you ask the right questions when the engineer hands you the spec sheet.

Concrete strength is reported in terms of *compressive strength at 28 days*, abbreviated *f'c*. The standard test is to pour a 6-inch by 12-inch (or 4-inch by 8-inch) cylinder during the pour, cure it under controlled conditions, and crush it in a hydraulic press at 28 days; the maximum stress the cylinder withstands before failure is *f'c*. For residential foundations the typical specification is 3,000 psi (about 21 MPa) for footings and stem walls, 3,500 psi (24 MPa) for slab-on-grade, and 4,000 psi (28 MPa) for post-tensioned slabs and structural columns. Higher strengths — 5,000, 6,000, even 8,000 psi — are used in high-rise foundations, prestressed elements, and exposed coastal applications. The Kathmandu Valley convention typically calls for M20 (20 MPa, around 2,900 psi) for residential footings and M25 (25 MPa, around 3,600 psi) for structural columns, following IS 456 mix designations. M30 (30 MPa) is used in larger commercial projects and increasingly in upper-end residential.

The 28-day strength is a contractual milestone but not a real one. Concrete continues to gain strength for years; a properly cured 28-day-3,000-psi concrete will reach perhaps 4,000 psi at one year and 4,500 psi at ten years. The 28-day spec exists because that is when the structure is typically loaded; in practice, the real long-term strength is meaningfully higher than the spec, which provides a margin of safety that engineers rely on without saying so.

The other side of concrete strength is *tensile strength*, which is roughly 10 percent of compressive strength — concrete is strong in compression and weak in tension. This is why concrete is reinforced with steel: the steel handles the tensile loads and the concrete handles the compressive loads, in an engineered partnership that makes possible everything from a residential footing to a 600-foot bridge span. We will treat reinforcement in detail in the next part.

Finally, curing. Concrete curing — the controlled retention of moisture and temperature during the first days after placement — is the single most under-appreciated factor in concrete performance. Concrete that is allowed to dry out in the first three days after placement does not develop full strength, no matter what the mix design specified. The cement particles need water to hydrate; if the water evaporates, the reaction stops, and the concrete is permanently weaker. Proper curing means keeping the concrete moist for at least 7 days (some specifications require 14 days for mass concrete), either by ponding water on the surface, covering with wet burlap, applying a curing compound that seals in moisture, or covering with plastic sheeting. In hot dry weather, freshly poured concrete can lose half its surface moisture in the first hour; in cold weather, the concrete must also be protected from freezing, which destroys the still-soft surface paste. The Kathmandu Valley convention of having the labour wet the curing concrete by hand, three or four times a day for a week, is not a quaint tradition — it is the basic minimum, and it works. The American convention of using a curing compound sprayed on the surface immediately after finishing also works, when applied properly. What does not work is leaving the concrete uncovered in the sun for three days, which is what happens on under-supervised projects in both countries, and which produces foundations that are 30 percent weaker than the engineer designed.

A short note on hot-weather concreting and cold-weather concreting. In hot weather (above about 90°F or 32°C), concrete sets faster, generates more heat, loses more water to evaporation, and is more prone to plastic shrinkage cracking. The standard hot-weather techniques are: pour at night or early morning; pre-cool the aggregate or use ice in the mix water; use retarders to slow the set; and cover the fresh concrete immediately to protect it from sun and wind. In cold weather (below about 40°F or 5°C), concrete sets slowly, hydration nearly stops below freezing, and freshly placed concrete can be permanently damaged if it freezes before reaching about 500 psi (typically the first 24 to 48 hours after placement). The standard cold-weather techniques are: heat the mix water; use accelerators; insulate the formwork (insulating blankets are common); and tent the pour with propane heaters running until the concrete has reached protected strength. In the northern US — Minnesota, North Dakota, interior Alaska — winter pouring is routine but expensive, and many residential foundations are simply scheduled for warmer months.

That is the chemistry and the craft of concrete. It looks, from the outside, like a uniform grey substance you order by the truckload. It is, in reality, an engineered material with dozens of moving parts, and the difference between a 100-year foundation and a 30-year foundation is decided in the details of mix design, placement, and curing — none of which the homeowner sees, all of which the homeowner pays for over the life of the building.

## Part 30. A long parade of failure case studies — what goes wrong and why

Let us close the body of the article with a series of brief case studies of foundation failures, drawn from the literature and from local experience. Each one illustrates a principle.

**Case 1: The Mexico City sinking buildings.** Mexico City sits on the soft lake-bed soils of the former Lake Texcoco. The entire city has settled by 5 to 10 metres over the past century, with downtown buildings now sitting metres below their original elevations. Older buildings on shallow foundations have settled differentially — the Palacio de Bellas Artes, for example, has visibly tilted and sunk. The lesson: very soft soils can produce very large settlements over time, and a foundation system that is locally rigid (a raft) but settles uniformly can preserve the building even when the ground subsides.

**Case 2: The Leaning Tower of Pisa.** The famous tower sits on a 3-metre-deep foundation on soft clay; the tower began tilting almost immediately during construction in the 12th century, and tilted progressively over 800 years until late-20th-century stabilisation work arrested the tilt. The lesson: even ancient construction needs a soil investigation; a tower with a 3-metre foundation on soft soil has no chance.

**Case 3: The Christchurch liquefaction failures (2010-2011).** The 2011 M6.3 Christchurch earthquake liquefied huge swaths of the city's eastern and southern suburbs, ejecting sand and water through driveway cracks, sinking houses unevenly into the ground, and rendering thousands of homes uninhabitable. The cause: shallow foundations on saturated, loose, fine sand. The lesson: liquefaction can destroy a building in seconds; foundations on liquefiable soils need either deep piles or ground improvement.

**Case 4: The 2015 Gorkha earthquake foundations in central Kathmandu.** The 2015 Nepal earthquake caused widespread structural damage but, surprisingly, relatively limited foundation failures in the central Valley — most of the damage was in the upper stories due to soft-storey effects, lack of confinement, and poor detailing. Local soil amplification, foundation problems, liquefaction-associated damage and local settlement-related damage were observed in the 2015 earthquake, particularly in unengineered buildings. The lesson: earthquakes test the entire structural system, not just the foundation, and the foundation is rarely the weakest link in a properly engineered building.

**Case 5: The Hurricane Ian Florida pile failures.** Hurricane Ian (2022) destroyed many older Florida coastal homes that had been grandfathered into low-elevation V-zone construction. The piles were inadequate for the actual storm-surge loads and scour. Houses built to current 2020 Florida Building Code standards generally survived. The lesson: code requirements reflect hard-won lessons; older non-conforming structures are vulnerable; periodic re-evaluation against current standards is wise.

**Case 6: The Texas slab failures.** Tens of thousands of Dallas–Fort Worth slab-on-grade homes have foundation distress at any given time, ranging from cosmetic cracking to severe settlement. The cause: expansive clay plus inadequate moisture management plus, frequently, slab design or construction defects. The lesson: expansive clay requires very specific design (post-tensioned slab) and active moisture management for the life of the building.

**Case 7: The Champlain Towers South collapse (Surfside, Florida, 2021).** The 12-storey condominium tower collapsed catastrophically on June 24, 2021, killing 98 people. Investigation pointed to long-term concrete deterioration in the foundation pool deck and parking structure, exacerbated by saltwater intrusion. The lesson: even if a foundation is initially properly designed, decades of inadequate maintenance can lead to failure. Concrete in coastal environments needs ongoing monitoring and timely repair.

**Case 8: The 1985 Mexico City earthquake building failures.** The 1985 M8.1 Michoacán earthquake destroyed many buildings in central Mexico City. The damage pattern was dominated by 8 to 18 storey buildings — the natural period of the city's soft soils matched the natural period of mid-rise buildings, producing devastating resonance amplification. The lesson: site soil response is as important as building structural response; high-amplification soils require special attention to building height and stiffness.

**Case 9: The 1995 Kobe earthquake foundations.** The Great Hanshin earthquake destroyed huge numbers of older Japanese houses, particularly those built before the 1981 seismic code. The damage was concentrated in older, soft-storey, soft-soil locations. Modern Japanese practice — base isolation, advanced seismic detailing, deep foundations — has substantially improved performance in subsequent earthquakes. The lesson: earthquake engineering knowledge accumulates over generations; older buildings often need retrofit.

**Case 10: The Boston molasses flood of 1919.** A 50-foot-tall steel tank holding 2.3 million gallons of molasses ruptured catastrophically, killing 21 people and injuring 150 more. The cause: the tank's foundation was inadequate for the load, the steel was substandard, and the design did not account for the temperature-induced expansion of the molasses inside. The lesson: even non-building structures need proper foundation engineering, and economising on the foundation has dramatic consequences.

## Part 32. Resources for further reading

If you have made it this far, you are deeply interested in foundations and probably want to read more. Here is an annotated reading list.

**For US residential practice:**

- 2024 International Residential Code (ICC). Available through codes.iccsafe.org. Chapter 4 is the relevant chapter; Chapters 3 (R301.2 has the climate tables), 5 (Floors), and 6 (Wall Construction) provide context.
- 2024 International Building Code (ICC). Chapter 18 covers soils and foundations for non-residential and large-residential buildings.
- ACI 318-25, *Building Code Requirements for Structural Concrete*. The American Concrete Institute. The 2025 edition was released in print on July 9, 2025. Most useful chapters for foundations: Chapter 13 (Foundations), Chapter 17 (Anchoring), Chapter 18 (Earthquake-Resistant Structures).
- ASCE 7-22 (Minimum Design Loads), and the upcoming ASCE 7-28 when published.
- ASCE 32-01, *Design and Construction of Frost-Protected Shallow Foundations*.
- FEMA Technical Bulletins, available at fema.gov/emergency-managers/risk-management/building-science. Especially relevant: TB 1 (Flood Openings), TB 5 (Free of Obstruction Requirements), TB 8 (Corrosion Protection), TB 9 (Breakaway Walls).
- *Foundation Design and Construction* by Tomlinson and Boorman. The classic British civil-engineering textbook; comprehensive and thorough.
- *Design of Reinforced Concrete* by Jack McCormac. Solid undergraduate-level treatment of structural concrete including foundations.

**For Nepal / Kathmandu Valley practice:**

- NBC 105:2020, *Seismic Design of Buildings in Nepal*. Available through DUDBC.
- NBC 201:1994, *Mandatory Rules of Thumb for RCC Buildings without Masonry Infill*.
- NBC 206:2003, *Architectural Design Requirements*.
- IS 456:2000, *Indian Standard for Plain and Reinforced Concrete Code of Practice*.
- IS 1893 (Part 1):2016, *Criteria for Earthquake Resistant Design of Structures*.
- IS 1904:1986, *Code of Practice for Design and Construction of Foundations in Soils*.
- *Engineering Properties of Fine Grained Soils of Kathmandu Valley* by Dahal et al. Useful local geotechnical reference.
- *Liquefaction Potential for the Kathmandu Valley* by Gilder et al. (Bulletin of Earthquake Engineering, 2021). Detailed analysis of the Valley's liquefaction hazard.

## Part 33. A closing thought

Foundations are the part of a building that nobody sees, that nobody admires, that nobody photographs for the architecture magazine, and that decides almost everything else about the building. A house with bad finishes is a cosmetic problem you can fix in a weekend. A house with bad cabinetry is a fix-when-you-have-money problem. A house with bad framing is a serious but tractable problem, addressable with a competent contractor and a structural engineer. A house with a bad foundation is a problem you may have for the rest of the building's life — sometimes a problem you have for the rest of your own life — and the only way to avoid it is to do the foundation right on day one.

That goes equally for a $5,000 garden shed and a $5 million coastal home. The principles are the same: understand the soil, understand the climate, choose the foundation that fits, build it with proper supervision, and maintain it patiently over the decades. The materials and the costs scale; the principles do not.

We have walked from a leaning concrete pad outside an office door, through soil mechanics and code, through every major foundation type ever built for residential and small commercial structures, through eight US regions and six Kathmandu Valley neighbourhoods, through sheds and decks and retaining walls, through drainage and damp-proofing, through construction and repair and 50-year maintenance. There is more to say — there is always more to say — but the bones of the subject are now in place.

If, after reading all this, you walk past a construction site and see the orange iron rods sticking up out of a hole, and you understand what they are doing there, and what soil they are bearing on, and roughly what loads they will eventually carry — then this article has done its quiet job. The foundation is, in the end, an act of careful patience: the patience of a soil engineer who walks the site, the patience of a designer who chooses the right details, the patience of a contractor who pours and cures the concrete properly, the patience of an inspector who catches the missed corner bar, the patience of a homeowner who keeps the gutters clean for fifty years.

A house, well-founded, is a house that disappears into the landscape and stays there. The marble does not roll. The doors close. The walls do not crack. Generations live in it without noticing what is under their feet. That is the highest praise any foundation can earn — that it has been forgotten about.

May yours, dear reader, be one of the forgotten ones.
