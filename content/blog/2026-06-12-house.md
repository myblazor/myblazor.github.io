## Where we are

Four days in. Day one was land and bylaws, day two was design, day three was the foundation and the structural frame, yesterday was the envelope — walls, roof, windows, doors, and all the air-sealing details. If you visited the site today, the house would look close to finished from the outside. The RCC frame is cast, the AAC walls are up, the windows are in, the doors are fitted, the roof is insulated and tiled. From the street, it looks like a house.

Inside, though, the rooms are still empty shells. There are no taps. There are no light switches. There are no pipes running up to the roof tank. There is no power coming to the tea shop. The walls have grooves chased into them for electrical conduits, and rough slots cut for plumbing lines, but nothing is connected. The house has not yet learned how to be a house.

Today, Part 5, is the day the systems go in. We talk about water — the two-tank architecture that every serious Kathmandu home uses to cope with unreliable municipal supply, the pump that lifts water from the underground tank to the overhead tank, the pipes that carry it to the taps, and the solar water heater on the roof that gives you free hot showers for most of the year. We talk about power — the load calculation that sizes the whole electrical system, the two meters (one residential, one commercial for the tea shop), the circuits, the breakers, the backup inverter, the earthing system that keeps people from getting electrocuted, and the state of rooftop solar in Nepal in 2026. We talk about plumbing — the waste pipes, the septic tank or sewer connection, and why this decision depends entirely on which ward your plot is in.

None of this is glamorous. You will not photograph the submersible pump or the main distribution board to show friends. But every day for the next fifty years, when you turn a tap and water comes out, or flip a switch and a light comes on, or take a hot shower on a January morning, you will be using what we build today. And when these systems fail — as all of them eventually do — the *specific decisions* you make today decide whether the failure is a two-hour inconvenience or a two-week disaster.

No programming. No code. Just the quiet plumbing and wiring that makes a house actually work.

## Part 1. The Kathmandu water problem, and the standard solution

Before we design anything, understand the problem. In a well-run city, water supply is a service you take for granted — you turn the tap, it runs, all day, every day, at reliable pressure, forever. In Kathmandu, none of that is true. **Municipal water supply from Kathmandu Upatyaka Khanepani Limited (KUKL)** delivers water to most Valley residences for only a few hours at a time, and in many neighbourhoods for only a few hours every few days. The pressure is low, the timing is unpredictable, and the quality varies. In peak dry season (March–May) it can stop entirely for a week at a time in some wards.

If you tried to live directly off the municipal tap, your life would revolve around the water schedule — rushing to fill buckets when the supply came on, going without showers during dry stretches, scheduling cooking around unpredictable tap timing. This is how many Kathmandu households lived in the 1980s and 1990s, and nobody wants to go back.

The solution, universal across modern Kathmandu residences, is the **two-tank gravity-fed water system.** The architecture is simple and it has not really changed in thirty years:

1. **An underground tank** (the *sump* or *undertank*) at ground level or below, sized to hold days of water. Whenever the municipal supply comes on, the underground tank fills passively from the municipal line. When a tanker delivers water (you call them when the underground tank runs low; they show up within a few hours for NPR 1,500–3,000 per 5,000-litre tanker), the tanker pumps into the underground tank.
2. **A submersible pump** that sits in the underground tank, lifting water up to the roof whenever the overhead tank drops below a set level.
3. **An overhead tank** (the *upper tank*) on the roof, typically 15–25 feet above the highest tap in the house.
4. **Gravity feed** from the overhead tank down to every tap in the house. No pump runs when you turn on a tap; the water falls naturally because the overhead tank is higher than the tap.

This system gives you four important things. **First, municipal outages disappear from your daily life** — you are drawing from the overhead tank, which is topped up from the underground tank, which is topped up from the municipal line or from tankers. **Second, water pressure is consistent** — it depends only on the height difference between the overhead tank and the tap, not on whatever KUKL happens to be doing that afternoon. **Third, if the power goes out for a few hours, you still have water** — the overhead tank gravity-feeds the taps without electricity. **Fourth, you have a buffer for emergencies** — during extended outages, water contamination events, or festival-season demand spikes, your tanks buy you days of normalcy.

We sized the underground tank yesterday — 7,500 to 10,000 litres, giving 9 to 12 days of supply for a family of five plus a small tea shop. Today we size the overhead tank, the pump, and the pipework.

## Part 2. The overhead tank — sizing, siting, construction

The overhead tank has a different sizing logic from the underground tank. The underground tank is a buffer against KUKL outages and needs to hold *days* of water. The overhead tank only needs to hold *the busy-hour demand* of the house — enough to cope with multiple taps running at once for the length of time between pump cycles.

A family of five, plus a small tea shop, with everyone showering in the morning and washing dishes in the evening, can draw down an overhead tank surprisingly fast. A shower uses 30–50 litres at typical flow rates; a toilet flush 5–10 litres; a basin tap 3–5 litres per minute. Multiply by a family doing morning routines simultaneously and you can drain 500 litres in an hour.

For sizing purposes:

- **1,000 litre overhead tank:** minimum for a small family. Tight — the pump will cycle several times a day.
- **1,500 litre overhead tank:** comfortable for a family of five.
- **2,000 to 3,000 litre overhead tank:** the standard specification for a family-of-five plus tea shop. Gives enough buffer that the pump only needs to run once or twice a day.
- **5,000 litre overhead tank:** for larger households or homes that irrigate a garden.

For your house, a **2,500-litre overhead tank** is a sensible choice. It gives a full-day buffer even on heavy-use days, and it is light enough (2,500 kg full) that the roof structure we designed in Part 3 carries it without any special provision.

### Tank material — plastic versus stainless versus concrete

Three common materials on Kathmandu roofs:

**Plastic (HDPE, polyethylene).** The dominant choice in 2026. Brands like Sintex, Hilltake, Nepatop, Kishan, and CG Digital sell triple-layer and quadruple-layer HDPE tanks with 5-year warranties. A 2,000-litre tank is about NPR 28,000–45,000, a 3,000-litre tank NPR 40,000–65,000. Lightweight (a 2,000-litre HDPE tank weighs about 80 kg empty), easy to carry up to the roof, resistant to algae if the tank is properly opaque, and replaceable in an afternoon's work. The triple-layer tanks have a black middle layer that blocks sunlight from reaching the water, which prevents algae growth.

**Stainless steel (SS304).** Premium choice. A 2,000-litre SS304 tank is NPR 75,000–120,000. Heavier, longer-lasting (easily 25–30 years versus the 10–15 year typical life of HDPE), and the mirror-finished outside reflects sunlight so the water stays cooler in summer. Hilltake, CG, and several others make these in Nepal. For a customer who plans to be in the house for thirty-plus years, the extra investment is worth it.

**Reinforced concrete.** An RCC tank cast integrally with the roof structure. Durable essentially forever (100+ years), but expensive, heavy (requires structural provision from day one), and impossible to replace easily if it develops a crack. Rare in modern small-residential construction; more common in apartment buildings and institutional structures.

**Recommendation:** A 2,500-litre triple-layer HDPE tank for your house. Upgrade to SS304 only if budget permits and long-term permanence matters more than short-term cost.

### Siting the tank

Four rules for where to put the overhead tank on the roof:

**1. Directly above the main plumbing stack.** The stack is the vertical pipe that carries water supply down through the floors to the taps. The tank outlet should be as close as possible to the top of this stack. Long horizontal runs between the tank outlet and the downward stack create pressure loss and slow fill times.

**2. Above a load-bearing column or wall.** A 2,500-litre tank weighs 2,500 kg full. That is not a load to put in the middle of an unsupported slab. The tank should sit directly above a column or a load-bearing wall below, so the weight transfers cleanly through the structure. Your architect and structural engineer will coordinate this during the structural drawings; as an owner, simply check at the naksa pass stage that the tank position is marked and is over a column.

**3. Accessible for cleaning.** At least once a year, someone needs to climb onto the tank, open the manhole lid, scrub the interior, and drain and refill it. A tank tucked against a parapet with no access space is a tank that does not get cleaned. Leave 600 mm (2 feet) clear on all sides.

**4. Not over a bedroom.** The pump running at 5 AM transmits vibration through the structure. A tank directly above a bedroom ceiling means a weekly 5 AM wake-up call for the sleepers below. Locate the tank over the stairwell, over the bathroom stack, or over the kitchen instead.

## Part 3. The pump — sizing, type, control

The submersible pump is the motor that lifts water from the underground tank to the overhead tank. For a three-storey Kathmandu house, the pump has to lift water about 35 to 40 feet (the underground tank is 10 feet below ground, the overhead tank is 25 to 30 feet above), against the pipe friction along the way.

Three common pump types for this application:

**Submersible pump in the underground tank.** The pump motor itself sits inside the underground tank, submerged in the water. Quiet operation (the water damps the motor sound), no priming required (it is always underwater), and the motor is cooled by the water around it. Common brands in Nepal: Crompton, Texmo, Kirloskar, V-Guard. A 1 HP (0.75 kW) submersible that handles the 35–40 foot head for a three-storey house costs NPR 18,000–28,000 for the pump and another NPR 5,000–8,000 for the control panel and wiring. **This is the recommended choice** for a typical Kathmandu residence.

**Surface-mounted centrifugal pump.** The pump sits beside the underground tank (usually in a pump cabinet on the ground floor) and sucks water from the tank up to itself, then pushes it to the overhead tank. Cheaper than a submersible (NPR 8,000–15,000 for the pump), but noisier, and requires priming if it ever runs dry. Not recommended for the primary lift in a modern house; fine as a booster if you ever need one.

**Pressure-boosted pump with pressure tank.** A pump plus a small pressure tank that delivers water at controlled pressure regardless of whether the overhead tank is full. Useful in apartment buildings, unnecessary in a three-storey house with gravity feed from an overhead tank.

### Pump control

Three control options, in increasing order of comfort and cost:

**Manual switch.** You flip a switch in the morning, the pump runs for 20 minutes, you flip the switch off. NPR 500 total for the switch. This is what most Kathmandu houses actually have, and it produces two failure modes: (a) the pump runs dry because you forgot to switch it off after the underground tank emptied, destroying the pump; (b) the overhead tank overflows because you forgot to switch off, wasting water and soaking the roof.

**Timer or float-switch automatic.** A float switch in the overhead tank detects when the tank is full and automatically cuts power to the pump. A second float switch in the underground tank detects when the underground tank is dry and also cuts power. NPR 3,000–5,000 for the switches and the control box. **This is the minimum acceptable standard for a new house.** Do not skip it.

**Full-featured pump controller with indicator panel.** A panel in the kitchen or the pump room shows current water levels in both tanks, pump run-time, and fault conditions. NPR 8,000–15,000. Nice to have for a data-conscious owner; not essential.

## Part 4. Pipes, fittings, and the plumbing layout

### Pipe materials

Three materials dominate modern Kathmandu residential plumbing:

**GI (Galvanised Iron).** The traditional Nepali pipe. Zinc-coated steel. Strong, available everywhere, repairable by any plumber. But it rusts from the inside over 15–25 years, gradually narrowing the pipe bore and contaminating the water with iron oxide flakes. Still fine for outdoor utility runs (to a garden tap, for example) but **not recommended for interior drinking-water supply in a new house.**

**CPVC (Chlorinated PolyVinyl Chloride).** A thermoplastic pipe, usually cream or beige coloured. Handles both cold and hot water up to about 90°C. Joints are made with solvent cement, which creates a chemical weld that is stronger than the pipe itself. Cheaper than PPR, widely available, works well. Brands: Astral, Supreme, Finolex, Prince.

**PPR (Polypropylene Random copolymer).** A thermoplastic pipe, usually green. Handles cold, hot, and drinking-water-quality applications. Joints are made with heat fusion — a special tool heats both the pipe end and the fitting socket to 260°C, then the two are pushed together and allowed to cool, forming a molecular weld. Joints are extremely reliable, but the fusion tool is expensive and not every plumber owns one. Slightly more expensive than CPVC. Brands: Panchakanya, Ashirvad, Supreme, Hilltake.

**Recommendation:** PPR for all indoor water supply (cold and hot) in your house. Panchakanya PPR is locally manufactured, widely available, and reliably good quality. Budget about NPR 50,000 to NPR 90,000 for all supply piping in a three-storey house including the PPR, the fittings, and the plumber's labour.

### Supply layout

The plumbing layout follows the two-tank logic:

1. **Inlet line from the municipal stop-cock** to the underground tank. 25 mm (1 inch) GI or PPR, with a valve just inside the property boundary for isolation during maintenance.
2. **Inlet line from the municipal truck delivery point** (a tanker fill pipe near the road, usually a 40 mm / 1.5-inch PPR with a cap). The tanker operator attaches their hose to this when delivering water.
3. **Suction/delivery line from the submersible pump** in the underground tank, rising up through the building to the overhead tank. Typically 25 mm PPR inside a protective conduit.
4. **Distribution line from the overhead tank outlet** down through the house. Typically 25 mm PPR, branching down to 20 mm at the floor distribution points and 15 mm at individual tap branches.
5. **Separate hot water supply line** from the solar water heater (or the electric geyser) on the roof, down to each bathroom and the kitchen. 20 mm PPR; kept parallel to the cold line but thermally insulated where it runs through cold spaces.
6. **Isolation valves at every major junction** — the main inlet, each floor's distribution point, before each toilet, before each shower. When something needs repair, you isolate a single fixture without turning off the whole house.

### Two things plumbers often skip

**Air-release valves at high points.** Whenever a water line has a high point (a peak followed by a drop), air gets trapped at the peak and creates an airlock that stops water flow. A small automatic air-release valve (NPR 500–1,000 each) at each high point in the system lets the trapped air escape. Cheap, invisible, essential. Plumbers often skip these; check the drawings.

**Access panels at every concealed valve.** A valve you cannot reach is a valve that fails at 2 AM on a Sunday. Every valve embedded in a wall or floor should have an access panel nearby. This is a design detail, so raise it with the architect before the plumbing goes in.

## Part 5. Solar water heater — cheap, reliable, a perfect fit for Kathmandu

A solar water heater is the single highest-return renewable-energy investment for a Kathmandu residence. The reason is simple: **Kathmandu receives six or more hours of sunshine per day from October through May**, which is exactly the period when hot water is most wanted. Summer sunshine (when you need less hot water because the tap water is already warm) is almost equally abundant. The only weak period is mid-monsoon, and even then partial sun is often available.

### How it works, in one paragraph

A solar water heater sits on the roof. It has two parts: a collector (either flat-plate panels or vacuum-tube assemblies) that absorbs sunlight, and an insulated storage tank behind it. Cold water from the overhead tank flows through the collector, gets heated by the sun, and rises by natural convection into the storage tank. Hot water flows from the storage tank to the bathroom taps on demand. No pumps required (the thermosiphon effect drives the flow), no electricity required, no moving parts.

### Sizing

For a family of five, a **200 to 300 litre solar water heater** is correctly sized. A 200-litre heater provides enough hot water for five people to shower in the morning, with some left over for kitchen and laundry use. A 300-litre heater gives a comfortable buffer.

### Nepali market, 2026 prices

The Nepali market has matured significantly in the last decade. Multiple brands are available at multiple price points. Current retail pricing from Kathmandu vendors:

- **200-litre vacuum tube heater**, mid-quality: NPR 32,000–40,000 (brands: Sunrise, Thermosun, Blazesun).
- **250-litre vacuum tube heater**, mid-quality: NPR 40,000–55,000 (CG, Apollo, Osmo, Sunshine).
- **300-litre vacuum tube heater**, mid-to-premium: NPR 47,000–75,000 (CG, Apollo, Osmo, Sunshine, Ultra Sun).
- **300-litre with SS304 tank and copper-flat-plate collector**, premium: NPR 90,000–135,000 (Osmo, CG premium lines).

Installation is typically NPR 3,000–8,000 extra, and free or discounted within Kathmandu Valley for many vendors.

### Specification for your house

For your six-aana house, a **250-litre vacuum-tube solar water heater with SS304 tank**, budget around NPR 50,000–65,000 installed, connected to feed the two bathrooms and the kitchen sink. Include an **electric backup element** (built into most modern units) that kicks in on cloudy winter days when the tank temperature drops below a set point — typically a 2 kW element on a separate switch so you can control when it runs.

Payback maths: a 250-litre solar water heater displaces roughly 300–500 units of electricity per year (for the electric geyser it replaces). At NPR 8–10 per unit average tariff, that is NPR 3,000–5,000 a year saved. Payback period: 10–15 years. And the unit typically lasts 20–25 years with basic maintenance.

This is not a speculative investment. This is free hot water for the bulk of the house's life, paid for in the first decade.

## Part 6. Rainwater harvesting — the forgotten bargain

While we are on the roof, talk about rainwater. The Kathmandu Valley receives about 1,300 mm of rainfall annually, the vast majority of which falls in the four monsoon months (June through September). On a 1,280 sq ft roof footprint (119 m²), that 1,300 mm of rainfall adds up to about **155,000 litres of water per year** that simply falls on your roof.

If you do nothing, every drop of that water runs off into the street storm drain, which in most of Kathmandu discharges into the river system. You pay for municipal water and tanker water during the dry months, while an abundance of potable water runs out of your gutters during the wet months.

Rainwater harvesting is the retention of this water. There are two levels of sophistication:

**Level 1: First-flush diversion and storage only.** The roof is fitted with gutters that channel rainwater into downspouts. The first few seconds of each rain (which washes accumulated dust off the roof) is diverted and discarded. The remaining rainwater is stored in a dedicated tank, either above ground or below, and used for **non-potable purposes** — toilet flushing, garden irrigation, washing the vehicle, cleaning the tea shop floor. A 5,000-litre rainwater tank costs NPR 50,000–80,000 installed and significantly reduces municipal water demand during the monsoon months.

**Level 2: Full treatment for potable use.** After first-flush diversion, the rainwater passes through a sediment filter, a UV sterilizer, and sometimes a membrane filter before being stored for drinking use. Adds NPR 80,000–150,000 to Level 1, and requires ongoing filter maintenance. For most Kathmandu families, Level 1 is sufficient — use municipal/tanker water for drinking and cooking, use rainwater for everything else.

### Groundwater recharge

If you have space in your plot for a recharge pit (a 2 m × 2 m × 2 m gravel-filled pit under a grille), excess rainwater after the storage tank is full can be diverted to the pit, where it percolates slowly back into the groundwater table. This is both a personal benefit (the Kathmandu water table has dropped significantly in the last few decades, affecting boring wells) and a civic good. NPR 25,000–50,000 to build. The Lalitpur and Kathmandu municipalities have occasionally offered small subsidies for groundwater recharge; check with your ward office.

For your six-aana plot, **a 3,000 to 5,000 litre rainwater tank plus a small recharge pit** is a realistic addition. Total additional cost: NPR 80,000–130,000. Annual water-cost savings: NPR 15,000–30,000 (reduced tanker deliveries alone). Payback: 3–8 years. And during a protracted dry-season crisis, you have the buffer.

## Part 7. Electrical load calculation — the foundation of the whole electrical system

Now we switch from water to power. The single most important number for your electrical system is the **total household load** — the maximum amount of power the house needs to draw from the grid when everything is running at once. This number decides the size of your main meter, the cross-section of your main cable, the capacity of your distribution board, and the rating of your circuit breakers.

Most Kathmandu houses are wildly over- or under-sized on this, because nobody does the arithmetic. Let us do it now.

### The method

List every electrical appliance and fixture in the house, estimate each one's power draw in watts, and then estimate what fraction of them run simultaneously in peak use. The simultaneous-use fraction is called the **diversity factor**, and for residential it is typically 0.5 to 0.7 (you rarely have *everything* on at once).

### A practical list for your house

| Load | Wattage | Quantity | Total watts |
|------|---------|----------|-------------|
| LED lights (10W each) | 10 | 35 | 350 |
| Ceiling fans (75W each) | 75 | 6 | 450 |
| Refrigerator (running) | 150 | 1 | 150 |
| Water pump (submersible 1 HP) | 750 | 1 | 750 |
| Washing machine (running) | 500 | 1 | 500 |
| Electric kettle / rice cooker | 1500 | 1 | 1500 |
| Microwave (heating) | 1200 | 1 | 1200 |
| Mixer-grinder (blending) | 750 | 1 | 750 |
| Television | 100 | 1 | 100 |
| Laptops / phone chargers | 50 | 6 | 300 |
| Router, modem | 30 | 1 | 30 |
| Geyser backup (2 kW) | 2000 | 1 | 2000 |
| AC master bedroom (1.5 ton, running) | 1500 | 1 | 1500 |
| Iron | 1000 | 1 | 1000 |
| Tea shop lights + fan + kettle | 400 | 1 | 400 |
| Tea shop refrigerator/display cooler | 200 | 1 | 200 |
| **Total connected load** | | | **~11,200 W** |

That is the **connected load** — the total if everything ran at once. Apply a diversity factor of 0.5 to get the **typical peak demand:** about 5,600 W or 5.6 kW.

### Meter sizing

NEA residential meters come in standard sizes: 5 ampere, 15 ampere, 30 ampere, 60 ampere (at 230 V single-phase), and three-phase options above that. A 15 ampere meter supports about 3.5 kW sustained load; 30 ampere supports about 7 kW; 60 ampere supports about 14 kW.

For your house, with a calculated peak demand of 5.6 kW, **a 30 ampere single-phase meter is the right size for the residence**, with headroom for future expansion (an extra AC, an electric vehicle charger in five years). The tea shop gets its own **15 ampere commercial meter** separately.

Do not undersize. The cost difference between a 15 A and a 30 A meter is small (service charges differ by NPR 200–300 per month). The cost of upgrading a meter later — trenching a new cable, re-applying to NEA, replacing the main breaker and panel — is a weekend of disruption and NPR 30,000–50,000.

### NEA tariff slabs — what you will actually pay

Current NEA domestic tariff slabs (subject to periodic revision; these are the working values as of early 2026):

- First 20 units/month: NPR 4.00 per unit
- 21 to 250 units/month: NPR 7.30 per unit
- 251+ units/month: NPR 9.90 per unit

A family of five in Kathmandu typically consumes 150–300 units per month, depending on how much hot water is used, how many fans run, and whether an AC runs in summer. At typical consumption, the monthly bill is NPR 1,500–2,500. An electric geyser in heavy winter use can add NPR 800–1,500 per month on top (which is exactly why the solar water heater pays back).

The commercial tariff for the tea shop runs higher — around NPR 9–11 per unit — which is exactly why separating the meters matters. Commercial and domestic energy cannot legally be billed on one meter once you are earning money from the space.

## Part 8. The two-meter imperative — domestic and commercial

This point is worth its own section, because the Part 1 advice about separate meters is the single most common thing that first-time tea-shop-owners get wrong.

**On day one of the new house, install two separate NEA meters.** One residential, one commercial.

Why this matters:

**1. Legal compliance.** NEA's tariff structure assumes that a meter supplies a specific class of consumer — domestic, commercial, industrial. Running commercial activity on a domestic meter is technically a tariff violation. When the tea shop earns enough to need VAT registration (annual turnover above NPR 50 lakh as of current rules), the Inland Revenue Department can ask why a commercial activity is powered off a domestic meter; answering that question retroactively is painful.

**2. Accounting simplicity.** If the tea shop and the residence share a meter, how do you figure out what the shop's monthly electricity cost is, for tax purposes or just to know whether the shop is profitable? You cannot, except by informal guesswork. Separate meters give you a clean monthly number for each.

**3. Independent control.** The shop and the residence have different needs. Maybe the shop wants to run a display freezer 24/7; maybe the residence wants to trip the main breaker during a festival trip. Separate meters mean separate control.

**4. Marginally, retroactive cost of separation is large.** Installing two meters on day one costs NPR 15,000–25,000 extra (one more meter application, one more set of service cables, one more main breaker). Adding a second meter to an existing house later costs NPR 40,000–80,000 — requires trenching, panel reworking, and often a new naksa pass amendment.

The on-day-one path is cheaper, simpler, and avoids a legal liability later. Take it.

## Part 9. The distribution board, circuits, and earthing

Once power enters the house through the meter, it flows to a **distribution board** (DB) — the panel that houses the main circuit breaker and the individual branch-circuit breakers. For your three-storey house, the right layout is:

**Main DB on the ground floor**, near the meter. Contains the main 30 A MCB (miniature circuit breaker) for the whole house, an RCCB (residual-current circuit breaker) for ground-fault protection, and a lightning / surge protection device (SPD). Next to it, a sub-DB for the ground floor and the tea shop side.

**Sub-DB on each upper floor**, with branch breakers serving that floor. Each branch circuit is a physically separate wire running from the sub-DB to a specific room or a specific group of outlets.

### Typical branch-circuit layout

For a three-storey residence with a tea shop:

- **Lighting circuits:** one 6 A MCB per floor, all lights and ceiling-fan fan controls on that floor. Three circuits total.
- **General power outlet circuits:** two 16 A MCBs per floor (one per bedroom group, one for living-room-plus-kitchen-light-duty). Six circuits total.
- **Kitchen high-load circuit:** one 20 A MCB dedicated to the kitchen for the microwave, mixer, toaster, kettle. Separate from general outlets.
- **Air conditioner circuit:** one 16 A MCB per AC, dedicated.
- **Water pump circuit:** one 16 A MCB at the ground floor, running up to the pump.
- **Geyser circuit:** one 16 A MCB per electric geyser, dedicated.
- **Washing machine circuit:** one 16 A MCB dedicated.
- **Tea shop:** a separate full sub-panel off the commercial meter, with its own MCBs for shop lights, fan, kettle, refrigerator.

Total branch circuits: roughly 15–18 for the residence, plus 4–5 for the shop. Each gets its own MCB. Each MCB is labelled clearly. This is the level of circuit separation that a well-designed modern house expects, and it is non-negotiable from a safety perspective.

### Wiring

**Copper, not aluminium.** Aluminium wiring was common in Nepal for decades and is a known fire hazard — aluminium oxidises at joints and develops high-resistance spots that heat up over time. Every NBC-compliant residential installation now uses copper conductors. Budget for this; it is not where you save money.

**Cross-sections:**
- 1.5 mm² copper for lighting circuits (6 A).
- 2.5 mm² copper for general power outlets (16 A).
- 4 mm² copper for dedicated high-load circuits (20 A: kitchen, geyser, AC, pump).
- 10 mm² copper for the main meter-to-DB feed.

**Conduit:** all wiring runs inside PVC conduit embedded in the walls. Not buried directly in plaster. Conduit allows wires to be replaced later without tearing up the wall.

### Earthing — the safety system most houses get wrong

**Earthing** is the system that takes fault currents (when a live wire touches the chassis of an appliance, for example) and directs them safely to ground, tripping the RCCB before anyone gets electrocuted. Every outlet should have a three-wire connection (live, neutral, earth), and the earth wires should all terminate at a proper **earth pit** — a buried copper electrode in moist soil, with a bed of conductive material (salt, charcoal) around it.

In practice, many Kathmandu houses have inadequate earthing: no earth pit at all, or an earth pit that was installed once in dry soil and no longer conducts. Check:

1. **Earth pit design.** Either a copper plate (600 × 600 × 3 mm) buried 3 metres deep in a pit filled with alternating layers of charcoal and salt, or a copper-clad steel rod driven 3 m into the ground with appropriate backfill. The pit should be located in soil that stays moist (not under a paved driveway).
2. **Earth resistance below 5 ohms.** An electrician with an earth resistance tester can measure this. If resistance is above 5 ohms, the pit is not doing its job.
3. **Continuous earth wire to every outlet.** No skipped grounds, no "saved time" on the third wire.

The cost of a proper earth pit is NPR 8,000–15,000. The cost of not having one is measured in lives.

### Surge protection

Kathmandu's power grid sees frequent voltage spikes from lightning, line faults, and switching operations. A single major spike can fry every electronic appliance in the house — TV, laptop, refrigerator inverter, washing-machine controller. **A surge-protection device (SPD) at the main distribution board** clamps these spikes before they reach your appliances. Costs NPR 3,000–8,000 installed. Pays for itself the first time a big thunderstorm comes through.

## Part 10. Backup power — inverter and battery

Kathmandu's grid has improved dramatically since the load-shedding years (2010–2016), when 16-hour daily blackouts were normal. As of 2026 the Valley has essentially 24-hour supply most of the time, with occasional short outages during peak demand or storms. But "essentially" is not "always," and for a family with a small business, even a 2-hour outage at the wrong moment is expensive.

### Options

**Option 1: No backup, accept occasional outages.** This is now feasible given the grid's reliability. For a family that can tolerate the rare 2-hour outage, this saves the NPR 80,000–200,000 that backup systems cost. A reasonable choice for many households.

**Option 2: UPS for essential loads only.** A small online UPS (NPR 20,000–40,000) that powers the router, a couple of LED lights, and perhaps one ceiling fan, running from a small battery for 2–4 hours. Preserves the Wi-Fi and keeps the house functional but unambiguous during short outages.

**Option 3: Inverter with battery, whole-house backup.** A 2 kVA to 3 kVA inverter (NPR 30,000–60,000) connected to a 150–200 Ah lead-acid battery bank (NPR 40,000–80,000). Powers most household loads (lights, fans, TV, router, refrigerator) during outages, typically for 4–6 hours. This is what most Kathmandu middle-class homes with any backup at all choose. Batteries need replacing every 3–5 years.

**Option 4: Lithium-iron-phosphate battery backup.** A 48 V, 5 kWh LFP battery pack with a hybrid inverter (NPR 150,000–300,000 all-in). Quieter, longer-lasting (10+ years), and with capacity to add solar PV later. More expensive upfront but substantially cheaper over 15 years. The right choice if you plan to add solar PV in the next few years.

For your house, **Option 3 is the pragmatic baseline** — a 2.5 kVA inverter with 200 Ah battery, total investment NPR 70,000–120,000, providing 4–6 hours of backup for lights, fans, refrigerator, TV, and Wi-Fi. Upgrade to Option 4 if budget allows and solar PV is part of the plan.

## Part 11. Solar PV and net metering in Nepal — the honest 2026 picture

Grid-connected rooftop solar with net metering has existed in Nepal since NEA announced the "Alternative Electricity Connected to Grid 2018" framework, which set the feed-in tariff at NPR 7.30 per unit for three years. In principle, you install solar panels on your roof, your meter runs backward when you produce more than you consume, and at the end of the month you are billed only on the net consumption.

In practice, the policy and administrative environment has been bumpy. Commentary in the Nepali press has described the net-metering regime as stalled in recent years, with NEA not consistently buying back solar electricity from rooftop installations, and a paperwork process that has discouraged many residential applicants. Subsidies are primarily targeted at off-grid systems through the Alternative Energy Promotion Centre (AEPC) rather than at on-grid residential.

### What works in 2026

**Self-consumption without net export.** You install solar panels sized to your *average daytime load* — not bigger. The electricity is consumed immediately inside the house, without being exported to the grid. Any surplus simply dissipates (the inverter tapers off). This sidesteps the net-metering paperwork entirely and is the most reliable approach for a residential owner in 2026.

**Self-consumption with battery storage.** Solar panels charge a battery during the day; the battery powers the house at night. No grid export, no net-metering dependency. More expensive upfront (batteries add NPR 100,000–200,000 to the system) but gives full independence during outages and maximises the solar utility.

**Grid-connected with net metering.** Technically available, practically bumpy. If you are motivated and patient, it is worth doing for the long term, but budget 3–9 month payback periods and persistent paperwork.

### A realistic residential solar specification

For your six-aana house:

- **3 kW of PV panels** on the roof — roughly 6–8 panels of 375–450 W each, occupying about 200 square feet of roof area. At current prices, NPR 150,000–220,000 for panels alone.
- **A 3 kW hybrid inverter** that can run both grid-tied and off-grid modes. NPR 60,000–120,000.
- **Optional: 5 kWh LFP battery** for night-time use. NPR 150,000–250,000.
- **Mounting, wiring, protection equipment, installation:** NPR 50,000–80,000.

**Total for a 3 kW grid-tied PV system without battery:** NPR 260,000–420,000, or about NPR 3–4 lakh.
**With a 5 kWh battery:** NPR 4.5–6.5 lakh.

Energy produced: about 4,000–4,500 kWh per year in Kathmandu's solar regime. At NPR 8 average tariff, that is NPR 32,000–36,000 per year of avoided electricity. Simple payback (PV only, no battery): 9–13 years. With battery, 13–18 years. Both are workable over a 25-year panel lifetime, but neither is a screaming financial deal at current tariffs. The non-financial benefits — partial independence from the grid, reduced environmental impact, daytime power even during outages — matter separately.

### Recommendation

**For 2026 construction, wire the house for future solar.** Install a rooftop conduit for DC cables from the roof to the main distribution board. Reserve space in the DB for a solar disconnect. Make the roof structurally capable of supporting panels (check with your structural engineer at the design stage; it is trivially included in the load calculation during construction).

**Install the actual PV system in year two or three**, after you have lived in the house, measured your real electricity consumption, and assessed whether NEA's net-metering regime has stabilised. The marginal cost of future-proofing is NPR 10,000–20,000 in wiring provisions. The cost of retrofitting a solar-ready infrastructure onto a finished house is NPR 50,000–100,000.

A solar water heater (covered in Part 5) remains strongly recommended for day-one installation — it has better economics than PV and none of the net-metering complexity.

## Part 12. Plumbing — drainage, waste, and sanitation

We have covered the supply side of water. Now the disposal side.

Every sink, every shower, every washing-machine outlet, every toilet produces wastewater, and all of it has to go somewhere. The direction of flow is down and out — through drainpipes, into a main waste stack, and from the main stack into either a sewer or a septic tank.

### The stack system

A **stack** is a vertical pipe that carries waste from upper floors down to ground level. Your three-storey house will have typically one or two main stacks: one for "grey water" (sinks, showers, washing machines) and one for "black water" (toilets), or a combined stack for both. Current practice in Nepal mostly uses combined stacks with each appliance's trap preventing sewer gas from backing up.

Key specifications for a modern stack:

- **100 mm (4-inch) PVC or UPVC** for combined stacks carrying toilet waste.
- **75 mm (3-inch)** for grey-water-only stacks.
- **Vent pipe extended above the roof** to relieve pressure and prevent the traps in the fixtures from being sucked dry. Without this vent, you get gurgling drains and smells.
- **Cleanout access at ground level** and at each floor level, so blockages can be cleared from outside the stack.

### Septic tank vs. sewer connection

This decision is made by geography, not by preference. Your municipality has a sewer network in some wards and not others. Find out which you are in before you finalise the plumbing layout.

**If your ward has a sewer line:** you connect the stack via a drain pipe out of the house to the sewer lateral, which connects to the main sewer in the street. Budget NPR 30,000–60,000 for the sewer connection, including the connection fee and the ward-office paperwork.

**If your ward does not have a sewer:** you need a **septic tank** on your own property. A septic tank is an underground concrete chamber (typically 2–3 cubic metres for a family-of-five residence), divided into two or three compartments. Waste water enters the first compartment; solids settle, anaerobic bacteria slowly digest them, and clarified water flows to the second compartment, from which it either percolates into a soak-pit below, or is pumped to a sewer further away if one exists nearby.

A properly-sized residential septic tank is about 2–3 cubic metres of internal volume — roughly 2 m × 1.5 m × 1 m. Budget NPR 80,000–150,000 for a septic tank plus soak-pit, built in RCC, with proper access for pumping (a *nali* truck has to come in every 3–5 years to pump out the settled solids).

**Check your ward situation at the naksa pass stage.** It changes the plot layout — if you need a septic tank, you need a piece of the plot that is accessible to a nali truck, usually adjacent to the driveway or a side passage.

### Kitchen and bathroom fittings — a brief overview

We will go deeper into interior finishing in Part 6 tomorrow, but the quick sanity check on plumbing fittings:

- **Kitchen sink:** stainless steel, single or double bowl, 200–300 mm deep. Budget NPR 6,000–15,000 for the sink, NPR 3,000–8,000 for the tap.
- **Bathroom basins:** ceramic wall-mounted or counter-top. Budget NPR 3,500–12,000 per basin.
- **Bathroom taps:** chrome-plated brass interior. Avoid all-plastic taps (fail in 2–5 years). Budget NPR 2,000–6,000 per tap.
- **Shower mixers:** good brass mixer-with-diverter for NPR 5,000–15,000. Cheap ones fail fast.
- **Toilets:** floor-mounted Western-style for family bathrooms, with dual-flush cistern. Budget NPR 8,000–20,000 per toilet. Consider a squatting pan in one of the "half" bathrooms if the family still prefers it — it is culturally common, hygienic, and uses less water per flush.

Indian brands (Hindware, Cera, Parryware, Jaquar) and European-origin lines (Kohler) are all readily available in Kathmandu. Go mid-range on fittings; the premium over budget brands buys 10–15 years of extra life for 20–30% more money, which is an easy call.

## Part 13. The commissioning checklist

Before the walls are plastered and the floors are tiled, the plumbing and electrical systems need to be tested. Standard commissioning tests:

**Plumbing pressure test.** Cap off all fixture openings. Fill the entire supply system with water to 8–10 bar (about 115–145 psi). Hold for 2 hours. No pressure drop means no leaks. If pressure drops, walk the system and locate the leak. **Do this before the walls are plastered** — a leak discovered after plastering costs five times what it costs now to fix.

**Drain test.** Cap off all fixture connections. Fill every drain pipe with water to the roof vent. Hold for 30 minutes. Watch for leaks at every joint. Fix and retest.

**Earth resistance test.** Measure the resistance from every earth point in the house to the earth pit. Should be below 5 ohms everywhere. If not, rework the earthing.

**Continuity test of every circuit.** An electrician with a multimeter checks every outlet, switch, and light fixture. Every one must light up, every switch must operate, every outlet must have correct live/neutral/earth polarity.

**Insulation resistance test.** A megger test on the main cable and each branch circuit. Above 1 megaohm is acceptable; below, there is a fault somewhere.

**RCCB trip test.** A button on the RCCB simulates a fault current; the breaker should trip instantly. If it does not, the RCCB is faulty and must be replaced.

**Load test.** Turn on every appliance simultaneously and run for 30 minutes. Any hot points, smell, flickering? Fix.

These tests together take about two to three days at the end of the plumbing/electrical work, cost NPR 5,000–15,000 of testing equipment rental, and find essentially every defect before the walls are finished. Do them.

## Resources and further reading

For the Kathmandu-specific resources:

- **NEA tariff schedule** — published on nea.org.np and updated periodically. Get current numbers before committing to a meter size.
- **AEPC Grid-Connected Solar PV Guidelines** — aepc.gov.np has current PDFs on the subsidy and net-metering framework.
- **KUKL municipal water supply schedules** — ask your ward office which days and hours your area receives municipal water. Essential for sizing your underground tank.
- **Panchakanya Group** — the local manufacturer for PPR piping, electrical cables, and UPVC. Widely available, reliably good quality.
- **Hilltake** — the Kathmandu-based water tank manufacturer. 77-district distribution and long warranty on their HDPE tanks.
- **Nepal Electricity Authority website** — current tariffs, application forms, and consumer-service portal at nea.org.np.

For deeper technical reading:

- **NBC 112 "Timber"** and **NBC 114 "Construction Safety"** for wiring and plumbing guidance in the Nepali context.
- **Indian standard IS 732** for residential electrical installation; used as a de facto reference in Nepal.
- **Indian standard IS 1172** for water supply requirements in buildings.
- **IS 15700** for septic tank design.

For an international reader: the two-tank water system we described is not unique to Kathmandu — it is standard in most of South Asia and in parts of Africa. The electrical load calculation method is universal; the specific tariff numbers and earthing standards change by country.

## Part 16. The short version, in one paragraph

On the six-aana house we have been designing, the water system is the classic Kathmandu two-tank architecture — a 7,500–10,000-litre underground tank integrated into the foundation (covered in Part 3), a 1 HP submersible pump with automatic float-switch control (NPR 30,000 installed), a 2,500-litre HDPE triple-layer overhead tank on the roof (NPR 45,000), gravity-fed PPR piping throughout (NPR 80,000 in parts and labour), a 250-litre vacuum-tube solar water heater (NPR 60,000, paying back in 10–15 years), and a 3,000–5,000-litre rainwater harvesting tank with a groundwater recharge pit (NPR 80,000–130,000); the electrical system uses two NEA meters from day one — a 30 A domestic meter and a 15 A commercial for the tea shop — feeding a layered distribution board with per-floor sub-DBs, 15–18 branch circuits with dedicated MCBs for high-load appliances, copper wiring sized to NEC/IS732 specification (1.5/2.5/4 mm²), a proper copper-plate earth pit below 5 Ω resistance, a main surge protection device, and a 2.5 kVA inverter with 200 Ah battery for backup (NPR 90,000); solar PV is wired-for but typically not installed at construction time in 2026 given the bumpy net-metering regime; plumbing is 100 mm uPVC stacks with roof venting and ground-level cleanouts, connecting to either a municipal sewer (NPR 30,000–60,000) or a 2–3 cubic-metre RCC septic tank with soak-pit (NPR 80,000–150,000) depending on your ward's infrastructure; and every system is commissioned with pressure testing, continuity testing, earth resistance testing, and RCCB trip testing before the walls are plastered — the single most important phase for catching defects cheaply. All together, water plus electrical plus sanitary fittings runs about NPR 13 lakh, well within the Part 1 allocation of NPR 27 lakh across these categories.
