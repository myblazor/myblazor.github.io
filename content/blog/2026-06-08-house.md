## A Thursday afternoon in Bhaktapur

Imagine it is a Thursday afternoon in June. You are standing on six aana of land in the Bhaktapur side of the Kathmandu Valley. The monsoon clouds are gathering over the hills to the north, and the air has that particular Kathmandu smell — damp earth, a little bit of wood smoke from somebody's kitchen, the faintest hint of jasmine from a neighbour's wall.

You own this land. The lalpurja is in order. You have saved for years. You are now, finally, ready to build a house.

And you realise, quietly, that you have no idea where to begin.

That is the position most first-time builders find themselves in, and it is the position this seven-part series is written for. Over the next seven days, we are going to walk — patiently, in plain English, without jargon and without any programming or code — through the full arc of building a modest, earthquake-safe, comfortable family home. The land is six aana. The budget ceiling is hard: **two crore Nepali rupees, not a paisa more.** The brief is modest and practical: a small tea shop on the ground floor that can earn a little income, two bedrooms, a kitchen, a living room, and two-and-a-half bathrooms laid out the way most Nepali families actually use them — two for bathing, three for toileting, which on paper reads as "two-and-a-half" because one room does both jobs.

There are seven parts. This is Part 1. If you are reading this on June 8th, 2026, you are exactly on time. If you are reading it later, that is also fine — the series is evergreen, and each part links to the next.

Today we will cover the work that happens *before* the first brick is laid, and there is far more of that work than most first-time builders realise. Get Part 1 right and the next six parts mostly execute themselves. Get Part 1 wrong and you will be paying for that mistake for as long as the house stands.

## Part 1. What a house actually is

Before we talk about setbacks and permits and budgets, it is worth spending a quiet minute on a question that sounds silly but is not: **what is a house, actually?**

A house is a box. The box keeps rain out, keeps heat in during winter and out during summer, holds up under its own weight, and does not fall down when the earth shakes. Everything else — the bathroom tiles, the kitchen granite, the colour of the paint, the height of the gate — is decoration. Important decoration, but decoration. The box is the thing.

This matters because of how Nepali families (and honestly, families everywhere) tend to spend their construction money. We walk into the showroom. We see the glossy granite and the imported ceiling fan and the Italian bathroom fittings. The box — the concrete frame, the rebar, the roof, the wall insulation, the weather stripping around the windows — is invisible by the time the house is done. It is under the plaster. So it feels like the place to save money.

This is the single most expensive mistake you can make. Every shortcut on the box shows up, not as an immediate failure, but as a slow leak in your bank account for the next thirty years. A thin roof slab with poor insulation costs you a fan in June and a heater in January, every year, forever. A wall that was not sealed against air leaks costs you an air conditioner that runs two hours longer every afternoon. A foundation that was cast on the wrong soil gradient costs you cracks in the plaster that you will keep patching and never quite fix.

So the mental model to carry through this whole series is:

> **Spend on the box. Decorate simply.**

Granite can be upgraded in five years. A foundation cannot. A thin roof slab cannot be thickened once the building is up. Windows can be changed, but only at great cost. You pay for the box once, and the box decides the next thirty years of your comfort and running costs. Decoration is the easy part.

Everyone, and I mean everyone, who has built a house in Kathmandu will tell you the same thing if you get them to talk honestly over tea: "I wish I had spent more on the things I cannot see and less on the things I can."

Write that on a sticky note and keep it on the fridge for the next eighteen months.

## Part 2. Understanding what six aana actually gives you

Let us start with the land, because everything else follows from it. "Six aana" is a phrase every Nepali adult knows, but if you stop and ask people to convert it to square feet, most of them will guess and get it wrong. So let us pin it down once and for all.

One aana, in the Kathmandu Valley measurement system, is exactly 342.25 square feet. That is about 31.80 square metres. Six aana is therefore:

- **6 × 342.25 = 2,053.5 square feet**
- **6 × 31.80 = 190.8 square metres**

For reference:

- **One ropani = 16 aana = 5,476 square feet** (about 508.7 square metres).
- **One paisa = one-quarter of an aana = 85.56 square feet.**
- **One daam = one-quarter of a paisa = 21.39 square feet** (the smallest unit you will see on a lalpurja).

Six aana is, in round numbers, about three-eighths of a ropani. It is a common urban plot size in the Valley. It is enough to build a proper three-storey family home on, but it is not generous. You will feel the constraint every time you think about parking, garden, or setbacks, which we will discuss next.

Here is a picture that might help. If your six-aana plot were a perfect rectangle twice as long as it is wide — which is a common shape — it would be roughly 32 feet wide by 64 feet deep. (Any rectangle whose area is 2,053 square feet and whose sides are in 1:2 ratio would be about 32 by 64; the exact numbers vary with the real shape of your plot.) That is about ten metres wide and twenty metres deep. A bedroom in a well-designed Nepali home is typically about twelve feet by eleven feet, so the whole plot is only about two-and-a-half bedroom-widths across. That is tight. It is workable, but tight.

This is why, in almost every Kathmandu house on six aana or less, the building goes *up* rather than *out*. You cannot fit everything on one floor. You cannot really fit everything on two. Three floors is the honest answer, with each floor doing a different job.

We will lay out the floor plan in detail in Part 2, tomorrow. For today, just carry this mental picture: a narrow-ish, three-storey building, tucked inside a plot that feels about the size of a generous classroom.

## Part 3. Setbacks, coverage, and what you are actually allowed to build

Here is the hard part that sneaks up on first-time builders. **You do not own the right to build on every square inch of your land.** You own the land, yes. But the municipality decides how much of it you are allowed to cover with a building, how close you can get to your neighbour's wall, and how high you can go. These rules are called **building bylaws** in English and **bhawan bidhi** or **nirman bidhi** in Nepali.

After the 2015 earthquake, the Nepal Government issued a set of fundamental construction bylaws — the *Fundamental Construction Bylaws on Settlement Development, Urban Planning and Building Construction 2015* — that every municipality in the country has to follow as a minimum. Individual municipalities (KMC, Lalitpur, Bhaktapur, Madhyapur Thimi, Kirtipur, Suryabinayak, Changunarayan, and so on) can add stricter local rules on top, and most have. The Bhaktapur side of the Valley is governed by Bhaktapur Municipality, Madhyapur Thimi Municipality, Suryabinayak Municipality, or Changunarayan Municipality depending on exactly where your plot is, so the first practical thing to do after reading this article is to find out which ward your land is in and which municipality that ward belongs to.

Three terms will come up over and over again. Learn them today.

**Setback.** This is the minimum distance your building has to be kept back from the edge of your plot on each side. If the setback is 1.5 metres on the front, and your plot is 20 metres deep, then the first 1.5 metres of depth is a no-build zone. The same applies to the back, the left, and the right. The purpose is partly to stop buildings from leaning on each other (which caused a large share of the 2015 earthquake collapses, when row houses pulled each other down), partly to let light and air reach the ground, and partly to leave room for drainage and fire access. In the post-2015 rules, non-public buildings up to seventeen metres tall have to leave a minimum setback of two metres, and taller buildings have to leave at least five metres. Individual municipalities often require more than the national minimum, especially on plots facing narrow roads.

**Ground coverage.** This is the percentage of your total plot area that the footprint of your building is allowed to occupy. If the coverage is 70%, and your plot is 2,053 square feet, then your footprint can be no more than 0.7 × 2,053 = 1,437 square feet. The rest has to be open space — courtyard, driveway, garden, or simply air.

**Floor Area Ratio (FAR).** This is the total built-up area (ground floor + first floor + second floor + everything else) divided by the plot area. If the FAR is 2.5 on a 2,053 square foot plot, you can build a total of 2,053 × 2.5 = 5,132 square feet across all floors combined. FAR is the single most important number for deciding how tall and how dense a house can be on your particular piece of land, and it varies by municipality and by the width of the road your plot faces.

These three numbers — setback, coverage, FAR — together decide the shape of the envelope your house has to fit inside. They are not negotiable. The municipality will not approve your drawings if you exceed them, and if you somehow build beyond them without approval, the penalties range from fines to partial demolition orders.

### A concrete example for six aana

Let us do the arithmetic for a plausible scenario. You have six aana in, say, Suryabinayak Municipality, facing a road that is six metres wide. Suppose the local bylaw requires (and please confirm these exact numbers for your plot with your own ward office — I am using them as a realistic illustration, not as authoritative numbers for your specific site):

- Front setback: 1.5 metres
- Back setback: 1.5 metres
- Side setbacks: 1.5 metres each
- Ground coverage: 70%
- FAR: 2.5

Your plot is about 2,053 square feet, or about 10 × 20 metres if we assume a 1:2 rectangle.

After deducting 1.5 metres on each side, your usable footprint shrinks to 7 metres wide × 17 metres deep = 119 square metres, or about 1,281 square feet.

Ground coverage limit: 70% of 190.8 m² = 133.6 m², about 1,438 sq ft. Your setback-limited footprint (1,281 sq ft) is already tighter than the coverage limit, so **setback is the binding constraint**, not coverage. This is the typical situation on small urban plots: setbacks eat more than the coverage cap would.

FAR limit: 2.5 × 2,053 = 5,132 sq ft total built-up. If each floor is 1,281 sq ft, four full floors would be 5,124 sq ft — right at the FAR limit. But post-2015 rules discourage row-house-style tall, thin residences on small plots. A three-storey building with a partial terrace, or three full floors plus a small terrace room (called *tahakha* or *penthouse* locally), is the common and safer interpretation.

So for your six aana, the realistic envelope is roughly:

- Footprint: about 1,280 square feet
- Three full floors, built-up total about 3,840 square feet
- Optional small terrace room on top, perhaps 400–500 square feet
- Surrounding open space of about 770 square feet, enough for a small courtyard and a narrow driveway

That is, by modest Nepali standards, a comfortable house. Not a bungalow. But comfortable.

**Important caveat:** The numbers above are illustrative. Every municipality in the Valley has its own bylaw, and within each municipality the rules differ by zone (residential, mixed-use, commercial, heritage, agricultural-turned-residential, and so on) and by the width of the road your plot faces. The only way to know the exact numbers for your plot is to either:

1. Visit your ward office and ask to see the current bylaw document, or
2. Hire a registered architect (we will talk about this in Part 2), who will know or will look up the exact numbers as the first step of their work.

Do not try to guess, and do not trust the numbers quoted by your contractor in conversation. Get the written bylaw, or the architect's written interpretation of it. Keep a printed copy in the file.

## Part 4. The tea shop complication

You have told me the plan includes a tea shop on the ground floor. This is a smart move. A small tea shop is an income hedge — a few thousand rupees a day of revenue that insulates the household budget from bad months. It also makes the ground floor of the house useful every day of the year, which is often not the case in pure residential Nepali homes where the ground floor becomes a storeroom for old bicycles and lapsi drums.

But the tea shop turns your building into a **mixed-use building** in the eyes of the municipality, and that changes some of the rules.

First, you need to check whether your plot is in a zone that allows mixed residential-commercial use. Most urban wards in the Valley do; some heritage-protected zones near, for example, Bhaktapur Durbar Square or Changu Narayan, do not. Again, ward office, written confirmation, keep a copy.

Second, the ground floor will need a few commercial-building features that a pure residential ground floor does not:

- **Higher ceiling height.** Residential ceilings are typically 9 to 10 feet. A tea shop feels oppressive at 9 feet. Aim for 10 to 11 feet on the ground floor. This adds a bit to the structural cost (slightly longer columns, a little more concrete) but the comfort difference is enormous.
- **A separate electrical meter.** This is non-negotiable. Do it on day one. The Nepal Electricity Authority will bill commercial power at a different rate than domestic power, and mixing the two on a single meter creates both billing headaches and, eventually, legal complications when the tea shop earns enough to need a VAT registration. Separate meter, separate circuit breaker panel, separate earthing. The marginal cost at construction time is tiny. The marginal cost of separating them later is large.
- **A rolling shutter.** You have listed this. Good. Budget NPR 1.5 to 2.5 lakh for a decent motorised or hand-operated shutter, plus the rolled-steel lintel above it.
- **A shop-side toilet.** Customers will ask. Even if you plan to refuse, having a basic half-bath with a separate external door near the shop makes the shop far more usable. This is the "half" in "2.5 bathrooms" in your plan.
- **Street frontage.** The shop door should open onto the street side of the plot. The residential entrance should ideally open to a different face (the side or the back, via a small courtyard) so that family traffic and shop traffic do not mix.

Third, the mixed-use classification may affect your naksa (drawing) fees and your permit timeline. Commercial and mixed-use permits go through slightly more scrutiny than pure residential ones, and the fee is a little higher. Budget for this in Part 2.

The tea shop is a good plan. Just know going in that it adds a few decisions to the pre-construction checklist.

## Part 5. Naksa pass in plain English

*Naksa* means "drawing." *Naksa pass* means "drawing-approval" — the process by which the municipality reviews your architect's drawings and gives you the written permission to build. Without a naksa pass certificate, any construction you do is illegal, and the municipality can order it demolished at your cost. It happens. Do not be that household.

The process has roughly six steps. The exact order and names vary slightly between municipalities, but the substance is the same everywhere in the Valley.

**Step 1: Apply for pre-approval.** You visit the ward office with your lalpurja, a citizenship certificate, and a sketch of what you want to build. They confirm the zoning, the setbacks, the FAR, and the coverage that apply to your specific plot. They give you a written pre-approval slip. Cost: a few hundred rupees. Time: one to three visits. Do not skip this step — it is where you confirm the exact numbers we estimated in Part 3.

**Step 2: Hire a registered architect and structural engineer.** The architect produces the architectural drawings — the floor plans, elevations, sections. The structural engineer produces the structural drawings — the column-and-beam layout, the foundation design, the reinforcement schedule, the seismic calculations. **Both must be registered with the Nepal Engineers' Council (NEC).** The municipality will reject drawings signed by an unregistered professional, and hiring an unregistered person to save money is a false economy because you cannot actually use what they produce. More on finding the right professional in Part 2.

**Step 3: Submit the drawings and supporting documents to the municipality.** The full submission package typically includes: the architectural drawings (plans, elevations, sections, roof plan), the structural drawings and calculations, a site plan showing setbacks from the property boundary and distance from neighbouring buildings, a soil test report (discussed in Part 3), a copy of the lalpurja, copies of the owners' citizenship certificates, tax clearance, and the application form with the required fee. The municipality stamps and dates the submission.

**Step 4: Technical review.** A municipal engineer reviews the drawings for compliance with the bylaws and with the Nepal National Building Code. If there are issues, they send back comments and your architect revises. One or two rounds of revision is normal. Three or more is a sign that either your architect does not know the rules, or you have asked for something the rules do not allow, or the reviewing engineer is looking for a small gift, which — to state this plainly — is a thing that happens, and you do not have to participate. If you keep everything in writing and the submissions are clean, the process eventually goes through.

**Step 5: Fee payment and certificate issuance.** Once approved, you pay the municipal fee and receive a naksa pass certificate. The fee in Kathmandu Valley municipalities typically runs into the tens of thousands of rupees for a three-storey mixed-use house, with the exact amount depending on the built-up area. Budget NPR 50,000 to NPR 150,000 for the full stack of fees (pre-approval, naksa pass, utility connections, and the post-construction completion certificate). Keep the certificate. Laminate it. Do not lose it.

**Step 6: Site inspections during construction.** The municipality may send an engineer to inspect the foundation, the columns at plinth level, and the completion. Some municipalities are strict about this, some are lax, but strict is better for you — an inspector who catches a mistake at plinth level saves you a crisis at the fourth floor.

From pre-approval to certificate, a well-prepared naksa pass takes two to four months. A poorly prepared one can take six months to a year. The difference is almost entirely down to the quality of the architect and structural engineer you hired in step 2. Pay for good professionals. They pay for themselves in the naksa pass timeline alone, before we even talk about what they save you during construction.

## Part 6. The seismic code, and why it is not optional

Nepal sits on the collision zone between the Indian and Eurasian tectonic plates. The two plates grind into each other at a few centimetres a year, and every few decades the accumulated stress releases as an earthquake. The 1934 Bihar-Nepal earthquake destroyed much of Kathmandu. The 2015 Gorkha earthquake — almost a century later — killed nearly 9,000 people and damaged or destroyed more than 700,000 buildings. The historical pattern suggests another large earthquake is not a possibility but a certainty, on a timescale we cannot predict but that is within the lifespan of the house you are about to build.

This is why the **Nepal National Building Code (NBC)** exists, and why compliance is legally mandatory for all new construction.

The key document for residential buildings is **NBC 105, "Seismic Design of Buildings in Nepal."** The history in one paragraph: the original version was NBC 105:1994, which was the rulebook when most of the pre-2015 housing stock in the Valley was built. After the 2015 earthquake, the government began a major revision, which was published as NBC 105:2020. Practitioner feedback and lessons from smaller post-2020 earthquakes drove a second revision. The Nepal National Building Code NBC 105:2025 is the officially released Second Revision, updating the 2020 edition with clearer definitions, improved load combinations, updated seismic gap calculations, revised soil classification based on shear wave velocity (VS₃₀), and additions for structural stability, serviceability, and ductile detailing. As of mid-2026, NBC 105:2025 is what your structural engineer must design to.

For a residential building you do not need to know the mathematics. You do need to know what the code requires your structural engineer to do, so that you can tell whether you are being given a serious design or a photocopy.

A compliant three-storey RCC (reinforced cement concrete) residential building in the Kathmandu Valley has all of these features:

- A **clearly defined structural system** — in almost all cases, an RCC moment-resisting frame with masonry infill. This means concrete columns connected to concrete beams, with brick walls *filling in between the columns* rather than *holding the building up*. The columns and beams are the structure; the walls are cladding and partition.
- **Columns on a regular grid.** No random columns, no columns that suddenly disappear between the ground and first floor, no columns that jog sideways. NBC 105 is explicit that irregular structures are more dangerous. If your architect's plan requires dropping or shifting a column to make a room bigger, push back hard, because the house will pay for that compromise in the next earthquake.
- **Ductile detailing** in the reinforcement. This is a technical phrase meaning the rebar inside the concrete is arranged so that, under earthquake loads, the building bends and yields rather than snapping. The specific details involve things like closely spaced stirrups at the ends of columns and beams, correctly shaped hooks in the stirrup bends, and the right lap-length for splicing rebar. Your structural engineer will produce a reinforcement schedule that specifies all of this. You do not need to read it. You do need to make sure the contractor on site is actually following it, which we will cover in Part 3.
- **A proper foundation.** For three-storey construction on Kathmandu Valley soils, this is almost always an isolated-footing foundation or a mat (raft) foundation, designed after a site-specific soil test. No "we built my uncle's house like this so we'll do the same here." Every plot has its own soil, and the foundation must be designed for it.
- **Separation from neighbours.** The 2015 bylaws require buildings to be at least 1.5 metres apart — the so-called *seismic gap* — so that two adjacent buildings swaying under earthquake motion do not hammer into each other. This is a minimum; the NBC 105:2025 seismic gap calculation may require more for taller buildings or soft soils.
- **A registered structural engineer's stamp and signature** on the drawings.

If your contractor offers to build "the usual way, same as the houses around here, no need for engineer" — run. In a country where the 2015 earthquake killed 9,000 people and destroyed hundreds of thousands of buildings, unengineered residential construction is a choice to gamble with lives. A registered structural engineer for a three-storey house costs roughly NPR 50,000 to NPR 150,000 depending on the complexity — a fraction of one percent of your total budget. Pay it.

One more thing about the code. It is possible to comply with the letter of the code and still build a dangerous building, if the construction quality on site is poor. Rebar that was tied with gaps between the stirrups, concrete that was mixed with the wrong water-cement ratio, columns that were cast with concrete that had already started setting, a beam-column joint where the stirrups were skipped because the space was tight — these are the small on-site decisions that decide whether the code on paper becomes a safe building in reality. Part 3 of this series is about how to make sure it does.

## Part 7. The budget framework

Now we come to money, which is where most households either succeed or fail at the house-building project.

Your ceiling is NPR 2 crore, assuming the land is already paid for. Let us break that down into a framework, so that in Parts 2 through 7 we can keep checking whether each decision stays within the envelope.

### Cost per square foot in Kathmandu Valley, 2026

Based on recent market data, the average cost of building a house in Nepal ranges from NPR 3,500 to NPR 5,500 per square foot, with a 1,000 sq. ft. house potentially costing between NPR 35 lakhs to NPR 55 lakhs depending on materials, labor, and location. Kathmandu Valley runs a little higher than the national average — the first floor typically costs around NPR 3,700 per square foot and additional floors around NPR 3,000 per square foot in Kathmandu Valley. Recent 2025 sources converge on three price tiers:

- **Basic finish: NPR 3,000 to NPR 3,500 per square foot.** Simple RCC frame, plain plaster, basic tiles (not marble), standard electrical fittings, Indian ceramic sanitary ware, no false ceiling, no premium hardware.
- **Mid-range finish: NPR 4,000 to NPR 5,500 per square foot.** Better tiles, basic granite in kitchen, mid-range bathroom fittings (Jaquar or similar Indian brand), better paint, aluminium windows instead of wooden, decent light fittings, modest false ceiling in the living room only.
- **Premium finish: NPR 6,000+ per square foot.** Imported fittings, marble or premium tile, full false ceiling, UPVC or powder-coated aluminium windows, higher-end paint, branded electrical accessories.

These are construction-only numbers. They do not include the cost of land, nor the cost of professional fees, nor the cost of furniture.

### The 3,840 square foot house

Let us do the math for your project. Three floors of roughly 1,280 square feet each gives 3,840 sq ft of built-up area. Add a small terrace room of about 400 sq ft if budget permits — call the total 4,240 sq ft for budgeting purposes, and 3,840 sq ft for the "safe" version without the terrace room.

**At basic finish, 3,840 sq ft:** 3,840 × NPR 3,250 = **NPR 1.248 crore.**

**At mid-range finish, 3,840 sq ft:** 3,840 × NPR 4,750 = **NPR 1.824 crore.**

**At premium finish, 3,840 sq ft:** 3,840 × NPR 6,000 = **NPR 2.304 crore.** — Over budget.

The honest reading of this is that a **mid-range finish on a 3,840 sq ft house gets you right up to the budget ceiling**, with no cushion for overruns. And construction projects *always* have overruns. Three kinds:

1. **Material price inflation during the build.** Cement, rebar, and sand prices have moved 10–20% in a single year in recent Nepal history. A fifteen-month build can see real price movement.
2. **Scope creep.** The family member who said "we don't need a guest room" looks at the half-finished second floor six months in, and says "actually, what if we just extended this a little..." This is human, and it eats budget.
3. **Quality-driven overruns.** You realise at some point that the cheap option you had specified is visibly cheap, and you upgrade. This happens with tiles, with paint, with bathroom fittings, with the main door. Individually each upgrade is small. Collectively they can add 5–10% to the final bill.

So the practical framework is:

- **Aim for a total build cost of NPR 1.6 crore**, which at 3,840 sq ft means about NPR 4,150 per sq ft. That is solidly mid-range but leaning toward the economical end.
- **Hold NPR 40 lakh (20% of the NPR 2 crore ceiling) in reserve** for overruns, professional fees, utility connections, and furniture.
- **Do not start with a premium finish.** The finish is the easiest thing to upgrade in year five. The box is not.

### The category breakdown

Here is a realistic distribution of the NPR 1.6 crore target across construction categories. The percentages come from industry rules of thumb for a mid-range Kathmandu Valley build.

| Category | Share | NPR (of 1.6 cr target) |
|----------|-------|------------------------|
| Foundation + underground water tank | 8% | 12.8 lakh |
| Structural RCC frame (columns, beams, slabs, staircase) | 30% | 48 lakh |
| Brickwork, blockwork, plaster | 15% | 24 lakh |
| Roof + insulation + waterproofing | 5% | 8 lakh |
| Doors and windows | 7% | 11.2 lakh |
| Flooring and tiles | 8% | 12.8 lakh |
| Electrical wiring + fittings | 6% | 9.6 lakh |
| Plumbing + overhead tank + pump | 6% | 9.6 lakh |
| Bathrooms (sanitary ware, tiles, fittings) | 5% | 8 lakh |
| Kitchen (cabinets, granite, sink) | 3% | 4.8 lakh |
| Paint + finishing | 3% | 4.8 lakh |
| Tea shop shutter + commercial fit-out | 2% | 3.2 lakh |

Treat this as a first-pass planning budget, not a contract. The real number for each line item will come out of the quotations you get from contractors and suppliers, and the architect's bill of quantities (BOQ). But the shape of the budget — how much goes to the box versus the decoration — is worth carrying forward through the whole series. Look at it. Notice that the structural frame plus brickwork plus foundation plus roof is 58% of the build. That is the box. Everything below "roof" is decoration.

Spend on the box. Decorate simply. We will keep coming back to that.

## Part 8. A note on the hidden costs you are about to meet

Most first-time builders budget for the big things — concrete, rebar, tiles — and miss the smaller costs that add up to a surprisingly large total. Mention them once, now, so they do not surprise you in month six.

- **Soil testing:** NPR 25,000 to NPR 80,000 depending on the number of boreholes required. Mandatory for a three-storey naksa pass in most Valley municipalities. Discussed in Part 3.
- **Utility connections:** Separate electricity meters (domestic + commercial), water meter, sewer connection, internet, cable. Budget NPR 1 lakh to 2 lakh for the full set.
- **Property tax for construction period:** The municipality will levy a construction-period property tax. A few thousand rupees a year.
- **Temporary site facilities:** A small shed for the workers, a temporary toilet, a temporary electricity connection for the concrete mixer and the vibrator. NPR 50,000 to 1 lakh. Your contractor should cover this out of their rate, but confirm it in writing.
- **Scaffolding and formwork:** Bamboo scaffolding, timber or steel shuttering for the concrete pours. Usually included in the contractor's rate, but confirm.
- **Curing water:** Concrete needs to be kept wet for 7 to 14 days after each pour, so that the chemical reaction (called hydration) completes properly and the concrete reaches its design strength. Curing water is often overlooked — either it comes from municipal supply with a bill to match, or it comes from tanker water at commercial rates. Budget NPR 20,000 to 40,000 for curing water across the project.
- **Transport and labour mobilisation:** Bringing skilled masons in from outside the immediate neighbourhood often involves a per-head transport and accommodation allowance. Depending on the source of your labour, NPR 50,000 to 2 lakh across the project.
- **Extra concrete and rebar "wastage":** Real construction has about 3–5% material wastage (offcuts, spills, mistakes). A good contract calculates this in; a bad contract charges for it as an extra at the end. Check which yours is.
- **Completion certificate:** Once the house is built, the municipality issues a completion certificate (*sampanna pramaan patra*), which is what you need to get a permanent electricity connection and a water meter. Another fee, a few thousand to a few tens of thousands of rupees.

Collectively these "small" items are 3 to 6 percent of the project — NPR 5 lakh to NPR 10 lakh on your budget. They belong in the contingency reserve.

## Part 9. Two documents you must write before you hire anyone

Before you talk to a single architect or contractor, write two documents. These two pieces of paper will save you more money and more heartbreak than any other piece of advice in this series.

### Document 1: The one-page written brief

On a single sheet of paper, write down:

1. **What you are building.** Example: "A three-storey mixed-use house with a tea shop on the ground floor (street-facing), a kitchen and living room on the first floor, and two bedrooms plus two-and-a-half bathrooms on the second floor. A small terrace room on top if budget allows, otherwise just an open terrace."
2. **Who will live in it.** Example: "Two adults, two children, and occasional overnight guests. One elderly parent who may move in after five years and needs a bedroom without stairs — *consider placing one of the upstairs bedrooms on the first floor if feasible.*"
3. **The budget ceiling.** Example: "Total construction cost must not exceed NPR 1.6 crore, with a reserve of NPR 40 lakh for overruns and professional fees. Absolute maximum NPR 2 crore."
4. **Non-negotiable features.** Example: "Underground water tank. Overhead water tank. Separate electrical meter for the tea shop. Roof insulation. Cross-ventilation in every habitable room. Earthquake-resistant RCC frame compliant with NBC 105:2025."
5. **Nice-to-haves.** Example: "Guest bedroom, small garden, solar water heater, motorised shutter for the shop."
6. **Absolute no-gos.** Example: "No compromising on structural design. No borrowed columns or cantilevered extensions. No vinyl flooring. No plastic water pipes on external walls."
7. **Timeline.** Example: "Foundation dig starts by August 2026, move-in before the end of 2027."

One page. Hand-written is fine. You will revise it three times over the next week as you think things through, and that revision process itself will teach you what you actually want.

When you interview architects in Part 2, show them this page and watch their faces. A good architect will ask questions. A great architect will push back on something and explain why. A bad architect will nod and say "yes sir, we can do anything." Hire the one who pushes back.

### Document 2: The decision log

The second document is a simple log — a notebook, or a spreadsheet, or even a plain text file — in which you write down every significant decision you make during the build, with the date, the reason, and the cost implication. It looks like this:

> **3 July 2026.** Decided to go with 5-inch (125 mm) exterior walls instead of 9-inch (230 mm). Reason: architect recommended AAC blocks, which at 5 inches give better insulation than 9-inch solid brick. Cost implication: roughly NPR 2 lakh saving on brickwork, offset by NPR 50,000 extra for AAC blocks. Net saving about NPR 1.5 lakh. Check with structural engineer before confirming.

> **11 July 2026.** Architect proposed moving the stairwell to the front of the house. Pushed back — we want the shop to have the full street frontage. Architect agreed. No cost implication.

> **20 July 2026.** Contractor quoted NPR 4,800 per sq ft all-in; too high. Got second quote at NPR 4,200 per sq ft. Going with the second contractor pending reference check.

You will be amazed, at month twelve, how often you cannot remember why you made a decision you are now having second thoughts about. The decision log is your memory. It is also invaluable if a dispute arises with the contractor, because it documents what was agreed and when.

Both of these documents are boring. Both are free. Both are the single highest-leverage tools in the entire project.

## Part 10. The team you will need to hire

You will be working with six categories of people over the next eighteen months. Know them all, know what they each do, and know how they are paid, before you sign any contract.

**1. The architect.** Produces the architectural drawings. Responsible for the layout, the aesthetics, the light and ventilation, and for coordinating with the structural engineer. Fee is typically 1% to 3% of the construction cost for a small residential project, or a flat NPR 1 lakh to 3 lakh for a three-storey house. Must be NEC-registered.

**2. The structural engineer.** Produces the structural drawings and the seismic calculations. Fee is typically NPR 50,000 to 1.5 lakh for a three-storey residence. Must be NEC-registered. Sometimes the architect and structural engineer work as a team and bill jointly.

**3. The main contractor (thekedaar).** Executes the build. Two payment models in common use:

- **Lump-sum contract:** You agree on a total price for the whole build. The contractor manages materials, labour, and schedule. You get price certainty; they take the risk of overruns. Works well when the drawings are detailed and the scope is clear.
- **Labour-only contract (with you supplying materials):** You buy all materials yourself; the contractor provides only labour at a per-day or per-square-foot rate. You take the price risk on materials; you save the contractor's margin on materials, which can be 10-15%. Works only if you have the time and local knowledge to manage material procurement. Most first-time owners should not attempt this unless someone in the family has construction experience.

For a first-time builder, a well-drafted lump-sum contract with a reputable contractor is almost always the right choice, even if you pay a few percent more than the theoretical minimum.

**4. Specialist subcontractors.** Plumbing, electrical, tiles, woodwork, painting. Some main contractors include these; others subcontract them; others require you to hire separately. Clarify this in the main contract.

**5. The soil testing agency.** One-time engagement. NPR 25,000 to 80,000. Choose a well-regarded local firm; the structural engineer can recommend one.

**6. The municipal liaison (sometimes).** Some owners hire a local fixer to manage the naksa pass paperwork and the ward office visits. A good liaison knows the office, knows the officers, and gets you through the paperwork faster. A bad liaison is a corruption conduit. Most owners do not need one; a good architect handles the paperwork. If you are offered a "liaison" who hints at needing money for "speed," refuse and route the paperwork through your architect's office.

If you want to come prepared, spend a little time between now and tomorrow doing three things:

1. **Walk the plot at three different times of day** — morning, noon, and late afternoon. Notice where the sun falls, where the shadows are, where the wind comes from. Take photos. This is free, and it will shape tomorrow's design more than anything else.
2. **Drive or walk past ten houses in your neighbourhood** that you like, and ten that you do not. In each case, write one sentence about *what* you like or dislike. ## Resources and further reading

The Nepal National Building Code is available in full, in English, from the Department of Urban Development and Building Construction (DUDBC). The documents you will want to look at are:

- **NBC 000–114, the general series**, covering loads, seismic design, fire, concrete, steel, timber, masonry, and construction safety. The key documents for residential work are NBC 105 (seismic design), NBC 110 (plain and reinforced concrete), and NBC 114 (construction safety).
- **NBC 201, "Mandatory Rules of Thumb for Reinforced Concrete Buildings with Masonry Infill."** This is the simplified rule-set that most small residential buildings are designed to, as an alternative to full analytical design under NBC 105. Your structural engineer will typically use a combination of NBC 201 rules of thumb and NBC 105 calculations.
- **The fundamental construction bylaws for your municipality.** Available from the ward office, usually also as a PDF on the municipality's website.

If you want one English-language textbook on Nepali house construction to keep on the shelf, the DUDBC publications are free and authoritative, though dry. For something more readable, the post-2015 guidance documents published by the National Reconstruction Authority (NRA) during the reconstruction programme are excellent plain-language explainers of earthquake-resistant masonry and RCC practice.

For current market rates, price-per-square-foot data, and contractor directories, the Nepali real estate portals — meroproperty, aafnai ghar, gharsansar, housedesigninnepal — update their tables every few months. Treat their numbers as indicative, not authoritative; always get your own quotations from at least three contractors.

For the international reader following along: the core building-physics ideas in this series (envelope-first design, thermal mass, air-tightness, passive ventilation) apply anywhere in the world. The specific codes and bylaws do not. If you are building outside Nepal, use the structure of these articles but substitute your local codes and measurements.

## Part 12. The short version, in one paragraph

On six aana of Bhaktapur-area land, within a hard budget ceiling of NPR 2 crore, a three-storey mixed-use house with a ground-floor tea shop, a first-floor kitchen-and-living-room, and a second-floor with two bedrooms and two-and-a-half bathrooms is feasible, legal, comfortable, and earthquake-safe, provided that you (a) confirm the exact setback, coverage, and FAR rules for your specific plot with the ward office; (b) hire a registered architect and a registered structural engineer who will design to NBC 105:2025; (c) budget NPR 1.6 crore for the build and hold NPR 40 lakh in reserve; (d) spend on the structural box and the building envelope rather than on decoration; (e) get a proper soil test before the foundation is designed; (f) follow the naksa pass process patiently and do not attempt shortcuts; and (g) write down your one-page brief and your decision log on day one and keep them updated through the entire project.

Everything in Parts 2 through 7 will build on those seven points. If that paragraph is the only thing you take away from Part 1, you are already well ahead of most first-time builders.
