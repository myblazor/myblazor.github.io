## Three days in, three days out

Three days ago we stood on six aana of Bhaktapur-area land and stared at the setback rules, the naksa pass process, and the seismic code. Two days ago we walked the plot at three times of day, traced the sun path, designed a floor plan for three storeys that fits under the NPR 2 crore ceiling, and figured out which five questions to ask the three architects we should interview before hiring one.

Today, Part 3, is the day we go underground.

This is the most important technical article in the series, and it is also the one that nobody reads carefully enough. Once the foundation is cast, you cannot change it. Once the columns are poured, you cannot re-route the rebar. Once the concrete has set, it has set. A mistake on the second-floor bathroom tile is a weekend's work to fix. A mistake at plinth level is a reason to pull the whole building down and start again. So today we slow down even more than usual, and we cover the work that disappears forever under the plaster — the work that quietly decides whether your house shrugs off the next Kathmandu Valley earthquake or turns into a pile of rubble with people inside.

No programming. No code. Just the quiet engineering that a house stands on.

## Part 1. What a foundation actually is

Think of your three-storey house as a small mountain of concrete and brick, roughly 4,000 square feet in plan, weighing something on the order of three to four hundred tonnes when finished and furnished. That is the weight of about fifty-five adult elephants, packed onto a footprint smaller than a tennis court.

All of that weight has to go somewhere. It goes into the ground. The **foundation** is the part of the building that spreads the load from the columns and walls above into a large enough area of soil that the ground can actually carry it without giving way.

Soil, for our purposes today, is not a single thing. It is layers. At the top you usually have a thin layer of topsoil — organic matter, roots, old bricks, bits of whatever was there before. Underneath that is whatever the valley put there — silty clay in most of Kathmandu, sometimes with sand lenses, sometimes with pockets of filled material, sometimes with an ancient pond or paddy field buried a metre or two down. Further below is firmer ground, and further still is rock or very dense glacial deposit.

The job of a foundation engineer is to figure out two things. **First**: how much weight the soil can carry per unit of area without compressing too much or slumping sideways. This number is called the **soil bearing capacity**, or SBC, measured in kilonewtons per square metre (kN/m²) or equivalently kilopascals (kPa). **Second**: how wide the base of the building's foundation has to be so that the building's weight, divided by that base area, stays below the soil's bearing capacity, with a healthy safety margin.

That is the whole logic. Building weight, divided by foundation area, must be less than soil bearing capacity divided by safety factor. Everything else — pile foundations, mat foundations, isolated footings, strip footings — is just different geometric ways to achieve the same arithmetic on different kinds of soil.

## Part 2. The soil test, in plain English

Now here is the catch. The soil bearing capacity of your plot is *not a number you can look up on a chart.* Every plot is different. The plot twenty metres away from yours can have completely different soil if it was a paddy field two generations ago and yours was stable upland. Even two corners of the same plot can differ.

Study after study of the Kathmandu Valley has confirmed this. A 2019 study by researchers at Lumbini Buddhist University found that **the soil bearing capacity of the Kathmandu Valley is generally low, with most areas consisting of silty clay of low-to-medium consistency. Measured SBC values ranged from about 62 kN/m² (low end, Lalitpur) to about 151 kN/m² (high end, Lalitpur)**, with broadly similar ranges in Kathmandu and Bhaktapur districts. A separate Institute of Engineering study surveying boreholes across the Valley found **ultimate bearing capacity values ranging from 49 kPa to 307 kPa** depending on location and depth. That is a six-fold range. You cannot guess where on that range your specific plot sits.

This is why the **soil test** exists, and this is why the post-2015 bylaws and NBC 105:2025 expect one for any serious residential build in the Valley.

### What a soil test actually involves

The process is called a **geotechnical investigation**, or more informally a **soil test** or *maato test*. For a small residential plot, the procedure is modest:

1. **Borehole drilling.** A small-diameter drill (typically 75 mm to 150 mm) is used to bore one or more holes into the plot. For a plot under 10 aana, one central borehole is usually sufficient. For larger or irregular plots, two or three boreholes are drilled at strategic corners. Depth is typically 5 to 10 metres, or until a sufficiently stiff layer is encountered. The drill brings up samples of soil from each depth — every 1.5 metres is standard.
2. **Standard Penetration Test (SPT).** As the borehole goes down, a standard split-spoon sampler is driven into the soil at regular intervals by a standard hammer dropping a standard height. The number of blows required to drive the sampler 300 mm is called the **SPT N-value**. The N-value is a rough proxy for soil strength — higher N means stiffer, stronger soil. Kathmandu Valley soils typically give N-values of 4 to 8 near the surface (soft to medium-soft), increasing with depth. In the post-2015 code updates and the NBC 105:2025 revision, soil classification is increasingly based on shear wave velocity (VS₃₀), but SPT remains the workhorse test for small residential projects.
3. **Laboratory tests.** The soil samples are taken to a lab for further analysis — moisture content, specific gravity, grain-size analysis, Atterberg limits, unconfined compressive strength. From these lab values, the geotechnical engineer calculates the ultimate bearing capacity (the soil's absolute maximum), applies a factor of safety of typically 2.5 to 3, and produces the **allowable bearing capacity** — the number your structural engineer will design to.
4. **A written report.** The final output is a short report (usually 20 to 40 pages) that describes the soil profile, lists the SPT values at each depth, gives the allowable bearing capacity at the recommended foundation depth, flags any liquefaction concerns (more on this in a moment), and recommends a foundation type. Your structural engineer reads this report and designs the foundation accordingly.

### Cost and timing

For a plot of six aana in the Kathmandu Valley, a basic soil test with one borehole costs NPR 15,000 to NPR 30,000 in 2026 prices. A more thorough test with multiple boreholes and additional lab work costs NPR 40,000 to NPR 80,000. Either way, it is a small fraction of the project — half a percent of the NPR 1.6 crore construction target — and it can save you lakhs in over-designed or under-designed foundations. Every budget guide on the topic reaches the same conclusion: skimping on the soil test is a false economy.

The test takes one to three days of field work, plus one to two weeks of lab work and report writing. Schedule it **before** your architect and structural engineer finalise the design. The foundation design depends on the soil; if you commission the design first and the soil test later, you will either have to redesign the foundation (expensive in professional fees) or ignore the soil test (dangerous).

### How to read the report

You are not expected to read the full soil test report. Your structural engineer will. But there are three numbers to glance at, for your own peace of mind:

1. **Allowable bearing capacity at foundation depth.** This should be somewhere in the range of 100 to 200 kN/m² for a typical Valley site. If it is under 80 kN/m², the soil is weak and you should expect the report to recommend a mat foundation or piles.
2. **Depth to stiff stratum.** How far down does the soil become firm? If the answer is "2 metres or less," you can use shallow foundations; if the answer is "5 metres or more," the structural engineer will have to think about deeper solutions.
3. **Liquefaction potential.** Liquefaction is what happens when saturated sandy or silty soil, shaken by an earthquake, temporarily behaves like a liquid. Buildings on liquefiable soil can sink, tilt, or fall in minutes. Kathmandu Valley has pockets of liquefiable soil, particularly near old riverbeds, reclaimed paddy fields, and filled land. The report should explicitly say whether your plot has liquefaction potential and, if so, what mitigations are needed (usually a deeper foundation or ground improvement).

### A practical note on Bhaktapur-area soils

The Bhaktapur side of the Valley sits on generally older, more stable glacial and lacustrine deposits than some of the younger river terraces near central Kathmandu and Lalitpur. That is good news on average, but "on average" is not "always." Former paddy fields, plots near the Hanumante Khola floodplain, reclaimed land, and any site that was levelled with imported fill in the last thirty years can all carry surprises. The only way to know is to test.

If your neighbours' houses have cracks running diagonally up the corners, or doors that stick because the frame has racked, the soil under their foundations has moved. Those houses are a warning about what your soil might do. Flag any such observations in your notes from the Part 1 plot walk and share them with the geotechnical firm you hire. Good geotechnical engineers are always interested in what the buildings nearby have been doing.

## Part 3. Choosing the foundation type

For a three-storey reinforced-concrete residential building in the Kathmandu Valley, there are really three foundation types on the table:

**Isolated footings.** Each column sits on its own square or rectangular concrete pad (the "footing"), sized to spread the column's load over enough soil area. The footings are connected to each other by horizontal concrete beams called **plinth beams** or **ground beams**, which tie the building together at ground level so that it moves as one unit in an earthquake rather than as a collection of independent columns. This is the most common and most economical foundation for Kathmandu Valley residences when the SBC is adequate (roughly 150 kN/m² and above at foundation depth).

**Mat (raft) foundation.** Instead of separate footings under each column, a single thick slab of reinforced concrete — typically 300 to 600 mm thick — is cast under the entire footprint of the building. The slab spreads the total building weight over the full footprint area, so the pressure on any one square metre of soil is low. This is the right foundation when the soil is weak (SBC under 100 kN/m² roughly), or when the soil conditions are variable across the plot, or when the building has an underground water tank or basement that needs a watertight floor anyway. Nepal Valley geotechnical literature explicitly recommends raft foundations where the soil has very low bearing capacity. More expensive than isolated footings (more concrete, more rebar) but often unavoidable on Kathmandu Valley silty-clay sites.

**Pile foundation.** Vertical concrete columns (piles) are bored or driven deep into the ground — often 10 to 20 metres — to reach a firm stratum, and the building sits on a grid of piles. This is the right foundation where the surface soil is very weak and deep good soil exists below. Expensive — usually 15% to 30% more than a raft — and overkill for most three-storey residential buildings in the Valley unless the soil test specifically recommends it. Required in genuinely difficult sites (liquefiable zones, very soft alluvium, filled ground with unknown history).

For your six-aana plot, the honest likely answer is **isolated footings if the soil is decent, mat foundation if the soil is weak, and an underground water tank integrated either way.** Your structural engineer will make the final call after reading the geotechnical report. Budget-wise, plan for the mat foundation as the "upper bound" — if isolated footings turn out to be adequate, you will have budget to spare elsewhere. Working the other way (budgeting for isolated footings and being surprised by a mat recommendation) creates mid-project cost pressure.

### A rough cost comparison for a 1,280 sq ft footprint

- **Isolated footings with plinth beams:** roughly NPR 8 to 12 lakh (6–8% of the NPR 1.6 crore target).
- **Raft foundation:** roughly NPR 12 to 18 lakh (8–11% of target).
- **Raft + underground water tank integrated:** roughly NPR 15 to 20 lakh (includes the tank, not separately).
- **Pile foundation (if ever needed):** roughly NPR 20 to 30 lakh.

These are ballpark numbers. Your structural engineer will produce a proper bill of quantities once the design is set.

## Part 4. The underground water tank, as part of the foundation

Here is one of those small decisions that has large consequences if made late. In Kathmandu, municipal water supply is unreliable — the Kathmandu Upatyaka Khanepani Limited (KUKL) typically delivers water for only a few hours a day, and in many parts of the Valley for only a few hours every few days. Every Kathmandu residence therefore has a water-storage system built around two tanks: an **underground tank** (called a *sump* or *undertank*) that receives whatever comes from the municipal line and from tanker deliveries, and an **overhead tank** (*upper tank*) on the roof that is filled by a pump from the undertank and that gravity-feeds the taps throughout the house.

We will cover the overhead tank and the pump in Part 5 tomorrow. Today we care about the underground tank, because it has to be designed into the foundation from day one. Adding an underground tank after the foundation is poured is extraordinarily expensive — you would have to break up the floor, excavate under the slab, cast a new tank, and reconnect everything. Better to get it right the first time.

### Sizing the tank

A family of five people in Kathmandu uses roughly 675 to 750 litres of water per day — perhaps a bit less if water is tight, a bit more if you irrigate a small garden or run a small shop. For a tea shop on the ground floor that serves perhaps two hundred cups of tea a day, add another 100 litres. Round up to 850 litres per day for a family-plus-shop combined household.

The question is how much storage buffer you want. The logic goes:

- **1 day of storage (850 L):** useless in Kathmandu. One municipal outage and you are dry.
- **3 days of storage (2,500 L):** adequate for short outages but not for the week-long dry stretches that happen in some neighbourhoods.
- **7 days of storage (6,000 L):** a realistic baseline. You can ride out most outages, and a tanker delivery fills you to capacity.
- **10 days of storage (8,500 L):** comfortable. You are insulated from most of what Kathmandu can throw at you.
- **15 days of storage (13,000 L):** over-provisioned for most plots, but justified in the drier parts of the Valley.

A reasonable target for your house is a **7,500 to 10,000 litre underground tank**, giving you 9 to 12 days of supply for family-plus-shop. At 1 cubic metre = 1,000 litres, that means a rectangular tank of internal dimensions roughly 3 m × 2 m × 1.5 m (= 9,000 litres / 9 cubic metres), or similar proportions.

### Siting the tank within the foundation

Three common approaches:

1. **Under the front courtyard or driveway.** The tank sits outside the building footprint, with its top slab serving as the driveway surface. Easy to clean and inspect; some risk of contamination from vehicle oil and surface runoff; can be integrated with the plinth system rather than the main foundation.
2. **Under a ground-floor interior room.** Typically under a storage room or under the tea shop back area. The tank sits below the ground floor slab, with an access manhole in the floor above. Cleaner than the courtyard version, but harder to access for maintenance.
3. **Integrated into a raft foundation.** The raft foundation itself forms the bottom and walls of the tank, with an extra internal wall sealing the tank area from the rest of the basement-level space. Most efficient use of concrete if you are already pouring a raft; requires careful waterproofing design.

For your plot, option 1 or 3 is most likely. The engineer's call.

### Construction details that matter

The underground tank has to be watertight in both directions — water should not leak out (wasting the water and weakening the soil around the foundation) and groundwater should not leak in (contaminating the drinking supply). Four details do most of the work:

- **Integral waterproofing additives in the concrete mix.** A chemical additive (brands like Dr. Fixit Pidiproof or Sika WT 200) makes the concrete itself resistant to water passage. Cost: about 1–2% on top of concrete.
- **A continuous waterproofing membrane on the outside** of the tank walls and under the bottom slab. Usually a bituminous membrane or a liquid-applied polyurethane coating.
- **Proper construction joints.** Where two pours meet (for example, where the wall is cast on top of the bottom slab after the slab has set), a waterstop — a PVC or rubber strip embedded in both pours — prevents water from tracking along the joint.
- **An inspection manhole with an airtight lid.** Access for cleaning; the lid must seal against insects, small animals, and surface water.

And one sanity check: the tank must be *deep enough that the municipal supply line can feed into it by gravity.* In most Kathmandu neighbourhoods the municipal line runs at about 1 metre below the road surface, so the inlet pipe into the tank should be at 1.2 m to 1.5 m below road level. The tank bottom is therefore at about 2.7 m to 3.0 m below road. This depth is integrated into the foundation design.

## Part 5. The RCC frame — columns, beams, slabs

Now we come out of the ground and start building up. The structural system for a three-storey Kathmandu residence is almost certainly a **reinforced concrete moment-resisting frame with masonry infill.** Let us unpack that phrase.

**Reinforced concrete** means concrete with steel bars (rebar) cast inside it. Concrete is strong in compression (resists being squashed) but weak in tension (pulls apart easily under stretching). Steel is strong in both compression and tension. Put them together — concrete surrounding steel — and you get a composite material that is strong in both. Every important structural element in your house is reinforced concrete.

**Moment-resisting frame** means the columns and beams are rigidly joined together at their intersections, so that the whole assembly bends and sways as one unit under lateral loads (wind, earthquake) rather than collapsing like a stack of loose blocks. The "moment" in the name is the bending force that is transferred at each joint. This is why the beam-column joint is the single most important detail in the whole structure, and why sloppy work at the joints is the most common cause of building failure in Kathmandu earthquakes.

**With masonry infill** means the walls between the columns are built of brick or block, and those walls *fill in* between the frame but do not *hold up* the frame. The columns and beams carry the load; the walls are essentially cladding. This distinction matters enormously, because masonry infill walls can contribute in unpredictable ways to the frame's earthquake response — they can trap columns into short-column behaviour (where a strong, short section of column is surrounded by walls and a weaker unbraced top emerges above, creating a dangerous failure pattern), or they can cause soft-storey effects when the ground floor has fewer walls than upper floors. NBC 201 — the Nepali mandatory rules of thumb for RCC buildings with masonry infill — specifically exists to give builders ready-to-use dimensions that account for these effects.

For your house, here is how each structural element works.

### Columns

**Columns** are the vertical members that carry the building's weight down to the foundation. For a three-storey residence, columns are typically 230 mm × 230 mm (9 inches square) or 250 mm × 300 mm in cross-section. Each column has four, six, or eight longitudinal bars running up its full height, plus closed stirrups (horizontal ties) at regular spacing around those longitudinal bars.

Key numbers from NBC 201 for three-storey construction:

- **Column cross-section:** not less than 230 × 230 mm for a three-storey house; 250 × 250 mm is more common in current practice.
- **Longitudinal reinforcement:** 4 to 8 bars of 16 mm or 20 mm diameter Fe500 or Fe500D, arranged around the perimeter. The exact amount depends on the load and is specified in the structural drawings.
- **Stirrups (horizontal ties):** 8 mm or 10 mm Fe500 bars, bent into closed rectangles with 135° hooks at the ends. Spacing: 150 mm in the middle portion of the column, reduced to 75 mm near the top and bottom where the column meets a beam (the critical zone). The 135° hooks are not optional; straight-hooked stirrups pop open under earthquake loads and the column loses its confinement.
- **Cover to reinforcement:** 40 mm of concrete between the rebar and the outside face of the column, for fire resistance, corrosion protection, and the concrete to have enough area to bond to the steel.

### Beams

**Beams** are the horizontal members that span from column to column, carrying the weight of the slab above and transferring it to the columns. For a three-storey residence, beams are typically 230 mm wide × 350 mm to 400 mm deep.

Key numbers:

- **Beam cross-section:** 230 × 350 mm is the NBC 201 default for typical residential spans (up to about 4.5 m clear span).
- **Longitudinal reinforcement:** typically 2 to 4 bars top and bottom of 12 mm or 16 mm diameter, with additional bars added near the supports (where bending moments are largest) and curtailed toward the middle.
- **Stirrups:** 8 mm Fe500 bars spaced more closely near the supports (where shear forces are largest) and more widely in the middle.
- **Lap length:** where two bars meet end-to-end, they have to overlap for a minimum length so that the force transfers between them through the surrounding concrete. NBC 201 specifies a minimum lap of **56 times the bar diameter** for typical residential bars. A 16 mm bar therefore needs a 900 mm (roughly 3 feet) overlap. Short laps are a common shortcut on untrained sites and they are a direct cause of beam failure in earthquakes.

### Slabs

**Slabs** are the horizontal concrete floors that span between the beams. In Kathmandu Valley residential construction, slabs are typically 125 mm thick (5 inches), reinforced with a two-way grid of 8 mm or 10 mm Fe500 bars at 150 mm centres in both directions, with additional bars in high-stress regions near the edges.

Key numbers:

- **Slab thickness:** 125 mm for spans up to about 4.5 m between beam supports. Larger spans (up to 6 m) need 150 mm or more.
- **Clear cover:** 20 mm for slabs (less than for columns because slabs are less exposed).
- **Top mesh and bottom mesh.** The bottom mesh resists the mid-span sagging tension; the top mesh resists the tension at the supports where the slab is held down by the beams. Both meshes are essential. A common mistake on undersupervised sites is to skip or reduce the top mesh, which is invisible once the concrete is poured but which causes progressive cracking and eventual failure over the years.

### The beam-column joint

This is where most earthquake-caused collapses start. The joint is the small volume of concrete at the intersection of a column and one or more beams, where all their rebar converges. Done right, the joint is a tightly confined box of concrete surrounded by closely spaced stirrups, and it transfers forces cleanly between columns and beams. Done wrong, the joint is a congested mess where stirrups have been skipped because "there wasn't enough space" and the concrete has voids because the mix could not flow through the rebar forest.

What "done right" looks like:

- **Stirrups continue through the joint**, spaced at 75 mm or less (not 150 mm). This is the rule with no exceptions.
- **Beam bars are anchored correctly** — they run through the column if possible, or hook into it with a 90° bend of at least 12 times the bar diameter.
- **Column ties (stirrups) are maintained right up to the underside of the slab above**, not terminated at the top of the column.

When your structural engineer visits the site during construction, the joints are what they are looking at most carefully. If the contractor's crew skips joint stirrups or uses wider spacing there, it is not a detail you can catch from the finished plaster.

## Part 6. Rebar quality — Fe500 versus Fe500D

The steel rebar used in your house is graded by two numbers: the minimum yield strength (in MPa) and a letter for ductility. In Nepal the two common grades are:

- **Fe500.** 500 MPa minimum yield strength, 12% minimum elongation at break. Carbon content up to 0.30% max. Sulphur and phosphorus up to standard IS:1786 limits.
- **Fe500D.** 500 MPa minimum yield strength, 16% minimum elongation at break. Carbon content reduced to 0.25% max. Sulphur and phosphorus lower. The "D" stands for ductility.

The practical difference is that **Fe500D stretches further before breaking than Fe500 does.** When an earthquake hits and the building starts swaying, the beams and columns bend, and the rebar inside them stretches. Fe500 rebar can stretch about 12% of its length before snapping; Fe500D can stretch 16%. Those four percentage points are what turn a brittle collapse into a visible warning crack. The building might still be damaged, but it gives occupants time to get out.

For a residential building in Nepal — a country where seismic zones are 4 and 5, the two highest categories — **Fe500D is the right choice.** The cost difference is small, typically NPR 5 to 10 per kg on top of Fe500 prices. For a 3,840 sq ft three-storey house, you will use roughly 12 to 15 tonnes of rebar total, so the premium for Fe500D is about NPR 60,000 to NPR 150,000 over Fe500. That is less than 0.1% of your total build cost, for the component of the building that most directly decides whether people walk out of an earthquake alive.

Nepali brands available in 2026 include **Rhino 500D from Jagdamba Steels** (marketed as the first Fe500D-certified rebar in Nepal), **Jagdamba E**, **Shree Steels 500D**, **Panchakanya**, **Hama Steel**, and several others. Prices as of late 2025 and early 2026 run roughly NPR 120 to 135 per kg depending on brand and diameter, with Fe500D at the higher end of that range.

What to check when the rebar arrives on site:

1. **The manufacturer's label and batch number.** Every bundle of rebar comes with a tag. Keep photos of all tags.
2. **NS 191 certification.** The Nepal Bureau of Standards and Metrology (NBSM) certifies TMT rebar against NS 191, which is aligned with IS:1786. Look for the certification mark.
3. **The weight per metre matches the declared grade.** A 12 mm Fe500 bar weighs about 0.89 kg/m; a 16 mm weighs about 1.58 kg/m; a 20 mm weighs about 2.47 kg/m. Underweight bars are counterfeit or rolled out-of-spec, and they are a well-known scam in Nepal's construction supply chain. Weigh a one-metre sample of each diameter delivered.
4. **The surface has proper deformation ribs.** TMT rebar should have a clearly visible rib pattern along its length. Smooth rebar is not structural-grade.
5. **No excessive rust.** A light surface rust from storage is fine and actually improves bond with concrete. Heavy flaking rust is a problem and the bar should be rejected.

Save the delivery receipts and the mill test certificates for your records. They become part of the house's building history.

## Part 7. Concrete — cement, mix, and grade

Concrete is a mixture of cement (the binder), aggregate (sand and stone), and water, in carefully controlled proportions. The grade of concrete is specified by its **compressive strength at 28 days**, measured in N/mm² or MPa.

For your house, the commonly specified grades are:

- **M20** (20 MPa compressive strength at 28 days): adequate for slabs and non-critical elements in low-rise construction. Mix ratio by volume is roughly 1 part cement : 1.5 parts sand : 3 parts coarse aggregate (1:1.5:3).
- **M25** (25 MPa): the workhorse grade for columns and beams in three-storey construction. Mix ratio roughly 1:1:2.
- **M30** (30 MPa): used where loads are higher, e.g., very long spans, heavily loaded columns. Not usually required for a typical three-storey residence but sometimes specified for the basement and ground-floor columns of taller buildings.

For your three-storey house, your structural engineer will typically specify **M25 for columns and beams, M20 or M25 for slabs, and M20 for plinth beams and the foundation (with the foundation sometimes bumped up to M25 if the loads are high).** The mix ratios are the engineer's call; do not let the contractor substitute a weaker mix to save on cement.

### Cement choice

In Nepal in 2026, two broad categories of cement are available:

- **Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC)** — plain cement, sold as OPC 43 grade or OPC 53 grade (the number is the 28-day strength in MPa of the cement itself, not the concrete). Use OPC for structural elements: columns, beams, slabs, foundation. Brands: Shivam, Hongshi, Arghakhanchi, Ultratech, Sarbottam — all reputable in the Nepali market.
- **Portland Pozzolana Cement (PPC)** — OPC blended with a pozzolan (fly ash or calcined clay). Cheaper than OPC, gains strength more slowly, better for plastering, brickwork, and non-structural applications. Do *not* use PPC for structural pours.

A common contractor's shortcut is to use PPC everywhere because it is cheaper. Resist this. OPC for structure, PPC for cosmetic work, clearly labelled on site.

### The water-cement ratio

This is the single most important number in concrete quality, and the one most commonly compromised on undersupervised sites. The strength of concrete depends heavily on how much water was added relative to cement. Less water = stronger, more durable concrete, but harder to place. More water = weaker concrete, but easier to pour and easier for an unskilled crew.

Target water-cement ratios by grade (by weight):

- M20: about 0.50 (50 kg water per 100 kg cement)
- M25: about 0.45
- M30: about 0.42

On a well-run site, these ratios are measured by weight, or at least by a known-volume bucket. On a poorly-run site, the mason adds water "until it looks right," which always means more water than was specified. The concrete that comes out looks easier to place, but its 28-day strength is often 20% to 40% below spec. By the time anybody tests it, the pour is done and the concrete is in the structure forever.

Two defences against this:

1. **Require a slump test at every major pour.** A slump test measures how much a standard cone of fresh concrete "slumps" when the cone is removed. A slump of 50–100 mm is typical for structural concrete. Higher slumps indicate too much water. The test is simple and cheap; every serious contractor has the equipment.
2. **Take concrete cube samples at every major pour.** Three cubes of 150 mm × 150 mm × 150 mm, cured in water for 28 days, then crushed in a lab to measure actual compressive strength. NPR 500 to 1,500 per set of cubes. Do this at every pour. If any cube falls below spec by more than 10%, there is a problem, and the structural engineer decides what to do about it.

### Curing

Concrete does not dry. It cures. The chemical reaction between cement and water (called **hydration**) continues for weeks, and it only works properly if the concrete is kept wet. If concrete dries out in the first 7 to 14 days, the reaction stops before it has produced the designed strength.

Curing in practice means keeping every freshly poured concrete element wet for at least 7 days, and ideally 14 days. For slabs this is done by covering with wet sacking and watering twice a day, or by flooding the slab with 50 mm of water held in by a low mud bund. For columns and beams it is done by wrapping in wet sacking.

A contractor who cuts corners on curing produces concrete that looks fine at year one, starts cracking visibly at year five, and loses structural capacity by year fifteen. Curing takes discipline and water. Insist on it.

## Part 8. Ductile detailing — the twelve rules

"Ductile detailing" is the engineer's term for all the small decisions in how the rebar is arranged inside the concrete so that the building bends rather than breaks in an earthquake. NBC 105:2025 and NBC 201:1994 (still current via reprint) together specify all of this in detail. Your structural engineer will implement it. But as the owner on site, twelve rules are worth knowing so you can spot violations.

**Rule 1. Stirrups in columns have 135° hooks, not 90° hooks.** The hook angle is checked by looking at the end of each stirrup before concrete is poured. Wrong hook? Reject the cage, rebend.

**Rule 2. Stirrup spacing is reduced near joints.** In a typical column, stirrups are at 150 mm in the middle third and 75 mm in the top and bottom thirds. Check this on any cage that is about to be cast.

**Rule 3. Column ties continue through the beam-column joint.** The joint is where earthquakes break columns. Stirrups in the joint, at 75 mm or less spacing, are non-negotiable.

**Rule 4. Laps are 56 times the bar diameter minimum.** 12 mm bar = 675 mm lap; 16 mm bar = 900 mm lap; 20 mm bar = 1,120 mm lap. Short laps are a common shortcut. Measure them.

**Rule 5. Not more than 50% of bars are spliced at one section.** Even with adequate lap length, if all bars in a column are lapped at the same elevation, the column is weak at that spot. Stagger the laps.

**Rule 6. Beam top bars are lapped only in the middle third of the span.** Not near the supports. The tension forces at supports are at the top of the beam; lapping there weakens the critical zone.

**Rule 7. Beam bottom bars are lapped only near the supports, and never in the middle third.** The tension forces at mid-span are at the bottom of the beam; same logic.

**Rule 8. Main bars are never curtailed in a beam framing infill walls.** Don't make beams partially un-reinforced where walls are attached. NBC 201 is explicit.

**Rule 9. Clear cover is maintained everywhere.** 40 mm for columns, 25 mm for beams, 20 mm for slabs. Use spacer blocks of mortar or plastic to keep rebar at the right distance from the formwork before the pour.

**Rule 10. Columns are not shifted or dropped between floors.** Every column runs straight up from foundation to roof. No jogs. No disappearing columns. No cantilevered column continuations.

**Rule 11. Plinth beams tie all columns at ground level.** Every column is connected to adjacent columns at the plinth level by a reinforced concrete beam, so the building moves as one unit at its base.

**Rule 12. The seismic gap with neighbouring buildings is at least 1.5 metres** (the post-2015 minimum) and may be more based on NBC 105:2025 building-height calculations. If your plot boundary is closer than this to a neighbour's building, the setback has to accommodate it.

## Part 9. What to look for on site

Your architect and structural engineer will visit the site. So should you, ideally once or twice a week during the structural work. You are not looking for engineering defects; you are looking for sloppy execution that contradicts what the drawings say. Ten practical checks:

**1. Are the column positions as drawn?** Walk the plinth level with the drawings in hand. Each column should be exactly where the drawing shows, at the exact spacing, at the exact cross-section. A column that is 150 mm off location changes the beam spans, and it is usually the sign of a crew that is not reading the drawings carefully.

**2. Count the longitudinal bars in each column cage.** Before concrete is poured around a column cage, count the bars. 4? 6? 8? Match the drawing. Easy check, catches ambitious shortcuts.

**3. Measure stirrup spacing.** Bring a tape measure. Stirrups at 150 mm mid-height, at 75 mm near ends. If you see 200 mm spacing anywhere in a column cage, reject it.

**4. Check stirrup hook angles.** 135° hooks mean the free end of the stirrup bends back into the column core by at least 75 mm. Look at every free end; at least a quarter of them should be visible at any time in a properly tied cage.

**5. Verify the rebar brand and diameter.** Random sample the rebar on site against the delivery tags. Call the vendor if any stack is unlabelled.

**6. Watch a concrete pour.** Pours happen on specific days; ask the contractor for the schedule and be there for at least the first pour of every floor. Watch the mixing (cement, sand, stone, and water should be measured, not eyeballed), watch the slump test (there should be one), watch the cube sampling.

**7. Check cover before the pour.** The rebar cage should have spacer blocks (small mortar cubes or plastic spacers) keeping it 40 mm from the formwork on every face. If you can push a finger through and touch rebar, the cover is wrong.

**8. Observe the curing.** Day after a pour, check that the concrete is wet. Seven to fourteen days after, check that it is still wet. Dry concrete in week one is a problem.

**9. Test the cube strength.** At 28 days after each major pour, ask for the lab report on the cube samples. If any cube is more than 10% below spec, ask the engineer what the fix is.

**10. Photograph everything.** Before every concrete pour, walk the formwork and rebar with your phone and take photos from every angle. These photos are documentation of what you built. If a problem appears years later, the photos are the evidence.

None of this is confrontational. You are checking what should be happening anyway, on a well-run site. If the contractor objects to these checks, something is wrong.

## Part 10. Common mistakes and how to prevent them

Six failure modes recur in post-2015 Kathmandu investigations. Know them, watch for them.

**Short column effect.** Happens when an infill wall partially encloses a column, creating a short free span that takes all of the building's lateral displacement in an earthquake. Most common cause: ventilators or small high windows in a room where the rest of the wall is solid masonry. The column section above the ventilator becomes short and stiff, and in an earthquake it snaps. Prevention: either have full-height walls with proper seismic gaps, or full openings up to the ceiling, never partial walls that create short columns.

**Soft-storey effect.** Happens when the ground floor has fewer walls than the floors above — a common situation for houses with parking or a shop on the ground floor. The ground floor is "soft" because it has less stiffness, and it takes all of the earthquake's deflection. Many 2015 collapses were exactly this. Prevention: make the ground floor columns larger, add shear walls at the ground-floor level, or distribute the stiffness so the soft storey does not exist.

**Strong beam–weak column.** The code wants *weak beams and strong columns* — so that when something has to yield in an earthquake, it is the beam, not the column. If the beam is stronger than the column, the column fails first, and the whole building above it comes down. Prevention: the structural engineer's job, but an owner should know the principle and ask the engineer to explain how their design implements it.

**Inadequate lap length.** A lap of 30 bar diameters where it should be 56 — invisible once the concrete is poured, catastrophic under earthquake loads. Prevention: measure laps before each pour.

**Inadequate stirrup confinement at joints.** Stirrups skipped in the beam-column joint because "there was no room." Prevention: inspect joints before every concrete pour.

**Poor concrete quality.** Too much water, poor curing, wrong aggregate, wrong cement. Prevention: slump tests, cube tests, and an insistence on OPC for structural work.

## Part 11. The honest budget check

We have covered a lot of ground. Let us tie it back to the budget.

In Part 1 we allocated:
- Foundation + underground water tank: 8% of NPR 1.6 crore = NPR 12.8 lakh
- RCC structural frame: 30% = NPR 48 lakh
- Brickwork, blockwork, plaster: 15% = NPR 24 lakh

Together, the "box" from foundation through structural frame through masonry is 53% of the budget: NPR 84.8 lakh. That is the part that the rest of this article has been about. It is most of your spend, and it is the part that disappears completely under the plaster and the tiles.

Fe500D rebar over Fe500 adds NPR 60,000 to NPR 150,000. Proper OPC for structural pours and PPC elsewhere, no substitution, is essentially a rounding error — the cost is in the discipline, not the product. Proper cube testing and a soil test together are under NPR 50,000. Proper curing costs water, which on the Kathmandu construction budget is a small line item — NPR 20,000 to NPR 40,000 across the project.

For the cost of less than 1% of the total budget — perhaps NPR 2 lakh — you upgrade from an "adequate" box to an "excellent" box. This is the highest-return investment in the whole project, and it is invisible forever. Make it.

## Resources and further reading

For the technical reader who wants to go deeper into what we covered today:

- **NBC 105:2025 (Seismic Design of Buildings in Nepal, Second Revision)** — the authoritative code for seismic analysis, published by DUDBC.
- **NBC 201:1994 (Mandatory Rules of Thumb for Reinforced Concrete Buildings with Masonry Infill)** — the simplified rules your structural engineer will use for a small residential build. Download free from the DUDBC website.
- **NBC 110 (Plain and Reinforced Concrete)** — the companion code for concrete design, specifying cement, aggregate, water-cement ratios, and reinforcement details.
- **NBC 205 (Mandatory Rules of Thumb for Reinforced Concrete Buildings without Masonry Infill)** — a parallel document for buildings where the infill walls are deliberately detached from the frame. Less common for residential; mentioned for completeness.
- **NS 191 (Nepal Standard for Deformed Steel Bars)** — the rebar certification standard, maintained by the Nepal Bureau of Standards and Metrology.

For a non-technical reader who wants to understand earthquakes in the Nepali context better, the National Reconstruction Authority's (NRA) post-2015 outreach documents are excellent plain-language explainers. Many are available in Nepali as well as English.

For a glimpse of what good RCC practice looks like in photographs, the DUDBC training materials for local masons — produced after 2015 for the reconstruction programme — contain side-by-side "good" and "bad" photos that are worth scrolling through once.

For an international reader: the structural principles in this article — soil bearing capacity driving foundation choice, ductile detailing at joints, weak-beam/strong-column philosophy, Fe500-grade rebar with high elongation for seismic zones — are essentially universal. Only the specific code reference numbers change. If you are building in a seismic region anywhere in the world, the questions to ask are the same.

On a six-aana Kathmandu Valley plot with typical silty-clay subsurface, the structural foundation should be designed after a site-specific soil test (borehole, SPT, laboratory analysis, NPR 15,000 to 80,000) that yields an allowable bearing capacity and a recommendation between isolated footings (SBC adequate, NPR 8–12 lakh), a raft foundation (weak or variable soil, NPR 12–18 lakh), or piles (rare for three-storey, NPR 20–30 lakh); the underground water tank (7,500–10,000 litres, giving 9–12 days of storage for a family plus tea shop) must be integrated into the foundation from the start rather than added later; the superstructure is a reinforced concrete moment-resisting frame with masonry infill, built to NBC 201 dimensions (230 × 230 mm columns minimum, 230 × 350 mm beams, 125 mm slabs) and NBC 105:2025 seismic detailing; Fe500D TMT rebar is the right grade for Nepal's seismic zones with less than 0.1% cost premium over Fe500; M25 concrete (OPC, water-cement ratio 0.45) is the workhorse grade for columns and beams with cube-tested quality control; twelve ductile-detailing rules, enforced by the structural engineer's site visits and the owner's weekly inspections, decide whether the house survives the next major earthquake; and the total spend on foundation plus structural frame plus masonry — about 53% of the overall budget — is the part of the house that becomes invisible under the plaster and that is permanent. Spend on the box.
