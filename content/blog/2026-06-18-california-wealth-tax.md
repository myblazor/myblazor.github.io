## Introduction: A Thursday Afternoon in April, and a Number That Changed Everything

Picture a Thursday afternoon, 26 April 2026. In a press release drafted somewhere in a union office in Sacramento, a spokesperson for the Service Employees International Union United Healthcare Workers West typed the sentence that crystallised a year of furious organising: more than **1.5 million Californians** had signed a petition asking the state to tax its wealthiest two hundred residents a total of five cents on every dollar they own.

That number — 1.5 million — is nearly double the 875,000 valid signatures required under California law to put a citizen initiative onto the November statewide ballot. When county election authorities finish verifying names and forward the results to the Secretary of State, Californians will be asked to vote, probably on 3 November 2026, on what its backers call the **2026 Billionaire Tax Act** (Initiative No. 25-0024). The initiative is as simple as tax law ever gets: if you lived in California on 1 January 2026 and your net worth exceeds one billion dollars, you owe the state a one-time payment equal to five per cent of that net worth, payable in full in 2027 or spread across five annual instalments with a modest deferral charge.

For roughly two hundred people it is an extraordinary sum. For the eighth-richest person on earth — Jensen Huang of Nvidia, net worth approximately $167 billion as of early 2026 — it would amount to about $8.35 billion. He told a panel at Stanford's Graduate School of Business earlier this year that he thinks people should move *to* California, not leave it, "even though it's the highest taxes in the world." He said it in the same breath as acknowledging that this tax would cost him personally more money than the gross domestic product of several small nations.

That collision — a Silicon Valley titan cheerfully contemplating a multibillion-dollar tax bill while his peers spend tens of millions to stop the ballot measure from passing — is the story of California in 2026. But it is also a much older story about wealth, democracy, the price of civilisation, and who gets to decide what that price is.

This article is a complete guide to all of it. We begin with the healthcare emergency that gave the initiative its political momentum, then work through the legal text of the measure, the economics of wealth taxation, the opposition campaign and the personalities behind it, the constitutional arguments likely to appear in every courtroom from San Francisco to Washington DC if the measure passes, and the broader national and international context that explains why California's vote matters far beyond the Golden State.

We have tried to cover every corner of this subject. If at the end you feel you need to look something up, the Resources section at the end will point you there.

## Part 1: The Crisis That Made the Tax Imaginable

### 1.1 Sixty Years of Medicaid, and the Bill That Rewrote It Overnight

To understand why 1.5 million Californians signed a petition that would redistribute wealth on a scale without precedent in American state history, you first have to understand what happened on 4 July 2025.

President Donald Trump signed into law the **One Big Beautiful Bill Act** — known in legislative shorthand as H.R. 1 — on Independence Day 2025. The law was, by multiple measures, the largest restructuring of the American social safety net since the Great Society programmes of the 1960s. The nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office estimated that the legislation would cut nearly **$1 trillion from Medicaid and the Children's Health Insurance Programme (CHIP) over ten years** — the largest single reduction to either programme in their sixty-year histories.

For California, the numbers were staggering. The California Health and Human Services Agency projected that H.R. 1 would cost the state roughly **$30 billion per year** in lost federal Medi-Cal funding. The California Legislative Analyst's Office, working from similar assumptions, arrived at comparable figures. The California Budget and Policy Centre used the phrase "existential threat" to describe what those cuts would mean for safety-net hospitals — facilities that serve communities in which Medi-Cal patients make up sixty to eighty per cent of all inpatient cases.

Let us pause on that figure: $30 billion per year. California's total state General Fund is roughly $225 billion. Thirty billion dollars is more than the entire annual budget of the state Department of Corrections and Rehabilitation, the University of California system, and the California State University system combined. It is not a rounding error. It is a structural wound.

### 1.2 How Medi-Cal Works — and Why It Is Uniquely Vulnerable

Medi-Cal is California's implementation of the federal Medicaid programme. It covers more than fourteen million Californians — approximately one third of the state's entire population. That includes:

- More than half of all children born in the state;
- 2.2 million seniors and people with disabilities;
- One in five working Californians in jobs that do not offer employer-sponsored health insurance;
- Millions of people in low-income households who would otherwise have no coverage.

The programme works on a matching-funds model. The federal government typically contributes between fifty and sixty cents for every dollar California spends on Medi-Cal. This means that when the federal government cuts its share, the loss is not merely dollar-for-dollar — it is amplified, because the state either has to find matching funds to replace the federal share or reduce coverage and services.

H.R. 1 hit Medi-Cal through several simultaneous mechanisms:

**Work requirements.** The law requires certain Medicaid recipients to work, volunteer, or attend school for eighty hours per month. Work requirements have been studied extensively; in every jurisdiction where they have been implemented, the primary effect has been to kick eligible people off the rolls due to administrative complexity rather than because they are not actually working. The work requirement takes effect 1 January 2027.

**More frequent eligibility redeterminations.** Eligibility was previously reviewed annually. H.R. 1 requires redetermination every six months for certain recipients, taking effect 31 December 2026. More frequent reviews mean more administrative burden, more errors, and more eligible people losing coverage in the bureaucratic shuffle.

**Caps on Disproportionate Share Hospital (DSH) payments.** Safety-net hospitals that serve high proportions of Medicaid and uninsured patients receive supplemental DSH payments to compensate for the uncompensated care they provide. H.R. 1 imposes new caps on these payments. For hospitals in areas like East Los Angeles, South Los Angeles, Lynwood, Huntington Park, and Santa Ana — communities where sixty to eighty per cent of residents rely on Medi-Cal — this is not an abstraction. It is the difference between keeping an emergency department open and closing it.

**Provider rate reductions.** Beginning in 2028, H.R. 1 will cap the funds states use to close the gap between Medicaid reimbursement rates and rates paid by commercial insurers. This means that over time, hospitals treating Medi-Cal patients will be paid rates approaching Medicare levels — substantially lower than what commercial insurers pay. Rural hospitals, already operating on thin margins, will be hardest hit, though the Public Citizen report makes clear that urban safety-net hospitals are equally exposed in California's specific case.

**Loss of Enhanced Premium Tax Credits in the ACA Marketplace.** H.R. 1 did not renew the Enhanced Premium Tax Credits that subsidised premiums for millions of Californians buying coverage through Covered California, the state's ACA marketplace. The Credits expired 31 December 2025. Absent federal action, over 660,000 Californians who relied on Covered California could lose coverage or face premium increases of seventy-five per cent or more.

### 1.3 The Hospitals at Risk

A report by Public Citizen, a nonprofit consumer advocacy organisation, identified **83 hospitals in California** as being at heightened risk of closing, cutting services, or laying off workers due to the federal funding cuts. California had the highest number of at-risk hospitals of any state — among 446 hospitals at elevated risk across 44 states and Washington DC.

The at-risk hospitals in California share a common financial profile: heavy reliance on Medi-Cal reimbursements and sustained thin or negative operating margins in recent years. In good times, these hospitals survive on the assumption that federal funding is reliable and the reimbursement model, however imperfect, stays intact. Remove thirty billion dollars a year from that model and the equilibrium collapses.

The distribution of at-risk facilities challenges a narrative that has been used to justify the cuts. The political framing around Medicaid reform in Washington has often invoked rural communities and "flyover country" as the intended beneficiaries of fiscal conservatism. In California, the at-risk hospitals are concentrated overwhelmingly in urban, densely populated, Democratic-leaning congressional districts: East LA, South LA, Anaheim, Santa Ana. Martin Luther King Jr. Community Hospital in Los Angeles — named for a civil rights leader and serving one of the city's most historically underserved communities — is among those flagged.

Three hospitals in San Diego County alone were identified as at elevated risk. The pattern repeats across the state: the communities most dependent on safety-net healthcare are the communities whose hospitals are most threatened.

### 1.4 Who Loses Coverage

The California Health and Human Services Agency projected that, under H.R. 1 as written, **up to two million Californians** could lose Medi-Cal coverage. The Cal Budget Centre, using Congressional Budget Office modelling, estimated the figure could reach 3.4 million over the following decade. These are not hypothetical people. They are:

- Children whose parents cannot afford private insurance;
- Elderly residents in care homes whose families cannot self-fund;
- People with chronic conditions — diabetes, heart disease, HIV — who rely on continuous Medi-Cal coverage to access maintenance medication and regular monitoring;
- Pregnant women in communities where private obstetric coverage is unaffordable;
- Undocumented immigrants who, under state-funded Medi-Cal extensions that preceded H.R. 1, had access to primary and preventive care.

Some immigrant populations — including victims of human trafficking and domestic violence who had been lawfully residing in California — will lose full-scope Medi-Cal eligibility starting 1 October 2026, retaining access only to emergency services.

The human mathematics of this are difficult to absorb in aggregate. In individual terms: a diabetic who loses Medi-Cal coverage and cannot afford private insulin at market rates faces a medical crisis within weeks. A child who loses preventive dental coverage under Medi-Cal may not see a dentist for years. A person managing a serious mental illness who loses their Medi-Cal-covered psychiatrist may end up in the emergency department — which is the most expensive and least appropriate setting for psychiatric care — or, in the worst cases, may end up homeless, incarcerated, or dead.

### 1.5 The Union That Decided to Fight Back

The Service Employees International Union United Healthcare Workers West is not a small organisation. It represents over **120,000 healthcare workers** in California — nurses, nursing assistants, medical technicians, hospital kitchen and laundry staff, home care aides. Its members work the overnight shifts at the safety-net hospitals now classified as at risk. They are the people who actually feel, in their daily working lives, what a surge in uncompensated care means: more patients presenting in worse condition because they delayed seeking care; more administrative burden because coverage has lapsed; more violence in emergency departments from patients in psychiatric crisis who have nowhere else to go.

In late 2025, SEIU-UHW decided to put that experience into a ballot initiative. The concept was straightforward, even if the execution was not: California has approximately 200 people with net worth exceeding one billion dollars. Their collective wealth has grown from approximately $300 billion in 2011 to over $2 trillion in 2025 — a roughly six-fold increase in fourteen years. A one-time five per cent levy on that $2 trillion would raise approximately $100 billion. That is, not coincidentally, approximately what H.R. 1 is projected to take out of California's healthcare system over the same period.

The initiative's lead spokesperson and SEIU-UHW Chief of Staff, **Suzanne Jimenez**, described the effort in terms of a contest between labour and capital that Californians would recognise from their own economic history: "A very small group of the most controversial billionaires on the planet tried to stop Californians from being able to save their local emergency rooms and hospitals — but our current signature tally proves frontline healthcare workers will prevail."

Whether that proves true in November 2026 is a separate question. What is not in dispute is that the signature campaign exceeded expectations dramatically: 1.5 million signatures against a required 875,000 is a margin of nearly two to one.

## Part 2: The Legal Text of the Initiative — What Would Actually Happen

### 2.1 The Official Title

The initiative is formally titled the **California One-Time Wealth Tax for State-Funded Health Care Programs Initiative**, or the **2026 Billionaire Tax Act**, bearing Attorney General file number 25-0024. It would add Section 37 to Article XIII of the California Constitution. This is significant: a constitutional amendment requires only a simple majority to pass by citizen initiative in California, but cannot be overridden by the legislature without returning to voters. If the measure passes, the legislature cannot repeal it. Only another ballot measure could.

Wait — actually, the initiative as drafted is a state statute, not a constitutional amendment. Let us be precise about this: under California Elections Code, an initiated state statute requires a signature threshold equal to five per cent of votes cast in the last gubernatorial election (which produced the 875,000 figure). It can pass with a simple majority. The legislature *can* amend or repeal it, but only with a two-thirds supermajority in both chambers and the Governor's signature. In practice, that is nearly as durable as a constitutional amendment given the current partisan balance at Sacramento.

### 2.2 Who Is Taxed

The tax applies to:

- **California resident individuals** with net worth of **$1 billion or more** as of **1 January 2026**;
- **Applicable trusts** with net worth meeting the same threshold.

The residency determination date — 1 January 2026 — is unusual. California's ordinary income tax residency rules examine domicile and time-spent factors over longer periods. This bright-line snapshot date creates a specific and well-defined group: if you were a California resident on New Year's Day 2026, and you were worth a billion dollars, you owe this tax.

As the Foley & Lardner legal analysis notes, taxpayers who were California residents at any point in 2025 should expect close scrutiny of their status as of 1 January 2026. State tax authorities will examine social media, frequent flier records, vehicle registrations, school enrolment records for children, country club memberships, and any other evidence bearing on domicile. California's Franchise Tax Board is famously aggressive in auditing claims of departure.

Proponents estimate approximately **200 California residents** are subject to the tax. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (ITEP) analysis concurs with that estimate, noting that Forbes data indicates 72 per cent of California billionaire wealth is held in the form of publicly traded stock, where valuation is straightforward and ownership is formally recorded with the SEC.

### 2.3 What Is Taxed

The tax applies to **worldwide net worth**, measured as the fair market value of all assets minus all liabilities, as of **31 December 2026** (note: the valuation date is end of year 2026, even though residency is determined as of 1 January 2026).

**Included in net worth:**
- Publicly traded stocks, bonds, and other securities;
- Interests in private companies and businesses;
- Other financial interests, including partnership interests, LLCs, trusts;
- Intellectual property with ascertainable market value;
- Artwork, collectibles, jewellery, and other personal property of significant value;
- Cash and cash equivalents.

**Excluded from net worth:**
- **Directly held real estate** (i.e., real property titled directly in the billionaire's name is excluded);
- Pension and retirement accounts;
- Real estate held through a business entity owned by a billionaire IS included — the exclusion is specifically for directly held property.

This real estate exclusion is notable for two reasons. First, it reduces the tax base somewhat: California billionaires own significant real estate. Second, it creates a potential planning opportunity — billionaires who hold real estate through corporate or LLC structures cannot benefit from the exclusion without restructuring, and the valuation date of 31 December 2026 gives limited time for complex restructuring.

**The charitable pledge rule** is an important anti-avoidance provision. A pledge to contribute to charity made **after 15 October 2025** does not reduce net worth for purposes of the tax. This prevents a simple manoeuvre: announcing a massive charitable pledge to reduce taxable net worth on paper while retaining effective control of the assets. However, completed charitable gifts made **before 31 December 2026** can reduce net worth. Billionaires who want to reduce their tax liability by giving to charity must actually transfer the assets, not merely promise to do so.

### 2.4 How Much Is Owed

The rate is a flat **five per cent** of net worth above the $1 billion threshold.

For a billionaire worth exactly $1 billion, the tax is $50 million.
For a billionaire worth $10 billion, the tax is $500 million.
For Jensen Huang at $167 billion, the tax is approximately $8.35 billion.

The tax is due in full in **2027**. Alternatively, taxpayers may elect to pay in five annual instalments — effectively 1% of their December 2026 net worth per year through 2031 — but with a modest deferral charge to compensate the state for the time value of money. The instalments option makes the tax more manageable cash-flow-wise and reduces the pressure to liquidate assets in a single tax year.

### 2.5 Where the Money Goes

Revenue is deposited into the **2026 Billionaire Tax Reserve Fund**, a new dedicated account. Spending from the fund is earmarked:

- **90 per cent** to a **Billionaire Tax Health Account**, restricted to healthcare services for the public;
- The remaining **10 per cent** to a **Billionaire Tax Education and Food Assistance Account**, to cover administration of the tax, K-14 public education, and food assistance.

The earmarking is legally significant. California's Proposition 98, which requires a minimum percentage of the General Fund to be spent on K-14 education, does **not** apply to this dedicated fund. This means the $100 billion raised goes on top of, not in lieu of, existing education funding — though the flexibility on the education sub-account does allow some of the ten per cent to be directed toward schools.

The health account is specifically intended to offset the Medi-Cal cuts from H.R. 1. Proponents argue this creates a direct, legible connection: Sergey Brin's tax payment funds the emergency departments his departure would otherwise help close. Whether that framing is persuasive to swing voters is a different question.

### 2.6 What Happens If You Try to Leave

The initiative's bright-line residency date — 1 January 2026 — means there is no point in leaving California *after* that date to avoid the tax. You were a resident on 1 January 2026. The liability is fixed. The state can pursue you for payment regardless of where you subsequently move, subject to the constitutional limitations on state taxation of non-residents.

This is actually one of the initiative's most notable features from a tax policy standpoint. Unlike an annual wealth tax, where departing mid-year might reduce liability, this one-time levy on a snapshot valuation date eliminates the "move before the tax year ends" strategy. The Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy made this point explicitly: "There is no economic incentive to move as any resident as of January 1st, 2026 has to pay the tax in full."

The anti-avoidance provisions include robust enforcement mechanisms specific to the small number of affected taxpayers. With approximately 200 billionaires subject to the tax, the Franchise Tax Board can dedicate substantial audit resources to each individual. There is no hiding in a large pool of similar taxpayers.

### 2.7 Constitutional Structure

The initiative amends the California Revenue and Taxation Code and, in some readings, requires a constitutional vehicle (Article XIII, Section 37) to insulate the revenue from existing formula spending requirements. The precise constitutional structure has been a subject of debate among tax lawyers, and the Foley & Lardner analysis flags that litigation is "all but certain post-enactment." We discuss the constitutional questions in depth in Part 7.

## Part 3: The Revenue Projections — How Much Will It Actually Raise?

### 3.1 The $100 Billion Claim

The initiative's backers project **$100 billion in one-time revenue**. This is the headline figure that appears in every press release and every news story. It has enormous emotional resonance — it matches, approximately, the projected cost of Medi-Cal cuts under H.R. 1 over the same period — and it has been endorsed by ITEP, whose analysis was prepared by George M. Yin, the Robert Kramer Research Professor of Law at the University of Virginia School of Law.

The ITEP analysis breaks down as follows:

- California has approximately 200 billionaires with collective net worth exceeding **$2 trillion** as of late 2025;
- Five per cent of $2 trillion is $100 billion;
- ITEP factors in a **ten per cent avoidance rate** (from restructuring, charitable giving, and other legal tax reduction strategies) and still arrives at approximately $90–100 billion collected over the five-year payment period;
- The analysis notes that 72 per cent of California billionaire wealth is in publicly traded stock, where valuation is straightforward and the tax base is relatively resistant to manipulation.

ITEP also notes that the ultimate revenue depends on stock market performance between now and 31 December 2026. The valuation date is end of 2026, not early 2026 when the initiative qualified for the ballot. If markets rise between April and December 2026, the tax base grows; if they fall, it shrinks. This introduces some uncertainty, but the basic order-of-magnitude estimate is robust.

### 3.2 What the LAO Says

The California Legislative Analyst's Office — a nonpartisan body that provides independent fiscal analysis of all ballot measures — offered a more cautious assessment. The LAO forecast **"tens of billions" in upfront revenue**, while specifically declining to endorse the $100 billion figure as a reliable single-point estimate.

The LAO's primary caution was behavioural: some billionaires would **move out of California** because of the tax, reducing the income tax revenue the state would otherwise collect from those individuals in future years. The LAO estimated this ongoing annual cost at potentially "hundreds of millions or more per year" — a number that, compounded over decades, could represent a significant cumulative loss.

There is an important asymmetry here that deserves careful attention. The LAO's concern about future lost income tax revenue is a real and legitimate point. But it requires some context:

- ITEP estimates California's 200 billionaires collectively pay approximately **$3 billion per year** in state income tax — about 2.5 per cent of total California income tax receipts, and about one per cent of the state's total annual revenue of approximately $300 billion;
- If even half of all 200 billionaires departed (which would be historically unprecedented), the annual income tax loss would be approximately $1.5 billion;
- This is a real number, but it needs to be weighed against the one-time revenue of $100 billion and the healthcare costs of *not* having that revenue.

The LAO's caution is genuine and the concern about behavioural response is worth taking seriously. But it does not, on the numbers, invalidate the case for the tax.

### 3.3 The California Tax Foundation's Warning

The California Tax Foundation — a business-aligned research organisation — produced a more pessimistic estimate. It projected that billionaire departures and the downstream economic ripple effects would reduce annual state tax revenue by between **$3.53 billion and $4.49 billion per year**.

This estimate assumes a substantially higher emigration rate than ITEP and uses multiplier assumptions about the broader economic impact of billionaire departures (reduced employment at their companies, reduced philanthropic spending in the state, etc.). Critics of the methodology note that many of the employment and economic effects of a billionaire's departure are uncertain — a billionaire who moves to Nevada or Texas may continue to employ the same people in California at the same Silicon Valley campus.

### 3.4 The Emigration Question in Historical Context

The fear that wealth taxes drive out wealthy residents is intuitive and politically powerful, but the empirical evidence is more nuanced than either side acknowledges.

**Evidence that emigration is limited:**

Studies of wealth tax implementation in France, Norway, and Sweden consistently find that the number of taxpayers who actually leave is small relative to predictions. France's ISF (Impôt de solidarité sur la fortune, the wealth tax in place from 1988 to 2017) is frequently cited as a cautionary tale — and it is true that a number of high-profile wealthy French citizens relocated to Belgium and Switzerland. But the magnitude of the effect was consistently smaller than projected.

More relevant to California specifically: the state's high income tax rates (13.3 per cent on the highest bracket — the highest marginal state income tax rate in the country) have been in place for years without triggering a mass exodus of high earners. Studies of the impact of California's Proposition 30 (2012), which raised the top marginal rate, found far smaller emigration effects than predicted.

For this specific initiative, the ITEP analysis makes the additional point that the **one-time nature** of the tax changes the emigration calculus fundamentally. An annual wealth tax creates a perpetual incentive to depart; a one-time tax, with a fixed residency determination date, does not. After 1 January 2026, there is no ongoing annual liability to motivate leaving. Those who did not leave before the deadline — the vast majority of California's billionaires — have no remaining tax-avoidance reason to depart after the fact.

**Evidence that some emigration occurred before the deadline:**

The initiative's opponents can point to specific, named departures. Google co-founders **Larry Page** and **Sergey Brin** both reportedly established residency outside California before the proposed 1 January 2026 effective date. Brin, who went on to become the largest single donor to the anti-tax campaign (contributing more than $40 million to Building a Better California in the first months of 2026), apparently viewed the residency deadline as worth acting on — though whether he would have remained in California absent the initiative is unknowable.

The Foley & Lardner legal analysis notes that "taxpayers who were California residents at any point in 2025 or 2026 should assume close scrutiny of their status as of New Year's Day." This is not an idle warning. California's Franchise Tax Board has historically pursued aggressive audit strategies for high-income individuals claiming to have changed their state of residency. The audits examine everything from where children attend school to where the individual's most prized personal possessions — paintings, cars, pets — are kept. Establishing genuine out-of-state residency is not a weekend project.

### 3.5 The Volatility Problem

One genuine technical challenge with a wealth tax on billionaires is that their wealth is concentrated in a small number of highly volatile assets — primarily the stock of one or a few technology companies. Jensen Huang's net worth fluctuates by billions of dollars in a single trading session when Nvidia's stock moves. Elon Musk's net worth, if he were a California resident (he is not), would be essentially a leveraged bet on Tesla and SpaceX valuations.

This means the aggregate tax base can change dramatically between the date the initiative is drafted and the 31 December 2026 valuation date. A sharp market correction between mid-2026 and year-end could reduce the aggregate tax base by twenty or thirty per cent. A sustained bull market could increase it by a similar margin.

The initiative handles this implicitly by using the end-of-year 2026 valuation date — giving maximum time for current market conditions to be reflected — rather than locking in an earlier date. But revenue uncertainty remains real, and the LAO is right to note it.

## Part 4: The People Behind the Campaign — For and Against

### 4.1 The Pro-Tax Coalition

**SEIU-UHW** is the organisational backbone of the campaign. With 120,000 members in healthcare settings across the state, it can mobilise ground-level canvassing operations and has a direct, immediate stake in the outcome: its members work in the hospitals that face closure under H.R. 1's funding cuts.

**U.S. Senator Bernie Sanders** (I-VT) endorsed the initiative and co-sponsored the federal analogue — the Make Billionaires Pay Their Fair Share Act — with Congressman Ro Khanna. Sanders has been a consistent advocate for wealth taxation throughout his political career, and his endorsement gives the California measure a national profile it might not otherwise have had.

**U.S. Representative Ro Khanna** (D-CA-17, Silicon Valley) is perhaps the most interesting supporter: a Democratic congressman representing the heart of Silicon Valley, the very ecosystem that produces the billionaires who would be taxed. Khanna's position represents a strand of progressive Democratic politics that believes technology wealth and social justice are not inherently in conflict — that the tech industry can and should pay for the social infrastructure from which it benefits. He has said plainly: "It's a matter of values. We believe billionaires can pay a modest wealth tax so working-class Californians have Medicaid."

**Jensen Huang, CEO of Nvidia**, has not formally endorsed the initiative but has publicly refused to oppose it. His Stanford Graduate School of Business comments — "Move to California, don't leave. It's the highest taxes in the world, but it's OK" — amount to the most consequential non-endorsement in California political history. A man with a $167 billion net worth who would personally pay approximately $8 billion under the initiative and who responds to that prospect with a shrug and a sales pitch for the state is a devastating counter-narrative for the opposition campaign.

### 4.2 The Anti-Tax Coalition

The opposition is dramatically better funded, at least in the early months of 2026.

**Building a Better California** raised more than **$80 million** in the first few months of 2026. The largest single contributor was **Sergey Brin** of Google, who contributed more than $40 million. Brin had already established non-California residency before the 1 January 2026 deadline — a fact that proponents note with some bitterness, arguing that the money opposing the measure comes largely from people who are no longer directly subject to it.

**Eric Schmidt**, former Google CEO, contributed millions to Building a Better California. Schmidt has been publicly active in opposing the measure, framing it as a threat to California's innovation economy.

**Michael Moritz**, a venture capitalist and co-founder and chairman of the SF Standard — the publication that broke several of the major stories about this initiative — contributed substantially. The SF Standard has disclosed this relationship but continues to cover the measure.

**California Business Roundtable** — a business advocacy group — received $3 million from **Peter Thiel**, Palantir co-founder, and $500,000 from **Chris Larsen**, Ripple chairman. Eric Schmidt donated at least another million to this group separately.

**Eric Schmidt and the broader opposition arguments** follow a consistent line: the tax will damage California's competitiveness, drive innovation out of the state, reduce the philanthropic giving of high-net-worth individuals, and create perverse incentives for future wealth accumulation in California.

**Notable opponents among elected officials:**

**Governor Gavin Newsom** has opposed the measure, arguing it risks pushing wealthy residents and the tax revenue they generate out of the state. Newsom has proposed alternative approaches to closing the healthcare funding gap but has been criticised for not being specific about what those alternatives would generate.

**Tom Steyer**, Democrat, hedge fund billionaire turned environmental activist and likely gubernatorial candidate, opposes this specific initiative. He has proposed levying new taxes on large corporations as an alternative. Steyer's position is awkward — he is a billionaire who in principle supports wealth redistribution but objects to this particular mechanism. He argues corporate taxes would produce more stable, predictable revenue.

**Congresswoman Katie Porter** supports taxing corporations while simultaneously cutting taxes on working families. She has not been a vocal opponent of the wealth tax but has not endorsed it either.

**Former Health Secretary Xavier Becerra** has acknowledged that billionaires paying lower effective tax rates than middle-class workers is inequitable, but argues that a one-time wealth levy is the wrong tool — structurally unsuited to addressing what he sees as a permanent structural funding problem that requires a permanent structural solution.

**San Jose Mayor Matt Mahan** has echoed Newsom in calling the billionaire tax a threat to innovation.

**The opposition argument in its strongest form** can be summarised this way: even if you believe billionaires should pay more taxes — and many of the initiative's opponents say they do — a one-time, narrow-base tax on approximately 200 individuals is the wrong instrument. A handful of relocations could materially alter the outcome. The revenue is volatile, because it depends on stock valuations on a single date. And it solves a structural problem (chronic healthcare underfunding) with a non-recurring revenue source, meaning the state will face the same crisis again in a few years without the same resource to draw on.

This is a serious argument that deserves serious engagement, which we do in Part 6.

## Part 5: The Numbers in Human Context — What $100 Billion Means

### 5.1 The Healthcare Gap in Concrete Terms

H.R. 1 is projected to remove approximately $30 billion per year from Medi-Cal funding. Over the five-year period during which the wealth tax would be collected (2027–2031), that represents approximately $150 billion in total federal funding reduction — more than the $100 billion the initiative would raise.

But timing matters. The most acute crisis is in the near term, as hospitals adjust to lower reimbursements and eligibility redetermination processes kick people off coverage. A large upfront revenue infusion — even one that does not fully cover the long-term gap — buys time for the state to find structural solutions, transition safety-net hospitals to more sustainable operating models, and lobby for federal changes.

The California Budget and Policy Centre has described the situation in terms of a "funding cliff": the state's healthcare system is walking toward the edge of a cliff, and the wealth tax revenue, if collected, would allow it to stop before it goes over. Whether it can then step back from the edge permanently is a different question.

### 5.2 Medi-Cal Numbers That Ground the Abstract

Fourteen million Californians are enrolled in Medi-Cal. That is approximately 35 per cent of the state's population of roughly 39 million. Consider what that number means:

- In Los Angeles County, with a population of approximately 10 million, roughly 3.5 million people are on Medi-Cal;
- Martin Luther King Jr. Community Hospital in Willowbrook serves a neighbourhood where the majority of residents rely on Medi-Cal. It opened in 2015 after its predecessor, King/Drew Medical Centre, closed amid controversy in 2007. If it closes again, the residents of Willowbrook face emergency care distances of thirty minutes or more;
- The 83 hospitals identified as at elevated risk employ tens of thousands of workers — SEIU-UHW members and others — whose livelihoods depend on the hospitals remaining viable;
- The CBO projects that nationally, H.R. 1's Medicaid changes will leave 10 million additional Americans uninsured by 2034. California's proportional share of that, if current conditions hold, is approximately 800,000 to one million people.

### 5.3 The Wealth Comparison That Grounds the Other Side

The ITEP analysis offers a perspective that the initiative's backers return to repeatedly: "The collective wealth of California billionaires has surged from $300 billion in 2011 to $700 billion in 2019 to over $2 trillion in 2025."

That is a six-fold increase in fourteen years. The five per cent wealth tax would collect, at most, $100 billion from a group whose wealth grew by approximately $1.3 trillion in the six years between 2019 and 2025 alone. Put differently: the tax would take back less than the typical annual gain of the taxed group, based on the historical average growth rate of their wealth.

The initiative's text makes this point directly: "The wealth tax imposed by this Act would only modestly slow this explosive growth, collecting less than the billionaires typically gain in just a single year."

Opponents will note that past wealth growth does not guarantee future growth, and that wealth held in concentrated stock positions can decline as rapidly as it increased. Both points are true. But the scale of the disparity between what is asked and what the affected group has accumulated is striking.

### 5.4 The Asymmetry of the Campaign Itself

There is a meta-level argument that proponents have begun to make explicitly: the fact that the anti-tax campaign has raised $80 million to defeat a measure targeting approximately 200 people is itself evidence for the measure.

Sergey Brin contributed more than $40 million — more than double the signature-gathering cost for the entire pro-tax campaign — to stop a vote on whether he should pay $8 billion in tax. He then left California. The campaign against this measure is, in a very direct sense, a display of the concentrated political power that concentrated wealth purchases. Whether that argument moves California voters in November is an empirical question. But it is not a frivolous argument.

## Part 6: The Economic Arguments — Deep Dive

### 6.1 The Case for a Wealth Tax

The theoretical case for taxing wealth rather than (or in addition to) income rests on several distinct arguments.

**The Haig-Simons framework.** In orthodox public finance theory, income is defined as consumption plus change in net worth. Under this definition, the appreciation of an asset is income regardless of whether it has been realised through a sale. A billionaire who holds $10 billion in stock and watches it grow to $11 billion has, in the Haig-Simons sense, $1 billion in income — but under current US tax law, owes zero income tax on that gain until the stock is sold. A wealth tax directly reaches this "unrealised income" that the income tax system misses.

The Ballotpedia commentary on this initiative includes a useful observation from Alex Skopic: "Ordinary people are taxed this way all the time. A property tax on the value of your house is also a tax on 'unrealised gains,' because you haven't actually sold the house and received the money." The property tax — which disproportionately falls on the middle class, whose wealth is concentrated in housing — is a wealth tax. The conceptual novelty of taxing billionaire wealth on the same basis is largely political, not economic.

**The wealth concentration problem.** Since the 1980s, wealth in the United States has become more concentrated at the top of the distribution. The Federal Reserve's Distributional Financial Accounts data consistently shows the share of total US wealth held by the top one per cent rising over this period. This concentration has consequences beyond inequality per se: concentrated wealth translates into concentrated political influence, concentrated market power, and concentrated media ownership. The California initiative's supporters argue that reducing wealth concentration — even modestly — has systemic benefits that extend beyond the direct revenue impact.

**The public goods argument.** Tech billionaires in California accumulated their wealth within a specific ecosystem: public university research (Stanford, Berkeley, UC San Diego) that generated the foundational technologies; public infrastructure (roads, ports, electrical grids) that enables commerce; public health systems that keep workers healthy; immigration policy that brought global talent to Silicon Valley. The ITEP analysis cites a 2025 California Law Review article arguing that the state is "uniquely positioned to address both the well-documented crisis of wealth inequality in the United States and the emerging and interrelated crises the state faces." The wealth tax is, on this view, a partial return on a public investment in the conditions that made extraordinary private wealth possible.

**The opportunity cost argument.** California spends substantial sums managing the consequences of inadequate preventive care: emergency department overcrowding, street-level psychiatric crises, the costs of incarceration for people who might have been diverted by better mental health and addiction services. Money spent on Medi-Cal is not simply a transfer to the poor — it is partly a reduction in future costs to everyone.

### 6.2 The Case Against a Wealth Tax

The theoretical case against wealth taxes is also serious and should be engaged honestly.

**Liquidity constraints.** A five per cent tax on net worth may exceed the liquid assets of some billionaires whose wealth is concentrated in illiquid private company equity or real estate. They cannot pay the tax without selling assets — which may force sales at depressed prices, harm other shareholders, or in extreme cases destabilise companies. The initiative attempts to address this with the five-year instalment option, but the instalment option still requires significant asset liquidation for truly illiquid wealth.

**Valuation difficulties.** Seventy-two per cent of California billionaire wealth is in publicly traded stock, where valuation is easy. But the remaining twenty-eight per cent involves private companies, partnership interests, real estate (to the extent it is taxable under this measure), intellectual property, and other assets where valuation is contested and expensive. Litigation over valuations is likely to be extensive and costly for both the state and taxpayers.

**The constitutional questions** are discussed in Part 7, but the short version is: courts have never definitively ruled on whether a state wealth tax is constitutional, and the initiative's opponents are likely to challenge it the moment it passes.

**The double-taxation argument.** Income from which wealth was accumulated was already taxed at the time it was earned (or when gains were realised). A wealth tax taxes the accumulated result of previous economic activity a second time. This is philosophically contested — proponents note that property taxes already do this routinely — but it is a real concern in a tax system designed to avoid discouraging investment.

**The structural vs. one-time mismatch.** Even opponents who are sympathetic to the goal of funding healthcare acknowledge that a one-time tax is a poor instrument for addressing a recurring funding gap. If H.R. 1's Medi-Cal cuts are $30 billion per year into perpetuity, a $100 billion one-time payment covers approximately 3.3 years. After 2031, when the last instalment is paid, the state faces the same structural problem without the same resource. Former Health Secretary Becerra's objection — that this is the wrong tool for the problem — is most compelling on this point.

**The innovation ecosystem argument.** Opponents argue that California's unique position as the world's leading technology innovation hub depends on a specific set of conditions, including a culture that allows extraordinary wealth accumulation. Whether a one-time tax of this scale materially affects that culture is genuinely uncertain. But there is a legitimate question about what signal a five per cent wealth tax sends to the next generation of entrepreneurs who have not yet started their companies. The argument is speculative — we cannot know what companies will not be founded — but it is not absurd.

### 6.3 The Evidence From Other Jurisdictions

**France's ISF (1988–2017).** France implemented a wealth tax for nearly three decades before President Emmanuel Macron replaced it with a narrower tax on real estate wealth only. The French experience is used by both sides. Opponents cite the departure of prominent wealthy individuals (most famously the actor Gérard Depardieu, though his departure had as much to do with criminal proceedings as with taxes). Proponents note that the ISF raised substantial revenue over its lifetime, that France did not experience the economic collapse predicted by opponents, and that the reform in 2017 was primarily driven by a specific argument about productive capital — the concern that the ISF was hitting investment in businesses as well as passive wealth.

**Norway's wealth tax.** Norway maintains an annual wealth tax of 1.1 per cent on net assets above approximately $170,000 USD equivalent. It has been in place for decades. Norway is also one of the richest countries per capita in the world, and has one of the highest rates of new business formation relative to population in the OECD. The Norwegian experience does not support the proposition that wealth taxes inevitably suppress entrepreneurship.

**Sweden** had a wealth tax from 1947 to 2007. Its abolition was driven by a specific concern about capital flight to lower-tax jurisdictions within the European Union — a dynamic that does not apply within the United States, where residents cannot escape federal tax by moving between states, and where California's tax authority over income earned in the state is robust.

**Washington state's capital gains tax.** Washington state has enacted a 9.9 per cent tax on capital gains for high earners, effective 2028. It is not a wealth tax per se, but it targets the same population from the income side and has faced similar constitutional challenges.

**Maine's millionaire surcharge.** Maine passed a 2 per cent income tax surcharge for millionaires. This has not produced the catastrophic results predicted by opponents; Maine's economy continues to function.

**Spain's temporary wealth tax.** Spain introduced a temporary 1.7 per cent wealth tax in 2023. It collected revenue, faced political controversy, and has been extended in modified form. The temporary framing has a parallel to California's one-time proposal.

The international evidence does not prove that wealth taxes always work well; implementation matters enormously. But it does disprove the strongest form of the opposition argument — that wealth taxes inevitably drive away all wealth and collapse economic activity.

## Part 7: The Constitutional Questions

### 7.1 Why Courts Will Almost Certainly Weigh In

The 2026 Billionaire Tax Act will face legal challenge immediately upon passage, if it passes. The Foley & Lardner analysis describes litigation as "all but certain post-enactment." The constitutional questions are genuine and unresolved, and understanding them is important for anyone trying to assess whether the measure would actually collect what its backers project.

### 7.2 The Due Process and Commerce Clause Concerns

**Due process.** The Fifth Amendment's due process clause, applied to the states through the Fourteenth Amendment, requires that taxation be rationally connected to the taxing state's interests. A tax on net worth that includes assets held entirely outside California — foreign stocks, overseas business interests — may face challenge on due process grounds. California taxes worldwide income for residents; the extension of this principle to worldwide wealth is legally novel.

**The dormant Commerce Clause.** Federal courts have interpreted the Commerce Clause to prohibit state taxes that discriminate against interstate commerce or that burden it disproportionately. A wealth tax that applies to assets in other states may raise Commerce Clause concerns. California's lawyers will argue that the tax falls on the resident, not the asset — the same theory that supports taxing worldwide income — but this has not been definitively tested for a net worth tax.

### 7.3 The Equal Protection Question

The initiative applies specifically to residents with net worth above $1 billion. This is a facially classification-based tax. Under the rational basis standard — the lowest tier of equal protection scrutiny, applied to economic regulations that do not involve suspect classifications — the classification merely needs to be rationally related to a legitimate government interest. Funding healthcare and reducing wealth concentration are both plausible legitimate interests. A rational basis challenge is unlikely to succeed.

But opponents have signalled that they will argue for heightened scrutiny based on the measure's narrow targeting of a small, identifiable group. This argument is creative but faces significant doctrinal obstacles; courts have consistently applied rational basis review to tax classifications.

### 7.4 The Constitutional Amendment Route

Some tax lawyers have argued that the initiative, as a statutory measure, should instead have been structured as a constitutional amendment to insulate it from legislative amendment and to provide a more durable foundation. The proponents chose the statutory route because constitutional amendments require the same majority to pass but are perceived as having higher barriers to qualification and a more complex drafting process.

Others argue the opposite: that a statutory measure is preferable because a constitutional amendment entrenching a novel tax creates difficult questions about future fiscal flexibility.

### 7.5 Federal Constitutional Limitations on State Wealth Taxes

There is no federal constitutional provision that explicitly prohibits state wealth taxes. The federal Constitution's direct tax clause — which required income taxes to be apportioned among states by population until the Sixteenth Amendment overrode it for income taxes — does not apply to state taxes. States have broad taxing authority under the Tenth Amendment, subject to the limitations noted above.

The strongest constitutional argument against the measure is probably the due process argument about extraterritorial taxation of assets with no connection to California other than the residency of their owner. Courts have sometimes struck down state taxes on this basis, and the issue is genuinely open for a net worth tax (as opposed to an income tax, where the source of the income can provide an independent nexus).

### 7.6 The Likely Timeline of Litigation

If the measure passes in November 2026, expect:
- An immediate challenge in California state court by November or December 2026;
- A parallel federal court challenge in early 2027;
- Potential injunctions staying collection while the litigation proceeds (which could delay the 2027 payment date);
- Appeals to the Ninth Circuit and potentially the U.S. Supreme Court;
- A resolution on the merits unlikely before 2029 or 2030 at the earliest.

This timeline creates real uncertainty for revenue projections. The state would be unwise to budget the $100 billion as committed revenue before the courts have spoken.

## Part 8: The Political Context — California in 2026

### 8.1 The Gubernatorial Race

Governor Gavin Newsom is term-limited and cannot run for re-election. The 2026 California gubernatorial race is open, and the wealth tax has become a litmus test for candidates.

**Newsom** has been consistent in his opposition, emphasising the risk of billionaire departures and offering vague commitments to alternative revenue measures. His opposition carries weight with the Democratic establishment but has drawn criticism from the progressive wing of the party.

**Tom Steyer**, the hedge fund billionaire and environmental activist who ran for the Democratic presidential nomination in 2020, is running for governor. His opposition to the initiative sits awkwardly with his self-image as a progressive and his own enormous personal wealth. His proposed alternative — new corporate taxes — is more popular with the party establishment but has not generated the same grassroots enthusiasm as the wealth tax.

**Katie Porter**, the congresswoman from Orange County known for her whiteboard questioning of executives during congressional hearings, has staked out a position supporting corporate tax increases and middle-class tax relief without specifically endorsing or opposing the wealth tax.

**Xavier Becerra**, former California Attorney General and later Health and Human Services Secretary in the Biden administration, has the most complex position: he explicitly acknowledges that billionaires paying lower effective tax rates than the middle class is unjust, but argues against this specific mechanism. His objection is substantive rather than political, which makes him harder for proponents to dismiss.

**Matt Mahan**, Mayor of San Jose, has aligned himself with Newsom's innovation-economy argument.

None of California's leading gubernatorial candidates support the initiative. This is a remarkable fact given that 52 per cent of California voters, according to a UC Berkeley Institute of Governmental Studies poll released in early 2026, support it. The gap between elite opinion and popular sentiment on this measure is stark — and revealing.

### 8.2 The National Context

The California initiative does not exist in isolation. Congressman Ro Khanna and Senator Bernie Sanders co-sponsored the **Make Billionaires Pay Their Fair Share Act**, which would impose a federal five per cent wealth tax on more than 1,000 billionaires based in the United States. The federal bill is unlikely to pass in the current Congress, but its introduction signals that the California initiative is part of a broader political movement, not an isolated experiment.

Washington state's 9.9 per cent capital gains tax on high earners, effective 2028, is moving forward. Maine's millionaire surcharge is in place. New York City Mayor Zohran Mamdani is supporting Governor Kathy Hochul's proposal for a pied-à-terre tax on homes worth $5 million or more — a more targeted wealth-adjacent measure. The direction of policy innovation in large blue states is unmistakable.

The California initiative, if it passes, would be the most significant state-level wealth tax in American history and would almost certainly accelerate legislative and ballot-measure activity in other states. If it fails — or if it passes but is struck down by the courts — it would represent a significant setback for the wealth-tax movement nationally.

### 8.3 The Polling

As of early 2026, the UC Berkeley Institute of Governmental Studies poll found:
- **52 per cent** of California voters support the initiative;
- **33 per cent** oppose it;
- **15 per cent** are undecided.

A majority in favour with 15 per cent undecided is a comfortable position for a ballot measure that requires only a simple majority. But ballot measures almost always lose undecided voters disproportionately as Election Day approaches, especially when the opposition is well-funded and runs a sustained advertising campaign.

The Foley & Lardner analysis, based on early polling, noted that support was "hovering below 50% and softening after voters hear arguments from both sides." This contradicts the UC Berkeley figure somewhat, suggesting that the numbers shift depending on how the question is framed — with support declining when voters are presented with the opposition's arguments about innovation and emigration.

The $80 million in opposition funding — against a pro-tax campaign that relied primarily on volunteer signature-gathering — will translate into a very large television, digital, and mail advertising operation. Modern California ballot measure campaigns routinely spend $20–30 million on advertising in the final six weeks. The opposition has the resources to far exceed that.

### 8.4 The Signature Campaign Itself as a Political Signal

The 1.5 million signatures — nearly double the required number — are not merely a logistical achievement. They are a political statement. Ballot initiatives in California frequently struggle to reach the signature threshold; barely clearing the bar is common. Blowing past it by nearly a factor of two, on a measure involving the taxation of two hundred individuals, reflects genuine popular engagement.

SEIU-UHW's ground operation for signature collection was built around the hospitals and healthcare facilities where its members work. Workers asked patients, visitors, and colleagues to sign. The framing — "sign this to keep your local hospital open" — is direct, legible, and emotionally grounded. It is a more effective message than "sign this because wealth inequality is a structural problem in late-stage capitalism."

## Part 9: The Human Stories Behind the Headlines

### 9.1 The Healthcare Worker's Perspective

Consider a nurse working night shifts in a safety-net hospital in the Watts neighbourhood of Los Angeles. She has worked there for eleven years. She knows her patients by name. She knows that many of them come to the emergency department for conditions that could have been managed in a primary care clinic — but primary care clinics in Watts are scarce, and for patients without Medi-Cal coverage, they are effectively inaccessible.

Under H.R. 1's funding cuts, her hospital faces the prospect of either dramatically reducing services or closing specific departments. The emergency department is the most expensive to operate and the least profitable under any reimbursement model. If cuts force a choice, the emergency department — the hospital's lifeline for the surrounding community — may be first to face reductions.

She signed the ballot petition. Her colleagues signed the ballot petition. Her patients, in some cases, signed the ballot petition. The 1.5 million signatures represent, in part, exactly this network of institutional care: healthcare workers who understand concretely what a funding cut means, and who are asking voters to tax two hundred people to prevent it.

### 9.2 The Billionaire Who Stayed

Jensen Huang's position is worth examining carefully because it defies the standard narrative about wealth and self-interest.

Huang was born in Taiwan, raised partly in Thailand, and emigrated to the United States as a child. He co-founded Nvidia in 1993 in a Denny's in San Jose — a detail he returns to repeatedly in public appearances, not as false modesty but as a genuine statement about the specific conditions that made his company possible. Silicon Valley, with its density of engineering talent (much of it produced by state universities), its culture of risk-taking, its proximity to both academic research and venture capital, enabled something that could not easily have happened anywhere else.

When Huang says California's high taxes are "OK," he is making an implicit argument about value for money. The public goods that made Nvidia possible — from UC Berkeley's engineering programme to the freeways that connect Silicon Valley to San Francisco to the cultural infrastructure of one of the most diverse and intellectually vibrant places on earth — cost money. Someone has to pay for them.

He is also making a calculation about where the best engineers want to live, which is relevant to Nvidia's competitive position. The best engineers, many of them drawn from global talent pools, disproportionately want to live in California. That preference is partly about the weather and the culture and partly about the concentration of other smart people and interesting companies. A California that imposes a one-time wealth tax on its two hundred wealthiest residents but maintains its public university system, its healthcare infrastructure, and its cultural richness is, on Huang's view, a better place to run a company than a Texas or Nevada that has lower taxes but less of everything else that makes California California.

### 9.3 The Billionaire Who Left

Sergey Brin's departure and subsequent $40 million donation to oppose the tax tells a different story. Brin, co-founder of Google, built his fortune in the same Valley as Huang and at the same public research institutions — Sergey Brin met Larry Page at Stanford, which receives substantial federal and state funding. He now lives elsewhere, and he has committed more money to stopping this tax than the entire annual budget of many California state programmes.

It is worth noting, without attributing bad faith, that Brin's $40 million donation is tax-deductible as a political contribution (certain types of political activity through non-profits may qualify for deduction, though direct campaign donations do not — the precise tax treatment depends on how the contribution was structured). He may receive a federal and state income tax benefit for the contribution. The net cost to him of $40 million in opposition spending may be substantially less than $40 million. The tax that he is opposing would cost him, on his pre-departure net worth, something in the range of $4–6 billion. He is spending $40 million to potentially avoid $4–6 billion. That is a reasonable investment even before any tax benefit on the contribution.

This is not an argument for or against the initiative. It is simply a description of the rational incentives at work.

### 9.4 The Working Families at the Centre

A 45-year-old woman in East Los Angeles manages her mother's Type 2 diabetes. Her mother is on Medi-Cal. Under the current system, she receives regular endocrinologist visits, continuous glucose monitoring supplies, and her insulin through the Medi-Cal formulary. Governor Newsom's insulin programme has reduced the cost of long-acting insulin pens to $11 for Medi-Cal patients — down from prices that were effectively inaccessible without coverage.

If her mother loses Medi-Cal coverage — as a result of the work requirement, the six-month redetermination, or any of the other eligibility restrictions in H.R. 1 — the cost of managing her diabetes without coverage would run to several hundred dollars per month at minimum, rising sharply if hospitalisations occur. A single diabetic crisis requiring hospitalisation costs tens of thousands of dollars. The family cannot pay that. The hospital cannot absorb it indefinitely without compensation from somewhere.

This is the concrete human reality that the 1.5 million signatures represent. Whether taxing two hundred people is the right way to address that reality is a policy question. That the reality is real is not in dispute.

## Part 10: Broader Context — Wealth Inequality in America and California Specifically

### 10.1 The Numbers Behind the Numbers

The initiative's text cites data on the growth of California billionaire wealth that, when examined in context, is genuinely striking:

- **$300 billion** in collective billionaire wealth in California in 2011;
- **$700 billion** in 2019;
- **Over $2 trillion** in 2025.

This is not primarily the result of new wealth creation through new businesses, though there is some of that. It is primarily the result of asset price inflation — the rise of the stock market, which has consistently outperformed GDP growth and wage growth since the post-financial-crisis recovery. The people who owned large quantities of stock in 2011 are much wealthier today not primarily because they worked harder or produced more economic value, but because the discount rate applied to future earnings has fallen (partly due to low interest rates that have since risen) and because the earnings themselves have grown.

The Federal Reserve's Distributional Financial Accounts show that the top 1 per cent of Americans hold approximately 30 per cent of all US household wealth. The top 0.1 per cent — the 130,000 or so households with net worth exceeding $38 million — hold approximately 18 per cent. California's concentration is somewhat higher, given its technology sector.

### 10.2 Why California Specifically?

California's billionaire population is not evenly distributed across industries. It is heavily concentrated in technology and, to a lesser degree, entertainment and real estate. This matters for the wealth tax debate because:

1. **Technology wealth is highly concentrated in equity.** A technology founder or early investor typically holds most of their wealth in the stock of one company. This creates both the high valuations that make the tax productive and the volatility that makes revenue uncertain.

2. **Technology wealth is geographically mobile in a way that other wealth is not.** A factory owner in Fresno cannot easily move the factory to Nevada. A technology founder who holds Nvidia stock can live anywhere. This is why the emigration concern is more salient for this population than it might be for, say, a wealth tax targeting agricultural landowners.

3. **The specific cluster effect.** Silicon Valley's productivity depends on proximity — on the informal network of venture capital, engineering talent, academic research, and startup culture that requires physical concentration. This is a partial offset to the mobility of individual billionaires: even if Jensen Huang moved to Nevada, Nvidia would still be in Santa Clara, employing tens of thousands of people who pay California taxes.

### 10.3 The Accumulation Problem and the "Benign Neglect" Hypothesis

One perspective on wealth inequality, advanced by economists including Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman at UC Berkeley, is that the extraordinary concentration of wealth since the 1980s is not the inevitable result of meritocratic market outcomes but is partly a policy choice — the result of tax and regulatory decisions that systematically favoured capital over labour and existing wealth over new wealth creation.

If that analysis is correct, then a wealth tax is not punishing success; it is partially unwinding a decades-long set of policy choices that allowed wealth to accumulate beyond what a properly functioning market and tax system would have produced.

Opponents of this analysis argue that it underestimates the genuine productivity growth enabled by technology, that the entrepreneurs behind California's billionaire class created real economic value, and that taxing the results of genuine value creation is counterproductive. Both positions contain truth. The argument is fundamentally about how much of the wealth accumulation is "earned" in a morally or economically meaningful sense, and that is not a question with a clean empirical answer.

## Part 11: What Happens in November — Scenarios and Consequences

### 11.1 Scenario A: The Measure Passes

If California voters approve the initiative in November 2026, the following sequence is likely:

**Immediate legal challenge.** Within days or weeks of certification of the results, expect a lawsuit filed in either California state court or federal district court (or both) seeking to stay implementation pending review. The most likely grounds: due process concerns about extraterritorial taxation, Commerce Clause arguments, and potentially a challenge to the valuation methodology. Courts may or may not grant an injunction; the balance of hardships argument (the state needs the revenue; the taxpayers can afford to post a bond) may favour denying an injunction.

**FTB rulemaking.** The California Franchise Tax Board would need to issue guidance on valuation methodology for complex assets (private company equity, partnership interests, etc.), the definition of residency, the treatment of trusts, and the mechanics of the instalment option. This rulemaking would take at least several months and would itself be subject to legal challenge.

**Compliance and payment in 2027.** Assuming the courts do not grant a stay, taxpayers would file returns and make payments starting in 2027. The Foley & Lardner analysis expects "intense rulemaking, and potential legislative cleanup if voters approve the measure."

**Revenue flows to the health fund.** If collections proceed as projected, the state would begin receiving substantial revenue in 2027, which could be deployed for Medi-Cal and related healthcare programmes.

**Long-term litigation.** Even if initial injunction requests fail, the constitutional questions will proceed to final resolution on the merits. It is plausible that the measure is ultimately upheld; it is also plausible that it is struck down on due process grounds as applied to assets with no California nexus. The uncertainty will persist for years.

### 11.2 Scenario B: The Measure Fails

If the initiative fails in November 2026, the immediate consequence is that the $100 billion revenue projection disappears and the state must address the Medi-Cal funding gap through other means.

The alternative mechanisms that opponents have proposed — corporate tax increases, changes to the state income tax structure, federal lobbying — are all real options but face their own political difficulties. Corporate tax increases in California require broad legislative consensus that has proved elusive; Newsom's proposed alternatives have been criticised for being vague. Federal lobbying to reverse H.R. 1 faces the reality of a Republican-controlled Congress for at least the near term.

The most likely outcome of a No vote is that California makes a series of painful cuts to Medi-Cal coverage and hospital reimbursement rates that fall short of the catastrophic scenarios projected by initiative supporters but are, on any honest assessment, very bad: fewer people covered, more uncompensated care, more hospital financial distress, more emergency department use for conditions that could be managed in primary care.

The defeat of the initiative would also signal to the national wealth-tax movement that this policy tool is politically unviable even in the most left-leaning large state in the country, and would likely slow or halt similar efforts in other states.

### 11.3 Scenario C: The Measure Passes But Is Struck Down

This is perhaps the most consequential scenario from a long-term policy perspective. If the initiative passes and is then invalidated by federal courts on constitutional grounds, the result would be:

- Several years of legal uncertainty during which no revenue is collected;
- A definitive ruling that informs (and constrains) future wealth tax attempts in all fifty states;
- Significant political backlash that could complicate progressive tax policy for a generation.

Proponents argue the constitutional case for the measure is strong. Opponents argue it is untested and fragile. The truth is that no one knows, because the Supreme Court has never directly ruled on a state wealth tax of this structure. The legal risk is real and deserves honest acknowledgment.

## Part 12: Practical Implications for Affected Taxpayers

This section is not legal or tax advice. It is a description of the practical landscape as of mid-2026. Anyone potentially subject to this tax should consult qualified California tax counsel immediately.

### 12.1 The Residency Question

The most important practical question for anyone potentially subject to the tax is: **were you a California resident on 1 January 2026?**

California uses a "domicile" test for residency. Your domicile is the state you intend as your permanent home. California also uses a "safe harbour" rule: if you were in California for more than nine months of the tax year, you are presumed a resident. For the one-time wealth tax, the relevant date is the snapshot of 1 January 2026.

The FTB will look at:
- Where you registered to vote;
- Where your vehicles were registered;
- Where your professional licences are issued;
- Where your closest family members live;
- Where your primary bank accounts are held;
- Where you received mail;
- Social media posts geolocated to California.

The standard for establishing non-California residency is high, and the FTB's audit capability for high-net-worth individuals is extensive.

### 12.2 Asset Valuation Strategy

For the twenty-eight per cent of California billionaire wealth that is not in publicly traded securities, valuation is a significant issue. Private company equity, partnership interests, and similar assets require qualified appraisals. The initiative's enforcement mechanisms include provisions for auditing valuations, and disputes will be resolved through administrative and ultimately judicial processes.

The charitable giving provisions deserve attention: completed charitable gifts before 31 December 2026 can reduce net worth for purposes of the tax. Charitable pledge rules as of 15 October 2025 are grandfathered in certain cases. Early consultation with estate planning counsel is advisable.

### 12.3 The Instalment Option

The five-year instalment option — paying approximately one per cent of December 2026 net worth per year from 2027 through 2031 — is likely to be attractive to taxpayers whose wealth is concentrated in illiquid assets. The deferral charge is designed to be modest, reflecting a borrowing-rate calculation, but the precise rate has not been set in the initiative text and will require rulemaking.

For taxpayers who can service the annual payments from income or dividends without significant asset liquidation, the instalment option preserves asset positions and avoids forced selling.

### 12.4 The Constitutional Hedge

Given the genuine constitutional uncertainty, some taxpayers and their advisors may consider filing under protest — paying the tax while simultaneously asserting constitutional objections — to preserve litigation rights. This approach is common in novel tax situations and would allow refund claims if a court subsequently strikes down the measure.

## Part 13: The Broader Question — What Kind of Society Do We Want?

The 2026 California Billionaire Tax Act is, at its deepest level, not a tax question. It is a question about the social contract in a period of extraordinary economic polarisation.

California has, simultaneously, some of the most valuable real estate and some of the largest homeless encampments in the world. It has produced more billionaires than any other state and some of the most severely underfunded public hospitals. It has the most innovative technology ecosystem in the world and among the most underfunded public school systems when measured by per-student outcomes in low-income communities.

The tax does not resolve these contradictions. Even $100 billion — an enormous sum — does not fully close a $30 billion per year structural funding gap if that gap persists indefinitely. The initiative's opponents are right that a one-time fix to a structural problem is insufficient on its own.

But the initiative's supporters are also right about something that the opponents' alternative proposals do not fully address: the problem is not merely insufficient revenue in the aggregate. It is that the distribution of who bears the cost of public goods — healthcare, education, infrastructure — has shifted dramatically toward the middle and working class over the past forty years, while the distribution of the benefits of economic growth has shifted dramatically toward the top. A tax that asks the two hundred wealthiest Californians to contribute, for the first time in a meaningful way, to the infrastructure from which they have benefited is not merely a fiscal measure. It is a statement about fairness.

Whether that statement is made by California voters in November 2026 will tell us something important not just about California but about whether democratic institutions can, in the current political environment, reach across the widest wealth gap in American history and ask the people at the top to help maintain the society that made them wealthy.

## Conclusion: The Vote That Will Tell Us Where We Are

On or around 3 November 2026, California voters will make one of the most consequential fiscal decisions in state history. The question on the ballot will be narrow — five per cent of net worth, 200 people, one time — but the implications are broad.

A Yes vote is a statement that the social contract in the wealthiest state of the wealthiest country has room to ask its most fortunate residents to contribute to the infrastructure from which they have benefited. It is also a bet that the constitutional challenges will fail or be navigated, that the revenue will be collected and deployed effectively, and that the signal sent to other states will accelerate progressive fiscal reform at a moment of acute need.

A No vote is a statement that even in California, even in 2026, even after 1.5 million citizens signed a petition asking for it, concentrated wealth retains sufficient political influence to block redistribution. It is also a bet — a bet that the alternative mechanisms proposed by the initiative's opponents will prove more effective, more durable, and faster to implement than a clean ballot-measure wealth tax.

Jensen Huang has already told us where he stands: in California, with full knowledge of what that costs. He is building a trillion-dollar company in the state and has no intention of leaving. The question is whether the 200 other people on the Forbes California billionaire list see the state the same way — and whether, if they do not, California voters believe their departure is worth more than the hospitals that might stay open in their absence.

The answers will be counted in November.
