## Where we are

Six days ago we stood on six aana of Bhaktapur-side land with nothing but a title deed and a budget ceiling. Over the week we walked through the bylaws and the naksa pass, we designed the floor plan, we cast the foundation and raised the reinforced concrete frame, we built the envelope with AAC walls and an insulated roof, we put in the plumbing and the electrical and the solar water heater, and yesterday we finished the interior — the tiles, the paint, the kitchen, the bathrooms, the tea shop fit-out.

Today, the house is finished.

The workers have packed up their tools. The last of the construction debris has been swept away. The final coat of paint has dried. The water runs. The lights turn on. The shutter rolls up. The kitchen sink has a cloth hanging on it that the cleaner left behind. The smell of fresh paint still hangs in the upstairs bedrooms. If you stood at the gate and looked in, you would see a house that is ready to be lived in.

But ready is not the same as yours. There is a difference between a house that has been built and a house that has been *commissioned, accepted, and moved into*. That difference is what Part 7 is about. We walk through the handover day — the systematic defects walk-through, the final paperwork with the contractor, the utility connections being energised, the move-in logistics. We right-size the HVAC based on how the envelope we built is actually performing, not on the generic rules of thumb that assume a poorly-insulated house. We set up the maintenance schedule that will keep the house healthy for the next twenty years. We look honestly at the tea shop as a small income stream and what its first year of operation will be like. We cover the property tax, rental tax, and insurance decisions that nobody briefed you on during construction. And we reconcile the final, honest budget against the NPR 2 crore hard ceiling that has governed every decision in the project.

We also, finally, step back and look at the whole seven-day arc from altitude. What the series covered, what it could not cover, and how to think about the house over the coming twenty years of living in it.

No programming. No code. Just the patient arithmetic of living in what you have built.

## Part 1. Handover day — the commissioning walkthrough

Handover day is the day you receive the keys from the contractor. It is also the single most important day for catching defects. Once you have signed the handover acceptance and paid the final instalment, the contractor's motivation to come back and fix problems drops sharply. Whatever is documented on handover day is what you can reasonably demand be fixed; whatever is missed is something you will fix at your own cost later.

Plan for handover day to take **three to four hours**. Bring a notebook, a torch, a cold cup of water (so you can test a tap), and if possible a friend who has built before and knows what to look for. Walk the house systematically — not dashing from room to room, but slowly, floor by floor, with a checklist.

Here is the checklist. Use it.

### Exterior and approach

1. **Gate and boundary wall.** The gate should open and close smoothly, latch cleanly, and not drag on the ground. The boundary wall should have no visible cracks, settling, or plaster defects.
2. **Drainage.** Pour a bucket of water on the driveway and watch where it goes. It should flow away from the house toward the street drain, not pool near the plinth. If there is ponding near any wall, the drainage grade is wrong.
3. **Façade paint.** Walk around the building at a distance. Look for paint thinning, brush marks, or uneven colour. The exterior paint should look uniform from 10 feet away.
4. **Roof parapet and drains.** Climb up to the roof. The parapet should be plumb, sealed on top, and free of cracks. The roof drain outlets should be clean and clear. Pour a bucket of water near each drain and watch it flow out.
5. **Tea shop shutter.** Roll it up. Roll it down. Roll it up again. Three times. Listen for grinding. Feel for sticky spots. The shutter should glide through its full range on a single pull.

### Water system

6. **Turn on every tap in the house in sequence.** Watch for flow at full pressure within 2 seconds of turning the tap. Weak flow means the overhead tank is not fully connected or a valve is partially closed. No flow means the tap is dead.
7. **Run each tap for 30 seconds.** Check for leaks around the base of the tap, around the trap under the basin, and at the wall joint.
8. **Flush every toilet.** Watch the cistern refill completely within 2 minutes. A toilet that refills slowly has a damaged inlet valve or is plumbed at the wrong diameter.
9. **Run the shower hot and cold.** The hot water should come on within about 20 seconds after the cold stream (the length of hot-water piping between the solar tank and the shower). If hot never arrives, something is wrong in the hot-water loop.
10. **Climb to the roof and verify the overhead tank.** Lift the lid. The tank should be full. The float switch should click when you depress it.
11. **Turn on the pump manually.** Listen for a healthy start-up hum without any mechanical grinding. Watch the overhead tank fill over 15–20 minutes.
12. **Check the solar water heater.** The temperature gauge should read the current water temperature (typically 50–65°C on a sunny afternoon).

### Electrical system

13. **Turn on every light switch in the house, one at a time.** Every light should come on within 1 second. None should flicker.
14. **Test every outlet.** Plug in the charger that you brought. Every outlet should work, and the charger's indicator light should come on.
15. **Press the RCCB test button.** The main RCCB should trip cleanly. Reset it.
16. **Test the earth pit.** The contractor should have the resistance measurement paperwork ready; if not, have an electrician come back with a tester.
17. **Run multiple loads simultaneously.** Fridge plus microwave plus geyser plus AC. The system should handle this without tripping the main breaker, flickering lights, or warming cables.
18. **Check the tea shop electrical sub-panel.** Separate meter reading should show zero consumption on day one (no load yet).
19. **Check backup inverter.** Turn off the main supply (the contractor can arrange this momentarily). The inverter should take over within 2 seconds and the lights should stay on. Run on battery for 30 minutes and confirm the system is stable.

### Interior

20. **Walk every room with your torch.** Shine the light along the walls, the floors, the ceiling joints. Paint defects, plaster cracks, tile edges that do not line up, grout gaps — note everything.
21. **Open every door, one by one.** Each door should swing smoothly, latch cleanly, and not rub the frame or the floor. Check the weather stripping on external doors for a clean seal.
22. **Open every window.** Each should operate smoothly through its full range. The lock mechanism should engage cleanly.
23. **Open every cabinet door, drawer, and wardrobe.** Soft-close mechanisms should work. Hinges should not creak. Drawers should slide smoothly.
24. **Run the kitchen sink tap. Run the kitchen chimney.** Both should work at full flow with no drips or leaks.
25. **Flush each toilet twice; run each bathroom exhaust fan.** Confirm they actually work.

### Documentation

Before you sign the handover, collect all of this documentation from the contractor:

1. **Naksa pass certificate** and municipal approval drawings.
2. **Completion certificate** (sampanna pramaan patra) from the municipality.
3. **Structural drawings and calculations** from the structural engineer.
4. **Electrical as-built drawings** showing the actual wiring and circuit layout.
5. **Plumbing as-built drawings** showing actual pipe routing.
6. **Soil test report** from Part 3.
7. **Warranty certificates** for every major installed item — water tank, pump, solar water heater, inverter, battery, windows, doors, kitchen cabinets, bathroom fittings.
8. **Appliance manuals** for every installed device — chimney, AC, inverter, geyser, solar water heater, water pump.
9. **Receipts** for every major purchase, for your own records and for warranty claims.

Organise all of this into a **"House File"** — a physical binder that stays in a single drawer in the house for the next twenty years. Every future repair, every insurance claim, every tax assessment, every resale conversation will benefit from having this file intact.

### The punch list

At the end of your walkthrough, you will have a list of 15 to 40 defects, ranging from trivial (a paint smudge in the staircase) to significant (a tap that does not turn off all the way). Hand the written list to the contractor and set a **two-week deadline** for completion of the punch list items. Hold the final 5% of the construction payment (typically 5% is customary in Nepali building contracts specifically for this purpose) until the punch list is complete.

When the contractor returns and completes the list, walk the house again with the list in hand. Tick off each item. Any item still not fixed stays on the list and on the retained payment. Most punch lists close out within two to four weeks of handover.

Do not sign the acceptance letter or release the final payment before the punch list is done. The leverage you have today disappears the moment you sign.

## Part 2. Right-sizing the HVAC

Now for the question that has been deferred across six days: how big should the air conditioner be, and where should it be installed?

The honest answer is that it depends on two things — the climate (which for Kathmandu is moderate) and your envelope (which for this house is deliberately above-standard). Because we built a better-than-standard envelope in Part 4 — AAC walls, XPS roof insulation, double-glazed UPVC windows, full weather stripping — our house loses and gains heat at a fraction of the rate a traditional brick-and-aluminium-window house does. **This lets us right-size the AC down one full step from the generic rules of thumb.**

### The arithmetic

The generic rule of thumb is **20 BTU per square foot of conditioned space**, with 1 ton of AC capacity equalling 12,000 BTU per hour. For a typical Kathmandu house with standard construction and 230 mm brick walls, this is a fair starting point — maybe slightly conservative on the generous side.

For our upgraded envelope, a better rule is **15 to 17 BTU per square foot.** The extra insulation reduces heat ingress by roughly 20–25%, and the AC does not have to work as hard. Let us apply both rules to the two rooms where we want AC:

**Master bedroom.** Approximately 180 sq ft, with two exterior walls (south and east), double-pane UPVC windows totalling about 24 sq ft, and no significant internal heat load.

- Generic rule: 180 × 20 = 3,600 BTU → 0.5 ton is too small; 1.0 ton (12,000 BTU) is comfortable oversizing.
- Envelope-adjusted: 180 × 16 = 2,880 BTU → 0.75 ton (9,000 BTU) is well-calibrated.

**Living room.** Approximately 350 sq ft, with one large exterior wall (south) and a balcony glass door, plus some internal heat load from the kitchen during cooking.

- Generic rule: 350 × 20 = 7,000 BTU → 0.75 ton marginal; 1.0 ton well-calibrated.
- Envelope-adjusted: 350 × 17 = 5,950 BTU → 0.75 ton (9,000 BTU) adequate; 1.0 ton comfortable.

**Recommendation for your house:** one **0.75-ton split AC in the master bedroom** and one **1.0-ton split AC in the living room.** Both as inverter units with a 5-star energy efficiency rating.

### Choosing the brand

As we discussed briefly in the reference material at the start of the series, the Nepali AC market has four broad brand tiers:

- **Premium.** Daikin, Mitsubishi. Best energy efficiency, quietest operation, longest compressor life (typically 12–15 years). NPR 90,000–140,000 for a 1-ton inverter split. Worth the premium for the master bedroom, which is the room you are most sensitive to on a bad sleep night.
- **Mid-premium.** LG, Samsung. Reliable, good efficiency, decent after-sales network in Kathmandu. NPR 65,000–90,000 for a 1-ton inverter.
- **Mid-range.** Gree, Haier, Blue Star. Chinese-origin brands with growing presence in Nepal. NPR 50,000–70,000. Acceptable.
- **Budget.** Himstar, Sansui. Economical but iffy longevity. NPR 35,000–50,000. Avoid unless budget is truly constrained.

For your house, **Daikin 0.75-ton for the master bedroom (NPR 80,000) and LG 1.0-ton for the living room (NPR 75,000)**, plus installation at NPR 8,000–12,000 per unit, is a sensible combined spec. Total: **NPR 175,000** for both AC units installed.

### Annual running cost math

A 1-ton inverter AC running 6 hours a day during Kathmandu's 6–10 hottest weeks of the year consumes roughly 6 units per day. Six weeks of 6 units per day = 252 units per summer. At NEA commercial-adjacent domestic tariff (around NPR 8 per unit on average for a household in our consumption range), that is roughly **NPR 2,000 per summer per AC unit.** For two ACs, NPR 4,000 per year.

This is not a big number. The insulation upgrades in Part 4 are paying for themselves not by eliminating the AC, but by making the AC work less often and for less time per day. In a non-insulated Kathmandu house, the same 1-ton AC would consume 10–12 units per day for the same comfort — roughly double. That difference is your Part 4 envelope returning the investment.

### Heating — the equally important other season

Kathmandu winters go down to 2–5°C indoors in an uninsulated house. A well-insulated house with a small active heater might hold 15–18°C. That is a qualitative difference — the house goes from "we wear sweaters and huddle" to "we wear shirts and function."

For heating, three options:

**Electric room heater.** Portable, NPR 3,000–8,000 per unit. Effective for a single room, expensive to run (1.5–2 kW for 4–5 hours a day in January = 6–10 units per day). Each January month at this rate adds NPR 1,500–2,500 to the electric bill.

**Gas heater.** LPG-powered. NPR 8,000–15,000 per unit. Warmer output for the energy, but emits combustion products indoors (CO2 and water vapour), so the room must be ventilated. Safety risk if vented improperly.

**Reverse-cycle AC (heat pump).** The AC units we just specified can also heat. A 1-ton inverter AC running in heating mode uses roughly 70% of the electricity it uses for cooling at the same capacity. For the same 6 hours of operation per day in January, about 4 units per day, or NPR 800–1,000 per month. **This is the lowest-running-cost heating option for Kathmandu winters,** and it requires zero additional equipment beyond the ACs we are installing.

For your house, rely on the **reverse-cycle AC function in the master bedroom for winter nights** and the **reverse-cycle AC in the living room for winter evenings.** Supplement with a single portable electric heater for the common bathroom on cold mornings — the smallest investment that deals with the practical reality that nobody wants a cold bathroom at 6 AM in January.

## Part 3. Moving in — the first two weeks

The house is yours. Here is what happens next.

**Day 1.** Move the essentials in — mattresses (so you can sleep), a kettle and two cups, cleaning supplies, toilet paper, towels. Do not try to move everything in on day one. A minimalist first night is fine.

**Days 2–7.** Live in the house. Not camp in it; live in it. Cook a meal. Run every system. Turn on the AC for a few hours in the afternoon. Run the washing machine once. Take a shower. Make tea. Feel where the light falls in the morning, where the breeze comes from in the evening, which spot in the living room is the comfortable one to read in. This week is for calibration — understanding how the house actually behaves as a house, not as an architect's drawing.

**Week 2.** Start moving in furniture and belongings. The order matters: bedrooms first (you need to sleep), kitchen second (you need to cook), living room third (it can be sparse for a while), storage last. Resist the urge to fill every space; live with some emptiness for a month and see what furniture you actually need.

**The two-week defect re-check.** At the end of two weeks, walk the house again with the handover punch list. Anything that the contractor fixed after handover — did the fix hold? Anything new that has emerged in two weeks of living — a sticking door, a loose tile, a leaky tap, a light switch that feels wrong — add to the list. Call the contractor back for a final round of fixes. Most contractors honour this; it is a good practice to set the expectation at handover that there will be a two-week check.

### What to buy right away

Some items you cannot move into the house without. Others wait.

**Immediately (within the first week):**

- A reliable internet connection. You cannot order anything or arrange deliveries without one. NPR 1,500–2,500 per month for a good fibre package in Kathmandu.
- A refrigerator. You need to store food within two days of moving in. NPR 40,000–80,000 for a family-sized 300-litre unit. Samsung, LG, Hitachi, or Whirlpool are all reliable in Nepal.
- A washing machine. You can wash clothes by hand for a while, but not happily. NPR 25,000–50,000 for a front-load 6–7 kg machine.
- A gas cylinder and connection. LPG delivered to the kitchen counter. NPR 1,800 per refill, lasts 4–6 weeks for a family of five.
- Basic kitchen utensils.
- Beds and basic bedding.
- Basic dining furniture (a table and four chairs is enough to start).

**Within the first month:**

- Sofas and living-room furniture. Do not rush this — sitting in a sparse living room for a few weeks helps you understand what you actually want.
- Dining table and chairs (upgrade from the initial basic set).
- Curtains and blinds. Off-the-shelf curtains are fine for two bedrooms (NPR 3,000–8,000 per window); custom tailoring is NPR 500–1,500 per running foot of fabric.
- Wardrobes (if not built-in from Part 6).
- A front doormat.
- A water filter under the kitchen sink. NPR 5,000–12,000. Even with municipal supply, a final-stage filter gives you confident drinking water.

**Within the first three months:**

- Artwork, plants, decorative items. These come last, when you know the house.

### Budget reserve for move-in

From the Part 1 budget framework, we held NPR 40 lakh in reserve against the NPR 2 crore ceiling. Of that reserve, allocate:

- **NPR 8–12 lakh for furniture and appliances** (refrigerator, washing machine, sofas, dining set, beds, curtains, kitchen items).
- **NPR 3–5 lakh for utility connection fees, municipal completion fees, meter deposits.**
- **NPR 5 lakh for construction overruns that materialised and are being settled now.**
- **NPR 3–5 lakh as a genuine contingency** for unforeseen first-year expenses.

That leaves roughly NPR 15–20 lakh of the reserve genuinely unused — a real cushion against surprises in years one and two. Most Kathmandu households find they spend this down over the first three to five years on incremental upgrades, appliances, and the terrace-room phase-2 construction we deferred in Part 2.

## Part 4. The maintenance schedule — twenty years on one page

A house that gets no maintenance fails slowly, invisibly, and expensively. A house that gets the right maintenance at the right intervals lasts fifty years. The maintenance schedule is not glamorous. It is, however, the single most important thing you can do to protect the investment we have just completed.

### Annually (every year)

- **Underground water tank cleaning.** Open the manhole cover, pump the residual water, scrub the interior with a mild chlorine solution, rinse, refill. NPR 3,000–5,000 if done by a professional cleaning service; free if you do it yourself. Late February is the best time, after the dry season has reduced tank levels.
- **Overhead water tank cleaning.** Same process, smaller tank. NPR 2,000–3,000.
- **Submersible pump inspection.** Check the electrical connections for corrosion, listen for abnormal motor noise, verify the float switches are still operating. NPR 1,500–3,000 if done by a technician.
- **Exterior paint wash.** A soft-bristled brush and mild soap on the façade walls, focussing on the mud and dust accumulation on the lower 6 feet. Keeps the paint looking fresh and extends its life by 3–5 years.
- **Gutter and drain cleaning.** Remove leaves, dust, and debris from every roof drain and every ground-level drain. Essential before each monsoon.
- **Electrical safety check.** Have an electrician test the earth pit resistance, verify the RCCB, inspect all MCBs for signs of heating. NPR 2,000–4,000 annual service call.
- **Solar water heater inspection.** Check the vacuum tubes for breakage, verify the thermostat is working, drain and refill the tank. NPR 1,000–2,000.
- **AC service.** Clean the filter, check refrigerant levels, clean the outdoor unit. NPR 1,200–2,000 per unit, once before summer.
- **Termite inspection.** Nepal has termites. Inspect external wall bases and wooden doors for mud tubes. If found, chemical treatment costs NPR 15,000–40,000 and the earlier the better.

Annual maintenance total: **NPR 15,000 to NPR 30,000 per year** depending on how much you do yourself versus hire out. Over twenty years, that is NPR 3–6 lakh cumulatively — less than 3% of the original build cost. Non-negotiable.

### Every 3–5 years

- **Interior paint refresh.** Touch up high-wear areas (kitchen, hallways, children's rooms). NPR 30,000–50,000. Every 5 years for full repaint of one floor; every 10 years for the entire house.
- **Bathroom re-sealing.** Replace silicone sealant around basins, shower trays, and tile edges. NPR 8,000–15,000 per bathroom.
- **Exterior paint inspection.** If fading or peeling is noticeable, re-paint externally. Every 6–8 years is typical. NPR 1.5–3 lakh for the whole exterior.
- **Pump replacement consideration.** Submersible pumps typically last 8–12 years in Kathmandu water conditions. After year 8, budget for replacement.
- **Battery replacement for the inverter.** Lead-acid battery banks last 3–5 years; LFP banks 8–12 years. Budget NPR 40,000–80,000 for lead-acid replacement, NPR 120,000–200,000 for LFP replacement.
- **Water tank replacement.** HDPE tanks typically last 10–15 years; SS304 tanks 25+ years. After year 12, watch the HDPE tank for cracking or discolouration.

### Every 10 years (mid-life)

- **Full interior repaint.** The tired-but-not-failing interior gets refreshed. NPR 3–5 lakh.
- **Roof waterproofing inspection and re-coating.** Reflective elastomeric roof coating typically needs refreshing at the 8–12 year mark. NPR 50,000–1 lakh.
- **Solar water heater tank replacement.** After 15 years, the SS304 tank of most solar water heaters starts to show corrosion. Budget NPR 40,000–70,000 for the full unit.
- **Electrical panel upgrade** (if needed). Modern loads may have grown; verify that the main breaker and main cable are still adequate.
- **Kitchen modernisation** (optional). After 15 years, the modular kitchen is dated even if it is still functional. An interior refresh of doors and countertop (without replacing the carcass) runs NPR 1.5–3 lakh.

### Every 20–25 years (major)

- **Bathroom renovation.** Tile styles date; sanitary ware aging. Full bathroom overhaul runs NPR 1.5–2.5 lakh per bathroom.
- **Window refurbishment or replacement.** UPVC windows last 20–25 years; at that mark inspect seals and hinges. Replacement costs roughly the original installation cost.
- **Foundation and structural inspection.** A structural engineer inspects for cracks, settlement, and any signs of distress. NPR 10,000–25,000 for the inspection. The structural integrity decision.

Over twenty years, typical total maintenance and mid-life refresh spending on a house of this size runs **NPR 12–20 lakh**, or about 8–12% of the original build cost. Large in absolute terms; modest as a percentage of protecting the investment.

## Part 5. The tea shop as an income hedge

Now a long-deferred conversation: **the tea shop on the ground floor.**

In Part 1 we described the tea shop as "an income hedge" and built the commercial meter and the separate sub-panel around this. Now that the house is operational, let us think concretely about what the shop is and is not, what it will earn, and what it will cost.

### What a tea shop is

A typical Kathmandu-neighbourhood tea shop sells:

- **Tea** (chai, milk tea, black tea) at NPR 15–30 per cup, perhaps 50–80 cups a day.
- **Coffee** at NPR 30–50 per cup, perhaps 10–15 cups a day.
- **Snacks** — sel roti, samosas, biscuits, simple sandwiches — at NPR 20–80 per item, perhaps 30–50 items a day.
- **Packaged goods** — soft drinks, packaged biscuits, chips, cigarettes — at varying prices, perhaps NPR 500–1,500 per day turnover.

### What it will earn

A modest tea shop in a Bhaktapur residential neighbourhood, operating from 6 AM to 8 PM, staffed by one or two family members (so no external labour cost), can realistically gross:

- Tea: 65 cups × NPR 20 = NPR 1,300 per day
- Coffee: 12 cups × NPR 40 = NPR 480 per day
- Snacks: 40 items × NPR 40 = NPR 1,600 per day
- Packaged goods: NPR 800 per day

**Total daily turnover: roughly NPR 4,180.**

Monthly turnover at 25 working days: **NPR 1.05 lakh**.

Annual turnover: **NPR 12.5 lakh**.

This is a modest small-business number. It is not a fortune. But it is also not nothing.

### What it will cost

From that turnover, subtract:

- **Raw materials.** Tea leaves, sugar, milk, coffee, snack ingredients, packaged goods purchase price. Roughly 60% of turnover = NPR 7.5 lakh annually.
- **Fuel.** LPG, stove, minor fuels. NPR 15,000–30,000 annually.
- **Utilities.** Electricity for the shop (refrigerator + lights + kettle + mixer). NPR 3,000–5,000 per month = NPR 35,000–60,000 annually.
- **Cleaning, consumables, incidentals.** NPR 10,000–20,000 annually.
- **Rental tax** (see next section). If the shop operates informally within a family-owned building, rental tax does not apply (no rental is being paid). But other local taxes do — a small registration fee, maybe a small business profit tax.

**Total annual operating costs: roughly NPR 8.2–8.8 lakh.**

**Net annual profit: roughly NPR 3.5–4.5 lakh.**

That is about NPR 30,000–37,500 per month of net income from a part-time family effort. It will not make anyone rich. It is also solid, recession-proof income that pads out a household budget, and it fills the ground floor of a three-storey house that would otherwise be half-used.

### The five-year view

The tea shop has another, less obvious benefit: it is a **path toward something bigger if the family chooses**. Over five years, a well-run tea shop might:

- Expand the menu — hot food items, takeaway lunches.
- Add a small outdoor seating area on a previously-empty part of the plot.
- Formalise into a registered business (if turnover crosses NPR 50 lakh annually, VAT registration becomes legally required; below that, the informal model works fine).
- Build a neighbourhood relationship that supports other business activities — delivery, catering, event snacks.

Alternatively, the tea shop might stay modest, profitable, and unchanged for twenty years. Both outcomes are acceptable. The point is that the ground floor is earning rather than sitting idle.

### The risk side

Three risks to flag:

1. **Neighbourhood change.** If a chain coffee shop opens three doors away, your margins will compress. Adapt or accept.
2. **Family labour availability.** The tea shop requires someone to be there from 6 AM. If the family member who runs it moves away, leaves for college, or cannot continue, the shop either closes or needs hired help at NPR 15,000–25,000/month in wages — which approximately eliminates the profit.
3. **Regulatory change.** VAT thresholds and small-business taxes can change. Stay informed; a sudden mandatory registration requirement can turn a profitable informal shop into an unprofitable formal one overnight.

## Part 6. Property tax, rental tax, and insurance

Three financial obligations that new homeowners in Kathmandu Valley should know about.

### Property tax (Sampati Kar / Ghar Jagga Kar)

Every property in Kathmandu Valley is subject to an **annual property tax** assessed by the local municipality. Post-2017 reforms integrated separate land and house taxes into a single property tax.

Rates vary by municipality and by assessed value bands, typically ranging from **0.05% to 0.5% of the assessed property value** for residential properties, with higher rates for commercial or mixed-use. The Kathmandu Metropolitan City reference for a property worth NPR 1 crore is roughly 0.01% minimum = NPR 1,000 minimum, scaling up from there. Mixed-use properties are typically assessed at rates 20–50% higher than purely residential.

For your house in the Bhaktapur side (likely Bhaktapur Municipality, Madhyapur Thimi, Suryabinayak, or Changunarayan depending on ward), expect **NPR 3,000 to NPR 15,000 per year** in property tax for a residential-mixed-use property of this size and age.

**Important: Nepal typically allows a 2-year construction grace period** before property tax kicks in on a newly-completed building. The clock starts when the municipal completion certificate is issued. So for your house, property tax first falls due in year 3.

Miss the deadline (usually Ashad end, mid-July) and penalties can add 10–25% to the tax amount. Pay annually and on time. Kathmandu Metropolitan City offers online payment; most valley municipalities do too. Budget about NPR 10,000 per year for property tax and keep the receipt in your House File.

### Rental tax (if you rent out any portion)

If you rent out any portion of the house — the tea shop to a tenant, a bedroom to a boarder, the terrace to a telecom antenna — **rental income is taxable at 10% at the local level in Kathmandu Metropolitan City**, with rates ranging 10–17% depending on municipality.

The tax is paid to the ward office where the property sits. Filing is annual, due end of fiscal year.

For your specific situation — if the family runs the tea shop themselves — there is no rental income and no rental tax. If you ever lease the shop to an outsider (say you want to stop running it and collect NPR 20,000/month from a tenant), budget 10% = NPR 24,000/year in rental tax.

### Home insurance

Home insurance is not universal in Nepal, but it is widely available and increasingly recommended, especially after the 2015 earthquake.

Three types of coverage to consider:

**Fire and allied perils insurance.** Covers damage from fire, flood, earthquake, vandalism. **Annual premium: 0.1–0.3% of sum insured.** For a NPR 1.6 crore replacement value, that is NPR 16,000–48,000 per year. Reasonable, and a prudent investment. Major insurers in Nepal offering this coverage include Nepal Insurance Company, Shikhar Insurance, Siddhartha Insurance, and several others.

**Earthquake-specific coverage.** Sometimes bundled, sometimes separate. Verify the terms carefully — some "earthquake" coverage has a large deductible that makes small-to-moderate claims uneconomical.

**Contents insurance.** Covers furniture, electronics, jewellery, and personal effects. Usually a separate rider, 0.3–0.5% of contents value per year. For a household with NPR 10 lakh of contents, NPR 3,000–5,000 per year.

**Recommendation:** a basic fire-and-earthquake policy on the house structure only, NPR 20,000–30,000 per year. Add contents coverage if you have significant electronics or jewellery. Skip the premium comprehensive packages that try to cover every possible risk; most of them have complicated exclusions that limit their practical utility.

## Part 8. What this series could not cover

A seven-part series of 50,000-odd words is a lot of material. It is also very much not everything.

The series did not cover:

**Negotiating the contract with the main contractor.** This is a topic that deserves its own dedicated article — the specifics of lump-sum versus cost-plus, the clauses around delay liability, the mechanics of progress payments, the inspection regime, the change-order process. We flagged it in Part 1 but did not go deep.

**The psychology of a long construction project.** A fifteen-month build has ups and downs. There will be a monsoon week where nothing happens. There will be a contractor conflict. There will be a day in month nine when the whole thing feels impossible. Managing your own morale and that of your family over this arc is a skill that nobody writes about but that matters a lot.

**Regional variations within Kathmandu Valley.** Building in central Kathmandu is different from building in Bhaktapur, which is different from building in Godawari or Changunarayan. We focused on Bhaktapur but most of the principles translate to other wards. Specific bylaws and soil conditions do not.

**Retrofitting.** This series assumes new construction on empty land. Building an addition onto an existing house, renovating a decades-old Newari building, or converting a commercial building to residential are all different projects with their own specific constraints.

**The detailed science of structural engineering.** We gave you enough vocabulary to have an informed conversation with your structural engineer. We did not teach you to be one. Hire a registered one.

**Advanced sustainability — full passive house design, high-performance heating systems, greywater recycling.** These are possible and increasingly affordable, but they require specialist design and a budget ceiling well above NPR 2 crore. If you are building to those standards, a different series would be needed.

**The long tail of cultural, family, and spiritual considerations.** Vastu, bhumi-puja, the ritual first brick, the timing of the moving-in date. These are real considerations that Nepali families weight carefully, and we did not address them because they are highly individual. Your family, your tradition, your decisions.

**Furniture and interior styling.** We said a few words in Part 6 and Part 7, but this is effectively a whole separate discipline. The series gave you a functioning house; making it a home is its own project.

## Part 9. Looking back, at twenty-year altitude

If you built this house in 2026 and hold it for twenty years, by 2046 here is what the house has become.

It has weathered one monsoon after another, each one testing the roof and the drainage. None has ever leaked, because we invested in proper waterproofing and roof insulation.

It has been through at least one full interior repaint, possibly two. The colours have shifted with family taste — the yellow in the children's room from 2028 is now a calm blue for the grandchildren's visits in 2044. The envelope, the structure, the foundations have not moved. The framework is unchanged.

The tea shop has either expanded, stayed the same, or closed. If it expanded, the ground floor now contains a small but profitable neighborhood business that became one of the family's financial anchors. If it closed, the space became a home office or a guest bedroom. Either outcome is fine because we designed the space flexibly.

The solar water heater has been replaced once, around year 15, with a newer and more efficient model. The water tanks have been replaced once. The submersible pump has been replaced twice. Each replacement was straightforward because the house was designed for service access.

Kathmandu has, almost certainly, seen at least one moderate-to-significant earthquake in this twenty-year span. The house stood. Some plaster cracks appeared and were patched. The structure, designed to NBC 105:2025, absorbed the motion as intended.

Energy costs rose, slowly, over twenty years. The envelope upgrade in Part 4 means your electricity bill in 2046 is roughly half of what your cousin's (in his uninsulated traditional house down the street) is. Over twenty years of bills, that envelope upgrade has paid for itself three or four times over.

The NPR 11 lakh reserve from handover day is long gone — spent on the terrace room in year 3, furniture upgrades in years 5 and 10, kitchen refresh in year 15. But it was all spent purposefully, not scrambled together during emergencies.

And somewhere around year 18 — on a quiet Thursday afternoon, perhaps in June, not unlike the day this series began — you walk into the house, sit at the kitchen table, and realise the house has outlived any of the small decisions that went into it. The conversation about AAC versus brick. The debate about UPVC windows. The pushback on the contractor over stirrup spacing. The struggle to keep the flooring budget under 3.6% of the total. All of it, now, is simply *the house*. Invisible. Permanent. Yours.

That is what this series was for.

## Part 10. A last note on why this matters

We did not build a mansion. We did not build anything ambitious. The house we designed, costed, and commissioned together is a **modest family home** — three floors, 3,840 square feet, a small ground-floor shop, two bedrooms for a family of five, total cost within a NPR 2 crore hard budget.

What we did do is **refuse to compromise in the places where compromising would matter later.** The foundation was designed to the soil test. The structure was built to the code. The envelope was insulated. The windows were UPVC double-glazed. The weather stripping was specified. The solar water heater was installed. The tea shop was given its own meter. The budget was tracked against the ceiling through every decision.

Any Nepali family with six aana and NPR 2 crore can build what we described. None of it is exotic. All of it is available from local suppliers. The hard part is not the materials or the money. The hard part is the discipline to make every decision consciously, with full information, rather than letting the contractor default to the cheapest option or the flashiest one.

This series was written as a companion for that discipline. If it helps one family in Bhaktapur think more carefully about the naksa pass, or one contractor specify Fe500D rebar instead of Fe500, or one builder insist on a 50mm EPS layer over the roof slab — the series has done its job.

The house is commissioned through a three-to-four-hour handover walkthrough using a structured checklist that catches every defect before the final 5% of the contract payment is released; HVAC is right-sized to the upgraded envelope — one 0.75-ton inverter AC in the master bedroom (Daikin, NPR 80,000) and one 1.0-ton in the living room (LG, NPR 75,000), both operating in heat-pump mode for winter use at roughly NPR 4,000 per year total running cost; move-in proceeds over two weeks with bedrooms first, kitchen second, furnishing gradually while the handover punch list is completed; annual maintenance runs NPR 20,000–30,000 covering tank cleaning, pump service, AC servicing, gutter cleaning, earth pit check, and paint touch-ups, with mid-life refresh at years 10 and 20 totalling NPR 12–20 lakh over two decades; the tea shop at ground floor generates roughly NPR 4,180 daily turnover and NPR 3.5–4.5 lakh annual net profit on a family-labour basis, which roughly offsets annual cost of ownership (property tax ~NPR 10,000, electricity NPR 30,000, water NPR 12,000, insurance NPR 25,000, maintenance NPR 20,000, gas NPR 22,000, internet NPR 25,000, misc NPR 15,000, total ~NPR 160,000 per year); property tax kicks in after a 2-year construction grace period at 0.05–0.5% of assessed value per year; basic fire-plus-earthquake insurance costs NPR 20,000–30,000 per year and is recommended; the final budget reconciliation shows approximately NPR 1.89 crore spent against the NPR 2 crore hard ceiling, with NPR 11 lakh of real cushion in the reserve for year-one and year-two surprises; and over twenty years the deliberate decisions we made on structure, envelope, water system, and electrical infrastructure quietly pay back several times their initial cost in avoided energy bills, avoided repairs, and avoided surprises.
