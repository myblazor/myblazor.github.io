There is a particular Tuesday morning in early June in Newport News when the air, after a small rain the night before, smells like crepe myrtle and the salt of the lower James. A homeowner stands on the back step with a coffee in one hand and a tape measure in the other, looking at a roughly thirty-by-forty patch of fenced grass and trying to imagine what it would feel like to look out, instead, at a small second building — a little dwelling with French doors onto a sunroom, a bedroom for an aging parent, a tiny kitchen with two burners and a sink, and a bathroom with a step-in shower. He does not know, on this particular morning, that what he is imagining is, in Virginia and specifically in Newport News, now legal by right; that there is a body of law and a body of building code and a body of construction practice that will say yes to almost every part of his daydream; and that the entire path from this Tuesday morning to the day his mother moves in will take him about fourteen months, cost him somewhere between one hundred and seventy thousand and two hundred and ninety thousand dollars depending on choices he has not yet made, and produce — if he is patient and lucky and chooses his contractor well — a building that will outlive him.

## Part 1. Why This Article Exists Now: SB 531, the October 2024 Newport News Ordinance, and the Quiet Revolution in Virginia Backyards

For about forty years, building a second small dwelling on the same lot as a single-family home in Virginia was either impossible, technically possible but practically prohibited, or possible in some jurisdictions and not in others. The pattern was not unique to the Commonwealth — it was the pattern of nearly every American suburb. Detached secondary dwellings were called accessory dwelling units, or ADUs, or in-law suites, or carriage houses, or granny flats, or backyard cottages, depending on who was talking. In some cities they were treated as a routine accessory use; in others they were prohibited outright in single-family zones; in still others they were technically allowed but required a special use permit, a public hearing, a discretionary vote, neighbour notification, owner-occupancy, a family relationship between primary and secondary occupants, and fees that could exceed twenty thousand dollars before a single shovel hit the dirt. The result, in aggregate, was that ADUs as a national housing supply were effectively a suburban California phenomenon by about 2016, a Pacific Northwest phenomenon by about 2018, and almost nowhere else.

Virginia began to chip away at that pattern slowly, then quickly. The chipping is what gives this article its present-tense urgency. There are three layers of law you need to understand before you spend a single hour of your weekend on a sketch of a backyard cottage, and they nest like onion skins:

1. The federal layer — minimal, mostly limited to fair-housing law, FEMA flood-plain regulation through the National Flood Insurance Program, and, for some financing options, federal mortgage-insurance rules.
2. The state layer — Virginia Code § 15.2-2291 (the long-standing accessory-apartment statute), the Virginia Uniform Statewide Building Code (USBC), the 2021 Virginia Residential Code (VRC), and the brand-new Senate Bill 531 of the 2026 session, signed into law by Governor Abigail Spanberger and effective 1 July 2027.
3. The local layer — the City of Newport News Zoning Ordinance, specifically the amendments adopted by City Council in September 2024 and effective 1 October 2024, and the various supporting chapters of the Newport News City Code.

We will work through them in that order, because each layer constrains the one beneath it but cannot relax it from below. A federal flood-plain rule cannot be overridden by a state or city law; the Virginia Building Code cannot be overridden by the city; and the city zoning ordinance — which is itself written under authority delegated by the General Assembly — cannot demand standards that exceed what state law permits.

### 1.1 The federal layer

Federal law touches your ADU in essentially three places, and only three.

The first is the Fair Housing Act, which prohibits discrimination in housing on the basis of race, colour, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability. For an ADU, the practical implication is that you cannot, when you eventually rent the unit, refuse a tenant on any of those grounds, and you cannot advertise in a way that signals a preference. If your ADU is built specifically as an accessible unit for an aging parent and you intend to rent it later, the design choices you make for accessibility (a thirty-six-inch door swing, a curbless shower, lever handles instead of knobs) are a feature, not a regulatory burden, because they widen the pool of legal tenants under federal disability-discrimination rules.

The second is FEMA's National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) and the federal floodplain-management regulations issued under 44 CFR Part 60. If your lot is in a Special Flood Hazard Area (SFHA) — the FEMA-designated 1% annual chance floodplain, often called the "100-year floodplain" — federal rules, enforced through Newport News's participation in the NFIP, will dictate the elevation of the lowest finished floor of any new habitable structure. We will return to this in Part 6, because Newport News has roughly 3,600 acres in SFHA and another 900 acres in the 0.2% annual chance floodplain, and depending on where your lot sits the entire foundation typology of your ADU will be decided by FEMA.

The third is HUD and Energy Star and various federal financing layers — most homeowners will encounter these only if they take out an FHA-insured renovation loan or one of the Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac products that now formally allow ADU rental income to be counted toward a mortgage qualification. The Federal Housing Finance Agency formally permitted Fannie and Freddie to count seventy-five per cent of projected ADU rental income toward HomeStyle Renovation loan qualification beginning in 2023, which was a quiet but materially important change for homeowners trying to finance a small backyard build.

That is the federal layer. There is no federal ADU law as such; the federal layer touches your project only when it crosses a flood-plain or a financing decision.

### 1.2 The state layer: § 15.2-2291, the USBC and VRC, and Senate Bill 531

Virginia is what zoning lawyers call a "Dillon Rule" state. The Dillon Rule, named after the nineteenth-century Iowa judge John Forrest Dillon, holds that local governments only have the powers expressly delegated to them by the state legislature. In practical terms, your city council in Newport News cannot pass any zoning ordinance about ADUs that the General Assembly has not first authorised it to pass. The state therefore has both a permissive layer (you may do this) and, increasingly, a mandatory layer (you must allow this).

The permissive layer is anchored by Virginia Code § 15.2-2291, which has long authorised but did not require localities to allow "accessory apartments" in single-family residential zones. Until 2024, individual cities decided whether to opt in. Some — Charlottesville, Arlington, Alexandria, Fairfax County in a limited form — did. Many — including most of Hampton Roads — either did not or did so under tight conditions.

The Virginia Uniform Statewide Building Code (USBC), and within it the 2021 Virginia Residential Code (VRC), is the technical foundation. Virginia adopts a customised version of the International Residential Code (IRC) every three years, with the current cycle being the 2021 USBC, which adopts the 2021 IRC with state-specific amendments. The 2021 VRC matters to your ADU in roughly thirty-five places. The most consequential are: the minimum room dimensions and ceiling heights of Chapter 3, the egress and emergency-escape requirements (R310), the energy-efficiency provisions of Chapter 11 (Climate Zone 4A for all of Hampton Roads), the foundation provisions of Chapter 4 (frost depth in Newport News is taken as twelve inches), the wall-bracing provisions of Chapter 6, the roofing and weather-resistance provisions of Chapter 9, and the electrical, plumbing, and mechanical chapters that pull in the National Electrical Code (currently 2020 NEC adopted by Virginia), the Virginia Plumbing Code, and the Virginia Mechanical Code. We will reference specific sections throughout the article. The 2021 VRC requires R-49 ceiling insulation and R-19 wall insulation as a baseline in Climate Zone 4A; some Hampton Roads jurisdictions have moved toward R-60 ceiling insulation in advance of the 2024 IRC adoption. Confirm with the Newport News Codes Compliance Department, because the answer can change between when this article is written and when you pull a permit.

Now we come to the part that motivated this article in particular: Senate Bill 531 of the 2026 session.

[SB 531](https://virginiamercury.com/2026/04/16/a-new-law-will-make-it-easier-to-build-a-tiny-house-in-your-back-yard-starting-next-year/) was carried by Senator Kannan Srinivasan (D-Loudoun) and Senator Saddam Salim (D-Fairfax), neither of whom represents Newport News. The bill was a successor to several earlier attempts that had passed one chamber and died in the other; the most recent of those, SB 304 (Salim) of 2024, had passed the Senate 22-18 but was watered down or stalled in the House. SB 531 cleared both chambers in the 2026 session and was signed by Governor Spanberger in mid-April 2026. It takes effect 1 July 2027.

What does SB 531 do? In a single sentence: it makes ADUs a by-right use in any single-family residential zoning district anywhere in Virginia, and caps the burdens a locality may impose. The specific provisions, expressed in the form a homeowner needs to understand them, are these:

1. **By-right approval.** Effective 1 July 2027, every Virginia locality must permit ADUs as a by-right use in districts zoned for single-family homes. "By-right" means no special use permit, no public hearing, no discretionary vote, no neighbour notification process — if the application meets the objective standards in the ordinance, the city must approve it.

2. **Permit fee cap.** Localities may not charge more than five hundred dollars for an ADU permit. (This is a cap on the zoning permit fee, not on building permit fees, plan-review fees, or impact fees, which are governed separately. This distinction matters; we will return to it.)

3. **Setback parity.** Localities may not require larger setbacks for ADUs than they would for the primary dwelling. The earlier SB 304 had used the formulation "the setback required for the primary dwelling, or four feet, whichever is less" — SB 531 is broadly aligned with this principle but localities retain some flexibility on side and rear setbacks above the four-foot floor.

4. **No family-relationship requirement.** The old Virginia Beach-style rule that an ADU could only be occupied by a family member or domestic employee of the primary residence is now prohibited. You may rent your ADU to any tenant — subject, of course, to the local short-term rental ordinance, which in Newport News currently prohibits stays of less than thirty days in an ADU.

5. **No owner-occupancy requirement that exceeds state norms.** Localities may continue to require owner-occupancy of either the primary or the secondary unit, but the bill bars some of the more aggressive owner-occupancy ordinances that effectively prohibited absentee landlords from ever building.

It is important to be honest about what SB 531 does *not* do. It does not pre-empt the building code, the energy code, the floodplain ordinance, the historic-district overlay, or the stormwater regulations. It does not require a locality to permit an ADU on a non-conforming lot. It does not eliminate impact fees, water and sewer connection fees, or building permit fees. It does not require a locality to permit two ADUs on the same lot. It does not authorise an ADU in a deed-restricted HOA community whose covenants prohibit secondary dwellings, although there is unsettled litigation in several states over whether HOA covenants can override state ADU statutes (California has explicitly resolved this in favour of homeowners; Virginia has not yet had a published appellate decision).

So: SB 531 takes effect 1 July 2027. It is now June 2026. Why does this article matter today, and not on 1 July 2027?

Two reasons. First, Newport News has already adopted its own by-right ADU ordinance, effective 1 October 2024. You do not need SB 531 to build an ADU in Newport News today; you only need to comply with the existing City Code. Second, even where the local ordinance is more restrictive than SB 531, a homeowner who begins design now will likely be permitting in the second half of 2027 or in 2028, by which time SB 531 will be operative. Designing now to comply with both the current city ordinance *and* SB 531 means your design will not need to be revised when the law takes effect.

### 1.3 The local layer: the Newport News ADU ordinance, effective 1 October 2024

In August 2024, the [Newport News City Council](https://www.13newsnow.com/article/news/local/mycity/newport-news/newport-news-considers-accessory-dwelling-units-policy/291-b0da4c6f-5484-4d73-a0f7-ac620160cbb5) began publicly discussing an ADU ordinance, with Mayor Phillip Jones leading the conversation. The motivation was straightforward: Newport News, as the mayor noted, is "essentially built out," meaning that very little undeveloped residential land remains within the city limits. The city's housing supply must therefore grow up (denser construction on existing lots) or grow within (ADUs, basement conversions, lot splits) rather than out. The council adopted the ordinance unanimously [in September 2024](https://mrwilliamsburg.com/newport-news-expands-housing-options-with-approval-of-accessory-dwelling-units-for-single-family-lots/), with an effective date of 1 October 2024.

The ordinance, codified in the Newport News Zoning Ordinance, makes the following provisions. Confirm every one of these directly with the Newport News Department of Planning before you submit, because the ordinance will be amended at least once before SB 531 takes effect, and individual interpretations vary by parcel.

**Permitted zoning districts.** ADUs are permitted in all zoning districts in which single-family dwellings are permitted as a by-right use. In Newport News this includes the R-1, R-2, R-3, R-5, R-6, R-7, and several PUD-designated zones. Some commercial-mixed zones permit single-family dwellings as a non-conforming use; ADUs are not automatically allowed in those.

**Form factors permitted.** Detached ADUs (free-standing buildings), attached ADUs (additions sharing a wall with the primary dwelling), conversion ADUs (turning a garage, basement, or attic into a habitable unit), and addition ADUs (adding a second-floor unit on top of a single-storey home or on top of an existing garage) are all permitted, subject to size and form requirements.

**Maximum size.** The ADU may not exceed 1,200 square feet of finished floor area, and may not exceed fifty per cent of the heated square footage of the primary dwelling, whichever is less. For a typical Newport News rancher of about 1,500 square feet, this means an ADU up to 750 square feet. For a 2,200-square-foot two-storey colonial, the cap is 1,100 square feet (because 50% × 2,200 = 1,100, which is less than 1,200). For a 3,000-square-foot home, the cap is 1,200. We will design to roughly 800 square feet — comfortably under both caps for any home larger than 1,600 square feet.

**Maximum height.** Detached ADUs are limited to the height of the primary dwelling or twenty-five feet, whichever is less, when the ADU is at least ten feet from the primary dwelling. Attached ADUs and second-storey ADUs follow the underlying district's height limit.

**Setbacks.** Standard accessory-structure setbacks apply: typically five feet to the side property line and ten feet to the rear, but this varies by zoning district and by lot. SB 531 will, after 1 July 2027, override any city rule that requires more than the principal-dwelling setback.

**Owner-occupancy.** Either the primary dwelling or the ADU must be owner-occupied. This is recorded as a deed restriction, which means that future buyers of the property are formally on notice that one of the two units must be owner-occupied. SB 531 does not eliminate this in Virginia, although it may be revisited in subsequent sessions.

**Architectural compatibility.** The ADU must use exterior materials, roof pitch, and trim details "compatible with" the primary dwelling. This is a design-review hook: a brick-clad rancher with a gable roof should not get a flat-roofed stucco ADU. The standard is enforced at plan review, not by a separate design review board, but it is enforced.

**Rental restrictions.** The ADU may be rented as a long-term rental (lease term thirty days or longer). It may not be used as a short-term rental (Airbnb, VRBO, less than thirty days). The short-term rental prohibition is enforced through the city's separate STR ordinance.

**Parking.** One additional off-street parking space is required for the ADU. This may be tandem (in front of an existing space in the driveway), provided the driveway can accommodate it without obstructing the public right-of-way.

**Utilities.** The ADU may share water and sewer connections with the primary dwelling, in which case it does not require a separate Newport News Waterworks meter. If you choose a separate meter (which is sometimes desirable for rental purposes — easier billing, separation of utility responsibility), there is a separate connection fee.

**Building code.** The ADU is a Group R-3 occupancy under the 2021 USBC and must meet all VRC requirements. Fire separation between an attached ADU and the primary dwelling is generally required (one-hour rated wall) unless an NFPA 13D residential sprinkler system is installed throughout, which is a meaningful cost trade-off we will discuss.

**Permit fees.** As of mid-2026, Newport News charges roughly $250 for the zoning permit, $0.30 per square foot of construction for the building permit (with a minimum), separate trade permit fees for plumbing, electrical, and mechanical, and $1,500–$3,500 in water and sewer connection fees if the ADU is metered separately. Total fees are typically $3,500 to $7,500 for a project of the size we are designing.

That is the law. Let us now walk a real lot.

## Part 3. The Site: Soil, Slope, Trees, Sun, and the Things You Cannot Move

Long before you draw a floor plan, you walk the lot. The most expensive line items in your final budget are usually the things you did not see on the lot before you started, and the most beautiful design choices are usually the ones that responded to something already there. Take the Tuesday morning seriously. Walk the lot at 7 a.m., 1 p.m., 5 p.m., and 9 p.m. on a clear day. Take photos. Note where the sun comes up and where it goes down. Note the prevailing breeze. Note where the puddles form after a rain.

### 3.1 Soil: what is under the grass on the Virginia Peninsula

The Virginia Peninsula sits on the Atlantic Coastal Plain, a sedimentary deposit that has been laid down by tens of millions of years of rivers, sea-level changes, and sediment from the Appalachian Mountains. The result is a layered soil profile that, for our purposes, is mostly forgiving but has three things you must check.

The first is the surface soil, typically a sandy loam — Sassafras, Suffolk, Pamunkey, Bojac, and a few others are the most common series in the Newport News area, classified by the USDA Natural Resources Conservation Service. A sandy loam at the surface drains reasonably well and does not have the expansive-clay problem that bedevils foundations in Texas or central Oklahoma. You can build a strip footing on undisturbed sandy loam without expensive overengineering, and most Newport News ADUs sit on a continuous footing or a monolithic slab that bears directly on the sandy loam at frost depth.

The second is the layer beneath. Below the surface horizon, you may encounter: a dense clay layer (the Yorktown Formation in older parts of the city), a high water table, a buried wood debris layer (in older neighbourhoods that were once tidal marsh and were filled in the early twentieth century), or — in the East End and parts of Newport News South — old fill of unknown composition. None of these are dealbreakers, but they decide the foundation type. A high water table within four feet of grade rules out a basement and may rule out a crawl space; it points toward a slab-on-grade. Old fill of unknown composition usually means a soil report — an inexpensive geotechnical investigation by a firm like Schnabel Engineering or GET Solutions will run $1,500 to $3,500 — to confirm bearing capacity.

The third is the historic loamy-clay marine deposit found in some areas of the city, which can have moderately compressible characteristics under load. For a building as light as an 800-square-foot ADU on a continuous footing, this is rarely a problem; the load per linear foot of footing is low enough that even a modestly compressible bearing soil will not produce visible settlement. For a two-storey ADU on a tight footprint, your structural engineer may specify a deeper footing or a thicker slab.

For Mr Calloway's lot, the soil report (which we will recommend even though it is not strictly required) confirms Sassafras sandy loam to seventeen feet, with a water table at approximately nine feet, and an allowable bearing capacity of 2,500 pounds per square foot at frost depth. This is plenty for a continuous twelve-inch-wide concrete footing carrying a wood-frame single-storey ADU. We will go with a monolithic slab-on-grade with thickened edges — sometimes called a "turn-down slab" — which is the most economical and durable option for this site.

For Mr and Mrs Park's lot, similar conditions; we will use a slab-on-grade for the ADU but with deeper edge thickenings to carry the second-storey load.

For Ms Robinson's lot, the picture is different. The water table is high — roughly four to five feet below grade — and the lot sits closer to sea level. We will design a slab-on-grade with the finished floor elevated approximately twelve inches above grade (a small step up from the lot), with a vapor barrier and rigid insulation under the slab. If the lot were within the SFHA, we would have to elevate further; it is not, but we will design conservatively.

### 3.2 Slope and drainage

Newport News is mostly flat. There are not many lots with significant slope. But "mostly flat" hides a lot of small puddling that decides whether your ADU's foundation stays dry. Take a hose. Run it for ten minutes on the area where you plan to build. Watch where the water goes. That is, very nearly, what will happen in a rainstorm.

A continuous footing ADU should be built on ground that drains away from the structure on all four sides at a minimum slope of one foot in twenty (5%) for the first ten feet — so the grade should drop six inches over the first ten feet from the foundation. This is the IRC R401.3 requirement, and it is enforced at the final inspection. Most Newport News lots have less natural slope than this, which means you will be doing some grading. Plan on $2,000 to $5,000 for grading, depending on lot.

If the natural drainage path runs *toward* a neighbour's property, you cannot simply redirect water to flow onto their lot. Newport News municipal code prohibits stormwater runoff increases to adjacent properties, and the city's stormwater ordinance requires that any new construction not increase stormwater runoff to adjacent parcels or to the storm drain system. For a small ADU, this usually means a French drain along the property line nearest the ADU and a discharge into a dry well or a swale that holds the runoff long enough for it to soak in. A French drain is $25 to $50 per linear foot installed; a dry well is $1,500 to $4,000 depending on size.

### 3.3 Trees: the cost of keeping them, the cost of removing them

Newport News has many mature trees — loblolly pine, willow oak, red oak, southern magnolia, dogwood, crape myrtle — and the city's tree canopy is one of its most valuable shared assets. Many neighbourhoods have unwritten or written norms about tree preservation. The city does have a tree protection ordinance for certain protected species and for trees of certain sizes, and any tree more than six inches in diameter at breast height (DBH) on a lot subject to development review may require a tree-protection plan. Our three projects are all on residential lots and our city zoning permit will not trigger the formal tree-protection ordinance, but the city building inspector and your contractor will both flag a tree near the proposed foundation.

The general rule, which is not a city rule but a structural one: do not place a foundation within the drip line of a mature tree. The drip line is the circle on the ground directly under the outer branches. Roots within the drip line are the structural roots; cutting them either kills the tree or destabilises it, and either way the city inspector — and your homeowner's insurance underwriter — will note the proximity. A reasonable rule of thumb is that the foundation should be at least the radius of the canopy plus three feet from the trunk.

For Mr Calloway, the dogwood at the back of the lot has a canopy radius of about twelve feet. The proposed ADU is positioned twenty feet from the trunk, which is fine. For Ms Robinson, the oak tree at the side of the lot has a canopy radius of roughly twenty-five feet. We will position the ADU eighteen feet from the trunk and accept that we will lose four or five feet of canopy on the side facing the new building, which the arborist confirms is acceptable for a healthy oak of that age. For Mr and Mrs Park, the loblolly pines along the rear fence are tall but have shallow root systems that follow the surface; we will keep the foundation eight feet from the trunks and use a French drain to manage the runoff that would otherwise pool at the back of the new building.

If you must remove a tree, the cost in Hampton Roads in mid-2026 is $400 to $1,500 for a small tree, $1,500 to $4,000 for a mature tree, and $4,000 to $8,000 for a large tree near power lines or close to the house. Stump grinding is an additional $150 to $400. Before removing a tree, make sure you actually need to — it is one of the most regrettable decisions in residential construction.

### 3.4 Sun and prevailing breeze: orienting the sunroom

The sunroom — the part of this ADU that gives Patricia Calloway her morning view of the dogwood — should be oriented thoughtfully. This is one of the few decisions in the project where building physics, ergonomics, and emotional satisfaction all align if you do it right.

Newport News sits at approximately 37° North latitude. The sun rises slightly south of due east in winter and slightly north of due east in summer. A south-facing sunroom catches direct sun for the longest period of the day, but in a Climate Zone 4A coastal climate this can lead to summer overheating; the building physics fix is a generous roof overhang (twenty-four to thirty-six inches) on the south side to block the high summer sun while admitting the low winter sun. An east-facing sunroom catches the morning sun, which is what Patricia wants — the dogwood tree is east of the proposed cottage. A north-facing sunroom catches no direct sun and is essentially a glazed sitting room with even, indirect light; this is how an artist's studio is oriented. A west-facing sunroom catches the brutal late-afternoon summer sun and is generally a mistake unless you genuinely love watching the sunset and are committed to expensive solar-control glass.

For Mr Calloway, the sunroom is east-facing, with the bedroom to the south and the bath-and-kitchen to the north. The sunroom has roughly 60 square feet of glazing on the east wall (a bank of three casement windows over a knee wall), 24 square feet on the south wall, and 24 square feet on the north wall. The west wall is the interior wall to the bedroom. We will use Energy Star-certified low-E double-pane casement windows, with a U-factor of 0.27 and a solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC) of 0.30 — the 2021 Virginia Energy Conservation Code maximum SHGC for Climate Zone 4A on west and south orientations was reduced to 0.30 in the 2021 update, and we will hold to this on all elevations for safety.

The prevailing breeze in Newport News in summer is south-southwest, off the James River. A sunroom that opens with operable casement windows on both the east and the south walls will cross-ventilate naturally on most summer evenings, which means Patricia can sit there with the windows open from May through October without the air conditioning running. This is not just a comfort point; it materially reduces the cooling load on the HVAC system.

### 3.5 Setbacks, easements, and the things you cannot see on the surface

Before you draw the foundation footprint, walk the property with a copy of the deed and the most recent property survey. If you do not have a survey, get one. A boundary survey from a licensed Virginia surveyor costs $600 to $1,500 for a typical residential lot, and it is one of the few line items where the cost-to-confidence ratio is overwhelming. You will use the survey to mark setbacks, identify easements, and locate underground utilities.

Setbacks in Newport News for accessory structures in R-1, R-2, and R-3 are typically: ten feet from the rear property line, five feet from each side, and the same as the principal building from the front (usually thirty to forty feet, but the principal dwelling is already at the front, so this rarely binds). For corner lots like Ms Robinson's, the corner-side setback is generally larger — fifteen feet — to preserve sight lines for vehicles. SB 531, when it takes effect on 1 July 2027, will limit how aggressive these setbacks can be: localities will not be able to require setbacks larger than the setback required for the primary dwelling. For most Newport News lots this will not materially change the design; for some non-conforming lots it will. Confirm with the planner.

Easements are areas of your lot where a third party has a right to do something — typically a utility easement, where the city or a utility company has the right to access water, sewer, electric, gas, or telecom infrastructure. Easements show up on the deed and on the survey. You may not build a permanent structure within an easement. Common easements in Newport News are a five-foot or ten-foot utility easement along the rear property line, a five-foot side easement along one side, and a drainage easement at the lowest corner of the lot. For Mr Calloway's lot, the deed shows a seven-and-a-half-foot utility easement along the rear property line. The proposed ADU is set back ten feet from the rear, fully clear of the easement.

Underground utilities that are not in a recorded easement — your private water line from the meter at the curb, your private sewer lateral from the house to the city main, gas service, the electric service drop — are still there, and you must call Virginia 811 (the "Miss Utility" call-before-you-dig service) at least three working days before any excavation. The 811 ticket is free, the service is fast, and digging without one is both illegal under Virginia Code § 56-265.14 and a guaranteed way to hit something expensive. You will see the marks: red is electric, yellow is gas, blue is water, green is sewer, orange is telecom. Take photos before they wash off.

## Part 4. The Floor Plan: Designing 800 Square Feet for One Person to Live Well

Eight hundred square feet is small for an American house and large for a hotel room. It is roughly the size of a generous one-bedroom apartment in any American city — and the discipline of designing a small space well is, frankly, the discipline of designing any space well. Every square foot has to do work.

We will design Mr Calloway's ADU in detail. The principles transfer to the Park and Robinson projects with adjustments.

### 4.1 The programme: what the building must do

Before any walls are drawn, write down what the building must do. This is "requirements gathering," and the same engineering instinct you apply at work applies here. For Patricia Calloway, the requirements are:

1. A **sunroom** of approximately 140 to 160 square feet, east-facing, with seating for two and a small writing desk. Daylight is the primary feature; views of the dogwood are the secondary feature.
2. A **bedroom** of approximately 130 square feet, with closet space, room for a queen bed, two bedside tables, a small dresser, and a chair. Patricia uses a CPAP machine; the bedroom needs an outlet at the head of the bed within a foot of the pillow.
3. A **bathroom** of approximately 60 square feet, with a curbless walk-in shower (no tub), a comfort-height toilet (17 inches to seat), a vanity with a single sink, grab bars at the toilet and in the shower, and a window for natural ventilation. The door must be at least 32 inches wide for wheelchair compatibility. Lever handles throughout.
4. A **kitchen** of approximately 90 square feet, with a small refrigerator (24-inch), a two-burner induction cooktop, a built-in microwave/convection oven combo, a dishwasher (18-inch compact), a stainless single-bowl sink, and approximately eight linear feet of counter space.
5. A **living/dining/circulation area** of approximately 220 square feet that includes the entry foyer, the path between rooms, and a small seating area with a sofa and a television.
6. A **mechanical/laundry alcove** of approximately 40 square feet for a stacked washer/dryer (24-inch compact European-style), the heat-pump water heater, the air-handler/mini-split closet, and the electrical sub-panel.
7. **Closets and storage** distributed through the unit — at least 30 square feet of closet space, plus a small linen closet, and a shallow coat closet near the entry.

Total: roughly 780 to 820 square feet of conditioned space, depending on wall thickness. We will draw to 800.

### 4.2 The schematic plan

The plan we will draw is a roughly rectangular footprint, 32 feet wide by 25 feet deep, with the long axis running east-west. The east end is the sunroom, glassed on three sides. Behind the sunroom, on the south, is the bedroom; on the north, the bathroom; in the middle, an L-shaped open kitchen-living area; and at the west end, the entry, mechanical alcove, and closet stack. There is a single front door on the south side, near the west end, with a small covered porch (4 feet by 8 feet) and a concrete pad. There is a back service door on the north side near the mechanical alcove, opening onto the area where the heat pump's outdoor condenser sits.

Roof: a simple gable, 6:12 pitch, ridge running east-west. This matches the rancher's existing roof and is simple to frame, simple to insulate, and simple to keep watertight. The gable end on the east is over the sunroom; the gable end on the west is over the entry. Roof overhangs are 24 inches on the eaves and 12 inches on the gables.

Exterior cladding: brick veneer on the lower three courses (water table) and HardiePlank (fibre cement lap siding) above, painted to match the cream trim of the rancher. The colour palette intentionally echoes the primary dwelling without copying it — "compatibility" rather than "identicality" satisfies the city's architectural-compatibility requirement.

Foundation: monolithic slab-on-grade, 4 inches of concrete on 4 inches of compacted gravel on a 6-mil polyethylene vapor barrier on R-10 rigid foam insulation on undisturbed Sassafras sandy loam at 12-inch frost depth, with thickened edges (12 inches wide by 18 inches deep) at the perimeter and under all bearing walls.

Framing: 2×6 stud walls on 16-inch centres, with R-21 high-density fibreglass batts (or, for slightly better performance, R-23 mineral wool); R-49 blown cellulose in the attic; R-10 rigid foam under the slab and at the slab edge. We will discuss insulation in detail in Part 7.

Heating and cooling: a single-zone ductless mini-split heat pump (one outdoor unit, one indoor wall-mounted air handler in the living/dining area, plus a second small ducted air handler in the mechanical alcove serving the bedroom and bathroom through short ducts). The system is sized at approximately 18,000 BTU/h cooling and 24,000 BTU/h heating. We will choose a Mitsubishi MUZ-FH18NA or a Daikin Aurora equivalent; both are available through Hampton Roads dealers, both have HSPF ratings above 12, and both qualify for the Virginia Energy Sense rebate (which, depending on programme funding, returns approximately $400 to $700 to the homeowner).

Water heating: a 50-gallon hybrid heat-pump water heater (Rheem ProTerra or A.O. Smith Voltex), located in the mechanical alcove. Heat-pump water heaters are roughly four times more efficient than conventional electric resistance heaters; they cost more upfront ($1,800 to $2,400 for the unit plus $500 to $1,000 for installation, versus $500 to $900 for a conventional electric tank) and pay back over six to eight years.

Electrical: a 100-amp sub-panel fed from the main house panel via a 100-amp feeder (or, if the main panel is full, a separate 100-amp service from the utility, which adds connection costs). Twenty-circuit sub-panel, with dedicated circuits for the heat pump, the water heater, the cooktop, the dishwasher, the washer, the dryer, the microwave, and the bathroom GFCI; the rest of the unit is covered by general-purpose circuits.

Plumbing: a single one-and-a-half-inch supply line from the main, branching to the kitchen, bathroom, and laundry. A four-inch cast iron or PVC sewer lateral connects to the existing house lateral or runs directly to the city main; the connection method depends on lot configuration and on the local plumbing inspector.

That is the schematic. Now the long part begins.

## Part 5. Permits and Approvals: From Zoning Permit to Certificate of Occupancy

You cannot start the work, in any meaningful sense, until you have the permits. The permits process in Newport News for an ADU has four sequential steps and approximately seven inspections during construction.

### 5.1 Step 1: Pre-application meeting with the planner

This is free, optional, and the single most useful hour of the project. Call the Newport News Department of Planning at 757-933-2310 or visit them in City Hall at 2400 Washington Avenue and ask for a pre-application meeting for an ADU. Bring a sketch of the proposed footprint on a copy of your most recent property survey. The planner will tell you, in roughly forty-five minutes:

- Whether your zoning permits an ADU (yes if R-1, R-2, R-3, R-5, R-6, R-7).
- What setbacks apply to your specific lot.
- What the size cap is for your lot (50% of the primary dwelling's heated area, or 1,200 square feet, whichever is less).
- Whether your lot has any unusual constraints — flood zone, historic overlay, conservation easement, deed-restricted HOA.
- What architectural compatibility the planner will be looking for.
- What approximate timeline to expect.

Take notes. Save the planner's business card. The planner's note in your file will follow your application all the way through.

### 5.2 Step 2: The zoning permit

The zoning permit is the formal review of your ADU's compliance with the Newport News Zoning Ordinance. The application is filed with the Department of Planning. As of mid-2026, the fee is approximately $250. The application package includes:

1. A site plan, drawn to scale (1 inch = 10 feet is typical), showing the property lines, the existing dwelling, the proposed ADU, the dimensions and setbacks of both, the driveway and parking, the trees over six inches DBH, and the easements.
2. Floor plans of the proposed ADU, drawn to scale, showing room labels and approximate dimensions.
3. Elevations of the ADU showing height, materials, and roof pitch.
4. A statement of compliance with the architectural-compatibility requirement.
5. Confirmation of utility service plan (sharing with the primary dwelling, or separate).
6. The owner-occupancy declaration that one of the two units will be owner-occupied, and consent to the deed restriction that will be recorded after the certificate of occupancy.

Review takes two to four weeks. The planner may issue comments — minor revisions, usually — which you will address in a revised submission. Once approved, the zoning permit is valid for two years, which is typically enough time to complete construction.

For Mr Calloway, the zoning permit is approved on the first review with no comments, two and a half weeks after submission. For Mr and Mrs Park, the planner asks for a revision because the proposed two-storey ADU would have a window on the second floor whose sight line looks directly into the next-door neighbour's primary bedroom; the Parks add a high transom window instead of the originally planned full-height window, and the revision is approved a week later. For Ms Robinson, the planner notes that the corner-side setback is fifteen feet and her original sketch had the ADU at twelve feet from the corner side; she shifts the building three feet, which costs her three feet of back yard but is acceptable, and the permit is approved.

### 5.3 Step 3: The building permit and trade permits

With the zoning permit in hand, the next step is the building permit and the associated trade permits (plumbing, electrical, mechanical), all filed with the Newport News Department of Codes Compliance. The building permit fee, as of mid-2026, is approximately $0.30 per square foot of construction with a $150 minimum, so for an 800-square-foot ADU it is roughly $240. Each trade permit is approximately $90 to $150 depending on scope.

The building permit application is much more substantial than the zoning permit. You will need:

1. **Stamped construction drawings** by a Virginia-licensed architect or, for small projects, a builder-prepared plan that meets the prescriptive provisions of the VRC. For an 800-square-foot single-storey ADU using prescriptive methods, you can submit builder-drawn plans; for the Park's two-storey ADU on a tight lot with a complex roof, an architect will be involved.
2. **Structural calculations** for any element that exceeds the prescriptive provisions of the VRC. For our ADU, the prescriptive tables in IRC Tables R602.10.1 and R503.1 cover everything.
3. **Energy-code compliance** documentation. The Virginia Residential Code references the 2021 Virginia Energy Conservation Code (VECC) for new residential construction. You will document the prescriptive path (minimum R-values for each component) or the performance path (using REScheck or similar software).
4. **Site plan with grading and drainage**, including the slope away from the foundation (5% for the first 10 feet) and the stormwater management approach.
5. **Manual J load calculation** for the HVAC system.

Plan review by the Department of Codes Compliance typically takes three to six weeks. The reviewer will issue comments, which you address, and then the permit is issued. The building permit is also valid for two years, and work must commence within six months.

For Mr Calloway, the building permit takes four weeks and is approved with two comments — one asking for clarification on the slab-edge insulation detail (R-10 rigid foam wraps from under the slab to twelve inches above grade behind the brick veneer), and one asking that the egress window in the bedroom be confirmed as meeting the IRC R310 minimum opening dimensions (5.7 square feet, with at least 24 inches clear height and 20 inches clear width). Both are addressed in a one-page revision letter.

### 5.4 Step 4: Construction inspections

Once construction begins, the Newport News Department of Codes Compliance will inspect the work at the following milestones. Each inspection is scheduled by the contractor through the city's online portal at least 24 hours in advance.

1. **Footing inspection.** After the footings are excavated and the rebar is placed, before concrete is poured. The inspector confirms depth, width, and reinforcement.
2. **Slab inspection.** After the slab forms, vapor barrier, rigid insulation, and rebar/wire mesh are in place, before concrete is poured. (Some inspectors combine this with the footing inspection for a turn-down slab.)
3. **Underground rough-in (plumbing).** After the under-slab plumbing is installed and pressure-tested, before the slab is poured.
4. **Framing inspection.** After all framing is complete, the roof is dried in, windows and doors are installed, and rough-in plumbing, electrical, and HVAC are run, but before insulation goes in.
5. **Insulation inspection.** After insulation is installed, before drywall.
6. **Rough-in plumbing, electrical, HVAC.** Often combined with framing or done separately.
7. **Final inspection.** After all finishes, fixtures, appliances, and trim are in place. The inspector checks every item against the approved plans, tests GFCI outlets, confirms egress, confirms smoke and CO alarms (interconnected, hardwired with battery backup, in every bedroom and on every floor — IRC R314 and R315), and verifies the address number is visible from the street.

A clean inspection schedule for an 800-square-foot ADU runs about ten to fourteen weeks of construction with an average of one inspection every two weeks. Failures of inspections are common; a typical project might fail one or two inspections (often the final, where one detail is found out of compliance), correct it, and pass the re-inspection. There is no fee for a re-inspection up to the third visit; subsequent re-inspections incur a small fee.

### 5.5 Step 5: Certificate of occupancy

After the final inspection passes, the Department of Codes Compliance issues a Certificate of Occupancy (C of O). This is the legal document that says the building is fit for habitation. You cannot legally occupy the unit before the C of O; you cannot legally rent it before the C of O. The Newport News real estate assessor's office is notified by Codes Compliance, and the property will be reassessed for property tax purposes — we will return to this in Part 14.

## Part 6. Foundations: Slab-on-Grade in Climate Zone 4A on Sandy Loam

You are now standing on the lot with the contractor and the inspector for the footing-and-slab inspection. The site has been cleared, the topsoil stripped to about six inches and stockpiled at the back of the lot for later regrading, and the excavator has dug the slab footprint to the depth required. The forms are up, the gravel is placed and compacted, the vapor barrier is rolled out, the rigid foam insulation is in place, the rebar is tied. The day is warm. The concrete truck is on its way.

This is, statistically, the single most important inspection of the entire project. A foundation built wrong will haunt the building for fifty years; a foundation built right will be invisible for fifty years.

### 6.1 Why slab-on-grade

There are five common foundation types for residential buildings in the United States: full basement, crawl space, slab-on-grade, pier-and-beam, and frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF). For our Newport News ADU we have chosen slab-on-grade, and it is worth saying why explicitly.

A full basement is excavated below grade and provides usable space below the floor. In Newport News, with a water table that varies from four feet to fifteen feet below grade depending on the lot, a basement is usually expensive (extra excavation, waterproofing, drain tile, sump pump) and risky (water intrusion is common). An ADU at 800 square feet does not benefit from a basement; it would essentially double the square footage and the cost.

A crawl space is a shallower excavation, typically eighteen inches to four feet deep, with the floor framing above and a perimeter foundation wall. In humid Climate Zone 4A, crawl spaces are notorious for moisture problems — the air in the crawl space is cooler than the outside air, dew forms on the framing and the underside of the floor, mould grows, and the space becomes a chronic indoor air quality problem. A *conditioned* (sealed and insulated) crawl space solves the moisture problem at the cost of additional waterproofing, vapor barriers, and sometimes a small dehumidifier. For our ADU, a slab-on-grade is simpler and cheaper.

A slab-on-grade is a continuous concrete slab poured directly on prepared ground, with the building's bearing walls bearing on thickened slab edges. There is no crawl space, no basement, no floor framing. The mechanical systems run through the walls and the attic, not through a floor cavity. Plumbing comes up through the slab; once the slab is poured, you cannot easily relocate plumbing without breaking concrete. Slab-on-grade is the dominant foundation type for new residential construction in the southeastern United States.

A pier-and-beam foundation has discrete concrete piers under the building's bearing points, with wood or steel beams spanning between piers and floor framing on top. Pier-and-beam is common for elevated coastal buildings (Outer Banks, V-zone Florida) and for retrofits, but for a new infill ADU on a flat Newport News lot it offers no advantage.

A frost-protected shallow foundation (FPSF) is a slab-on-grade with rigid insulation arranged so that the slab can be founded shallower than the conventional frost depth, because the insulation prevents frost penetration to the slab edge. FPSF is governed by ASCE 32-01 and is included in IRC R403.3 by reference. For Newport News, with a frost depth of only twelve inches, FPSF is unnecessary; a conventional turn-down slab founded at twelve inches deep is already at frost depth. In a colder climate, FPSF would save several feet of excavation and a corresponding amount of concrete.

### 6.2 The actual slab: dimensions and reinforcement

The slab for Mr Calloway's ADU is 32 feet by 25 feet — exactly 800 square feet. The slab itself is four inches of concrete with embedded 6×6 W2.9×W2.9 welded wire reinforcement, sometimes called "wire mesh" but technically a flat sheet of welded wires. The wire is supported on chairs to keep it in the middle of the slab thickness, not lying on the gravel where it would do nothing structurally. The slab is poured at a 4,000 psi mix with 6% air entrainment for freeze-thaw durability.

The thickened edges — the part of the slab that bears on the soil and carries the building's weight — are 12 inches wide and 18 inches deep, with two #4 (half-inch) reinforcing bars running continuously around the perimeter, top and bottom. At inside corners and at the door openings where the slab will see point loads from the door frame, an additional #4 bar runs across the corner. The thickened edges run not only around the perimeter but also under interior bearing walls, which for our floor plan means under the wall between the bedroom and the sunroom, and under the wall between the bathroom and the kitchen.

Below the slab, in order from the concrete down: 6-mil polyethylene vapor barrier (overlapped 6 inches at seams and taped); R-10 extruded polystyrene rigid foam insulation (Owens Corning Foamular or equivalent, 2 inches thick, foundation-grade); 4 inches of clean #57 stone, compacted to 95% Proctor density; the stripped subgrade of undisturbed Sassafras sandy loam.

At the slab edge, the rigid foam wraps up the inside face of the foundation form to within six inches of the top of slab, then turns out at the top to act as a thermal break between the slab edge and the wood framing above. This detail is critical in Climate Zone 4A; without it, the slab edge becomes a thermal bridge and condensation can form on the inside of the slab edge during cold weather, leading to mould at the base of the wall.

The cost of the slab, for our project, is approximately:
- Excavation and grading: $4,500
- Gravel base and compaction: $1,800
- Vapor barrier and rigid foam: $2,400
- Rebar and wire mesh: $1,200
- Concrete (10 cubic yards at $230/yard): $2,300
- Concrete labour (forming, placing, finishing): $3,800
- Slab-edge insulation finishing detail: $400

Total slab: approximately $16,400, or about $20.50 per square foot of floor.

### 6.3 The slab pour day

The slab is poured early in the morning, ideally on a day forecast to be cool and not raining. Concrete cures by chemical reaction (hydration), not by drying, but the surface needs to stay damp during the first 24 to 48 hours to develop full strength. In Newport News in summer, with daytime temperatures in the low 90s, the contractor will mist the surface or cover it with curing blankets. In winter, with night-time temperatures below freezing, the slab is covered with insulating blankets to keep the curing water above 50°F.

The concrete arrives in a pump truck and is placed from one end of the slab to the other, with the finishers walking in the wet concrete (in tall waders) and screeding it level with a long aluminium straightedge. After screeding, the surface is bull-floated (a long wooden float on a handle) to bring fines to the surface; after bleed water has evaporated, the surface is steel-troweled to the desired smoothness. For an interior slab that will be tiled, a wood float finish is sufficient; for an exposed slab finish, multiple troweling passes are required.

The slab is allowed to cure for three days before any framing begins on it. During the cure, the contractor runs the under-slab plumbing — actually, the plumbing was run before the slab was poured, with stub-ups (vertical pipe ends) protruding through the slab at the future fixture locations. We will discuss plumbing in Part 9.

### 6.4 Foundation alternatives for the case studies

For Mr and Mrs Park's two-storey ADU on a smaller lot, the slab is similar but the thickened edges are deeper (24 inches) to carry the second-storey load, and the slab thickness is increased to 5 inches. Total slab cost: approximately $19,800.

For Ms Robinson's ADU on the East End lot with the high water table, the slab is similar but is elevated approximately 12 inches above grade to maintain a robust margin above the water table. The fill below the slab — instead of native soil — is compacted #57 stone for the bottom 18 inches, then the gravel base. This adds approximately $3,500 to the slab cost. Total slab cost: approximately $19,400.

## Part 7. Framing: Sticks, Sheathing, and the Building Envelope

The slab cures, the framers arrive. A two-person framing crew on a small ADU like ours can frame the entire shell — walls, floor (none, in our case), roof, sheathing — in approximately two weeks, weather permitting. Hampton Roads in summer is hot and humid, and most framing crews work from dawn to early afternoon to avoid the worst of the heat.

### 7.1 The wall system

The walls are 2×6 lumber on 16-inch centres. We use 2×6 instead of 2×4 for two reasons: it provides a deeper cavity for insulation (R-21 batts in 2×6 versus R-13 batts in 2×4, a 60% improvement in cavity insulation), and it provides a stiffer wall that handles the wind loads of coastal Virginia better. The 2×6 walls cost approximately 12% more than 2×4 walls in lumber and labour, and the energy savings repay that premium in roughly seven years at current Dominion Energy electricity rates. (Dominion's residential rate as of mid-2026 is approximately 14.2 cents per kilowatt-hour all-in; the rate has risen approximately 18% since 2023 and is projected to continue rising.)

The studs are kiln-dried Douglas fir or southern yellow pine, grade #2 or better, sourced from a Hampton Roads lumber yard such as Buchanan Lumber, Eastern Lumber, or one of the regional Builders FirstSource locations. A standard 2×6 stud is actually 1.5 inches by 5.5 inches in finished dimension, eight feet long for an 8-foot ceiling height (or nine feet long for a 9-foot ceiling, which we chose for the sunroom and living area; the bedroom and bath have an 8-foot ceiling).

Top plates are doubled (two 2×6 stacked), bottom plates are single 2×6 pressure-treated lumber bearing directly on the slab with a sill seal foam strip between the plate and the slab to prevent air leakage. The pressure treatment is required by IRC R317 because the bottom plate is in contact with concrete; untreated wood in contact with concrete will rot within five to ten years.

Sheathing is 7/16-inch oriented strand board (OSB) with a structural rating, applied with the long dimension perpendicular to the studs. OSB is fastened with 8d common nails (3 inches long) at 6 inches on centre at panel edges and 12 inches on centre in the field, per IRC Table R602.3(1).

### 7.2 The wall-bracing question

The IRC requires that exterior walls be braced against lateral loads — primarily wind, and in some places seismic. Newport News has an ASCE 7 ultimate design wind speed (Vult) of approximately 130 mph for Risk Category II buildings (residential), reflecting the city's coastal exposure to hurricanes. (Compare: Roanoke is 115 mph; Outer Banks is 150 mph; coastal Florida is 170 mph.) For our 2×6 wall on 16-inch centres with continuous 7/16-inch OSB sheathing, the prescriptive bracing provisions of IRC R602.10 are satisfied without needing to add diagonal let-in bracing or hold-downs. Our structural engineer confirms this on a one-page calculation.

For Mr and Mrs Park's two-storey ADU, the bracing requirements are stiffer because the second-storey weight increases the lateral load demand at the first-storey wall. The structural engineer specifies hold-downs at the corners (Simpson Strong-Tie HDU2 or HDU4) to anchor the wall framing to the slab, and continuous OSB sheathing without horizontal seams within the first eight feet of any corner.

### 7.3 The roof

The roof is a simple gable, 6:12 pitch (rise of 6 inches per 12 inches of run, or roughly 27 degrees), with the ridge running east-west. Trusses are used instead of stick-framed rafters; trusses are engineered, manufactured off-site, delivered on a flatbed, and craned into place in a single day. For our 32-by-25-foot footprint, we order eighteen trusses on 24-inch centres (the most common spacing for residential trusses), all 25 feet long with a 24-inch overhang at the eaves.

Truss cost: approximately $4,800 delivered, plus $800 for the crane day, plus $1,400 in installation labour. Roof sheathing is 5/8-inch OSB, plywood is also acceptable; the heavier sheathing reduces sag in long-span trusses and provides better wind resistance. Sheathing cost: approximately $1,800 in material plus $1,200 in labour.

Roof underlayment is 30-pound felt (or, increasingly common, a self-adhered synthetic underlayment such as GAF Tiger Paw or Owens Corning ProArmor), with a 36-inch-wide ice-and-water shield at the eaves and at any roof penetrations. In Climate Zone 4A, ice damming is rare but not impossible — the IRC requires the ice-and-water shield from the eave to a point 24 inches inside the wall line. For Newport News's mild winters, this is somewhat conservative, but it is cheap insurance and required by code.

Roofing material: architectural asphalt shingles, GAF Timberline HDZ or Owens Corning Duration, in a colour that matches the rancher's existing roof (typically a muted brown or grey). Cost installed: approximately $5.50 to $6.50 per square foot, so for a roof area of approximately 1,150 square feet (including overhangs), about $6,800.

### 7.4 Windows and doors

Windows are the place where energy code, comfort, and aesthetics all intersect. For a Climate Zone 4A coastal Virginia ADU, the right window is a low-E double-pane vinyl-framed casement with a U-factor of 0.27 and a SHGC of 0.30. Brand recommendations: Pella 250, Andersen 100, Marvin Essential, or one of the regional Hampton Roads suppliers (which often quote 15–20% lower than the national brands for similar performance).

For the sunroom, where the glazing area is large, we use the same window line but step up to a triple-pane in the largest east-facing unit (a 60-by-48-inch fixed picture window flanked by two 24-by-48-inch operable casements). The triple-pane unit has a U-factor of 0.20 and a SHGC of 0.30. The cost premium for triple-pane is approximately 25% over double-pane for the same size; for a single picture window in the sunroom, the premium is well worth it. The triple-pane window will also reduce sound transmission from the back yard, which Patricia will appreciate when the neighbour mows the lawn at 8 a.m. on a Saturday.

For the bedroom, the egress window must meet IRC R310: minimum opening of 5.7 square feet, minimum width of 20 inches, minimum height of 24 inches, sill not more than 44 inches above the finished floor. A 36-by-48-inch casement window meets this comfortably.

The front door is a 36-inch-wide insulated steel or fibreglass door with a nine-light glazed panel; we choose a Therma-Tru or Masonite line in a colour that complements the rancher. The door has a U-factor of 0.20 and weatherstripping on all four sides. The back service door is a 32-inch-wide insulated steel door, plain.

### 7.5 House wrap, flashing, and the air barrier

After sheathing is up but before siding goes on, the entire exterior is wrapped in a weather-resistive barrier — typically Tyvek HomeWrap or one of its competitors. The Tyvek is fastened with cap nails or staples and overlapped at seams. Around windows and doors, self-adhered flashing tape (Vycor or 3M FlexAir) is applied first to the rough opening, then the window is installed, then the Tyvek is cut and tucked over the flashing at the head and sides.

This sequence — flashing first, window second, weather-resistive barrier third over the head — is the difference between a window that stays watertight for forty years and a window that leaks within five. It is also the place where the framing crew, the window installer, and the siding crew often disagree about who is responsible for what. The contract should be unambiguous: the framer installs the Tyvek, the window installer flashes and sets the windows, and the siding crew installs the siding without disturbing either.

The air barrier — the continuous layer that prevents air leakage through the wall — is the Tyvek itself, joined at all transitions to a parallel air barrier at the slab (a strip of self-adhered membrane bridging the slab edge to the bottom of the OSB sheathing) and to the ceiling (the painted drywall ceiling, sealed at all penetrations and at the wall-ceiling junction). Air leakage is measured by a blower-door test at the end of construction; the 2021 VECC requires no more than 3 air changes per hour at 50 pascals (3 ACH50) for new residential construction in Climate Zone 4A. Typical good construction in Hampton Roads achieves 2.0 to 2.5 ACH50; tight construction (foam-sealed at every penetration, careful attention to all transitions) achieves 1.0 to 1.5 ACH50. For an 800-square-foot ADU, the difference between 3.0 and 1.5 ACH50 is approximately $200 per year in heating and cooling costs.

### 7.6 Insulation

Insulation goes in after the framing inspection passes and after the rough plumbing, electrical, and HVAC are run. For our walls, we use R-23 mineral wool batts (Roxul Comfortbatt) in the 2×6 cavity. Mineral wool is slightly more expensive than fibreglass ($1.20 per square foot versus $0.85), but it has three meaningful advantages: it does not slump over time (fibreglass batts can sag in a wall cavity, leaving gaps); it is hydrophobic (it does not absorb water if a leak occurs, and it dries out without losing R-value); and it has substantially better acoustic performance, which Patricia, sleeping in the bedroom adjacent to the noisy neighbourhood, will appreciate.

For the ceiling, we use R-49 blown cellulose. Cellulose is recycled newsprint treated with borate fire retardant; it is environmentally favourable, dense (it air-seals the attic floor better than fibreglass), and inexpensive. The cellulose is blown into the attic from a truck-mounted blower through a hose; the installer aims to a depth of approximately 14 inches (which gives R-49 at the typical density). Cost installed: approximately $1.20 per square foot of attic, so for our 800-square-foot footprint, about $960.

For the slab, we have already installed R-10 rigid foam under the slab and at the slab edge during the slab pour. We will not add additional floor insulation; the slab's R-10 plus the thermal mass of the slab itself provides comfortable floor temperatures for our climate.

Total insulation package: approximately $4,500 for the entire 800-square-foot ADU. This is one of the highest-return investments in the entire project. The marginal cost over a code-minimum package is approximately $1,500, and the energy savings over the life of the building exceed $30,000 in current dollars.

### 7.7 The vapour profile

Climate Zone 4A is mixed-humid: hot humid summers with vapor drives from outside to inside (warm humid outdoor air condensing on cool indoor surfaces), and moderately cold winters with vapor drives from inside to outside (warm humid indoor air condensing in cold wall cavities). Designing the wall to handle both directions requires a wall assembly that can dry to either side: outward in winter, inward in summer.

Our wall assembly does this:
- Interior: painted drywall (vapor-permeable, so the wall can dry inward in summer);
- Wall cavity: mineral wool insulation (hydrophobic, allows drying);
- Exterior sheathing: OSB (semi-permeable);
- Weather-resistive barrier: Tyvek HomeWrap (vapor-permeable, blocks liquid water);
- Cladding: HardiePlank with a 3/8-inch ventilated rain screen behind it (the rain screen allows airflow behind the siding, which dramatically improves drying potential).

The 3/8-inch rain screen — a furring strip of vertical 1×3 lumber installed over the Tyvek before the HardiePlank goes on — is the single best detail you can add to a Hampton Roads exterior wall. It costs approximately $0.50 per square foot of wall ($600 for our project) and reduces the moisture content of the sheathing by an average of 40% over a year, dramatically extending the life of the wall. It also reduces summer heat gain through the wall by about 15% by allowing the convective airflow behind the siding to carry away absorbed solar heat.

## Part 8. Mechanical: Heating, Cooling, and Ventilation

A properly insulated, properly air-sealed 800-square-foot building in Climate Zone 4A has remarkably modest heating and cooling loads. A Manual J calculation by the HVAC contractor — which the city building permit requires — yields these design loads for our ADU:

- Cooling design (peak summer): approximately 13,000 BTU/h sensible plus 4,000 BTU/h latent.
- Heating design (peak winter, 25°F outdoor): approximately 18,000 BTU/h.

The cooling design is dominated by the east-facing sunroom glazing (despite the low SHGC) and by the ventilation load (the air changes required by code). The heating design is dominated by the windows and the ventilation load.

### 8.1 Why a ductless mini-split

We choose a ductless mini-split heat pump rather than a traditional ducted central air conditioner with a gas furnace, for four reasons.

First, efficiency. A modern variable-speed inverter mini-split heat pump operates at a Heating Seasonal Performance Factor (HSPF) of 12 to 14 and a Seasonal Energy Efficiency Ratio (SEER2) of 22 to 25. These ratings are roughly twice the efficiency of a conventional 14-SEER, 8-HSPF central air conditioner with a gas furnace. For our design loads, the seasonal energy use is approximately:
- Cooling: 800 kWh per year.
- Heating: 1,400 kWh per year.
- Total HVAC: approximately 2,200 kWh per year, or about $310 per year at current Dominion rates.

A traditional system would use approximately 4,500 kWh per year for the same loads, costing $640 per year — a difference of $330 per year. Over a 20-year system life, the mini-split saves $6,600 in operating cost.

Second, the heat pump is electric only, with no gas. The Calloway property has gas service to the rancher (a gas water heater and a gas furnace), but extending gas service to the ADU would add roughly $2,500 in plumbing and a metered tap fee. Skipping gas entirely simplifies the project and removes the combustion-safety considerations that come with gas appliances.

Third, the mini-split is quiet. The outdoor unit, located on the north side of the ADU near the back service door, is rated at 52 dBA at one metre — quieter than a normal conversation. The indoor air handler is rated at 28 dBA on low fan speed — quieter than a whisper. Patricia will not hear it from the bedroom.

Fourth, the mini-split is zoned. Our system has two indoor air handlers — one in the open living-dining-kitchen area (a wall-mounted ductless head, 12,000 BTU/h capacity) and one ducted air handler in the mechanical alcove serving the bedroom and bathroom through short ducts (6,000 BTU/h capacity). Each zone has its own thermostat. Patricia can keep the bedroom at 68°F at night while the living area sits at 72°F during the day, without paying to overcool unused rooms.

Equipment: Mitsubishi MXZ-3C24NAHZ outdoor unit (24,000 BTU/h heating capacity at 47°F, 18,000 BTU/h cooling), with one MSZ-FH12NA wall-mount indoor unit (12,000 BTU/h) and one SVZ-KP12NA ducted indoor unit (12,000 BTU/h) — total indoor capacity slightly exceeding outdoor, which is normal practice. Equipment cost: approximately $5,800. Installation labour, refrigerant lines, electrical, and condensate drains: approximately $4,200. Total HVAC: approximately $10,000.

### 8.2 Ventilation

A tight building envelope (1.5 ACH50) does not provide enough natural air exchange for occupant health. The 2021 VECC and ASHRAE 62.2 require mechanical ventilation in tight new construction. For our 800-square-foot one-bedroom ADU with one occupant (or two), the required ventilation rate is approximately 50 cubic feet per minute (CFM) of continuous outdoor air supply.

We meet this with a Panasonic WhisperFresh Select 50 — a continuous-running bath fan with a built-in outdoor air duct and a programmable timer that can ramp up during showers. The fan is mounted in the bathroom ceiling, exhausts to the outside through an insulated 4-inch duct, and pulls 50 CFM continuously. Make-up air enters through small undercut gaps under the interior doors and through controlled inlets in the bedroom and living-area windows (small trickle vents in the window frames). Cost: $400 for the fan, $300 for installation.

For higher-end builds, an energy recovery ventilator (ERV) is preferred — it pre-conditions the incoming outdoor air against the outgoing stale air, recovering up to 70% of the temperature difference. For our budget-conscious project, the simple exhaust ventilation is adequate; for the Park's higher-budget two-storey ADU, we will specify a Panasonic Intelli-Balance 100 ERV at approximately $1,800 installed.

### 8.3 Water heating

The water heater is a 50-gallon hybrid heat-pump model — Rheem ProTerra 50 or A.O. Smith Voltex 50 — installed in the mechanical alcove. The heat pump on top of the tank pulls heat from the air in the alcove (which gets warmed by the tank's standby losses, by the air handler, and by the space itself) and uses it to heat the water. In hybrid mode, the unit operates at a uniform energy factor (UEF) of 3.45 — meaning it produces 3.45 units of heat output per unit of electricity input — compared to 0.93 for a conventional electric resistance tank. For our hot water demand (one occupant, two showers per day, dishwasher, washing machine), the annual energy use is approximately 1,000 kWh for the heat-pump unit versus 3,400 kWh for a resistance tank, a saving of $340 per year.

The heat-pump water heater costs approximately $2,100 for the unit and $900 for installation, including the condensate drain, the air-source intake louvres on the alcove door, and the 30-amp 240-volt circuit. A conventional electric tank costs approximately $700 for the unit and $400 for installation. The marginal cost of the heat-pump unit is approximately $1,900, repaying in roughly six years.

## Part 9. Plumbing: Water In, Waste Out

Plumbing is the trade where mistakes are most expensive to fix and most likely to cause invisible damage. Get it right at rough-in.

### 9.1 The water service

For our shared-utility ADU, the water supply branches off the existing house's main water line at a point just downstream of the meter, with a separate shut-off valve for the ADU. The branch is 1-inch PEX-A pipe (cross-linked polyethylene type A, the most common modern residential supply pipe) running underground from the existing house to the ADU, in a 2-inch diameter sleeve filled with sand. The supply line enters the ADU through the slab in the mechanical alcove.

Inside the ADU, the supply manifold splits into hot and cold trunks of 3/4-inch PEX, with 1/2-inch PEX home runs to each fixture. PEX is preferred over copper for several reasons: it is cheaper ($0.45 per foot for 1/2-inch PEX-A versus $2.40 for copper); it is freeze-resistant (PEX can expand up to 30% before bursting, compared to virtually no expansion for copper); it has fewer fittings (long continuous runs to each fixture eliminate concealed joints); and it is faster to install. The only place where copper is still preferred is the visible fixture stub-outs, where the aesthetic of polished copper is sometimes desired.

The total fixture unit count for our ADU is approximately 7.5 — kitchen sink (1.5), dishwasher (1.5), bathroom sink (1.0), shower (2.0), toilet (1.5). At a 1-inch supply pipe, this is well within the capacity of the line; the pressure drop from the main to the farthest fixture (the shower) is approximately 8 psi at full flow, leaving over 50 psi at the shower head, which is more than enough.

### 9.2 The drain-waste-vent (DWV) system

The DWV system is more complicated and has tighter constraints. The drains use 3-inch and 4-inch PVC pipe; the vents use 2-inch PVC. The horizontal drain pipes must slope at a minimum of 1/4 inch per foot (a 2% slope) toward the sewer lateral. Vents must extend through the roof to permit air to balance the drain system.

For our ADU:
- The kitchen sink drains into a 2-inch line that combines with the dishwasher discharge and runs under the slab to a 4-inch combination wye-and-eighth-bend, then into the 4-inch sewer lateral.
- The bathroom sink and shower drain into a 2-inch line that combines into the same 4-inch lateral.
- The toilet drains directly into the 4-inch lateral via a 4-inch closet flange set into the slab.
- The washer and dryer drain into a 2-inch standpipe in the mechanical alcove.

The 4-inch sewer lateral exits the slab on the south side of the building, runs underground at a 2% slope to a connection point with the existing house's lateral (or, if the main is closer, directly to the main), and connects via a wye fitting. The Hampton Roads Sanitation District requires a clean-out at the property line; this is a vertical 4-inch pipe with a screw cap, set in a small concrete pad.

Vents: two 2-inch vents extend from the drain system through the attic and out the roof. Each vent is flashed with a rubber boot at the roof penetration. The IRC requires that the vent terminate at least 6 inches above the roof and at least 10 feet from any window or air intake.

- Material (PEX, PVC, fixtures, valves, manifolds): $3,200
- Labour (rough-in, set, final): $5,800
- Fixtures (toilet, shower, sinks, faucets — see Part 11): included in Part 11
- Permit and inspections: $300

Total plumbing rough-in: approximately $9,300.

## Part 10. Electrical: Wiring, Service, and the Sub-panel

The ADU's electrical service is fed from the existing house's main panel via a 100-amp feeder, which is the most common arrangement and avoids a separate utility service drop. The feeder is a 100-amp 240-volt circuit with #2 copper conductors (or #1/0 aluminium, slightly cheaper) running underground in PVC conduit from the main panel to the ADU's mechanical alcove. The feeder is protected at the main panel by a 100-amp two-pole breaker.

Inside the ADU, a 100-amp 20-circuit sub-panel is mounted on the mechanical alcove wall. The sub-panel is rated for 100 amps with copper bus bars, surface-mounted (because the alcove wall is not deep enough for a flush mount with the rough-in already in place), and labelled clearly.

### 10.1 The circuit list

A typical 800-square-foot ADU has approximately 18 active circuits in the sub-panel:

1. Heat pump outdoor unit: 30-amp two-pole, #10 copper.
2. Heat pump indoor air handler 1 (wall-mount, living): 15-amp single-pole, #14 copper.
3. Heat pump indoor air handler 2 (ducted, bedroom/bath): 15-amp single-pole.
4. Water heater: 30-amp two-pole, #10 copper.
5. Cooktop (induction, 30-amp 240-volt): 30-amp two-pole, #10 copper.
6. Microwave/wall oven: 20-amp single-pole, #12 copper.
7. Dishwasher: 15-amp single-pole, #14 copper.
8. Refrigerator: 20-amp single-pole, dedicated.
9. Washer: 20-amp single-pole, dedicated.
10. Dryer (if heat-pump dryer, which we recommend): 30-amp two-pole; if conventional electric: 30-amp two-pole.
11. Bathroom GFCI: 20-amp single-pole, #12 copper, GFCI breaker.
12. Kitchen counter GFCI x2: 20-amp single-pole each, #12 copper, GFCI breakers (two circuits required by IRC E3703.2 for kitchens).
13. General-purpose lighting and outlets (sunroom, living, bedroom, hallway): 15-amp single-pole, #14 copper, multiple circuits (typically 3–4 for a small ADU).
14. Smoke and carbon monoxide alarms (interconnected, hardwired): 15-amp single-pole.
15. Exterior outlets and lighting (front porch, back service entry, sunroom): 20-amp single-pole, GFCI/weather-resistant.

Total connected load is approximately 95 amps if everything ran simultaneously, but the demand factor (the realistic concurrent draw) is approximately 50–60 amps, well within the 100-amp service.

### 10.2 The 2020 NEC and AFCI/GFCI requirements

The National Electrical Code, adopted in Virginia as the Virginia Electrical Code, requires Arc-Fault Circuit-Interrupter (AFCI) protection on virtually all general-use 15- and 20-amp branch circuits in dwelling units (NEC 210.12). Combined AFCI/GFCI breakers are now standard in residential sub-panels. Each AFCI/GFCI breaker costs approximately $35 to $55 versus $4 to $7 for a standard breaker, but they detect both ground faults and arcing faults that cause electrical fires. For the 18 circuits in our sub-panel, the AFCI/GFCI premium is roughly $700 over standard breakers — money very well spent.

### 10.3 Lighting

We use LED throughout. The 2021 VECC requires high-efficacy lamps in at least 90% of permanently installed fixtures. Recessed cans in the ceiling (4-inch and 6-inch) are sealed-and-gasketed to maintain the air barrier (an unsealed recessed can is one of the most significant air-leakage paths in residential construction). The sunroom has a single ceiling fan with integrated LED, switched at the entry.

Smart switches and dimmers are not required by code but are useful for accessibility. We use Lutron Caseta dimmers in the living area, the bedroom, and the sunroom; Patricia can control them from a small remote on the bedside table or a smartphone if she chooses.

## Part 11. The Interior: Drywall, Flooring, Cabinets, and the Hundred Small Decisions That Make It Feel Like a Home

After the framing inspection passes and the insulation is in, the inside transforms from a forest of studs into a series of rooms. Make your finish choices early; lock them in writing; do not change them mid-project unless absolutely necessary.

## Part 14. The First Year of Living With It: Property Tax, Insurance, Utilities, and Renting

The C of O is issued on a Wednesday afternoon in late August 2027. Patricia moves in on a Saturday in early September. The dogwood tree drops its first leaves a few weeks later. Marcus and his wife stand in the kitchen of the rancher and watch through the back-yard window as the lights in the new cottage come on at dusk for the first time.

The first year of life with the new building has a paperwork side and a daily-life side. Both matter.

### 14.1 Property tax reassessment

The Newport News Real Estate Assessor's office is notified by Codes Compliance when the C of O is issued. Within sixty to ninety days, an assessor visits the property, measures the new structure, and adds it to the assessment roll for the next assessment cycle. The property's assessed value increases by approximately 60 to 75 per cent of the construction cost — the assessor uses a depreciated replacement cost methodology, not the actual project cost, so the assessment increase is typically less than the dollars spent.

For Marcus, the property's prior assessed value was $312,000 (before the ADU). After the ADU, the assessed value is reassessed at $445,000. At the Newport News real estate tax rate of $1.20 per $100 of assessed value (mid-2026 rate), the annual property tax increase is approximately $1,600 per year. Marcus knows this is coming and has budgeted for it.

### 14.2 Homeowner's insurance

The homeowner's insurance policy must be amended to add the new structure. Most insurers handle the ADU as an additional structure on the same policy (Coverage B in standard ISO HO-3 policies), but some treat it as a separate dwelling requiring its own policy. The cost varies: for an 800-square-foot ADU added to an existing rancher policy, the typical premium increase is $400 to $700 per year. Marcus's State Farm policy increases by $540 per year.

Flood insurance is *not* required for Marcus's lot because it is in flood zone X. For Ms Robinson, whose lot is partially in the 0.2% annual chance zone, her lender requires flood insurance for the new structure; the annual premium under the National Flood Insurance Program's Risk Rating 2.0 methodology is approximately $720 per year for the ADU alone.

### 14.3 Utilities

The first year of utility costs for an 800-square-foot all-electric ADU in Newport News, with one occupant, are approximately:

- Electricity (HVAC + water heater + cooking + lights + everything): roughly 4,500 kWh per year, at Dominion Energy rates of approximately 14.2 cents per kWh, for an annual cost of about $640.
- Water and sewer (Newport News Waterworks plus HRSD): approximately $480 per year for one occupant.
- Trash (already included in primary dwelling's service): no incremental cost.
- Internet (if a separate service): approximately $720 per year, or zero if the primary dwelling's internet is shared via Wi-Fi extender.

Total first-year operating cost: approximately $1,840.

### 14.4 The deeper return

Marcus did not build the cottage to make money. He built it because his mother needed somewhere to live and his family needed her to live somewhere safe and close. The financial return on the project — in narrow terms — is the rental value Patricia would otherwise have paid for assisted-living-equivalent housing, which in Newport News in 2027 is approximately $3,200 per month for a one-bedroom in a senior community. Over five years (a reasonable estimate of the time Patricia will be able to live independently), the saved housing cost is roughly $192,000, which is nearly the full cost of the ADU.

The deeper return is the part the spreadsheet does not capture. Patricia, after eight months in the cottage, is sleeping better than she has in three years. Marcus's children walk across the back yard to her sunroom every Sunday afternoon for tea. The dogwood tree, in March, blooms full white through the east windows, and Patricia tells Marcus that it is the most beautiful thing she has seen in a long time.

## Part 15. The Park ADU and the Robinson ADU: Two Different Endings

We have followed Mr Calloway's project in detail. The Park and Robinson projects illustrate variations.

### 15.1 The Park two-storey ADU

The Parks' lot is narrow — twenty-four feet of usable back yard — which forces the ADU to be tall rather than wide. We design a 720-square-foot two-storey unit on a 24-by-15-foot footprint. The first floor is a sunroom (south-facing, the long dimension) plus a half-bath plus a small office area; the second floor is a bedroom, a full bath, and a small kitchenette. An external steel exterior staircase on the side of the building (compliant with IRC R311.7 stair geometry — 7.75-inch maximum riser, 10-inch minimum tread, 36-inch minimum width, handrails on both sides) serves as the second-floor access.

The structural design is more complex than Mr Calloway's: the slab edge is 24 inches deep (versus 18 inches), the second-floor system is engineered I-joists at 16-inch centres with 3/4-inch tongue-and-groove subfloor, the lateral system uses Simpson Strong-Tie hold-downs at the corners, and the roof is a low-slope (4:12) gable to keep the overall height at the city's 25-foot limit while preserving an 8-foot ceiling on the second floor.

The Parks' ADU initially functions as Helen's home office (first floor) and a guest bedroom (second floor). Their daughter goes to college in 2034; at that point, the Parks intend to convert the unit to a one-bedroom rental. The ADU is designed with that future in mind: the kitchenette on the second floor is plumbed for a full kitchen even though only a sink, a microwave, and a small refrigerator are installed initially, and the half-bath on the first floor is sized so that it can later be converted to a full bath if needed.

Total project cost: approximately $241,000. Estimated rental value in 2034 (in 2026 dollars): approximately $1,750 per month, or $21,000 per year — a 9.5% gross yield on the ADU's construction cost, which is excellent for residential real estate.

### 15.2 The Robinson East End ADU

Ms Robinson's project is the most constrained and the most rewarding. The 0.11-acre corner lot has setback constraints (15 feet on the corner side, 10 feet on the rear, 5 feet on the interior side), the high water table, the proximity of the oak tree, and the fact that the East End is a historic and culturally important neighbourhood — Janelle wants the ADU to feel right, not like a dropped-in suburban product.

The design is a 600-square-foot detached cottage in a vernacular Cape Cod style, with cedar shake siding (matching the primary dwelling's siding, which Janelle had restored in 2023), a 12:12 steep gable roof, a small front porch with turned wood columns, and double-hung windows with traditional six-over-six muntin patterns. The interior is a single great room (sunroom-living-kitchen open plan) with a bedroom and full bath partitioned at the back. The total floor area is right at the size cap of 50% of the primary dwelling's 1,140 square feet; we draw to 565 square feet to leave a margin.

Janelle's financing is the FHA 203(k) renovation loan, which allows her to roll the construction cost into a refinanced first mortgage at favourable rates (currently 6.25% for a 30-year fixed, 0.5% above the standard FHA rate). The project takes longer than the others because the FHA process requires multiple inspections and a HUD-approved consultant; total time from first sketch to C of O is twenty-two months.

Total project cost: approximately $178,000. Janelle rents the unit for $1,400 per month — a slightly below-market rate she chose deliberately because she wants a long-term tenant. Her gross yield is 9.4%; her net yield after taxes, insurance, vacancy allowance, and maintenance reserves is approximately 6.5%, comparable to a well-managed REIT.

## Closing: The Tuesday Morning, Eighteen Months Later

It is a Tuesday morning in early March 2028. The dogwood tree at the back of Marcus Calloway's lot is in full white bloom for the third spring in a row. Patricia is sitting in the sunroom of the cottage, with a book and a cup of tea, looking out at it. She is eighty-three. The cottage has been her home for almost eighteen months. She has had two minor falls; both happened in the bathroom of the sunroom-bedroom-bath-kitchen unit, and both were caught by the grab bars she initially thought were unnecessary. The HVAC has not failed. The roof has not leaked. The kitchen, after eighteen months of small dinners and many pots of tea, looks slightly used but not abused.

Marcus walks across the back yard at 8 a.m. to drop off the newspaper. He stays for ten minutes. They talk about nothing important. Then he goes to work.

This is what an ADU is for. Not the rental yield, not the property tax line, not the architectural compatibility, not the SB 531 by-right approval, not the Manual J calculation, not the foundation detail, not the 130 mph wind speed and not the R-23 mineral wool, but this: a small building, on the same lot as a larger building, that lets the people you love live near you for the years that they have left, comfortably and with dignity.

Everything else in this article — every section, every table, every cited code, every line item, every Hampton Roads contractor's quote, every paragraph on flashing or PEX or AFCI breakers — is in service of that.

If you are standing on your back step on a Tuesday morning with a tape measure, looking at a patch of grass, and wondering whether to take the leap, the answer in Newport News in 2026, after the October 2024 ordinance and before SB 531 takes effect, is yes. The law is on your side. The building code is on your side. The market is on your side. Your contractor, if you choose well, will be on your side.

Take the tape measure. Walk the lot. Sketch the cottage. Call the planner. The first conversation with the planner is free, and it is, in retrospect, the most useful hour of the entire project.

Then, when the time comes, pour a slab.
